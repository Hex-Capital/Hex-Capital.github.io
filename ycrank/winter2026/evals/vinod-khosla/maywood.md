# Maywood -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The customer testimonial tells me everything I need to know: "doubled capacity" and "on pace to double deal count next year with the same headcount." That is the consequence of success. Investment bankers produce CIMs and pitch decks faster. Banks run more deals per analyst. The $5 trillion M&A market continues to function exactly as it does today -- the same intermediaries, the same fee structures, the same information asymmetries -- just with fewer twenty-five-year-olds pulling all-nighters in midtown Manhattan. If Maywood fully succeeds, the world operates identically, except Goldman Sachs needs fewer associates. That is the textbook definition of reducing risk to the point where the consequences of success are, frankly, not worth my attention.

The team composition reinforces this concern. Drake Goodman spent his career at Blackstone. Kent Goodman did enterprise automation at BCG. Esteban Vizcaino did AI transformation at Balyasny. These are insiders who know exactly what's painful about M&A execution -- and that's the problem. They've built precisely the tool that an ex-Blackstone analyst would build: a faster way to generate the deliverables they used to create manually. Nobody in this room is asking the dangerous question: why does M&A require this intermediary layer at all? Why do we need CIMs when the underlying data could flow directly between principals? Why does a $50 million middle-market deal require $2 million in advisory fees for work that is fundamentally information synthesis? Those questions would terrify the banks Maywood is selling to, which is exactly why they're the right questions. Instead, Maywood is making the banks' existing workflow more efficient -- and the banks love it. When your customers enthusiastically validate your approach, you're probably not disrupting them.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. Square started as a dongle that made existing card payments easier for small merchants -- not exactly revolutionary-sounding -- and ended up rebuilding financial infrastructure for an entire class of businesses. Could Maywood's document generation layer become the operating system for deal execution, eventually shifting power dynamics in M&A? Possibly. The compliance moat is real: SOC 2 Type II at pre-seed stage, single-tenant deployment, self-hosted options -- that represents genuine investment that horizontal AI tools won't replicate casually. And working with "several banks" before your YC batch concludes is meaningful traction in an industry where procurement cycles typically run six to twelve months. The team is also exceptionally well-assembled for this specific problem -- domain expertise, AI research, and enterprise deployment experience all mapped to the venture's critical risks. If I were evaluating this as a business, not as a world-changing bet, I'd say the founders have done nearly everything right.

But I'm not evaluating businesses. I'm evaluating bets on the future. The technology here -- LLM-based document generation with firm-specific fine-tuning -- is a capability that Datasite, with $2 billion in funding and an aggressive acquisition strategy (BlueFlame AI, Grata, SourceScrub), can replicate or acquire. The moat is compliance infrastructure and domain tuning, not a fundamental technology discontinuity. When I backed Juniper, Cisco's CTO told me they'd never build a TCP/IP router -- the incumbents couldn't see the architecture shift. Here, Datasite is already buying AI companies. The incumbents see it clearly, which means the disruption potential is incremental, not structural. Maywood is competing on execution speed in a race the incumbents have already entered. The dossier itself acknowledges this: "Large language model providers could enable competitors to build similar generation capabilities." That's not the kind of asymmetry I invest in.

The timing is fine but not early. Everyone knows LLMs can generate structured documents -- this is 2026, not 2019 when I wrote my OpenAI check into what most people considered a research curiosity. AI for financial services is consensus. The 13.84% CAGR projection for AI agents in financial services is the kind of linear market forecast that tells me the exponential moment has already been priced in by the market's expectations. Maywood arrived at the party on time, not early. I look for founders building into a trajectory that won't be obvious for another two to three years. Document generation for investment banks is obvious today.

These founders are clearly talented -- Wharton summa cum laude, MIT EECS, Blackstone, BCG, Balyasny is a formidable combination. And that's what frustrates me. They've aimed their considerable abilities at a problem whose solution, even at maximum success, is a profitable vertical SaaS company serving investment banks. Not the transformation of how capital gets allocated. Not the elimination of information asymmetries that make M&A advisory necessary. Not a reimagination of how companies are bought and sold. A better content generation tool for the people already doing the work. I'd tell them what I tell every team this capable working on a problem this contained: go find something that actually scares you.
