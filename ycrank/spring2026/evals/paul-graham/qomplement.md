# qomplement -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Kerim Taray and Andres Garza are a two-person pair out of Tec de Monterrey who built RPA implementations for Mexican enterprises before qomplement, and they are now selling AI agents that read POs, freight docs and carrier invoices into legacy ERPs. The signature of qomplement in my portfolio is this: a strong long-collaboration pair attacking a problem they were literally paid to do by hand, with a thin hacker-trail and a Product Hunt page from May 2025 describing a totally different product. Organic founder-problem fit at the top of the stack, hacker genealogy at the bottom -- and a mid-stream pivot sitting in the middle making me squint.

The closest comparison on the founder-problem-fit axis is the Collisons at **Stripe** (2010), who had been hacking commerce flows at **Auctomatic** (2007) before they built the API they wanted to use. The mechanism that makes the analogy load-bearing is the same: founders who did the unglamorous version of the work for money, then realized the tool they kept rebuilding was the actual company. Kerim and Andres did RPA consulting in Mexico, watched grocery and trucking customers reconcile POs in spreadsheets, and qomplement is the agent layer that replaces the bots they used to ship. Where qomplement diverges from the Stripe rung is that the Collisons hit the commerce-API problem in their own product first; Kerim and Andres hit it in someone else's workflow. That's organic, but it's client-pain organic, not USB-stick organic. I'd score it like Stripe minus a tier.

The dimension where qomplement is weakest is hacker genealogy, and the failure mode I worry about is the polished AI-wrapper team from my own pass example -- credentials without a GitHub trail. The dossier says "No public repos found" for both founders. Tec de Monterrey CS degrees and a university AI research lab project on 15,000 minutes of wildlife footage is real technical work, but it's invisible to the hacker tribe in the way Patrick Collison's Young Scientist Lisp prize was visible before **Stripe** (2010). I don't need an open-source résumé to write a check -- Drew Houston didn't have one either -- but I do need a demo I can touch, and the dossier gives me a Cal.com demo link and a self-reported "10,000 documents/day across 20+ companies" with zero named logos. The pattern doesn't fully appear -- they have shipped something -- but it's adjacent.

On animal resourcefulness, the mechanism analysis is what concerns me. qomplement's Product Hunt page on May 3, 2025 launched "an AI agent that controls your personal computer," and twelve months later the YC company page is selling Agentic ERP for grocery and trucking. Pivots compound either into resourcefulness (Kan and Shear came back from **Kiko** (2005) and built **Twitch** (2007)) or into flailing. The Kiko→Twitch pattern had a clean batch separation and prior YC genealogy; qomplement's pivot happened mid-stream without that vouching layer. The redeeming evidence is that the new direction is exactly where their pre-YC RPA work pointed -- they ran toward what they already knew. I read that as animal behavior pulled back from a bad first answer, not flailing. Worth 20/35, not 25.

On schlep blindness, the counterfactual: for qomplement's score to flip a tier up, Pivot's $70M Series B (SiliconANGLE, May 2026) would have to not exist. The schlep is real -- carrier invoices in non-standard formats, item master updates, twenty separate ERP integrations -- and the schlep is exactly the kind of work software-only YC applicants route around. But schlep-as-moat is a moat only when nobody else is grinding the same schlep, and Pivot has €34.4M of European procurement-OS work already in market processing €2.5B of invoices a year. **Coinbase** (2012)'s compliance grind was a moat because no other YC applicant in the 2012 batch wanted to touch bitcoin regulation; qomplement is in a schlep zone that a well-funded Paris team has already mapped.

On cofounder configuration, the single-anchor parallel is the Collison brothers -- two technical co-founders, multi-year prior working relationship, complementary roles. Kerim and Andres met at Tec de Monterrey studying CS together, worked in the same university AI research lab, and were prior co-CTOs of a startup before qomplement. That's a stronger pre-startup collaboration history than most YC pairs walk in with. It's not brothers, but it's the next-closest thing on this axis, and the dimension reads cleanly positive.

My 36-month forecast on qomplement: $4-10M ARR in retail-grocery and trucking document operations by mid-2029, then a strategic acquisition by a logistics or retail-ERP incumbent -- Manhattan Associates, Blue Yonder, or NetSuite -- in the $50-120M range, as Pivot's horizontal expansion squeezes the vertical and SAP ships native agentic overlays inside the systems qomplement integrates with. Not the company that wins the No. 1 slot in a batch the way "Black Swan Farming" (September 2012) demands. A good outcome, not the outcome I'm farming for.

The strongest case against my forecast is the schlep-blindness bull mechanism: qomplement is doing per-customer document template work in grocery and trucking that horizontal players like Pivot will not stomach as they scale, and **Coinbase** (2012) taught me that schlep zones compound into moats over five-year windows in ways that look invisible at the seed stage. The bear evidence that wins for me is the unverified-logo claim ("largest grocery chains and trucking companies in the world" with no names) combined with the pivot from a totally unrelated PC-agent product twelve months ago -- two tells, stacked, that the founders may not yet have the conviction-from-customer-pull that turns a vertical schlep into a Coinbase. The condition that would flip my verdict is a named Fortune 500 retail or trucking logo with a public reference, plus a public artifact -- a doc-extraction benchmark, a GitHub repo, anything I can touch -- that confirms the technical genealogy is real and not just credentialed.
