# Unisson -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that jumped out at me isn't the product or the founders -- it's the competitive landscape, and what it tells me about where this company sits in the consensus cycle. Sierra just hit $100M ARR at a $10B valuation building AI agents for customer teams. Wonderful raised $134M. ChurnZero is adding AI agents. Totango and Catalyst merged and launched something literally called "Unison AI" in the same space. When I see a pre-seed company entering a category where multiple well-funded players are already operating, with a name that's nearly identical to an incumbent's product launch, I don't feel a jolt of energy. I feel the absence of one. The most promising companies I've backed -- SpaceX, Crusoe, Contraline -- were operating in spaces nobody else wanted to touch. AI agents for enterprise customer success in 2026 is not that. This is one of the most competed categories in B2B software right now, and that's exactly the kind of consensus I've trained myself to walk away from.

I always start with the founder's story, and here I'm working with very little. Varun went from UIUC to Peloton internship to Ambient.ai for five years, where he built agent products and worked on vision-language models. Tom came through Amazon and Chef Robotics, where he led perception engineering, and has some interesting quantum computing and ML research on his GitHub. These are capable engineers with relevant AI experience. But I can't find the thread that ties either of them to this specific problem -- the lived experience of being a technical CSM watching customer deployments stall because the one person who understood the product was on vacation, or the frustration of building integrations at 2 AM because tribal knowledge lived in someone's head and nowhere else. They're applying AI skills they developed elsewhere to a domain that looks promising from the outside. That's skill seeking a market, not a founder who can't stop themselves from solving a problem that haunts them. When I backed the Contraline founder, what struck me was that he'd spent years in academic research on male contraception when nobody cared -- his biography was inseparable from the mission. I don't see that biographical inevitability here.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. The positioning wedge is genuinely thoughtful: targeting internal technical teams -- sales engineers, implementation specialists, CS managers -- rather than the end-customer interaction that Sierra and Wonderful own. That's a real gap. If these agents can truly learn a complex product in 15-20 minutes and reliably execute multi-step tasks like health audits and custom integrations, the accumulated product knowledge becomes a switching cost. Varun's five years building agent products at Ambient.ai is directly relevant -- he's not a tourist in agentic AI. And the concept of an AI that functions as a subject-matter expert, available 24/7, could be the seed of something much larger: autonomous professional services. If the technical execution is exceptional and they can lock in design partners quickly through YC, this narrow wedge could expand. I can see why Diana Hu backed them.

But even granting all of that, this company doesn't activate the dimensions I care about most. It doesn't create economic access for people who lack it -- it makes enterprise knowledge workers more efficient, which is valuable but not the kind of positive-sum outcome I look for in my best investments. Uber created income for hundreds of thousands of drivers. Affirm gave transparent credit to people shut out by traditional finance. Unisson helps well-compensated CS teams at mid-market SaaS companies do their jobs faster. It doesn't make an invisible system visible or transparent in the way Carta revealed what was hidden in cap table spreadsheets. There's no regulatory moat, no stigmatized category that deters other investors, no complexity that rewards patience. It's a clean, well-positioned B2B SaaS play in a hot space. And for me, clean and well-positioned in a hot space is exactly the profile I pass on. I need the mess. I need the thing that makes other investors uncomfortable.

On co-founder dynamics, I have almost nothing to work with. No shared history at the same company or university, no public record of how they met, no signal about how they handle disagreement. After HQ Trivia taught me how devastating unresolved co-founder conflict can be, I probe this hard. The absence of information isn't a red flag on its own, but it means I can't give credit here either. Two technically strong people who found each other in the Bay Area AI ecosystem -- that's a starting point, not a tested partnership.

I respect the execution capability these founders bring and the precision of their market positioning. In a different framework, this might be a solid seed bet. But I invest my own money based on conviction that forms outside of consensus, and everything about this opportunity -- the category, the timing, the competitive intensity, the founder-market connection -- reads as consensus to me. This is a pass.
