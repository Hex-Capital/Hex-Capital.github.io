# Carson -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the tagline: "more capable than Cowork, more secure than OpenClaw." When your positioning is defined entirely by reference to two incumbents backed by the two largest AI labs on earth, you've already conceded the frame. You're not creating a new category — you're filling a gap in someone else's category. And gaps get filled. Anthropic can ship enterprise controls on Cowork in a quarter. The question isn't whether the gap exists today; it's whether you own something structural that persists after the incumbents notice you. I don't see what that is.

The specific knowledge test is where this falls apart for me. The founders are smart — Harvard CS, Stanford MS, Palantir, Robust Intelligence, Snowflake. That's an impressive engineering résumé. But what do they know about sales workflows that a competent team couldn't learn in six months of customer discovery? Palantir teaches you how to build secure enterprise software for government and defense. It doesn't teach you non-obvious truths about how sales teams actually create value, where the real friction points are, or what the sales process looks like when it's genuinely reimagined rather than automated. The team's specific knowledge is in building secure enterprise platforms — which is a skill, not a secret. When I backed Notion, Ivan Zhao had spent years obsessing over the relationship between tools and thought. That's knowledge you can't acquire from an employer. Carson's founders have professional expertise from prestigious employers, which is precisely the kind of knowledge that can be taught — and that competitors already possess.

The leverage architecture concerns me equally. Carson isn't creating new leverage — it's repackaging leverage created by foundation model companies and adding an enterprise wrapper. The dynamic UI generation concept is clever product design, but it's a feature, not a new form of leverage. Compare this to what Replit does: it gives people who couldn't code before the ability to build software. That's a qualitatively new capability. Carson gives sales teams who already use CRMs and outreach tools a slightly more unified interface with better access controls. That's incremental improvement in an existing workflow, not the creation of a new category of human capability. And the per-seat SaaS model is the most standard monetization in enterprise software — there's no permissionless, protocol-level, or network-effect-driven leverage mechanism here.

The bull case would require believing three things simultaneously: that the dynamic UI generation approach becomes a genuine technical moat as it accumulates workflow patterns across customers, that enterprise procurement cycles and security certifications (SOC 2, FedRAMP) create durable switching costs before incumbents add the same features, and that Milan Bhandari's experience scaling Bolto to $1M+ revenue translates into rapid execution in this adjacent but different domain. If all three are true, you get a company that builds a defensible wedge in enterprise AI agents before the market consolidates. Bhandari's prior founding experience is real signal — taking a company through YC, raising $5.6M, and reaching seven-figure revenue demonstrates execution ability. And Ketan Agrawal's time at Robust Intelligence (acquired by Cisco for its AI security capabilities) does provide genuine knowledge about AI security that maps to Carson's positioning. But even in the optimistic case, you're betting on a feature advantage in enterprise security holding long enough to build switching costs, against competitors with orders of magnitude more capital. That's a race, not a compounding position.

The timing is wrong by my framework. AI agents for sales is one of the most consensus categories in venture right now — Clay at $3.1B, Artisan at $46M raised, 11x at $230M+, Salesforce building Agentforce, HubSpot adding native agents. Everyone agrees AI will transform sales workflows. When everyone agrees, the asymmetric upside has been priced away. The best angel returns come from bets that look wrong — Twitter when microblogging seemed trivial, Uber when ride-sharing was illegal, Bitcoin when it was nerd money. Carson is entering a market where every smart investor already has a thesis and a position. You don't get paid for being right when everyone else is also right.

One additional concern: Milan Bhandari's departure from Bolto, which is still active and growing, raises the integrity-adjacent question of commitment and founder-problem authenticity. If your previous company is still running with co-founders and $1M+ in revenue, what drew you away? Sometimes the answer is that you found a bigger problem. But it can also signal that the founder's passion is for founding itself rather than for the specific problem — the exact pattern I filter against. Without understanding the nature and timing of that transition, it introduces uncertainty about long-term orientation.
