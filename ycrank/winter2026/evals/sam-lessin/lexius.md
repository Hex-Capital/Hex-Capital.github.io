# Lexius -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's my problem with Lexius: five companies have already raised a combined $300 million-plus to do essentially the same thing. Veesion has €53M and 5,000 stores across 25 countries. Everseen has ~$90M and serves 10 of the top 20 global retailers. Spot AI raised $93M. ArcadianAI literally uses the identical tagline -- "AI Security Guards" -- with the same camera-agnostic, behavior-driven approach. When I describe the best investments as a sane person with an insane idea, Lexius is the structural opposite: competent people with one of the most obviously good ideas in the current AI landscape. "Retail theft costs $112 billion, stores already have cameras, let's add AI" is a pitch that every VC on Sand Hill Road has already heard and funded. The returns in this category have been priced in by the market. David Elskamp and Liam Webster are clearly sharp -- founding a media agency at 14, Berkeley EECS, privacy research at BLUES Lab -- but nothing in their background provides the kind of domain-specific insanity that makes me lean in. These are talented young technologists pursuing the obvious opportunity, which is exactly how you end up in a knife fight with well-capitalized incumbents.

The deeper structural issue is that this is precisely the category I'm most bearish on: pure software where AI IS the product, not where AI is the cherry on top. My whole framework asks "would this business be good if AI didn't exist?" Strip the computer vision models from Lexius and there is literally nothing left -- no physical operations, no hardware installed, no regulatory moat, no protocol layer. The sub-10-minute remote installation they proudly advertise is actually the tell. If it takes ten minutes to deploy, it takes ten minutes for a retailer to swap you out for Veesion, or for Hikvision to ship an analytics update to their cameras, or for Google to launch a retail security product using Gemini's video understanding capabilities. Every improvement in foundation models for video comprehension makes Lexius's specific model training less differentiated. This is a SaaS business in the purest sense -- recurring software revenue with no physical-world moat -- and I've been saying publicly since 2024 that the SaaS era is over.

The one structurally interesting element is the cross-store known offender database. A shared intelligence layer connecting retailers to identify repeat offenders across chains has genuine network effect potential -- each additional store increases value for all participants. That's directionally a primitive, not an application. But Auror already has 45,000 stores and 3,000 law enforcement agencies in their crime intelligence network. The network has already been seeded at scale by someone else. Building a competing network from scratch at pre-seed against an entrenched player with NZ$82M in funding and institutional partnerships isn't impossible, but it requires a differentiated wedge that I don't see articulated here.

The bull case deserves genuine consideration. The enterprise logos -- 7-Eleven, Erewhon, Prada -- are remarkable for a pre-seed company. The case study metrics are tangible: 63% theft reduction at FineFare in 30 days, $3,000 monthly savings per store at Erewhon, 4.7x ROI in six weeks for an unnamed clothing retailer. If those are paid deployments rather than free pilots, this company has demonstrated real commercial traction that most pre-seed startups cannot match. The privacy-first approach using behavioral detection instead of facial recognition positions them favorably as the EU AI Act and U.S. biometric privacy laws tighten -- competitors relying on facial recognition databases like Facewatch face genuine regulatory headwinds. And the "retailers managing over 4,000 stores" waitlist, if real, suggests demand pull rather than sales push. What would have to be true for this to be great: Lexius would need to convert those enterprise logos into multi-location rollouts, build the offender database into a genuine network moat before Auror or Veesion lock up the ecosystem, and maintain technical differentiation long enough for switching costs to accumulate. It's a plausible path, but it requires winning a competitive sprint against opponents with 50-100x more capital, and the structural dynamics of AI commoditization work against them more with every passing quarter.

The narrative can expand -- shoplifting detection today, broader physical security and safety monitoring tomorrow (fall detection is already a feature), workplace safety, warehouse loss prevention, hospital security. But "AI video analytics for everything" is exactly where Spot AI is already positioned with $93M in funding. The expansion story isn't proprietary narrative space. It's shared territory with multiple well-funded competitors, which caps the "infinity optionality" I need to see at seed. This isn't like Helium where decentralized wireless infrastructure was a narrative that only Helium owned. This is a narrative that half a dozen companies are telling simultaneously with similar credibility.

I pass. The founders are capable, the market is real, and the early traction signals are genuinely impressive for the stage. But this is a consensus opportunity in a crowded category where the moat is pure software, AI is the product rather than the amplifier, and the competitive dynamics require outrunning opponents with orders-of-magnitude more capital. I'd rather fund a physical security company that uses AI to optimize guard deployment than an AI company trying to replace guards with software -- one gets stronger as AI improves, the other gets more commoditized.
