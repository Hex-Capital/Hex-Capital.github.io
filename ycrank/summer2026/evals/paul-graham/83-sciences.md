# 83 Sciences -- Paul Graham Evaluation

83 Sciences is what happens when a real MIT hacker gets wrapped in two BCG/HBS operators before the check clears -- Eric Riesel is the lead author on a JACS paper (Sep 2024) using generative ML to reconstruct crystal structures from powder diffraction, which is precisely the problem 83 Sciences is now productizing, and yet his two co-founders' bios read like the manager-mode team I warned about in "Founder Mode" (September 2024). The dimensional signature is a strong organic-idea score sitting on top of a weak animal-resourcefulness score, and that pairing tells me exactly what 83 Sciences is: a graduate student's honest research problem that got a professionalized go-to-market layer bolted on before anyone did anything scrappy.

Dropbox (2007) is the load-bearing analogy for the one dimension where 83 Sciences scores highest -- Drew Houston kept losing his USB stick at MIT, so he built the thing he needed, and the founder-problem fit was so tight it made the market obvious in retrospect. 83 Sciences has the equivalent thread: Eric Riesel spent his PhD in the Freedman Group at MIT working on exactly the "AI-on-messy-experimental-data" problem, and the Crystalyze work published at 67% accuracy on RRUFF patterns is the artifact-in-hand that says he lived the pain of unstructured lab data before anyone told him it was a startup. Where 83 Sciences diverges from Dropbox is that Houston was the CEO and the technical lead and the demo shipper all in one person; at 83 Sciences the Riesel thread is one-third of the founding narrative, diluted by Ian Naccarella (HBS MBA, ex-BCG Climate) and Yankang Yang (BCG Principal, "led BCG's internal AI program to 30,000+ users"), which is a resume for winning a McKinsey RFP, not for staying up all night to write code.

The Pass Example in my own file is the pattern I keep watching for -- the polished two-MBA team with a glossy deck, a TAM slide, and a "we'll hire engineers post-funding" plan -- and 83 Sciences has to be checked against it, because Naccarella and Yang together outnumber Riesel two-to-one. Does the pattern appear? Partly. The dossier shows zero shipped artifact from Naccarella or Yang, zero GitHub across the entire founding team, and the Precedence Research $251M TAM number is exactly the kind of sizing that gets dropped into a pre-seed deck to make a room nod. What rescues 83 Sciences from a clean invocation of that pass is that Riesel is not a hire, he is a founder, and the JACS paper is not a slide, it is an artifact. But when I ask my own animal-test question -- what did these three do before the YC check that was hard, unscalable, and revealed appetite for grind -- the dossier is silent. No cereal boxes. No pre-funding pilot revenue. No demo the way Armstrong's Android bitcoin wallet was a demo.

The schlep dimension is where 83 Sciences compounds well over 18 months if the founders don't flinch. "Deploy in-person to labs to capture experimental data at source" is not a SaaS phrase -- it is the same class of work that made Coinbase (2012)'s compliance grind into a moat, because materials R&D labs treat unpublished experimental data as IP, and negotiating access one lab at a time is exactly the offline schlep that CuspAI (with $130M and reportedly closing $400M at $2.6B) will not do because their culture is compute-and-model, not lab-and-handshake. Every signed data-sharing agreement compounds; every failed one teaches them where the IP walls actually sit. This is the one dimension where I lean toward 83 Sciences and away from the well-funded competitor set.

The counterfactual on hacker genealogy is what would have to be true to move 83 Sciences up a tier: a second founder with a shipped technical artifact. Riesel alone is real -- the pubs.acs.org DOI resolves, the crystalyze.org interface exists, MIT News covered it -- but the persona pattern is that hacker taste compounds when it's shared across the founding team, the way Patrick and John Collison both wrote code before Stripe (2010). At 83 Sciences the technical density is 1-of-3, and neither Naccarella nor Yang has a GitHub, an open-source contribution, or a shipping story in the dossier. If Yang had shipped even one internal ML tool during his BCG AI rollout with a public writeup, I would score this higher; the dossier gives me only headcount ("30,000+ users") which is a manager metric, not a hacker one.

Airbnb (2009) is the single-anchor parallel for co-founder configuration -- Chesky, Gebbia, and Blecharczyk had lived together and worked together for years before the cereal-box moment, which was why the animal test surfaced when I asked about the boxes on the table. 83 Sciences has three founders and a plausible connection ("Naccarella was Entrepreneurial Venture Fellow at The Engine and Riesel is Frontier Year Fellow at Engine Ventures"), but the dossier explicitly marks that link as "[Inferred]" and offers no history of prior joint building. It's a trio, not a solo, which clears the "What We Look for in Founders" (October 2010) minimum bar, but it's the ecosystem-introduction kind of trio rather than the dorm-roommate kind.

My 36-month forecast for 83 Sciences: they land 3-5 named enterprise pilots by month 18, reach $1-3M ARR, and get quietly acquired by a materials incumbent (Citrine, Uncountable) or absorbed as a data-team acquihire into CuspAI at a modest premium to invested capital, because the schlep advantage is real but the capital asymmetry against a $2.6B-valuation rival crushes the compounding window before the data moat matures. This is the Kiko (2005) outcome shape, not the Twitch (2007) outcome shape -- competent, sold, not a black swan.

The strongest bull case against my forecast is the Coinbase (2012) mechanism: schlep-as-moat compounds nonlinearly, and if 83 Sciences signs even five exclusive lab-data agreements in year one, the negative-results corpus becomes a dataset CuspAI's $400M cannot replicate because money doesn't buy lab trust, relationships do -- and Riesel's MIT/Freedman Group standing is exactly the currency that buys that trust. This case stings because it is structurally correct: the persona file explicitly elevates "frighteningly ambitious" ideas in mundane wrapping, and materials-AI-via-unpublished-data is that shape. What loses the bull case is the founder configuration -- the schlep-as-moat pattern at Coinbase worked because Armstrong personally wanted the compliance grind, and I do not yet see which of Naccarella, Riesel, or Yang wakes up excited to negotiate IP-sharing terms with a semiconductor R&D VP. Verdict flips to Invest if 83 Sciences shows me two signed named-counterparty lab-data agreements before Demo Day; without that, the neutral score stands.
