# Lab0 -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Rocketlane, a company with $105M raised and 750+ customers, actively excluded Lab0 -- a three-person YC pre-seed startup -- from their Propel conference in April 2026. That exclusion is the most analytically interesting signal in Lab0's dossier. When a well-funded incumbent gates a tiny competitor out of their distribution channel, it tells you the incumbent perceives a structural threat, not just a marginal one. Rocketlane, Certinia, and Kantata all launched AI agent products within a six-month window (Rocketlane Nitro in March 2026, Certinia Veda in April 2026, Kantata Expertise Engine in October 2025). ServiceNow acquired Moveworks in late 2025. Lab0 is entering a market where every significant player is already deploying AI into their installed base. The question I need to answer is whether Lab0's "full replacement" thesis -- automating the forward-deployed engineer entirely rather than augmenting human consultants -- constitutes a nonobvious bet inside what is otherwise one of the most consensus market timing calls in enterprise software today.

$451.6 billion in annual system integration spend is an enormous addressable market, and the structural catalyst is real: LLM capability has crossed the threshold where many enterprise configuration and integration tasks are automatable for the first time. Lab0's co-founder Lakshya Gupta co-authored "World of Workflows" (arXiv, January 2026), identifying "dynamics blindness" -- frontier models failing to predict cascading side effects in enterprise systems -- which points to a genuine technical gap that specialized solutions could exploit. The bull case writes itself: system integrators like Accenture and Deloitte face textbook business model cannibalization. They can't automate implementations without destroying their billable-hours revenue engine. Lab0, unburdened by legacy revenue, can attack the replacement thesis head-on. This maps to a pattern I've seen work -- when the incumbent's business model prevents them from building the disruption, the window stays open longer. And Lab0's approach, targeting "0-day Enterprise software Implementation" rather than augmenting existing consultant workflows, is genuinely differentiated from what Rocketlane Nitro and Certinia Veda are building. If Lab0 is right that full automation is possible within 18 months, incumbents pursuing the augmentation path will have trained their customers on a half-measure.

The problem is that this analysis conflates two different sets of competitors. Accenture and Deloitte face business model cannibalization, yes. But Rocketlane, Certinia, and Kantata are technology companies -- they have no consulting revenue to protect and can shift from augmentation to full automation the moment the technology allows it. Rocketlane doubled revenue last year and increased average deal size 4.5x since 2023. Lab0 isn't sneaking into an unguarded market the way Coinbase entered crypto infrastructure in 2012 when zero well-funded competitors existed. Lab0 is entering a market where the technology competitors are already deploying AI into their installed bases with years of customer workflow data. The nonobvious market timing score suffers directly from this: the structural shift is real but fully recognized. Three simultaneous AI agent launches by incumbents is the definition of consensus.

Lab0's infrastructure-layer positioning concerns me more than the timing question. Stripe didn't just process payments -- it became the API that thousands of companies built their payment workflows on. Lab0, as described, builds implementation agents that execute projects. That's an application, not a platform layer. Lab0 doesn't appear to offer an API that software vendors embed into their deployment pipelines. The dossier mentions no developer tools, no third-party integrations, no marketplace dynamics. Lab0 sits on top of third-party LLMs whose capabilities and pricing it doesn't control -- my "contested infrastructure layer" red flag applies directly. If Salesforce builds implementation agents native to its platform, or if ServiceNow extends Moveworks' capabilities into pre-deployment configuration, Lab0 loses its value proposition for those ecosystems without any structural defense. The data flywheel from "continuous learning from every rollout" is theoretically sound but entirely unproven with zero disclosed implementations.

Lab0's single-miracle test is where the evaluation tightens. The technology miracle -- LLMs handling enterprise implementation complexity -- is partially exogenous (models will keep improving regardless of Lab0), which is favorable. But the "dynamics blindness" finding from Gupta's own research suggests this miracle hasn't yet fully materialized for the most complex implementations. Meanwhile, Lab0 needs a separate distribution miracle: selling enterprise implementation services as a 3-person pre-seed team against incumbents with hundreds of customers, SOC 2 compliance, and established procurement relationships. Enterprise CIOs buying implementation services require proof-of-concept periods and reference customers that Lab0 cannot yet provide. That's two independent miracles -- technology accuracy AND enterprise distribution -- and my framework explicitly discounts compounding small odds.

Sujay Srivastava's prior founding experience with Ravvio (AI for BFSI sales and customer engagement) and Lakshya Gupta's enterprise AI research provide relevant domain grounding, but neither founder has operated at a company during its scaling phase or sold into enterprise procurement cycles. Onkar Borade's engineering experience at Oracle and Mudrex adds production-grade technical capability. Lab0's team is technically strong for pre-seed -- IIT Bombay and IIT Madras pedigrees, a published research paper directly relevant to their product -- but the enterprise sales muscle this category demands is conspicuously absent from their backgrounds. The margin architecture is entirely speculative: no pricing model disclosed, no revenue signals, no retention data. Per-implementation pricing could theoretically deliver high gross margins (software replacing $200/hour consulting labor), but the project-based nature of implementations creates inherent churn risk unless Lab0 becomes the ongoing implementation partner across a customer's entire software portfolio.

The verifiable condition that would flip me from Neutral to Invest: Lab0 demonstrates a completed enterprise implementation that compressed a typical 3-6 month deployment to under 2 weeks for a named customer, with a signed expansion contract. That would resolve both the technology miracle (it works) and the distribution miracle (enterprises will buy it from a startup) simultaneously. Without that evidence, Lab0 is a smart team with a relevant research paper entering a market where well-capitalized competitors are already deploying their own AI solutions. The market is real and enormous, the structural catalyst is genuine, and the business model cannibalization dynamic protecting Lab0 from consulting firms is durable -- but none of that is nonobvious, and application-layer positioning on contested LLM infrastructure limits the upside ceiling I need to see.
