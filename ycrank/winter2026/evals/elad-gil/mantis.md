# Mantis -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most interesting thing about Mantis isn't the product -- it's the founder trajectory. Georgia Witchel was CTO and co-founder of Louiza Labs, a YC S25 company that simulates human biology for medical device testing and raised $5M+. Six months later, she's back in YC as CEO of a separate company with overlapping technology themes -- physics simulation applied to biomedical data. YC accepting her into a second consecutive batch tells me Gustaf saw something. But from my seat, this raises an immediate question I can't resolve from public information: what happened at Louiza Labs, is there IP entanglement, and does this represent a founder who identified a bigger opportunity or a founder who couldn't make the first one work? The distinction matters enormously at pre-seed. When I backed Color Health, I was the operator who stayed for years -- I have strong pattern recognition for how founder-company separations play out in regulated biomedical markets, and the overlapping technology domains here create real friction risk.

The structural moment question is where this breaks down for me. Clinical data management is not a non-obvious market. Medidata, Oracle Health Sciences, and Veeva are well-established players with billions in combined revenue. The $3.46B market growing at 11% CAGR is linear, not inflecting. When I ask "what changed in the last 12-24 months," the answer is essentially "LLMs got better" -- which is true but consensus. Every data platform company on earth is integrating LLMs right now. The FDA and EMA guidance on digital twins is a more interesting signal, and it's genuinely new -- EMA qualified Unlearn's methods recently. But that regulatory shift benefits Unlearn ($130M+ raised, already EMA-qualified) far more than it benefits a 3-person pre-seed startup that hasn't demonstrated any regulatory relationships. This isn't the Coinbase pattern where regulatory complexity deters software investors and creates a moat for early movers. Here, the regulatory moat protects the incumbents who already have it.

The product positioning tells me the single miracle hasn't been identified. When a company describes itself variously as a "digital twin company," "Databricks for Biomedical and Clinical Data," "domain-aware data platform," and "unified biomedical device testing and regulatory platform," that's four different companies. I need founders who can name their one critical obstacle in one sentence. Mantis can't name their one product in one sentence. Worse, the path to success requires multiple sequential breakthroughs: build a technically complex product spanning physics simulation, LLMs, and clinical informatics; sell that product into pharma enterprises with 12-18 month sales cycles using a 3-person team; accumulate enough proprietary clinical data to create a defensible moat against Medidata's 30,000+ trials and 9M patients; and secure the regulatory credibility that pharma buyers require before touching clinical trial data. That's four miracles, and compounding low-probability events is exactly how ambitious companies fail.

The bull case I'd construct: clinical trials losing $15M each to data quality issues is a real, expensive problem. If you squint, "Databricks for Biomedical" could be a massive platform play -- Databricks is worth $60B+ and there's no equivalent in life sciences. Georgia's technical background spanning CS, computational math, and biomedical engineering is genuinely rare and directly relevant. The physics simulation plus LLM combination is technically differentiated and hard to replicate. And pharma companies are under enormous pressure to reduce trial costs and timelines, creating real buyer urgency. For this to work, Mantis would need to find a narrow wedge -- one specific data problem in one phase of clinical trials -- prove ROI with 2-3 pharma customers, and expand from there. That wedge strategy could work if the team identifies it and executes against it with the same intensity the Harvey team showed in legal AI. But I don't see evidence of that focused wedge thinking in anything public. The positioning sprawl suggests the opposite.

On distribution: I see no evidence the team has thought about how to sell into pharma enterprises. No named customers, no LOIs, no pilot references, no advisory board with pharma procurement connections. The YC network helps for developer tools and SaaS -- it doesn't help much for selling clinical data infrastructure to Pfizer or Roche. When I invested in companies targeting regulated enterprise buyers, the founders had either worked inside those organizations or had deep existing relationships. Georgia's background is in biomedical engineering and a sports tech startup, not pharma commercial operations. The distribution gap here isn't a minor concern -- it's structural. Enterprise pharma sales requires a specialized team, regulatory certifications, and reference customers that take years to develop.

Georgia Witchel is technically credible and clearly ambitious -- Harvey Mudd CS, UW bioengineering, building physics simulation systems at Louiza Labs. That combination of skills is rare. But technical credibility in this domain needs to be matched with commercial execution capability that I'm not seeing evidence of. The 7 Twitter followers and limited public presence suggest someone who builds in private rather than in public, which can work for developer tools but is a harder path for enterprise biomedical sales where trust and visibility matter. I'd want to see this team grow to include someone with deep pharma commercial experience before I'd get conviction.
