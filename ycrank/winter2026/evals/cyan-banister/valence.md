# Valence -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Valence: prediction markets are the hottest category in fintech right now. Kalshi just raised at an $11 billion valuation. Polymarket hit $9 billion. Robinhood launched predictions to its 24 million customers. DraftKings is spending $400 million on the category in 2026. Monthly active users in prediction markets grew from 4,000 to over 600,000 in a single year. And Valence is building a trading terminal that aggregates across these platforms -- smart order routing, co-location, API access for systematic traders. It's a logical product. It's a well-reasoned product. And that's exactly why it doesn't fit how I invest. When every major fintech platform is racing into a category, when the market has already identified the opportunity, when the consensus has formed -- that's when I get quiet. The most promising companies are not popular. Valence is building in a space that is very, very popular right now.

I wanted to hear the founders' stories -- not the business. What I found instead were resumes. Exceptional ones, I'll grant that. Daniel worked at Optiver and IMC Trading, building FPGA-level hardware for trading infrastructure. Neo interned at DRW doing latency-sensitive strategy research and Cubist Systematic Strategies. Arthur is a USAMO qualifier and USACO Platinum competitor. These are genuinely brilliant young engineers from UT Austin who all came up through competitive programming and quantitative trading firms. But nothing in the dossier tells me why prediction markets matter to them personally, what hardship forged their commitment to this problem, or why they'd persist through the inevitable crisis when the co-location contracts get expensive and Kalshi threatens to revoke API access. They noticed a market infrastructure gap from their professional vantage point and decided to fill it. That's a rational founding story, but it's not the kind of biographical thread I rely on when I'm writing a check from my own money.

The product itself is a known pattern transplanted from traditional finance into a new asset class. Smart order routing, co-location at exchange data centers, cross-venue aggregation, API access for automated strategies -- these are the building blocks of every institutional trading platform I've seen since my IronPort days in the mid-2000s. The technology risk here is essentially zero. This is execution risk dressed up in infrastructure language. I look for companies with real technology risk that isn't insurmountable -- Crusoe Energy had to figure out how to mine Bitcoin on flare gas in North Dakota, SpaceX had to solve reusable rocketry. Valence has to maintain API integrations and rent server racks near exchange data centers. That's operational work, not a frontier challenge.

To be fair to this team, let me articulate what would have to be true for this to be a great investment. If the claimed metrics are real -- $200 million in monthly volume, a billion contracts traded, 50% peak market share on individual events -- that's extraordinary for a three-person pre-seed team and would suggest they've already captured meaningful share of professional prediction market flow. The structural argument has teeth: Kalshi and Polymarket genuinely have no incentive to build cross-venue routing that sends volume to a competitor, which creates a wedge that an independent aggregator can exploit. If prediction markets fragment further as more exchanges launch (and the regulatory trend suggests they will), the aggregation layer becomes more valuable, not less. And these founders aren't just technically competent -- they're precisely the people who would build this. Daniel literally built an order book in Rust; Neo built an event-contract exchange prototype. They are their own customers. That founder-market fit is real. If I were a different kind of investor -- one who backs technically excellent teams building logical infrastructure plays in growing markets -- I'd probably lean in.

But I'm not that investor. Nothing about Valence creates economic access for people who lack it. This isn't Uber creating income for drivers or Affirm making credit transparent. This is co-location infrastructure for quantitative trading firms moving $150 million monthly through prediction markets. The people Valence serves are already among the most sophisticated, well-capitalized participants in financial markets. And the company sits in a dependent position -- its entire product relies on continued API access from exchanges that could view it as competitive at any moment. When I think about defensibility, I think about moats that compound over time. Valence's moat is operational complexity that any well-funded fintech infrastructure company could replicate. The 1inch DEX aggregator already proved this aggregation model works in crypto -- and also proved how quickly it can be commoditized.

The co-founder dynamics are the least concerning dimension here. Three founders from overlapping networks at UT Austin, connected through the computational finance club and shared internships at Citadel -- that's a cohesive founding team with shared context. It's not the stressed-tested conflict resolution I look for after what happened with HQ Trivia, but there's no 50/50 split, no obvious power imbalance, and their technical backgrounds are complementary enough that roles should be clear. This is fine. It's just not enough to overcome the fundamental mismatch between this company and how I allocate my own capital.
