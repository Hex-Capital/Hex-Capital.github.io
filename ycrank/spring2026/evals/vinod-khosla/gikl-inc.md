# Gikl, Inc -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The first thing I see when I look at ScienceSwarm is a community play wearing an AI costume. The dossier itself says it plainly: "The underlying AI capabilities (literature synthesis, hypothesis generation) are available via foundation model APIs." The technical components are replicable. The AI is commodity. What they're actually building is a marketplace for scientific contributions — and that means their real challenge is incentive design, cold-start dynamics, and community management. Those are important skills. But they are not the skills that produce 50x returns in my framework. When I backed Impossible Foods, the technology was the moat — synthesizing heme protein was genuinely novel chemistry that could not be replicated by assembling off-the-shelf ingredients. When the core technology is an API call anyone can make, the company is one Google feature launch away from obsolescence. And Google has already launched: AI Co-Scientist is deployed at 17 DOE National Labs and major universities today.

The consequence question is more interesting than the technology question. "Only 0.1% of humans do science" — if you could change that number by an order of magnitude, the compounding effects on drug discovery, materials science, energy, and agriculture would ripple through civilization for decades. Scientific progress is the master bottleneck. Everything I care about — making healthcare free, making energy limitless, feeding ten billion people — depends on the rate of scientific discovery. So the "what if this works" answer is genuinely large. Not fusion-large, not AGI-large, but substantial. The problem is that the mechanism — AI-augmented crowdsourced problem-solving by non-experts — has almost no historical evidence of working at meaningful scale. Foldit solved one protein structure with gamification. Terence Tao's Polymath Project cracked a handful of math problems with a Fields Medalist personally driving participation. These are charming anecdotes, not a systematic model. The coding assistant analogy is seductive but structurally wrong: code gives you immediate feedback — it compiles or it doesn't, the test passes or it fails. Science has no compiler. A wrong hypothesis doesn't throw an error. Verification in science is fundamentally harder than in software, and that asymmetry is the load-bearing wall of this entire thesis.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. If frontier AI models genuinely compress twelve years of domain training into hours of interactive onboarding — and the trajectory of capability improvement suggests this is directionally plausible — then the first platform that captures the "citizen scientist + AI" community could build network effects that are difficult to replicate. Every contributed approach, every verified solution, every failed attempt becomes training data and community capital. Stack Overflow proved that community-generated knowledge repositories can become indispensable infrastructure despite being technically simple. If ScienceSwarm becomes the Stack Overflow of open scientific problems, with AI augmentation making contributions accessible to non-experts, that is defensible even against Google's autonomous tools — because Google is building AI that replaces human researchers, while ScienceSwarm is building a community that empowers them. These are different products for different users. That distinction matters. And the founders — Vajda directing generative media at Meta for eleven years, Yamamoto in the core Llama group building LLM pre-training and inference — know how to build AI products at scale. They understand what it takes to ship AI capabilities to hundreds of millions of users.

But knowing how to build AI products at a big company is not the same as knowing how to ignite a community from zero. Both founders spent roughly a decade inside Meta. That is the opposite of the outsider energy I look for. When I funded the nineteen-year-old at Mach Industries building weapons to compete with Lockheed Martin, the irreverence was the feature. These founders have impressive resumes — seventy-seven publications, a co-authorship with a Fields Medalist, director-level roles at one of the largest AI labs in the world. But I have learned over forty years that impressive resumes predict incremental outcomes. I look for rate of learning, for evidence of rapid adaptation, for intellectual honesty about what surprised them. I see none of that signal here — no pivots, no iteration narrative, no evidence of what changed during YC. The credentials are real but static. And the team composition concerns me: both are AI researchers from the same company with overlapping technical skills. Where is the community builder? Where is the person who has started movements, who knows how to get a thousand people contributing to open problems before the AI is even good?

The competitive landscape is the final weight on my assessment. Elicit has $33M in funding and 400,000 monthly active users doing literature synthesis. FutureHouse raised $70M to build an autonomous AI scientist. Sakana AI has $379M and a $2.65B valuation doing automated end-to-end research. Google is deploying free tools at national labs. ScienceSwarm has zero public traction — no users, no revenue, no community, no contributions. The cold-start problem in a two-sided platform for scientific collaboration is brutal. You need interesting problems to attract contributors and interesting contributors to attract problems. There is no evidence this flywheel has begun to spin. I don't need traction to invest — I invested in OpenAI before it had a product — but I need either extraordinary consequence magnitude or extraordinary technical novelty. ScienceSwarm has moderate consequence magnitude built on an unproven social model, with commodity technology, expert-insider founders, and zero community traction competing against incumbents with hundreds of millions in funding. The option price here is not justified by the expected payoff structure.
