# Button Computer -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The wearable AI hardware category is a graveyard. Humane raised $230 million and sold to HP for less than half that. Rabbit R1 launched to terrible reviews. Limitless and Bee got absorbed by Meta and Amazon respectively. Button's founders looked at this wreckage and concluded the problem was over-engineering — that the right product is a $179 clip-on button with a speaker, not a $699 phone replacement. That's a reasonable competitive analysis. But competitive analysis is exactly what worries me. The best startup ideas aren't derived by studying why competitors failed and positioning against them. They're discovered by founders who personally needed something and couldn't find it.

When I ask "how did they find this problem?" the answer seems to be: they worked on Apple Vision Pro, saw AI wearables failing, and decided to build a simpler version. That's the startup idea equivalent of studying the market rather than living in it. I don't see the organic moment — the Drew-Houston-forgetting-his-USB-drive origin story. These founders have impressive credentials in hardware interaction design, but there's no evidence they were personally frustrated by pulling out their phone to talk to an AI. The "iPod Shuffle for AI" framing is revealing: the iPod Shuffle was iteration on an existing ecosystem Apple already owned. Button doesn't have an ecosystem. It's a peripheral that wraps someone else's API in commodity hardware. That analogy tells me more about how the founders conceptualize their product than they probably intended.

The schlep here is hardware manufacturing — and it's real. Getting from aluminum prototypes to mass production at $179 with acceptable margins involves supply chain nightmares, FCC certification, and quality control. But this isn't the kind of schlep that creates a structural moat. It's the same schlep every hardware startup faces. Stripe's schlep was banks and regulation — invisible to most programmers and impossible to shortcut once you'd done it. Button's schlep is logistics. Any team with hardware experience can navigate it, and several already have: Plaud has shipped over a million units at the same price point. The hard part isn't hidden. It's just hard.

The bull case deserves a fair hearing. These founders co-invented the gaze-and-tap interface for visionOS — they understand how humans interact with new hardware at a level very few people do. Burgoyne shipped Skyglass through Techstars to 95K downloads and $6K MRR, so he's not a first-timer who's never faced the market. The competitive landscape has genuinely cleared: every independent competitor has been acquired or killed, and big tech may struggle to ship a cheap accessory that cannibalizes their phone-based AI monetization. If LLM voice latency keeps improving and Button nails the industrial design — if using it feels as natural as talking to a friend — there's a real consumer product here. Plaud proved people will buy a $170 AI wearable. The question is whether "active conversation" is a bigger market than "passive recording." If it is, and if Button gets there first with a product people love, the economics could work.

But that's a lot of "ifs" stacked on commodity components with no technical moat. The software is API calls. The hardware is a mic, speaker, Bluetooth chip, and button. What stops Plaud — already profitable with a million units sold — from adding a speaker to their next model? What stops Apple from shipping a similar accessory integrated with Siri and Apple Intelligence? The answer is nothing except execution speed and taste, and those are thin moats for a two-person team competing against companies with thousands of engineers. I keep coming back to the same concern: the strongest signal for an early-stage hardware company is that the founders are building something nobody else could build, or that nobody else would build because the problem is invisible. Button is building something many teams could build, in a category everyone can see.

The founders are genuine technical builders — that's the strongest dimension here. Both are engineers who've shipped real products. Burgoyne's patent portfolio and Vision Pro work, combined with his Skyglass startup experience, make him exactly the kind of hacker-founder I respect. If they were applying this talent to a problem with more organic roots and a deeper structural moat, I'd be significantly more interested. As it stands, I see capable founders chasing a category rather than a calling.
