# Lamina Labs -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Two undergrads from Nepal — one at MIT studying CS and math, the other at Caltech studying physics — building mathematically precise educational animations. I want to like this more than I do. The founder-market fit is almost too perfect: a physicist with olympiad medals in three disciplines and a published astrophysics researcher, paired with a systems programmer who built the smallest OpenClaw implementation in C++. If you were casting a movie about who should build deterministic STEM animation tools, you'd cast these two. The question is whether that fit is coincidence or destiny — whether they stumbled into this problem because they lived it, or spotted it because the credentials made it look natural.

And that's what's missing from the dossier. I don't have the USB-drive moment. Drew Houston didn't build Dropbox because he had relevant credentials; he built it because he kept forgetting his thumb drive on a specific bus ride. The Octopart founders didn't start with "we have physics backgrounds so we should build a parts search engine"; they started with "I can't find this damn capacitor." Sudip and Rohan are exactly the kind of people who would have spent frustrating hours wrestling with Manim for a problem set or a research presentation. But I need to hear that story. Instead, the website pitches this as a market gap: "Billions are going into Hollywood-style AI video. Nearly no one builds animation designed for educational explanation." That's a market observation, not a personal frustration. It might be both — these two probably have genuinely cursed at Manim — but the framing worries me because it suggests they're thinking about positioning before they're thinking about the problem.

The schlep angle is real, though, and it's what keeps me interested. Educational content tooling is a space that makes most founders' eyes glaze over. The money in AI video is chasing avatar-based marketing clips and cinematic generation — the Synthesias and Runways. Nobody with prestige-seeking motives would choose to build animation infrastructure for math teachers. That's exactly the kind of domain that repels the founders it shouldn't. And the specific technical challenge — deterministic correctness rather than probabilistic generation — runs against the grain of every hot AI trend. When everyone is building diffusion models, building a structured rendering pipeline is contrarian in a way that might actually matter. If their "under 8 seconds" claim reflects a genuinely novel engine rather than a clever wrapper around Manim, they could own the infrastructure layer for AI-generated educational content as that market compounds.

But I have a real commoditization concern. Manim exists, it's open-source, it has 35,000 GitHub stars, and it produces exactly the kind of mathematically precise output Lamina claims to deliver. The obvious product is an LLM layer on top of Manim that converts text prompts to Python code and runs the renderer. A good engineer could prototype that in a weekend. The 8-second generation claim suggests Lamina might have built something more fundamental — Manim is slow because it renders frame-by-frame, so matching that quality in 8 seconds would require a different architecture — but I can't verify this. If they've actually built a purpose-designed rendering engine that achieves both speed and deterministic correctness, that's meaningful technical infrastructure. If they've built a prompt-to-Manim pipeline with some caching, that's a weekend project with no moat.

What would make me invest: if I could sit with Sudip and Rohan for ten minutes and hear them describe, with the specificity of people who've actually done it, the hours they spent trying to animate a Fourier transform for a presentation and the exact moment they realized the tools were broken. If they could show me the rendering engine and explain why it's architecturally different from wrapping Manim. If they had even five users — professors, YouTube creators, anyone — who were building their workflow around Pictor despite its rough edges. The raw intellectual horsepower is clearly there: olympiad medals across physics, math, and computing are not credentials you can fake, and Sudip leaving MIT to build this shows conviction. I've backed founders this young before — the Bindwell kids were 18 and 19 — but in those cases I had evidence of adaptive resourcefulness, not just impressive credentials. Credentials tell me they can think. I need to know they can ship, sell, and survive.

The honest assessment is that this is a bet I'd want to make if the ten-minute interview went well. The ingredients are promising: young, technically brilliant founders in an unsexy domain with genuine schlep, building against the consensus direction in AI video. But from what I can see today — waitlist stage, no users, no evidence of the kind of creative workarounds that distinguish great founders from smart students — I don't have enough signal to write a check from my own money. I'd want to talk to them.
