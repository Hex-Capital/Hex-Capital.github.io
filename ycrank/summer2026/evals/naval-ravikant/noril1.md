# Nori -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Antonio Sitong Li is a Columbia Graphics and UI Lab research fellow who worked on VR-demonstrated task goals for robot teams, and he is now shipping a $1,288 wheeled bimanual manipulator whose training method is leader-follower teleoperation -- which is, almost verbatim, the research problem he was already inside. That is the signature of Nori in one sentence: high on non-trainable specific knowledge, low on permissionless leverage. Nori is a hardware D2C business with a genuine domain-native founder, and the leverage check runs the wrong direction on my entry gate before I ever get to the team.

Twitter (2007) is the HIGH rung on my leverage architecture ladder -- media, zero marginal cost, anyone can post, no gatekeeper charges Ev Williams a per-tweet fee. Nori sells a $1,288 physical object with a BOM, a warranty tail, a shipping schedule ("shipping summer 2026"), and manufacturing "in San Francisco" for a four-person team. That is capital leverage requiring permission from suppliers, freight, customs, and consumer credit -- structurally the opposite of what code and media do. The skill-sharing marketplace on norirobotics.com *would* be code leverage stacked on top of Nori's atoms, and I take the claim seriously as a pivot vector, but "no cited installed base" is what the dossier actually says, so the code-leverage layer is a promise, not a mechanism. Nori sits one rung above the Anchorage Digital regulated-custody floor only because the atoms are consumer, not regulated.

Clearview AI is the LOW rung on the founder-trio ladder -- rare specific knowledge tolerated because integrity risk was priced into a one-X downside, and that is the exception, not the rule. Nori's founder signal is different in kind: no contested exits, no self-promotion about ethics, prior product (Founders Inc, 1.5M impressions, 2K users) shipped cleanly. What flags on Nori is not integrity -- it is that Antonio Sitong Li is the *only* founder listed on the YC page for a team of 4, and picking a co-founder is the most important decision a founder ever makes (Venture Hacks). Unresolved authority on Day 1 of a hardware company that has to ship physical units by summer 2026 is a load-bearing concern -- not a reason to score him down on the Buffett trio, but a reason not to score him up either.

Counterfactual on marketplace/network: the humanoid TAM is $5.41B in 2026 growing to $50.27B by 2035, and Nori's stated skill-sharing marketplace would, if it worked, be exactly the compounding-user-value architecture -- every Nori's teleoperation trajectory makes every other Nori better at dishwashing, laundry, coffee. For this dimension to flip from below-midpoint to top quartile inside 18 months, Nori would need a shipped installed base of hundreds of units producing cross-robot skill transfer demonstrated by third-party contributors -- the Stack Overflow (2008) pattern of user-created answers compounding without the founder in the loop. Today Nori has 726 X followers and 15.7K launch-day views. The mechanism is designed correctly; the flywheel has no fuel yet.

Notion (2013) is the single-anchor parallel on specific knowledge -- Ivan Zhao had been rebuilding personal productivity tools since adolescence, and the founder-product-market fit was legible before the product was. Antonio Sitong Li's Columbia research on VR-demonstrated task goals for robot teams *is* the teleoperation-to-imitation pipeline Nori ships; he did not identify a market from a McKinsey deck, he identified a product from his own research fellowship. This is the strongest thing about Nori by a wide margin and the reason the score does not collapse into the low-pass tier.

Mechanism analysis on market pivot-room: Unitree ships G1 at ~$16K and R1 at $5,900 with a $1.3B valuation and ByteDance/Alibaba/Tencent capital behind manufacturing scale (CNBC, June 2026). Over 18 months, either Unitree or AgiBot releases a sub-$1,500 wheeled bimanual and Nori's hardware differentiation compresses to zero, OR Nori pivots the demonstration-learning stack into a software layer sold to the humanoid incumbents (Figure at $39B, 1X at $20K NEO). The market absorbs that pivot cleanly because the underlying skill is data collection for embodied AI, which is what the whole category needs. Pivot-room exists; the founder just cannot afford to spend the first idea's runway on it.

Forecast, 36 months: Nori ships between 500 and 3,000 L2 units, hits BOM-competition from Unitree's next SKU release, and either shuts down by mid-2029 or pivots into a teleoperation-dataset / imitation-learning software play acquired for the stack (not the hardware) by Figure, 1X, or a Series B humanoid incumbent for sub-$50M. Nori does not become a household-robot brand; Antonio Sitong Li becomes an ML infrastructure hire at whoever wins the bipedal race.

The strongest opposing case is the Replit (April 2023) analogy -- Amjad Masad was a self-taught code-environment obsessive since 2011, and I wrote the check alongside a16z Growth precisely because founder-product-market fit was extreme and the code layer he built was distribution unto itself. Antonio Sitong Li's Columbia robotics research is legitimately domain-native, the skill marketplace is designed exactly like a data flywheel, and a $1,288 sub-iPhone-price consumer robot is the kind of cost-to-launch collapse ("$50,000 is enough") that historically produced my best entries. The reason it doesn't sting enough to flip the verdict: Replit's leverage was code all the way down -- Masad's product distributed itself through browsers. Nori's leverage is atoms all the way down until the marketplace layer exists, and it doesn't yet. Flip condition: if Nori ships 10,000+ units within 24 months AND third-party skill contributions on the marketplace exceed first-party skills by unit count, I re-underwrite -- because at that point the code-leverage layer has decoupled from the hardware and the whole geometry changes.
