# Dayjob -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

$450K ARR in seven months is the number that would make Paul Graham lean forward — and it is precisely the number that obscures the structural question I need answered first. Dayjob has built AI scheduling for skip hire operators in the UK, plugging into waste management ERPs like Weighsoft, PurGo, and Wastelogics to replace spreadsheet-based route planning. The traction is real. Coastal Recycling reports "5-10% increase in jobs per driver hour." The VWS partnership formalizes a co-marketing channel. But traction answers the question "do customers want this?" — not the question that matters: "what important truth has Dayjob discovered that the market actively disputes?" I cannot identify one. The proposition that waste operators need purpose-built scheduling software rather than general-purpose route optimization tools is correct, obvious, and shared by every vertical SaaS investor who has ever looked at logistics. AMCS already offers route optimization within its $225M-funded waste ERP suite. Routific, FarEye, and Descartes could add skip-size constraints to their existing platforms in a product cycle. Dayjob's insight is a market observation dressed as a secret — and market observations produce competitive businesses, not monopolies.

George Postlethwaite spent five years at Deliveroo launching the UK grocery business, giving him direct experience in fleet scheduling and last-mile operations. Fred Fooks brings supply chain analytics from Deloitte. Both attended Oxford; Pre-Seed Now describes them as "long-time friends." The co-founder prehistory is genuine, and the founder-market fit is above average for a pre-seed team. But Dayjob's own history reveals iterative discovery, not definite conviction: Postlethwaite and Fooks first built Gaea, a waste management ERP that generated $150K in revenue before they pivoted to Dayjob's scheduling agent in January 2025. A pivot is lean-startup methodology in action — the founder discovers through experimentation what they should have known through conviction. I do not see evidence of a bold, specific multi-year plan for Dayjob; I see competent operators who found a pain point in a niche they happened to be exploring, and built a useful product to address it. Useful products in competitive markets are not what my framework selects for.

Dayjob's distribution through ERP vendor partnerships — VWS/PurGo co-marketing, integrations with Weighsoft and Wastelogics — is the strongest structural element in the dossier. This is a solved path to customers that most pre-seed companies lack, and it explains how Dayjob reached $450K ARR so quickly. The channel strategy is sound: waste operators already trust their ERP vendors, and a scheduling overlay that plugs into existing systems faces lower adoption friction than a platform replacement. Palantir's early distribution followed a superficially similar pattern — embedding into existing institutional workflows — but the analogy collapses on inspection. Palantir's integration depth made displacement structurally impossible; intelligence agencies could not extract Palantir from their operational fabric without rebuilding entire analytical pipelines. Dayjob integrates with ERPs as an overlay that "most businesses" can deploy "within one day." What can be deployed in a day can be replaced in a day. The switching costs are transactional, not structural. The ERP partnerships that enable Dayjob's distribution simultaneously constrain its defensibility — VWS or Weighsoft could build native scheduling, partner with AMCS, or simply choose a different optimization vendor.

The power-law test is where Dayjob fails most decisively. Skip hire route optimization in the UK waste management sector is not a category with winner-take-all dynamics. The waste management software market is projected at $15.45B by 2029, but that figure encompasses the entire software stack across all geographies — Dayjob's serviceable market within UK skip hire scheduling is a fraction of a fraction. Even in the bull case where Dayjob expands beyond skip hire into general waste collection, then into adjacent logistics verticals across Europe and North America, the ceiling is a competent vertical SaaS business worth perhaps $300-500M in an optimistic acquisition scenario. That outcome is irrelevant to the power law. When I backed SpaceX, the question was whether reusable rockets could capture the launch market for decades — a category where a single winner could generate returns exceeding the rest of the fund combined. Dayjob's category is fragmented by geography, by waste type, by fleet size, and by ERP ecosystem. No structural mechanism exists to produce concentration.

The bull case an advocate would construct: Dayjob has identified the beachhead correctly — skip hire is a small, concentrated market with few competitors, exactly the "perfect target market" I describe in *Zero to One*. The $450K ARR proves product-market fit. The ERP partnership channel can scale across PurGo's, Weighsoft's, and Wastelogics' entire installed bases. From skip hire, Dayjob expands concentrically into general waste collection, then recycling logistics, then bulk haulage, then last-mile delivery broadly — following the PayPal pattern from eBay power sellers to all online transactions. The "AI agent" architecture represents a genuine technological discontinuity: real-time autonomous rescheduling was not feasible before 2024-2025 LLM capabilities, and Dayjob's domain-specific training data creates a compounding advantage. I have considered this case carefully. It fails because the concentric expansion path has no monopoly mechanism at each successive ring. PayPal's expansion worked because network effects compounded — every new user made PayPal more valuable to every existing user. Dayjob's expansion from skip hire to waste collection to haulage produces no network effect; each new vertical is an independent sales and integration effort in a competitive market. The "AI agent" framing is the 2025-2026 consensus thesis — dozens of YC companies in this batch alone are building "AI agents for X" using the same foundation models with the same architecture. When every company shares the same thesis, there is no secret, and the price of competition is destruction.

The condition that would flip me to Invest: Dayjob secures an exclusive, multi-year contract with a UK government waste authority — a local council or the Environment Agency — that embeds its scheduling into public waste infrastructure with regulatory compliance requirements. Government embedding creates the switching costs that commercial skip hire operators cannot. Without that structural lock-in, Dayjob is building a good product in a competitive market, and competition remains what it has always been.
