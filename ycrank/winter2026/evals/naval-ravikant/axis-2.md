# Axis -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I look for is what the founders know that can't be taught. Physical commodity trading — the movement and storage of metals, agricultural products, energy — is one of the most knowledge-intensive domains in finance. The traders at Vitol, Trafigura, Glencore operate on decades of accumulated intelligence about refinery relationships, shipping logistics, geopolitical dynamics, and bilateral OTC deal structures. This is deeply specific knowledge. So the question is: do these founders possess it? Eric Zhu studied math at UChicago and interned as a quantitative trader at TransMarket Group — a derivatives prop shop, not a physical commodities house. Ian Wang is a Yale '25 graduate described as "interested in trading." Quantitative trading and physical commodity trading share a vocabulary but not a knowledge base. The quant trader builds models on structured data. The physical commodity trader synthesizes unstructured intelligence — who controls storage in Rotterdam, what a refinery outage in Texas means for Gulf Coast spreads, how Red Sea disruptions ripple through agricultural shipping. I don't see evidence that either founder has lived inside this world long enough to know what commodity traders actually need from an AI system versus what an outsider imagines they need.

The leverage test reveals a structural constraint. Axis is software — code leverage exists by default. But distribution to physical commodity trading desks is the opposite of leveraged. The buyer base is concentrated: perhaps a few hundred institutional desks globally that would be serious customers. Each sale requires deep relationship-building in a trust-driven, reputation-gated industry. This is enterprise sales at its most linear — one desk at a time, with long cycles and high switching costs that work against you until you're the incumbent. Compare this to Stack Overflow, where every developer question answered made the platform more valuable and discoverable, or Replit, where distribution was inherent in the product. Axis has no network effects, no viral distribution, and no platform dynamics. It's a vertical analytics tool sold through direct sales. That's a real business — Molecule does $5.3M in ARR this way — but it's not the kind of leverage that produces the asymmetric outcomes I'm looking for.

The timing argument deserves engagement. LLMs crossing the threshold for reliable unstructured text parsing is genuine, and physical commodities is an underserved niche relative to equities or crypto. If I construct the strongest bull case: two quantitatively sharp founders from elite programs identify a real gap, embed themselves with commodity desks, build proprietary data pipelines that accumulate domain-specific training data, and create an AI analytical layer that the legacy CTRM vendors and data providers are structurally unable to replicate. The incumbents — Kpler, Vortexa, Enverus — sell structured data and transactional workflows; building an AI research layer would cannibalize their existing products. In this scenario, Axis could own the pre-trade analytical layer for commodities the way Bloomberg Terminal owns it for equities. The per-seat value would be enormous — commodity trading desks generate billions in annual P&L, and a tool that improves decision quality by even a few basis points is worth millions per desk.

But that bull case requires the founders to acquire specific knowledge they don't yet demonstrate, outrun well-funded incumbents who have existing customer relationships, and solve the hallucination problem in a domain where a wrong signal could cost a desk tens of millions. "AI for vertical X" in 2026 is the most consensus category in venture — the approach itself carries no contrarian premium. Every smart person agrees that LLMs should be applied to information-dense professional workflows. The disagreement is about execution, not thesis. And without a technical co-founder who has shipped visible product — no GitHub repos, no working website, no prototype evidence — I can't evaluate the technology because there isn't any to evaluate yet. When I invested in Uber at $4M, ride-sharing was illegal. When I backed Bitcoin in 2014, serious people called it a scam. Here, serious people would say "obviously AI will transform commodity trading analytics." That's not a bet against consensus — it's a bet on execution in a consensus category, with founders who are still building their domain knowledge rather than deploying knowledge they already have.

I respect the ambition of targeting an unglamorous, high-value market. Commodities make up a third of global exports and the analytical tooling is genuinely primitive. But the gap between identifying a real problem and possessing the specific knowledge to solve it better than anyone else is the gap between a good idea and a great investment. These founders are smart — elite programs, quantitative training — but smart and teachable is exactly the profile that doesn't clear my bar. What I need is knowledge that can't be replicated by another smart pair of UChicago and Yale grads who read the same market reports. I don't see it here.
