# Maywood -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the timing. "AI for M&A" in 2026 is not a contrarian bet -- it's consensus capital looking for a home. Every smart person in finance agrees that LLMs will automate document generation in banking. When I backed Uber in 2010, ride-sharing was illegal in most cities and investors thought it was insane. When I bought Bitcoin in 2014, serious people called it a scam. The asymmetric upside in those deals came from the gap between what I believed and what the market believed. With Maywood, there is no gap. The market already agrees this category will be big. Datasite is acquiring AI companies. McKinsey has published the TAM slide. Every fintech newsletter is writing about generative AI in financial services. You don't get paid for being right when everyone else is also right.

The specific knowledge question is where this team is interesting but not exceptional. Drake spent time at Blackstone executing deals -- he's felt the pain of reconciling updates across platforms, copying figures into CIMs at 2 AM. That's real. But it's also the experience of every analyst who's spent two years at a bulge bracket or PE shop. Thousands of people have this knowledge. It came through the standard institutional pipeline -- Wharton, then Blackstone -- not through years of obsessive, self-directed curiosity about how deal execution could work differently. Kent's neural stylistic transfer research at MIT is more compelling because it maps directly to the core product mechanic of emulating firm-specific document language. That's a non-obvious connection -- most AI researchers wouldn't think to apply style transfer to investment banking output. Esteban's combination of MIT EECS and Balyasny gives the team a real technologist, which clears my minimum bar. But I'm looking for the founder who spent five years thinking about this problem before anyone else thought it was a problem, and I don't see that here. The insight -- "M&A documents are manual and LLMs can generate them" -- is available to anyone with ChatGPT and a banking background.

The leverage architecture is real but bounded. One platform replacing dozens of analyst hours on CIM creation, pitch decks, and due diligence -- that's code as leverage, applied to some of the most expensive labor in the economy. A bank paying $200K per analyst that can now "double capacity with the same headcount" -- the unit economics work. But this is leverage for the institution, not the creation of a new form of leverage that didn't exist before. Compare to what Replit does: it gives coding leverage to people who previously couldn't code at all. Maywood gives existing bankers more throughput. That's valuable, that's a business, but it's optimization of an existing workflow rather than the creation of a new category of capability. I invest in companies that let a new type of person do a new type of work. Maywood lets the same type of person do the same type of work faster.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. The early traction -- "several banks" already using the product, with one reporting doubled deal capacity -- is genuinely strong for a pre-seed company selling into regulated enterprise buyers. Banking procurement is glacial; if they've already closed deals, the product-market pull must be intense. The firm-specific data flywheel could compound into real switching costs -- the more deals Maywood processes for a bank, the better it emulates that bank's standards, making it progressively harder to rip out. SOC 2 Type II certification at this stage shows unusual operational maturity. And the incumbents (Datasite, Midaxo) are virtual data room companies -- their architecture is fundamentally about document storage and access, not generative content creation. Building the generative layer would cannibalize their professional services revenue. If Maywood executes, they could own the content creation layer of M&A execution the way Alchemy owned the developer API layer for blockchain -- infrastructure that every deal flows through. That's a real outcome, and the brothers-co-founding dynamic suggests the long-term orientation to pursue it.

But for my portfolio, the core tension remains: the technology moat is thin. The underlying capability is LLM-based document generation with domain-specific fine-tuning. Any well-funded competitor with access to the same foundation models, a few ex-bankers, and six months of compliance work could build a comparable product. The moat is in the compliance certificates and workflow integration depth, not in difficult technology that compounds at the fundamental level. Compare to Extropic, where the physics is so novel that replication requires fundamental research. Maywood's technology could be replicated by a sufficiently motivated Datasite acquisition. The company will likely build a solid business -- the founders are smart, energetic, and appear to have integrity. But I'm looking for bets where the downside is 1x and the upside is 1,000x, and this reads more like a 10-20x outcome in a competitive enterprise SaaS market where the consensus has already priced in the opportunity.
