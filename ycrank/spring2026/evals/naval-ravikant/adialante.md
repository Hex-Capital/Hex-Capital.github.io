# Adialante -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The specific knowledge here is among the deepest I've seen at pre-seed. Torres didn't read a McKinsey report about MRI access gaps and decide to start a company. He spent years in one of the world's premier MRI research labs — UMN's Center for Magnetic Resonance Research — and invented a novel physics approach that eliminates gradient coils from MRI systems entirely. FREE technology is published in peer-reviewed journals, patent-pending, and the kind of knowledge that would take a competitor's team years of PhD-level work to replicate. Remove Torres and this company doesn't pivot — it ceases to exist. That's the test. And the team around him compounds the signal: an ISMRM Rabi Award winner (the award named after the Nobel laureate who discovered nuclear magnetic resonance), five PhDs spanning MRI physics and image reconstruction, an advisor who's a UMN Radiology professor. This isn't a team that was assembled — it's a team that formed organically around a shared obsession. Specific knowledge this dense, productized into hardware, reminds me of what I saw with Varda — people who understand physics at a level that creates capabilities no amount of capital can substitute for.

Now the leverage audit, and this is where it gets complicated. Mobile MRI units are not code. They are not media. They are trucks with technicians driving to clinics. Every scan requires a human, a vehicle, a schedule. This is labor leverage — the worst form in my hierarchy. A per-scan mobile imaging business scales linearly with headcount, not exponentially with users. You cannot build this with five people while you sleep. The structural contrast with, say, Notion — which scales to millions of users without proportional cost — is severe. But I've made this exception before. Varda creates leverage through microgravity — physics that cannot be replicated without going to space. Adialante creates leverage through novel MRI physics — a form factor (1/10th the size, 1/10th the power, silent operation) that Siemens and GE cannot achieve without abandoning their entire gradient coil architecture. When the physics itself is the moat, the leverage hierarchy shifts. It's not zero-marginal-cost leverage, but it is structurally irreplicable leverage. That distinction matters.

The principal-agent dynamics at incumbents are the real opening. Siemens sells $2M MRI systems with recurring service contracts worth hundreds of thousands per year. Their field sales teams are agents of that revenue model. A per-scan mobile service that eliminates the capital purchase? It's not that Siemens can't build it — it's that their internal incentive structure makes it irrational. Torres, as a principal with no legacy revenue, can move where their agents cannot. This is structurally identical to why Perplexity could challenge Google — the incumbent's own P&L becomes its prison. I find this dynamic more compelling than any competitive analysis of feature sets.

The access democratization pattern is strong and authentic. "Over one million men per year need prostate MRI, but almost no urology clinic has access." A mobile model with zero capital expenditure for clinics removes the $1-3M gatekeeper between patients and cancer screening. Torres grew up on the south side of Chicago and describes his mission as bringing MRI to underserved communities. This isn't a TAM slide — it's a life trajectory. The product gives millions of people access to imaging previously restricted to those near well-funded radiology centers. That's the pattern I backed with AngelList, CoinList, and Udemy — removing a structural barrier between people and something they need.

The concerns are real and I won't minimize them. Promaxo has FDA clearance, 69 installed systems, and $63-81M in funding — they are commercially shipping prostate MRI while Adialante is pre-submission. FDA timelines for novel MRI physics are uncertain. The mobile operations layer (fleet management, technician staffing, multi-jurisdiction compliance) is an entirely separate business from building MRI hardware, and no one on this team has run mobile healthcare operations before. These are academic founders — five PhDs, zero prior startup experience. The academic-to-commercial transition is where many deep-tech companies die. And team-to-value leverage is structurally poor: hardware manufacturing, FDA regulatory affairs, mobile fleet operations, and clinical support will require dozens of employees at scale. This will never be a 15-person company doing $10M in revenue.

The bull case requires believing three things: that FREE technology achieves clinically adequate image quality at its claimed cost and size (peer-reviewed publications suggest plausibility), that FDA clearance comes within a reasonable timeframe (the SBIR Phase II award through November 2026 funds development, and Hyperfine's 2020 clearance created regulatory precedent for novel low-field MRI), and that the per-scan mobile model proves more scalable than Promaxo's capital-sale model for urology clinics that cannot afford $500K+ systems. If all three hold, Adialante captures a segment of the prostate imaging market that no incumbent is structurally positioned to serve — the long tail of urology practices too small for capital equipment. The Promaxo threat is real but their model still requires clinics to buy hardware. That's a different customer entirely. Both can win.

I lean toward writing a small check. The specific knowledge is exceptional and the access democratization is genuine. The leverage architecture is my primary concern — this is a labor-intensive business dressed in physics — but the Varda precedent in my own portfolio tells me that when the technology creates capabilities that are physically impossible for incumbents to replicate, the leverage type matters less than the leverage uniqueness. Torres is a principal building something only he can build. That's the signal I weight most. But the FDA timeline, competitive gap with Promaxo, and operational complexity cap my conviction.
