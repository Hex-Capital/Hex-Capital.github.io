# Dispatch -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The most striking thing about Dispatch is the collision between the magnitude of the schlep and the size of the crowd already running toward it. Building vehicles that survive atmospheric reentry at Mach 20+ is genuinely one of the hardest engineering problems you can pick. The thermal protection, the guidance systems, the FAA licensing, the recovery logistics -- this is the kind of problem that should cause schlep blindness on a massive scale. And for most founders, it does. But here's what complicates the picture: the Factories in Space database lists 69 reentry vehicle entries. Varda has $329M and multiple completed orbital missions. Inversion has $54M plus a $71M Space Force contract. When I said the most impressive YC companies aren't working on AI, the principle extends to any category where a dozen well-funded companies are already converging. Schlep blindness works as a moat when the problem is still invisible. This problem has become visible.

The organic discovery question has a partial answer. Both founders worked at Astranis, which builds and operates satellites. If you're in the business of putting things in orbit, the asymmetry between up-mass and down-mass is something you encounter directly. This isn't two MBA students who read a report about the in-space manufacturing TAM and decided to start a company -- these are engineers who lived inside the orbital ecosystem and saw a gap. That's meaningful. But Astranis builds GEO communications satellites, not manufacturing platforms, so the connection to "returning manufactured goods from orbit" is one step removed from direct personal pain. The strongest organic discoveries come from founders who couldn't not build the thing. I'd want to hear the specific moment they decided this was their problem to solve.

The heat shield test is the detail that keeps pulling me back. They built a full-scale reentry heat shield "in an apartment," tested it in the Mojave Desert by holding it behind a rocket engine, and claim it cost 100x less than competitors' versions. That's genuinely resourceful. It reminds me of the scrappy engineering ethos that made early SpaceX work -- testing unconventionally, building cheaply, iterating in the desert instead of in a clean room. Andrew Mello's credentials reinforce this: avionics team lead at Astranis, prior experience at Zoox and Apple, dual degrees in EE and computer vision. These are technical founders who build things with their hands, not founders who will "hire a CTO after the raise." The technical dimension here is strong.

But the bull case requires you to believe something specific: that the in-space manufacturing market will actually materialize at scale, and that it will need dedicated downmass infrastructure rather than vertically integrated solutions. Right now, the most successful company in this space -- Varda -- builds its own capsules and returns its own payloads. If every orbital manufacturer follows the Varda model, there's no market for Dispatch's services. The opportunity exists only if the market fragments enough that specialized return logistics become more efficient than vertical integration. That's a plausible outcome, but it's not inevitable, and it depends on companies that themselves haven't proven their business models yet. You're building the return shipping infrastructure for a manufacturing sector that has maybe three or four active participants worldwide. The timing could be perfect -- or you could be five years early, which in a capital-intensive hardware business is the same as being wrong.

What would make me write a check? If these founders had a signed letter of intent from even one in-space manufacturer. If they'd demonstrated that their 100x cost advantage holds beyond ground testing. If the category had three competitors instead of fifteen. The raw ingredients are present -- real technical founders, genuine domain experience, a massive schlep, and demonstrated ability to build hardware cheaply. But the combination of a market that barely exists, competitors with 100x more funding, and no evidence of customer pull makes this a bet where the schlep alone isn't enough. The founders are building real things in the right problem space, and I wouldn't be surprised if they succeeded. But I need more signal before I invest my own money.
