# Robocurve -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Robocurve is a credentialed evals-research pair -- Jay Chooi (top-7% contributor to UK AISI's Inspect Evals) and Aris Zhu (co-author of GPC in IEEE RAL 2025) -- pointed at a real gap, but the leverage architecture underneath is hardware operations, not code. That pairing is the signature: specific knowledge at the top of my ladder, permissionless leverage near the bottom. When the highest dimension is the founder's non-trainable domain fluency and the lowest is the leverage the business runs on, I am looking at a research-quality team riding a services-quality business.

Stack Overflow is my anchor for the specific-knowledge dimension -- Atwood and Spolsky were developers writing tools for developers, and the moat was that no one else could have shipped it authentically. Chooi's ranking on UKGovernmentBEIS/inspect_evals is the direct analogue: he is not applying the Inspect framework to robots, he is one of the people who *is* Inspect, and Zhu's GPC paper in IEEE RAL 2025 means Robocurve's other half has shipped peer-reviewed generative-robot-policy work at Harvard SEAS. Where Robocurve diverges from the Stack Overflow rung is that the developer-pull at Stack Overflow arrived on day one through code the community adopted, whereas Robocurve's Inspect Robots v1 has one pilot on one frontier model and no disclosed adoption metric -- the specific knowledge is real, but the community-pull signal Atwood had is still absent.

Anchorage Digital sits at the bottom of my leverage ladder as a Known Exception -- I took it despite permissioned architecture because the OCC charter itself became the moat. Robocurve triggers the same failure mechanism without the same override: "operating and maintaining physical hardware and real-world setups" is the crux of the YC description, which means gross margin depends on capex-per-benchmark-run, not on marginal-cost-zero replication. The Inspect Robots open-source repo is a wedge, but the revenue surface is a services business with a fixed lab footprint -- capital and labor leverage, both of which "require permission" from suppliers, staff, and physical space. Unlike Anchorage, Robocurve has no regulatory-moat story that flips the permissioned architecture into a defensible structural asset; the PBC registration hints in the opposite direction.

On the network dimension -- Robocurve's inferred two-sidedness (labs submit models, benchmark authors publish tasks) is the mechanism that would compound over 18 months if it fires, and it is the only thing that upgrades this from a services shop to something my portfolio math tolerates. It has not fired yet. One pilot, no submission volume, no published benchmark authors outside the founders -- the compounding is a hypothesis, not a curve. The counterfactual for the market-pivot dimension is cleaner: for Robocurve to score a tier higher, the dossier would need to describe adjacent expansion into robotics data-collection infrastructure or a national-AISI contract vector; instead it describes robotics evals as a single category with academic substitutes (ManipulationNet across Rice, NIST, MIT, Berkeley, CMU) already free. On founder trio, Chooi's Rhodes Scholar 2026 and IOAA gold medal read as the intelligence-and-energy legs of Buffett's trio, and MATS Research Fellow work on AI safety is the closest thing to integrity signaling that shows through prior behavior rather than being volunteered -- clean, ten-year-partner-plausible, no red flags.

Robocurve in 36 months is either an acqui-hire into a frontier lab or an AI-safety-adjacent org (Anthropic, a national AISI) for $15-40M based on the team's Inspect-ecosystem gravity, or it stalls under $500K ARR because Physical Intelligence, Skild, 1X, and Figure keep self-reporting their own scores and academic consortia keep publishing free substitutes. The mechanism producing this bimodal outcome is the leverage mismatch: the founders are ten-thousand-x-quality humans running a one-x-geometry business, and the exit that respects both facts is a talent-driven absorption into an institution that can capitalize the hardware overhead.

The bull case that stings: Chooi's UK AISI Inspect Evals contribution is not just credential, it is a relationship, and if the UK AI Safety Institute or the US AI Safety Institute mandates third-party robotics evals the way MLPerf became the reference for compute, Robocurve inherits the standard by proximity. That flips the leverage story the way OCC-chartered custody flipped Anchorage -- permission-as-moat rather than permissionless-as-moat, and the PBC structure suddenly reads as strategic positioning for government contracting rather than as venture-scale hesitation. What dismantles it is timing and current evidence: Robocurve has one pilot, no disclosed government engagement, and the frontier labs racing to 2028 general-purpose robotics have no regulatory forcing function yet. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: a signed contract with a national AISI or a paid continuous-benchmarking agreement with one of Physical Intelligence, Skild, 1X, or Figure disclosed within twelve months. Absent that, this is the Pinterest-shaped trap in reverse -- I "get it," but the leverage math still says no.
