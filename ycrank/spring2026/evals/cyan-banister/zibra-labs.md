# Zibra Labs -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Zibra Labs is a competent distributed-systems team selling picks-and-shovels to the most affluent buyers in finance, and that pairing — high technical pedigree from Ibrahim Rabbani and Zac Policzer's Ray Core work at Anyscale, paired with hedge funds and prop shops as the entire customer universe — is the analytical signature here. When I run my opening question against Zibra Labs ("tell me your story, not the business"), the dossier gives me Anyscale and LinkedIn Venice. That's a résumé, not a crucible. And when I look at who Zibra Labs is built to serve — Bridgewater, Two Sigma, the firms casually name-checked as Ray reference customers — these are the most information-advantaged buyers on earth. There is no invisible system being made visible at Zibra Labs; there is a visible system being made faster.

Founder biographical forge is where Zibra Labs sits closest to my pass example. On the high rung is James Proud at Substrate — "forged in fire and came out unbroken," carrying a biography that made semiconductor sovereignty inevitable for him. On the mid rung is Max Levchin at Affirm — credentialed, technical, but the Affirm thesis was structurally so loaded that the founder narrative didn't have to do all the work. On the low rung is the pass-example AI wrapper founder who tells me "I was at Google and saw the opportunity." Zibra Labs sits between the low and mid rungs, closer to low: the dossier gives me Rabbani's GitHub bio "Building Ray Core at Anyscale" and Policzer's headline "Databases, Distributed systems, AI Infra" — these are career trajectories that pattern-matched into a startup, not biographies that left them no choice. Nothing in the Zibra Labs dossier tells me either founder was inside a hedge fund watching backtests fail, or that quant compute hurt them personally.

Cascading real-world impact is where Zibra Labs scores lowest, and it's the dimension that most clearly tells me this isn't a Long Journey shape. On the high rung is Uber expanding transportation beyond people who could hail taxis, and Serve Robotics extending delivery into geographies that gig workers couldn't economically reach. On the mid rung is Carta — democratized for founders, but founders are not the underserved. On the low rung is any enterprise tool whose success simply enriches the already-enriched. Zibra Labs sits at the low rung: the entire customer list is quant trading firms, an industry whose ten-year-out version with Zibra inside it is hedge funds running 6.4M parallel backtests to extract more alpha from public markets. That's not access expansion. That's amplifying the information asymmetry that made me homeless at fifteen, just at a different layer of the stack.

Contrarian conviction in hard spaces is similarly thin. Anduril sits on my high rung because defense tech was politically toxic in Silicon Valley when I wrote the check; Crusoe sits on the mid rung because flare-gas Bitcoin mining was weird but not stigmatized. The pass-example AI wrapper sits on the low rung as consensus AI in a consensus market. Zibra Labs sits between low and mid, closer to low: HPC-for-quant-trading is a category Bessemer led Coiled's $21M Series A into in 2021, and AWS has a published reference architecture for the exact backtesting workload. No room of sophisticated VCs shifts uncomfortably when you describe Zibra Labs at dinner. Invisible-system-made-visible is even worse — Uber attacked the medallion cartel for ordinary riders, Ownwell attacks property tax opacity for ordinary homeowners, and Zibra Labs' buyers are the people who *operate* the most opaque extractive system in capital markets. The dossier offers no opaque structure being challenged.

Infrastructure-grade defensibility is the one dimension where Zibra Labs nudges upward. Crusoe Energy sits on the high rung because portable data centers at stranded-energy sites are physically and contractually defensible; HQ Trivia sits on the low rung because attention without infrastructure evaporated. Zibra Labs sits between low and mid: Rabbani and Policzer have genuine Ray-core pedigree (upstream ray-project/ray has 42.4k stars, and Policzer led Venice at LinkedIn), which gives them real engineering leverage on multi-cloud spot scheduling at the 50,000-node, <50ms claim. But the dossier names Anyscale, Coiled, and AWS Batch as substitutes — and Ray itself is open source, built by these same founders' former team. The moat is execution speed, not structure. That gets a 7-9, not a 12.

In 36 months, my forecast for Zibra Labs is $3-8M ARR across 4-12 quant fund and prop shop accounts, followed by an acqui-hire into Anyscale or Coiled for the engineering team, or alternatively a Series A led by Bessemer or an enterprise-infra fund at a $40-70M post — a respectable infrastructure outcome that returns nothing meaningful on a Long Journey power-law fund. The mechanism producing this forecast is the dimensional signature: real engineering credibility (the high) wins them institutional logos, but the absence of biographical inevitability and impact (the lows) means there's no reason this becomes the defining company in its category instead of one of three competent options Bridgewater evaluates.

The strongest case against my pass is that Two Sigma and Bridgewater are compute whales whose individual contracts can exceed $5M/year, and Rabbani and Policzer's Ray network gives them privileged access to those exact buyers — the AI-generated-signal thesis ("hundreds per day vs. handful per quarter") is genuine, the bottleneck is real, and Zibra Labs could quietly compound to $50M ARR while I'm not paying attention. That bull case stings because the founders aren't fake; they're the right engineers for this problem. But my framework was built on Uber, Crusoe, Anduril — companies whose success expanded what was possible for people who had been locked out, not companies whose success made hedge funds 30% more efficient at extracting alpha. Zibra Labs has no Known Exception to lean on: it isn't culturally weird like Superplastic, isn't a narrow timing window like RTFKT, isn't a transformative product moment like HQ Trivia. The condition that flips my verdict is simple and verifiable: if a quant-domain co-founder joins Zibra Labs *and* the product opens a tier that lets independent researchers run institutional-scale backtests on stranded compute — bringing the invisible-system test back into play — I'd take a second meeting.
