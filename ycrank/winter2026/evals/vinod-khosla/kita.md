# Kita -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Kita is how intelligently scoped it is — and that's exactly my concern. They've identified a real gap: Ocrolus generates $252 million in revenue from US lenders and won't bother localizing for Filipino e-wallet statements; general-purpose VLMs can't yet handle the noisy, multilingual documents that constitute a borrower's financial life in Manila or Jakarta. So Kita slots into this gap with a hyperlocalized VLM-first platform that processes documents 70x faster than manual review. It's a smart wedge. The problem is that smart wedges often produce manageable businesses, not transformational ones. When I ask my defining question — if this works, does it change how an industry fundamentally operates? — the honest answer is: it makes an existing process faster. Lenders in the Philippines already underwrite loans. They just do it slowly. Making it faster is valuable. It's not the same as making the old way obsolete.

Now, there's a version of this story I find more compelling. If you reframe Kita not as a document processor but as the credit infrastructure layer for the world's 1.4 billion unbanked adults — a TransUnion for markets where TransUnion doesn't exist — the consequence of success changes dramatically. The data flywheel they describe, linking document signals to actual repayment outcomes, could evolve into something far more powerful than OCR: a proprietary risk signal that no lender can build internally and no incumbent has the localized training data to replicate. If that flywheel compounds across markets — Philippines, Indonesia, Mexico, eventually Africa — Kita could become the underwriting backbone for a multi-trillion-dollar global lending expansion. That version of the story is worth funding. But the dossier presents them as a document processing company, and that framing caps my enthusiasm.

The team is genuinely strong for what they're building. Rhea Malhotra's Firestone Medal — the highest honor for a CS thesis at Stanford — plus publications at ICRA and Humanoids on vision and perception, maps directly onto Kita's core technical challenge. She's bringing robotics-grade computer vision to a domain (financial documents) that's been stuck on legacy OCR. Carmel Limcaoco being from Manila isn't just a background detail; it's the localization moat personified. She has the cultural context, language familiarity, and presumably the network to get meetings with Philippine lenders that no US-based competitor can replicate. The six-figure paid pilot secured the week they built the prototype is strong evidence of real pull — that's not manufactured demand. The 30% month-over-month growth claim, if verifiable, suggests they've found genuine product-market fit in at least one geography. This team has executed well. My question isn't about execution — it's about ambition calibration.

The timing is favorable. VLMs crossed a reliability threshold in 2024-2025 that makes this possible for the first time without traditional OCR pipelines. Open finance APIs remain absent in their target markets, which means document-based underwriting isn't going anywhere for years. They're building into an accelerating capability curve while the market need persists. But this timing advantage cuts both ways: as VLMs improve further, the localization barrier shrinks. A year from now, GPT-5 or whatever follows may handle Filipino e-wallet statements natively, and Kita's moat reduces to the repayment-outcome data flywheel — which at two people and a few months of operation is still more aspiration than asset.

What I keep coming back to is the structural ceiling. Compare this to Square, where the technology didn't just speed up payments — it made the entire POS terminal infrastructure irrelevant for millions of small businesses who'd never had card processing before. Square created a new category of merchant. Kita, in its current articulation, makes existing lenders faster. It doesn't create a new category of lender or a new category of borrower. If they pivot toward becoming the risk signal layer — the entity that actually tells lenders *who to lend to*, not just *what's in this document* — the consequence magnitude shifts dramatically. But that's a company they haven't described yet, and I invest in what founders are building, not what I wish they were building. For my framework, this is a well-executed company attacking a real but bounded opportunity, and the consequence of success, while commercially meaningful, doesn't reach the scale that justifies the risk calculus I apply to my own capital.
