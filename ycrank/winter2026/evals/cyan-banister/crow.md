# Crow -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The thing that jumps out at me before anything else about Crow isn't the founders or the product — it's the competitive landscape, and what it tells me about consensus. Command AI was acquired by Amplitude for north of $45 million. SAP bought WalkMe for $1.5 billion. Intercom built Fin AI to $100 million in ARR. CopilotKit has ten thousand stars on GitHub as an open-source alternative. Every major player in enterprise software has already decided that in-app AI agents are valuable and worth building or buying. When I look at a deal and everyone in the room is already nodding, that's actually the moment I start backing away. The most promising companies I've backed — SpaceX, Crusoe, Contraline — all had in common that other investors thought they were nuts. Crow is building in a category where the consensus formed before they even launched. That's a signal I can't ignore.

When I sit down with founders, I always ask the same thing first: tell me your story. Not the business — you. How did you get here? With Aryan and Jai, what I see is two technically accomplished Berkeley graduates who met on day one of college, bonded over football, and went on to accumulate impressive résumés across Qualcomm, TikTok, Amazon, Tesla, and a prior YC company. That's a solid foundation for a startup. But it's not what makes me write a check. I'm listening for the biographical thread that makes this problem *theirs* — the specific hardship or lived experience that means they'll be the last ones standing when things get brutal. Aryan's military service in the Singapore Armed Forces is interesting and speaks to discipline, but I don't see the connection between that experience and "software companies need AI copilots." These feel like smart people who identified a market opportunity, not founders who are solving a problem that's been gnawing at them for years. The difference matters enormously at the earliest stages, because when the company hits its crisis — and every company does — the founders who survive are the ones who can't walk away from the problem.

Let me give Crow its strongest bull case, because I want to be honest about what I might be missing. The timing argument is real: LLM function-calling crossed a reliability threshold in 2024-2025, and there are tens of thousands of B2B software companies in the 5K-200K DAU range that will want AI copilot functionality but can't afford to build it from scratch. "Deploy in under a week" is a compelling value proposition for that mid-market. If Crow can execute fast enough to become the default embeddable agent — the way Stripe became the default for payments — the switching costs from workflow definitions, guardrails, and accumulated conversation patterns could create a real moat. Command AI's acquisition validated that this category produces acquirable companies, and being the independent pure-play after Command AI was absorbed gives Crow a structural opening. If I'm wrong about this deal, it'll be because I underestimated the power of being a focused horizontal layer while incumbents get distracted by their primary businesses.

But I don't think I'm wrong. The core capability — connecting an LLM to APIs through OpenAPI specs and executing actions — is technically reproducible by any competent team, and I say that as someone who spent years in engineering at IronPort before Cisco acquired us. The managed-service wrapper of proxy, auth, and observability is standard infrastructure, not the kind of deep technical moat that keeps me up at night wanting to invest. CopilotKit is already open source and free. OpenAI and Anthropic are adding function-calling tooling that makes this easier to build in-house every quarter. When I backed Flock Safety, Garrett had something nobody else had — those cameras were generating proprietary data about vehicle movements that got more valuable with every deployment. I don't see that kind of compounding advantage here. And when I think about the economic access dimension — does Crow create meaningful opportunity for people who currently lack it? — the honest answer is no. It makes existing software more convenient for existing users. That's fine. It's just not what lights me up.

The one genuinely positive signal is the co-founder relationship. Meeting on the first day of college, bonding over something personal like football, then choosing to code together and eventually start a company — that's a friendship-first dynamic I appreciate. After watching HQ Trivia implode from co-founder conflict despite having the right product at the right time, I take team resilience seriously. But friendship is a necessary condition, not a sufficient one, and I haven't seen evidence of how Aryan and Jai navigate real disagreement under pressure.
