# Deep Interactions -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Deep Interactions is the pedigreed-researcher version of the pass I wrote up in my own playbook -- Oxford DPhil Sruthi Viswanathan stepping into the single most contested wedge of YC Spring 2026, with Lovable at $400M ARR, Replit at a $9B valuation, and Bolt at $135M raised already swinging in the same swimlane. The signature is a high-academic-fit founder in the lowest-weirdness category I will look at. That pairing tells me what kind of company Deep Interactions is before I read another slide: a credentialed thesis paper aimed at a market that already has three funded incumbents shipping the same verb.

On the lived-experience axis, the closest portfolio analogue I reach for is Anduril-Luckey in 2017, where Palmer had actually shipped Oculus to scale before pivoting into defense -- the founder had built the comparable product in an adjacent domain before the pitch existed. Deep Interactions does not have that. Sruthi Viswanathan's lived material is Human-AI Interaction research at Oxford, UX Research Scientist at Naver Labs Europe 2018-2022, UX Researcher at Google India 2017-2018 -- which is genuine domain depth in *studying* how humans and AI collaborate, but it is not "I ran an enterprise AI pilot that failed, and I am now solving the bottleneck I personally hit." Neil Natarajan's DPhil under Reuben Binns and Sir Nigel Shadbolt is the same shape: an academic relationship to the problem, not a scar from running the back-office. The 95% AI-pilot abandonment statistic on the launch page is a McKinsey-flavored opening, not a founder origin moment.

Crusoe in ~2018 is the high rung for magically-weird, and I keep that ladder in front of me precisely so I do not flinch when something looks ridiculous on paper. Deep Interactions is the opposite shape. A "collaborative AI app builder for teams" with Lovable ($530M raised), Replit ($400M Series D), Bolt/StackBlitz ($135M), and v0/Vercel already named as competitors in the dossier is the most consensus square on the 2026 YC bingo card. Tom Blomfield as the YC partner, four-tool integration list, Calendly link on the homepage -- this is not the round two credible VCs have passed on for aesthetic reasons. This is the round where defensibility comes from the model and the workflow, not the founder, and Replit Agent 4 (March 2026) already ships parallel agents on a single project, meaning the multi-stakeholder wedge that Deep Interactions claims as its non-consensus angle is being closed from above by a $9B-valuation incumbent in the same quarter.

On governance, my HQ Trivia 2017 scar tissue is right there in the dossier: Sruthi Viswanathan is the only person listed as "Founder," with three other team members in "undefined founding roles." That is not a single-unambiguous-CEO setup; that is "we haven't had the conversation yet," which the dossier itself flags as ambiguity on technical leadership for an engineering-heavy product. Beni Ngwamah was previously CEO of WEKA. I have watched two prior-CEO co-founders chew through tiebreaker authority in the first eighteen months more times than I want to count, and the dossier gives me no operating agreement, no equity split, and no documented protocol to suggest Deep Interactions has resolved it.

The capital-intensity dimension compounds against Deep Interactions over the next eighteen months, not for it. SaaS AI builders with no proprietary data asset, no IP filing, no hardware artifact, and no regulated category lose their moat to whichever incumbent ships the feature first -- Lovable added $100M in revenue in a single month with 146 employees (TechCrunch, March 2026), which is the velocity profile that flattens differentiated wrappers within two quarters. The "context layer" the launch page names as the future moat is described as the product thesis, not a deployed asset. If Mindbloom is my benchmark for unsexy-regulated, Deep Interactions is on the opposite end: a Vercel-adjacent dev-tools abstraction whose technical defensibility I cannot touch.

On physical-world verifiability, the single-anchor parallel is Postmates 2011, where I bet because the delivery happened in front of me. Deep Interactions has 12 businesses live and a claimed 50K usage hits, which is real but not observable to me as an outsider -- the product is gated behind a founder Calendly and a Typeform waitlist, sits inside Slack/Gmail/Figma/GitHub/Vercel/Supabase as middleware, and produces outputs that look indistinguishable from Lovable's outputs to a non-engineer in the wild. I cannot ride this, scan this, or install this in 48 hours the way I could with Flock Safety or Pokémon Go.

My 36-month forecast for Deep Interactions: acquihire into Lovable, Replit, or StackBlitz at sub-$30M for the Oxford HCI team and the multi-stakeholder workflow IP, with the product itself sunsetted within a year of acquisition -- driven by the same mechanism that flattened every prompt-to-app wrapper that did not have a distribution moat: the well-capitalized incumbent ships the collaboration feature, the design partners churn, and the Calendly funnel cannot reach enterprise buyers fast enough to matter.

The strongest bull case against me is that Deep Interactions is genuinely solving a different problem than Lovable -- the failure mode of enterprise AI pilots really is the absence of shared PM/designer/engineer/stakeholder context, and Lovable/Replit/Bolt are structurally solo-builder products that cannot pivot to multi-human workflow without rewriting their core loop. Sruthi Viswanathan's Naver Labs UX research is the rare academic CV that actually maps to the wedge. The DeepMind exception applies: I have backed pedigreed Oxford researchers before when the polymath compounding was real. But the dossier flips that case open: Replit Agent 4 already ships parallel agents on a single project (March 2026), Bolt is in the Microsoft Marketplace (May 2026), and the moat thesis is described as the product thesis rather than a deployed asset -- meaning the incumbent has both the distribution and the runway to close the multi-stakeholder gap before Deep Interactions converts its 12 design partners into category-defining share. The verifiable condition that would flip my verdict: a signed enterprise contract above $250K ARR with a named Fortune 500 buyer whose AI-pilot failure rate is the documented wedge, sourced outside the Calendly funnel, before the next Replit or Lovable release ships team workflow. Until then, this is the consensus AI darling I pass on.
