# Astraea -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Astraea is a schlep-shaped company with invisible founders -- the regulated grind around CDISC/SDTM/ADaM and 21 CFR Part 11 audit trails is exactly the unpleasant work that scares off the rest of the Spring 2026 batch, but Joshua Wang and Sanmay Sarada arrive without a single pre-funding artifact I can point at. That inversion -- real schlep, missing animal -- is the entire evaluation.

Coinbase (2012) is the right comparison for the schlep axis, and it's the dimension where Astraea looks strongest. Brian Armstrong's edge wasn't a bitcoin wallet; it was his appetite for the compliance grind everyone else in the batch was unconsciously routing around. Astraea's territory -- CDISC-mapped SDTM, ADaM, Define-XML, aCRF, TFL outputs that have to survive FDA scrutiny under 21 CFR Part 11 -- is the same kind of unglamorous regulated knife-work. Where Astraea diverges from Coinbase is that Saama already shipped an "Agentic AI framework" SDTM Navigator into 50+ pharma customers and 1,500+ studies before Wang and Sarada launched, so the schlep is real but no longer unclaimed. Armstrong got to the regulator first. Astraea is arriving second to a vendor that already cleared the procurement maze.

The pass example I keep on the shelf -- the polished AI-wrapper pitched by an MBA pair with a glossy deck, no shipped artifact, no founder who personally suffered the problem -- is the failure mode I have to check Astraea against on animal resourcefulness, and the dossier does not pull it clear. Wang and Sarada have credentials (Stanford Math/CS, Stanford CS) and adjacent prior work, but the entire pre-funding artifact column is the phrase "no public repos found" repeated twice, plus a website claim of "Trusted across 10+ global pharma and biotech sponsors" with zero named logos. Drew Houston had a Hacker News video. Armstrong had a hand-built wallet that overturned a rejection. Astraea has self-reported "99.8% Validated SDTM" on its own marketing page. Animals leave tracks; I do not see any.

On organic founder-pain (dimension 2): the test from "How to Get Startup Ideas" (November 2012) is whether either founder personally lived inside the problem before building for it. Wang's bio cites prior multi-agent AI plus clinical data automation "for enterprise customers" -- adjacent, but that is consulting-shaped exposure, not the Drew-Houston-forgot-his-USB-stick organic origin. Neither Astraea founder was a biostatistician burning nine months on SAS programs and Excel specs. The idea is plausibly *informed*, not *lived*, and the difference compounds over eighteen months when the platform hits the weird edge cases that only ex-biostatistician founders would have anticipated.

On hacker genealogy (dimension 4): Patrick Collison won Ireland's Young Scientist for Lisp work in 2005, five years before Stripe (2010). Aaron Swartz co-authored the RSS spec before Reddit (2005). The pattern is that hackers leave a trail before they leave the company. Astraea's dossier is the absence of that trail spelled out four separate ways: "No public repos found" for Wang, "No public repos found" for Sarada, "No public account found" on Twitter for both, no Hacker News presence. A Stanford CS degree is the credential, not the artifact. This is the unstated pattern I over-index on and the dossier returns nothing.

On cofounder configuration (dimension 5) -- the counterfactual: if the dossier showed Wang and Sarada had roomed together at Stanford, or shipped a project together two years before Astraea, I'd move this score a full tier. Reddit (2005)'s Huffman and Ohanian were dorm roommates; Twitch (2007)'s Kan and Shear came out of Kiko (2005) together. What Astraea actually gives me is two Stanford CS affiliations and "no public data on prior shared employer." A pair beats a solo by default, but a pair without history is what the in-person interview is for, and I'm not in the room.

Forecast, 36 months: Astraea is most likely acqui-hired by Saama or Veeva for under $50M as an agentic-features tuck-in by mid-2029, or wound down after Saama's SDTM Navigator captures the Top-10 pharma accounts Astraea's website is targeting. The mechanism is straightforward -- regulated-buyer procurement cycles favor the incumbent with 1,500+ studies of audit history, and Astraea has no named customer logo, no founder cult-following, and no demo artifact to route around the procurement gate.

The bull case that stings: Coinbase (2012) looked exactly like this on paper -- a regulated grind already half-occupied by legacy compliance vendors, a founder with adjacent rather than lived experience, no early logos. Stripe (2010) beat legacy processors not because the Collisons had a moat but because services-attached incumbents like Saama cannot rebuild as agent-native without cannibalizing SAS-programming revenue, and agentic-native startups have repeatedly eaten services-coded incumbents. What dismantles the bull case is the specific Coinbase counter-evidence: Armstrong built the Android wallet *after* his written rejection. There is no equivalent Astraea moment in the dossier -- no rejection-rescue, no cereal-box stunt, no shipped HN demo. The verifiable condition that would flip me to invest: a named Top-3 pharma sponsor disclosed as a paying customer with a Phase II/III submission filed using Astraea outputs, or either founder surfacing a real hacker trail (substantive GitHub, prior shipped open-source CDISC tooling). Until one of those arrives, this is a pass.
