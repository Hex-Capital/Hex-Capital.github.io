# Approxima -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The most revealing fact in this entire dossier is not about Approxima — it is about Sweep AI. A YC S23 company occupied this precise niche eighteen months ago, building automated bug-fix pull requests via LLMs, and retreated from the position entirely, pivoting to JetBrains IDE tooling. When a predecessor abandons the exact territory you are entering, the question is not "why is the timing better now?" The question is "what do they know that you don't?" And the answer, almost certainly, is that the autonomous bug-fix layer is being subsumed by the platforms beneath it. Claude Code, Codex, Copilot — the foundation model providers are shipping autonomous coding capabilities directly. Building an application layer on top of someone else's model, in a niche that the model provider is actively entering, is not a business strategy. It is a timing arbitrage that closes the moment your supplier decides to compete with you.

What is the secret here? I cannot find one. "Software should heal itself" is not a contrarian truth — it is the consensus aspiration of every AI coding company in existence. Cognition has raised $400 million and grown to $73 million in ARR building autonomous software agents. Factory AI has enterprise deployments at MongoDB, EY, and Zapier. Augment Code has raised $252 million. OpenHands has $23.8 million and an open-source community. The "self-healing software" positioning is not a secret; it is a tagline that describes what the entire category is converging toward. When I ask my interview question — "What important truth do very few people agree with you on?" — I need an answer that most intelligent observers would actively argue against. Nobody argues against the proposition that AI should fix bugs autonomously. They simply disagree about who will capture the value, and the evidence overwhelmingly points to the foundation model providers and the well-capitalized incumbents who already own developer distribution.

The mimetic dynamics here are as destructive as anything I have seen. This is not a market with two or three competitors — it is a market where dozens of startups and every major platform company are converging on identical capabilities using identical underlying technology. The Girardian pattern is unmistakable: companies imitating each other's positioning, each claiming a slightly different angle on the same thesis, all burning capital to acquire the same engineering teams as customers. Approxima's differentiation — "proactive issue detection" versus "task-initiated code generation" — is a marketing distinction, not a structural one. Any agent that can write code can also be directed to find bugs. The boundary between "self-healing" and "autonomous development" dissolves the moment a competitor adds a continuous monitoring feature, which is trivial relative to the core agent capability.

The mid-batch pivot compounds my concern. The founders entered YC building SideKit, a mobile app SDK — an entirely different product for an entirely different market. They pivoted to AI agents during the batch. This is the opposite of definite optimism. A founder with a definite vision builds toward a specific future they have designed; they do not abandon their thesis mid-execution to chase the sector where venture capital is flowing most aggressively. I am not categorically opposed to pivots — some great companies emerge from them — but the pivot must reveal that the founders discovered a secret through their prior work. Here, the prior work (iOS and React Native SDKs) provides no technical carryover and no domain insight into autonomous code maintenance. The GitHub organization still contains only SideKit-era repositories. The Approxima website returned a connection error. This suggests a product in its earliest conceptual stages, not a pivot driven by insight.

The strongest bull case would require believing three things simultaneously: that "self-healing software" is a genuinely distinct category from autonomous coding agents, that this category will resist absorption by the foundation model providers, and that two recent Waterloo graduates with internship experience at Databricks and Microsoft possess a technical or strategic insight that Cognition's $400 million, Factory's $50 million, and Anthropic's and OpenAI's own engineering teams have all missed. I find none of these propositions credible. The first is a marketing distinction without structural basis. The second contradicts the observable trajectory of every major model provider. The third asks me to believe in an undisclosed secret from founders who have not yet built or demonstrated a product. Selvaraj's Databricks experience and Pronev's competitive programming background are respectable credentials, but they do not constitute the kind of deep, non-transferable domain expertise that would justify conviction in an overcrowded market.

I pass. This is a company entering the most mimetically competitive sector in technology, armed with commodity technology, no visible secret, no proprietary moat, and a mid-batch pivot that signals trend-following rather than conviction. The entire category is a value-destruction engine — companies imitating each other into margin compression while the platform providers absorb their features. My capital is better deployed where a founder has seen something the world has not.
