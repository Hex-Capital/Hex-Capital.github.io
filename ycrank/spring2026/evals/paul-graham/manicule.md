# Manicule -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing I keep coming back to with Manicule is that I once wrote "a startup is a company designed to grow fast. Being newly founded does not in itself make a company a startup." Manicule is a documentation agency -- a good one, apparently, and a profitable one -- but I can't find the mechanism by which doubling the quality of their work produces ten times the revenue. Five clients at roughly $3K/month each, a team of five people to serve them. That's a ratio. You can improve the ratio with AI tooling, and they're smart to be doing that, but you can't escape it entirely. When I evaluate whether a company exhibits superlinear returns -- where how well you've done determines how well you'll do -- I see a business where the next dollar of revenue requires roughly the next unit of human effort. That's a consultancy. Consultancies can be great businesses. They're just not startups.

What makes this complicated is that the problem discovery is the real thing. Shreyans was a founding engineer at Supermemory. Naman was freelancing as a technical writer for Supermemory. They watched great documentation drive a 30% improvement in answer success rates and help close enterprise deals. They didn't read a report about the documentation gap in developer tools -- they lived it. That's exactly the "live in the future, then build what's missing" origin story I look for. When I funded Dropbox, the signal was that Drew Houston kept forgetting his USB drive. When Naman and Shreyans saw their documentation work go viral on Twitter and directly contribute to deal closure, they had the same kind of authentic moment. That part is right.

But there's a crucial difference between discovering a problem organically and building the right kind of company to solve it. The organic discovery points toward a product -- some kind of AI-powered documentation system that developer tool companies can use to generate and maintain their own docs, perhaps with human review as an upsell. Instead, what they've built is an agency. And the competitive landscape makes the agency path especially precarious: Mintlify has $21M and $10M ARR building the self-serve SaaS version of documentation. They just acquired Trieve for RAG infrastructure. The historical pattern here is clear -- when both a software product and a services company attack the same problem, the software company wins because it scales differently. The documentation agency that can serve fifty clients needs fifty times the capacity. The documentation platform that can serve fifty clients needs slightly better servers.

The bull case would require something I don't see evidence of yet: a planned transition from services to product. If the AI tooling they're building internally to verify code snippets, audit links, and draft from OpenAPI specs is genuinely differentiated -- if it's not just standard LLM prompting but something that compounds with each client engagement into a proprietary system -- then the services revenue is funding the development of a future platform. That would change the math entirely. Think of it like Palantir, which started as a services company and gradually productized its internal tools. If Manicule's real asset is the AI pipeline they're building, not the writers they're employing, then there's a version of this that works at scale. But I see no public indication they're thinking about it this way. The website describes a services offering. The YC description describes a services offering. Maybe they have the pivot planned and I'm not seeing it -- but usually founders who are building toward a product tell you about the product, because that's what excites them.

On the founders themselves: Naman has three years of doing exactly this work, which gives him genuine domain expertise. Shreyans brings the engineering chops. They worked together at Duelance before this, so the co-founder relationship is tested. What I'm missing is the "animal" quality -- specific stories of creative problem-solving under constraint, of doing something so clever or determined that it surprises you. Being profitable at $15K MRR is solid fiscal discipline, but it's the natural outcome of formalizing an existing freelance practice, not a signal of exceptional resourcefulness. The most resourceful founders generate anecdotes that other people tell about them. I don't see those anecdotes here.

I respect what they've built. Profitable, real clients, authentic problem discovery. If they came to me and said "we're using services revenue to fund development of an AI documentation platform, and here's the internal tool that's already better than anything on the market," I'd lean forward. Right now, I'm looking at a well-run consultancy that correctly identified a real problem but chose a business model that doesn't compound. The idea doesn't repel people because they're wrong about it -- it repels investors because the scaling constraint is genuine.
