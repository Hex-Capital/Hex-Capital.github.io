# Clara -- Paul Graham Evaluation

George Favvas built Circle Medical (YC S15) to $100M+ annualized revenue over a decade as CEO. That's the fact that dominates everything else in Clara's dossier, and it's the kind of fact that makes me sit up and pay attention. When someone has already navigated the full obstacle course of scaling a healthcare company — state licensing, clinical hiring, HIPAA, malpractice, insurance billing — and come out the other side with nine-figure revenue, you know they have the schlep tolerance most founders lack. Clara's founding team is a reunion: Favvas as CEO, Zeeshan Ahmed who designed Circle Medical's EHR system for 200+ providers, and Caitlin Swift who ran clinical operations as a practicing NP. They chose to work together again. Co-founder reunions are an underappreciated signal — people who've been through the grind together and voluntarily sign up for round two have already stress-tested the relationship that kills most startups.

Clara's problem discovery is genuinely organic. Ahmed didn't read a McKinsey report about healthcare inefficiency — he built the EHR tools and watched 200+ providers struggle with patient data entry, reducing entry time by 25% through design iteration. Swift didn't observe primary care from the outside — she practiced it, managing chronic disease patients in Marin County. Favvas spent a decade inside digital primary care delivery. When Clara claims that primary care suffers from short appointments, fragmented records, and the gap between generic AI chatbots and clinical action, these aren't insights from customer interviews. They're complaints the founders lived with for years. This is the "live in the future, then build what's missing" origin story I look for — they lived inside telehealth at Circle Medical and are building the AI layer they saw was missing.

Clara's weakest dimension is one I weight heavily: the idea doesn't repel anyone. AI-first primary care is a category that a16z is funding (Counsel Health, $36M), that has $108M+ behind Galileo, and that Forward Health pursued with $650M before its November 2024 shutdown. When I use Peter Thiel's Venn diagram — the intersection of "seems like a bad idea" and "is a good idea" — Clara lands squarely outside it. The idea seems good to everyone. Every trend piece about healthcare mentions AI-driven primary care. Counsel Health already reports 100,000+ members. Nobody's first reaction to Clara is "that will never work." The reaction is "of course, someone should do that" — which is exactly the tell that the return premium has been competed away. Clara is entering a space where the consensus is already formed, and the question is execution speed and capital efficiency, not insight.

$36M at Counsel Health. $108M+ at Galileo. $650M burned by Forward Health. Clara enters with YC-scale funding against competitors who have years of head start, established provider networks, and multi-state operations. The bull case writes itself: these founders already built the playbook at Circle Medical, they know how to hire clinicians and navigate compliance faster than any first-time team, and the AI-first approach (human clinicians reviewing AI recommendations rather than delivering care directly) creates fundamentally different unit economics than Forward Health's CarePod hardware model. The ClassDojo parallel applies — Clara is tackling a schlep domain (healthcare regulation) where the founders' execution quality could overcome my sector preference and competitive concerns. The strongest version of the bull case says Favvas is the Patrick Collison of healthcare: built Auctomatic first, then saw the bigger opportunity and built Stripe. Circle Medical was the training run; Clara is the real company. But Stripe entered a space where developers were the customers and the idea seemed bad (another payment processor?). Clara enters a space where consumers are the customers and the idea seems obviously good. That difference matters in my framework.

Clara's founding team also triggers one of my red flags: none of the three named founders appear to be the person who builds the core AI product. Favvas is a CEO and product manager. Ahmed is a designer. Swift is a clinician. They're hiring for a "Founding Data Engineer" and "Founding Product Engineer" — the AI backbone of an AI-first company is being recruited, not cofounded. For a company whose entire thesis is that AI can serve as the primary care interface, the absence of a technical AI builder among the founders is a structural gap. Circle Medical's playbook was clinical operations and telehealth delivery; Clara's challenge is fundamentally different — it requires building an AI system that can reason over medical records with clinical accuracy. The team that scaled Circle Medical may not be the team that builds Clara's AI. I would need to understand who is actually building the model and whether that person has founder-level commitment.

One verifiable condition would flip me to Invest: if Clara can show me 5-7% weekly growth in active patients over a sustained period — even with a small base — that would demonstrate that the AI-first model generates the kind of compounding usage that justifies the consensus market. Alternatively, if the founding team added a technical AI cofounder with genuine machine learning depth, that would close the gap I see in their ability to build the core product. The dimension that most holds Clara back is the consensus nature of the idea — strong founders in a crowded, well-funded space where nobody thinks the idea is bad. The dimension that most supports Clara is organic problem discovery — a decade of lived experience in digital primary care is exactly the kind of authentic founder-problem relationship I prize. Favvas, Ahmed, and Swift clearly understand primary care delivery at a level their competitors' founding teams may not match. But understanding the problem and being positioned to win the market are different things, and Clara's capital disadvantage against $36M+ funded rivals executing similar models is real.
