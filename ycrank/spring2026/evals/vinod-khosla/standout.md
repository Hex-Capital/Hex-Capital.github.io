# Standout -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Dex raised $3.1M from a16z's Speedrun program in April 2025 building a candidate-side AI talent agent — the exact same thesis as Standout, launched earlier, with stronger distribution backing. When I see a pre-seed company enter a space where a well-funded competitor has already staked the identical claim, my first question isn't "who executes better?" — it's "is the prize worth fighting over?" And here, Standout's prize is a more convenient job search for engineers at Sequoia- and YC-backed startups. That's a feature, not a future. If Standout fully succeeds — every startup tech professional has an AI WhatsApp agent managing their career — what changes in the world? People find jobs slightly faster. They negotiate slightly better salaries. Nobody's life is fundamentally different. The consequence of full success is a profitable recruiting marketplace, which is fine for most investors but represents exactly the kind of moderate-return outcome I avoid. The dossier confirms Standout is focused on an already-privileged cohort at elite-backed startups, not democratizing opportunity for the billions who lack access to any career infrastructure at all.

Alexis Aftalion deserves credit — scaling Zealy to 1.5M MAU and $3M ARR in twelve months is a legitimate signal. That's not a vanity metric; that's real execution velocity in a community-building context, and his pivot from web3 to recruiting suggests adaptability rather than rigidity. Witold de La Chapelle's 100K MAU job board and engineering tenure at Dropbox, Samsara, and Chime provide genuine founder-market fit — he's been the target user. Standout has, by pre-seed standards, a credible founding team. But credible teams building incremental products don't clear my filter. The strongest bull case here invokes my DoorDash exception: immigrant founder, massive market ($968B global recruiting), marketplace dynamics. The parallel collapses on consequence magnitude. DoorDash transformed food access for hundreds of millions of people who previously couldn't get restaurant meals delivered. Standout optimizes job matching for people who already have LinkedIn, warm networks, and access to the best opportunities on the planet. The consequence asymmetry is not close.

Standout's core technology — an LLM conversational agent on WhatsApp that matches candidates to a scraped database of 3,000+ roles across 1,457 startups — is the definition of a wrapper on existing infrastructure. The dossier states it plainly: "The core technology (LLM-based conversational agent + job matching) is replicable." No proprietary model, no novel architecture, no data flywheel that compounds over time in a way competitors cannot replicate. Standout's 35% of beta users reaching a first interview within four days is a nice metric, but it measures operational execution, not a technical discontinuity. When I backed Rocket Lab, the Rutherford engine's electric turbopumps and 3D-printed combustion chambers represented a genuinely non-linear approach to cost reduction — something competitors could not simply replicate by calling the same API. Standout has no equivalent wedge. Every major LLM provider can power an identical agent tomorrow, and Dex is already doing it with voice calls instead of WhatsApp.

Standout is redistributing existing recruiting spend — employers already pay $968B annually to hire people. Standout wants to redirect those fees through a candidate-side agent rather than creating demand that didn't exist before. My highest-conviction investments create new markets: OpenAI didn't optimize existing software, it created an entirely new capability. Impossible Foods didn't make better soybeans, it synthesized a new protein. Standout makes an existing process marginally more efficient from the candidate's perspective. The "Hollywood agent" metaphor is clever marketing, but Hollywood agents take 10% of talent earnings in a winner-take-all industry with massive compensation variance — startup recruiting has neither the compensation concentration nor the loyalty dynamics that make talent representation a durable business model. Standout's monetization is unproven (the product is listed as "Free" on Product Hunt with no public revenue), and the employer willingness to pay placement fees to a candidate-side agent is an untested buying motion.

The one condition that would flip me: if Standout demonstrated that its AI agent could replace not just recruiters but entire career advisory functions — negotiation, skill development, career trajectory planning — at near-zero marginal cost for the 3 billion workers globally who have zero access to career infrastructure, and showed a proprietary data or model advantage that created compounding returns with scale. That would be a consequence worth backing. As it stands, Standout is a talented team building a nice product for a narrow, privileged market with no technical moat and a funded competitor that got there first. The team quality, anchored by Alexis Aftalion's Zealy track record, is the strongest dimension — but a strong athlete running a 100-meter dash when I'm looking for someone attempting to break the sound barrier still doesn't clear the bar.
