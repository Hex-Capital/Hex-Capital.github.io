# General Instinct -- Paul Graham Evaluation

General Instinct is a credentialed pair attacking a genuinely schleppy problem -- cross-vendor model compression across Jetson, Apple Neural Engine, Snapdragon, ARM, and mobile NPUs -- without the GitHub trails or tribal hacker markers I usually find on the founders I write checks into. Bill Jiao and Guanming Wang have the right resumes (Siemens multimodal foundation model; ex-DeepMind, Cambridge MPhil) and one production reference on Jetson Orin NX at 111 ms cold start, but the dossier reads more like a strong applied-research team than a **Reddit** (2005)- or **Stripe** (2010)-style hacker pair, and that gap is what I keep circling back to.

The strongest thing General Instinct has going for it is the schlep. **Coinbase** (2012) won my conviction because Brian Armstrong actually wanted to do the bitcoin compliance grind nobody else in the batch would touch, and the regulatory friction itself became the moat. General Instinct's surface-area problem is structurally similar: NVIDIA ships TensorRT, Apple ships Core ML, Qualcomm just bought Edge Impulse on Mar 10, 2025 and bundles its own SDK with Snapdragon -- a third-party cross-device runtime has to grind through certification, performance regressions, and channel conflict on each silicon vendor simultaneously. That is unpleasant work in exactly the way "Schlep Blindness" (January 2012) described, and most YC AI applicants this batch are routing around it toward agent frameworks and wrappers. Where General Instinct diverges from the **Coinbase** (2012) analog is that Armstrong's appetite for the grind was visible in his hand-built Android wallet that he sent after the rejection; in General Instinct's dossier I see the schlep as a market thesis, not yet as a demonstrated founder appetite.

The hacker-genealogy hole is where I get nervous. The Pass Example I keep in my head is the polished-AI-wrapper team with the glossy deck and no shipped artifact, and the failure mechanism there is that you can't tell whether the founders are technical-by-credential or technical-by-instinct until pressure hits. General Instinct's dossier explicitly returns "No public GitHub repos found" for both Bill Jiao and Guanming Wang. Wang won an Agno hackathon for $5,000 in 2025 and has 30k Rednote followers, which is signal but not the **Reddit** (2005)-Swartz-co-authored-RSS-spec kind of signal. DeepMind on a resume is hireable credentialism; an open-source compression project that physical-AI teams were already forking before YC would have been the artifact I needed to see. Hackers leave tracks, and General Instinct's tracks are conspicuously thin for a team claiming to compress frontier models onto five distinct silicon families.

On animal resourcefulness, the compounding question over 18 months is whether General Instinct's one shipped Jetson Orin NX demo expands into demos on Apple Neural Engine, Snapdragon, and ARM with comparable latency numbers, or whether the "any frontier model, any edge device, within any latency budget" tagline collapses to a Jetson-only product when reality intrudes. The 111 ms cold start / 100% within 150 ms / zero cloud calls result is real, and the tardigrade-protein patents Jiao filed in high school are an unusual data point that I weight more than most readers would -- that is an animal tell. But there is no cereal-box equivalent here, no pre-rejection comeback stunt, no unscalable revenue improvisation; absent that I apply something close to the default.

For idea-organicity, the counterfactual that would flip General Instinct a tier is straightforward: if Jiao or Wang had personally lost a robotics deployment because a Siemens multimodal model couldn't run offline on a factory-floor Jetson, and they had written that story up, this would read like **Dropbox** (2007)'s USB-stick origin. The dossier doesn't tell that story. It tells an adjacent story -- they worked on frontier models, they noticed frontier models don't deploy to edge, they built a compression runtime. That is "spotted a market opportunity" wearing research clothing, which is closer to my Pass Example than to the Houston-forgot-his-USB-stick canonical case.

The cofounder configuration on General Instinct is a pair, which is the right shape per "What We Look for in Founders" (October 2010) -- comparable to **Stripe** (2010)'s two Collisons in structure if not in relationship-depth. What the dossier doesn't establish is whether Jiao and Wang shipped anything together before General Instinct; "international students... dreamed of building startups since high school" is narrative without a prior artifact. Two strong individuals who met for the startup is a weaker signal than two who built something together first, and the dossier leaves me unable to tell which I'm looking at.

My 36-month forecast on General Instinct: acquired by NVIDIA or Qualcomm as a $30-80M talent-and-tech tuck-in once the cross-vendor promise collapses into a single-chip-vendor partnership, with the Wang/Jiao team landing inside the acquirer's edge-AI org. The mechanism is that the hardware-vendor channel conflict the dossier already names will force a choose-a-side moment within 18 months, and the founders' resume profile -- DeepMind, Siemens -- is exactly the profile chip vendors acquire when they want frontier-model compression expertise without building it. This is **Kiko** (2005)-shaped, not **Twitch** (2007)-shaped, and the absence of the hacker-genealogy markers is what tilts it.

The strongest bull case against my forecast is the **Coinbase** (2012) precedent itself: schlep blindness as moat. If General Instinct's cross-vendor compression catalog compounds into a per-device profile library that Qualcomm and NVIDIA can't easily replicate because their incentives stop at their own silicon, then the Jetson Orin NX demo is the first node in a defensible graph and the dossier is right that this is a structural moat the chipmakers can't close. That case stings because Armstrong's compliance grind looked like execution risk too. What dismantles it for me is the missing artifact -- Armstrong sent the wallet after rejection; General Instinct's founders sent one production reference and a tagline. The single condition that flips my verdict to Invest: a signed pilot with a named robotics OEM (Skydio, Boston Dynamics, ABB, Symbotic) within 12 months of the YC batch, paired with at least one second-platform demo (Apple Neural Engine or Snapdragon) matching the Jetson latency numbers. Until then, General Instinct sits in the neutral band where I watch but don't write.
