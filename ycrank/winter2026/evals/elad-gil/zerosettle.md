# ZeroSettle -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most striking structural feature of ZeroSettle's market position is a double-sided competitive conflict: Apple cannot build this product without cannibalizing $27 billion in annual commissions, and RevenueCat — the dominant mobile subscription infrastructure company with 70,000+ apps — cannot build it without jeopardizing its relationship with Apple and access to StoreKit APIs. When I see structural vacancies like this, where the two most logical competitors are organizationally incapable of entering, I pay attention. This is the same dynamic I recognized when Google's Project Maven cancellation created a defense AI vacuum that Anduril filled. The question is whether the vacancy is durable and whether this is the team to fill it.

The regulatory catalyst is real and specific — Judge Gonzalez Rogers' April 2025 contempt ruling, the EU DMA effective March 2024, Japan's Mobile Software Competition Act in December 2025. This is a genuine structural moment, not a gradual trend. Before April 2025, alternative iOS billing was legally impermissible in the U.S. After April 2025, it's legally required to be permitted. Binary regulatory shifts like this create market openings. But here's my concern: this is not a non-obvious market. It's the opposite. Every developer who pays Apple's 30% knows the opportunity. Every payments company has noticed. Paddle raised at a $1.4 billion valuation and explicitly cited Apple's ecosystem opening as a growth driver. Neon Pay raised $14M from Thrive and a16z. Stripe published its own guide for iOS developers. When I invested in PagerDuty, nobody thought operations alerting was a big market — that's what made it non-obvious. When I backed Coinbase, most investors thought crypto was speculative nonsense. ZeroSettle is entering a market where the TAM is literally in court filings and multiple well-capitalized players are already positioning. That's consensus, not contrarian.

The competitive exposure compounds with a platform dependency problem. ZeroSettle's entire payment processing runs on Stripe. Stripe already published guidance for exactly this use case. If Stripe decides to add migration campaign logic, retention flows, and entitlement syncing — none of which are technically novel — ZeroSettle's value-add layer becomes a thin wrapper on someone else's infrastructure. This triggers my "software-driven vs. software-aware" filter. Is the migration campaign logic ("Switch & Save," "Save the Sale") a genuine software moat, or is it a feature set that any competent team could build on top of Stripe in a few weeks? The dossier notes the core SDK technology is replicable and the campaign logic isn't deeply proprietary. That's concerning.

The strongest bull case: both founders worked as Apple systems software engineers, which gives them genuinely rare insider knowledge of App Store billing infrastructure, StoreKit internals, and how Apple enforces its policies. If you believe this domain expertise creates a 12-18 month execution advantage in navigating the compliance complexity across the U.S. (fees TBD), EU (5% CTC), and Japan (separate rules), then ZeroSettle could establish MoR switching costs before competitors arrive. The MoR role is the right strategic choice — once you're managing a developer's subscribers, migrating away means subscriber disruption, which is exactly the kind of integration depth that creates durable retention. If alternative billing adoption follows an exponential curve as regulatory clarity increases, ZeroSettle could become the default subscription infrastructure layer outside Apple's billing system. The Founding Partner Program (free for two years) is a smart distribution move to get embedded early. If this works, the product-to-distribution trajectory is real: billing → retention analytics → pricing optimization → full subscription lifecycle management. That path mirrors how Gusto went from payroll to HR platform.

But this bull case requires multiple things to go right simultaneously, which violates my single-miracle test. Miracle one: the Ninth Circuit's remanded question on whether Apple can charge fees on linked-out purchases must resolve favorably — if Apple imposes even a 10% fee, the savings gap between 30% and ZeroSettle's 5% narrows dramatically, especially when you factor in potentially lower conversion rates on web checkout versus native IAP. Miracle two: developers must be willing to risk Apple's displeasure through App Review, which Apple has historically used as a gatekeeping mechanism. Miracle three: ZeroSettle must establish sufficient market position before Paddle, Stripe, or another well-funded competitor builds a comparable turnkey solution. When the path to success requires a favorable regulatory resolution the team can't control, a behavioral shift in developer risk tolerance, AND a competitive timing window, that's compounding low-probability events. That's three miracles, not one.

The founders have relevant domain expertise, and the documentation site and multi-platform SDK (Swift, React Native, Flutter, Kotlin) suggest real engineering work has been done. But I see no evidence of shipped traction — no users, no revenue, no customer names, no press, no Product Hunt launch. At pre-seed that's not disqualifying, but combined with a consensus market and well-funded competition, it means there's no signal yet that this team can capture the moment faster than the alternatives. I'd want to see a few developers actively migrating subscribers through the platform before getting comfortable with the execution thesis.
