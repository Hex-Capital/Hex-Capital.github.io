# Clawvisor -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Eric Levine, solo founder, team size 1, shipping a Go gateway at 86 GitHub stars while Composio raises $25M and Nango raises $7.5M to ship 500+ and 700+ adapters respectively — that asymmetry IS what Clawvisor is. The signature: extreme team-to-value leverage paired with weak access-democratization pattern fit. Clawvisor is a shrinking-company infrastructure bet by a YC alum who already exited Berbix to Socure for ~$70M, not a democratization play that opens a gatekept resource to a new population. The question is whether one principal with the right adjacent specific knowledge can outpace four well-funded agent-led teams.

Team-to-value leverage is where Clawvisor scores highest. The HIGH rung is Midjourney at ~163 employees doing $500M revenue and Cursor at <50 people doing $500M ARR — the shrinking-company thesis I've been writing about since 2012. The LOW rung is Wish, where marketplace operations forced linear headcount and the leverage I thought I was buying never materialized. Clawvisor sits closer to the HIGH rung than any company I've looked at this batch: hiring=false, a single founder shipping a Go repo at 86 stars and 11 forks, 14+ adapters live, a Termo skill at 247 downloads, and a Claude Code plugins repo published. Levine is doing with one person what Composio is doing with a Series A team. That is the exact structure I want to back.

Access democratization is where Clawvisor scores lowest. The HIGH rung is AngelList and CoinList — products that removed structural gatekeepers (insider angel networks, securities lawyers for token launches) and gave millions of people access to something previously locked to thousands. The LOW rung is Clearview AI, which inverts the pattern entirely. Clawvisor sits near the bottom of the mid-band: it's a tool for builders, which fits my unstated portfolio pattern (Stack Overflow, Notion, Codecademy), but it does not unlock a gatekept resource. It makes agent authorization safer for developers who already have agents. That's useful infrastructure, not democratization. The dossier offers no signal that Clawvisor expands access to a new population — sales@clawvisor.com is an enterprise-sales email.

Specific knowledge productization is the dimension that decides this check. Aravind Srinivas at Perplexity is the HIGH rung — years at Google Brain and OpenAI productized directly into the company's core engine. The LOW rung is the AI-wrapper MBA archetype I pass on every batch: API-call companies with no founder knowledge that couldn't be replicated by any competent operator. Clawvisor's Levine sits closer to the HIGH rung than the LOW: five years of Airbnb Trust & Safety engineering plus Berbix's identity-document forensics work (spoofed/AI-generated ID detection) is genuinely non-trainable expertise in the credential-handling and abuse-detection problem space that Clawvisor targets. It's adjacent specific knowledge, not native specific knowledge — Berbix verified humans, Clawvisor authorizes agents — but the adjacency is real and rare.

Permissionless leverage architecture: Twitter and Notion are the HIGH rung — pure code, zero marginal cost, scaling to millions with small teams. Wish is the LOW rung — marketplace operations dressed up as a software business. Clawvisor's gateway architecture is pure code at the request path, which sits high. But adapter-building is structurally labor-leveraged work: Composio's 500+ and Nango's 700+ are not assembled by AI agents — they are assembled by engineers. Clawvisor's 14+ versus those numbers is a 30x deficit in the most labor-leveraged part of the product. Principal authenticity sits in similar territory: Sahil Lavingia rebuilding Gumroad solo after the Series A failure is the HIGH rung; the AI wrapper team that picked the idea because it was fundable is the LOW. Levine built the first version himself in Go, is operating solo, and the YC Visiting Group Partner role means he had every option to start a fundable consensus play and chose this instead. That reads as principal, not agent — though the YC VGP background is also exactly the profile that produces polished pitches I've trained myself to discount.

Forecast, 36 months: Clawvisor gets acquired by Okta (Auth0 parent) or WorkOS for $40-90M as a feature-and-team acquisition, mirroring Levine's Berbix-to-Socure $70M outcome — not a billion-dollar standalone company. The mechanism producing this is precisely the dimensional signature: extreme team-to-value leverage gets him to a credible product with proprietary risk-scoring data, but the absence of access-democratization or unique-leverage architecture means there is no structural reason this becomes a category-defining standalone. Levine has run this exact play before. The expected value is fine; the convexity is capped.

The bear case is harder than the bull case. Composio at $29M total funding with 500+ integrations and an RL-trained skills layer, Nango at 700+ APIs, Arcade at $12M with explicit MCP-runtime positioning and a Linux Foundation board seat, plus Auth0 Token Vault and WorkOS FGA shipping native agent auth from incumbent identity-graph distribution — that is six well-funded competitors, three of them with structural advantages Clawvisor cannot replicate solo. If MCP standardizes an authorization spec, the gateway architecture commoditizes into a protocol feature before the data flywheel can compound. The dossier shows no proprietary risk-scoring corpus today, no named enterprise customers, no signed LOIs. This could be a category being eaten by the protocol layer while Clawvisor ships its 15th adapter. What wins the argument is Berbix: Levine has shipped this exact shape of company before in an adjacent domain and exited it. The convex bet structure means I lose 1x and the upside is a real outcome. **Flip condition:** if Clawvisor signs three named enterprise customers (Stripe-tier or comparable) on credential vaulting before Auth0 ships full parity in Token Vault, the proprietary risk-scoring data starts compounding and this becomes a billion-dollar standalone rather than a $50M acqui-hire. I'd take the meeting and write the check.
