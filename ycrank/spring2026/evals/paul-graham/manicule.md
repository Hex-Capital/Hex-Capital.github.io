# Manicule -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The origin story here is honest in a way that most YC applications aren't. Naman spent three years doing technical documentation for devtools companies as a freelancer. Shreyans was a founding engineer at Supermemory. They worked together on Supermemory's docs -- Naman writing, Shreyans building -- and watched those docs go viral on Twitter and close enterprise deals. They didn't sit in a room and brainstorm "what's hot in AI." They were already doing the work, noticed it mattered enormously, and decided to build a company around it. That's organic problem discovery. It's the same pattern as Dropbox -- Houston didn't research the file sync market, he kept forgetting his USB drive. These founders didn't research the docs market, they kept watching documentation drive real business outcomes for their clients.

But here's where I get stuck. What they built is a services business. A documentation agency. An excellent one, apparently -- $15K MRR, profitable, five paying clients including Greptile and Reducto and Rootly. But the fundamental unit of production is a human writer assisted by AI tools, and revenue scales linearly with the number of writers they employ. Five people, five clients, $15K MRR -- that's $3K per client per month, which is roughly what you'd pay a part-time freelance technical writer. The "AI-native" framing describes their process, not their product. There's no public evidence of proprietary tooling, no SaaS component, no platform play that would create the kind of compounding returns startups need. I've seen many talented people start services businesses and do well -- but "doing well" for a services business means something very different than what venture investors need.

The schlep is real but not deep. Documentation is the classic neglected task -- everyone knows it matters, nobody wants to do it. That's a form of schlep blindness, but it's the shallow kind. The reason people avoid documentation isn't because it involves navigating banks and regulators the way payments did for Stripe, or because it requires building trust infrastructure between strangers the way lodging did for Airbnb. People avoid it because it's tedious. And "tedious" creates an addressable market, but it doesn't create structural competitive protection. Any capable technical writer who subscribes to Claude or GPT-4 can offer a similar service tomorrow. Mintlify, already at $10M ARR with $21M in funding, could bolt on a managed services tier if they wanted. The barrier to entry is craft and relationships, not technology or regulatory complexity.

The bull case is worth taking seriously. If these founders accumulate enough institutional knowledge about documentation patterns across dozens of devtools companies, they might discover the product hiding inside their service -- the way Viaweb started as building web stores for individual clients before we realized we could build a platform that let anyone build their own. The "why now" is legitimate: thousands of AI startups are launching developer tools and every one of them needs documentation that nobody on the team wants to write. And being profitable at pre-seed is a genuine signal of financial discipline -- they're default alive, which means they have time to figure out the product transition. But I see no evidence they're thinking about that transition. No public repo showing custom tooling. No mention of a platform roadmap. The AI agents they describe -- testing code snippets, auditing links, drafting from OpenAPI specs -- sound like scripts wrapping existing LLM APIs, not defensible technology.

What concerns me most is what this company would look like at 10x scale. At $150K MRR with 50 clients, you'd need perhaps 30-40 people. That's a consulting firm, not a startup. The founders would spend their time managing writers and client relationships rather than building product. That's the business model trap I've warned against -- when the path to growth requires becoming managers rather than makers, the company loses its speed advantage. The founding team is solid -- Shreyans is clearly a capable engineer, and Naman has genuine domain expertise in exactly this niche. Their co-founder relationship predates Manicule through both Duelance and the Supermemory engagement, which matters. But their skills are being deployed in service delivery, not product creation.

I'd want to invest in these founders if they were building documentation infrastructure -- the picks-and-shovels play for docs the way Stripe was picks-and-shovels for payments. Code verification tools that every devtools company runs in CI. Automated documentation generation that actually works, shipped as software. Something with zero marginal cost per additional user. The organic insight is there. The schlep awareness is there. What's missing is the leap from "we do this well for hire" to "we built something that does this at scale without us in the loop." If they make that leap, this becomes a very different company. Right now, they're a profitable freelance shop with strong taste and a good client list. That's a fine business. It's not a startup.
