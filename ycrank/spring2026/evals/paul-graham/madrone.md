# Madrone -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Madrone's signature is schlep-rich and animal-thin: Akshay Trikha and Erik Meike have picked a hardware grind — chiller-less dew-point cooling for Texas hyperscalers — that almost no software-default YC applicant would touch, but the dossier contains no pre-funding stunt, no installed pilot, and no named hyperscaler LOI. That asymmetry is the whole evaluation. The schlep is real. The animal isn't on the page yet.

The schlep itself is the strongest thing Madrone has, and it places between **Coinbase** (2012) and **DoorDash** (2013) on that ladder. Brian Armstrong won his slot because he wanted the bitcoin compliance grind nobody else in the batch wanted; Madrone is volunteering for multi-year qualification with hyperscale procurement, capex-heavy field trials at 45°C ambient, and direct cannibalization of Vertiv's and Munters' largest SKUs. That is the kind of work software founders unconsciously route around. Where Madrone diverges from the **Coinbase** rung is that Armstrong shipped an Android wallet prototype during his rejection week — a concrete artifact you could point at. Madrone's dossier names a "novel thermodynamic process" and a 20°C-supply-water claim, but no third-party measurement, no installed unit, no patent. The schlep is chosen; not yet entered.

My **AI-wrapper-team pass example** failed on animal resourcefulness because the dossier was glossy deck and no shipped artifact, and the same failure mechanism — credential-by-resume in place of pre-funding stunt — appears in Madrone, though more mildly. Trikha is described by his UC Berkeley materials science research and a QuantumScape role; Meike by Apple Hardware Development Engineer and a Founding Engineer title at Watney Robotics. Those are titles. The one near-artifact in the whole dossier is Meike's "compressor-eliminating residential A/C system with patent pending" from Harvey Mudd — which is genuinely on-thesis for what Madrone is now building, but it's a college project, not a hyperscaler pilot, not a cereal box. NVIDIA Inception membership is not a signal; thousands of companies are in it. There is no Drew Houston demo video here, no Brian Armstrong rescue prototype, no Brian Chesky cereal boxes. Absence of tracks where animals leave tracks.

Mechanism analysis on organic-pain: neither Madrone founder has run a data center hot in Texas at 3 a.m. Trikha's ML interatomic potential research at Berkeley and Meike's Apple consumer hardware work are adjacent disciplines, not the lived workflow of the customer. Over 18 months this compounds badly in hardware sales — when a Meta facilities engineer pushes back on wet-bulb assumptions in humid Gulf Coast summers, the founders who have personally watched a chiller plant fail will iterate faster than the founders who modeled it. Meike's compressor-less A/C patent gives Madrone partial credit on the build side of the "what do you wish someone would make for you" filter, but neither founder is the user.

Counterfactual on hacker genealogy: to flip this dimension a tier, Trikha's 24-repo GitHub would need one notable open-source thermal or ML systems project with real stars, or Meike would need a public competitive engineering trail — JITX-era PCB work shipped publicly, a HN-front-page hardware post, anything readable from outside. The dossier surfaces neither. Trikha published an ML scaling-laws research line at Berkeley, which is real technical work, but it isn't the Patrick Collison Young Scientist Lisp prize before **Stripe** (2010) or the Aaron Swartz RSS spec before **Reddit** (2005). Madrone reads as technical-by-credential, not hacker-by-tribal-marker.

Single-anchor parallel on cofounder configuration: the **Reddit** (2005) Huffman/Ohanian pair worked because they had been UVA dorm roommates and the chemistry was load-bearing through the pivot from Mmm to link aggregation. Madrone gives me two Bay Area founders with overlapping hardware exposure (QuantumScape, Watney Robotics, Apple) and no disclosed pre-startup working history. That doesn't fail the test; it leaves it unanswered, which is where the YC interview usually does the work I can't do from a dossier.

My 36-month forecast on Madrone: one Tier-2 Texas colocation pilot installed by month 18, stuck in qualification limbo with a hyperscaler, under $500K of revenue, raising a difficult bridge into a strategic round led by an industrial corp-dev arm — most plausibly Vertiv or Munters as defensive cover, possibly an acqui-hire of Meike into a Munters R&D group if the bridge fails. The mechanism is the gap between schlep-chosen and animal-demonstrated: a 2-person team without a pre-funding stunt rarely out-hustles a multi-year hyperscale procurement cycle against $9B-revenue incumbents already qualified into NVIDIA reference designs.

The strongest bear-flip on my pass is the **Airbnb** (2009) precedent — I rejected that idea inside my head and the cereal boxes overrode me. The Madrone bull case is that Meike's college compressor-less A/C patent IS the cereal-box equivalent: a contrarian thermodynamic bet the founder already made before the institutional check, paired with Texas hyperscale timing that wasn't acute until 2024-2025. I don't buy it. The cereal boxes were $40,000 of stunt revenue extracted from a hostile market in a quarter; Meike's patent is a 2021 college artifact with no field deployment and no customer. Madrone passes on what I can see today. The verifiable condition that flips me to invest: a signed pilot with a named hyperscaler — Meta, Microsoft, Oracle, or a top-5 Texas colo — disclosed within nine months, with installed-unit performance data. Until then, this is a clever idea with soft pre-funding evidence, which is exactly the pattern where I lose money.
