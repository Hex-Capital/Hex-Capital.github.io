# Terminal Use -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I see is that this is one of the most consensus markets in tech right now. Agent infrastructure has $174 million deployed across direct competitors -- E2B at $32M, Daytona at $24M, Modal at $111M -- plus OpenAI bundling hosted runtimes into Frontier. Every investor I know has seen a "Vercel for agents" deck. When I invested in Uber at a $4M valuation, ride-sharing was illegal. When I backed Bitcoin in 2014, people thought it was a joke. Terminal Use is entering a category where five well-funded teams are already racing, the model providers are vertically integrating, and the thesis -- "agents need infrastructure" -- is universally agreed upon. Universal agreement means the pricing already reflects the opportunity. Zero GitHub stars against competitors with Fortune 100 customers and seven-figure monthly revenue is not a timing advantage. It's a timing problem.

The specific knowledge question is more interesting. Three Palantir engineers, all overlapping -- Vivek led what the YC page describes as "one of the largest agent use cases across US hospitals," Stavros built coding infrastructure and dev tooling, Filip worked on the Ontology platform. Palantir is one of maybe five companies on Earth that has actually deployed complex agent systems in hostile production environments -- hospitals, government, defense. That's not knowledge you get from a bootcamp or six months of tinkering. Vivek has felt the pain of agent orchestration at scale in a domain where failure has consequences. This is genuine, non-transferable knowledge about how agents break in production. But there's a gap between "I deployed Palantir's agents in hospitals" and "I can build the platform that every other team deploys their agents on." Enterprise deployment knowledge and infrastructure-platform knowledge are adjacent, not identical. The founders have experienced the problem firsthand; the question is whether that experience translates into building a qualitatively better developer tool than teams who are already shipping and iterating with real users.

The leverage architecture is correct in form -- infrastructure platform, SDK-based, CLI deployment, consumption implied. This is the picks-and-shovels layer I generally prefer. But I distinguish between platforms that create new leverage and platforms that make existing workflows incrementally easier. Replit didn't just make coding faster for existing programmers -- it made programming accessible to people who would never have written code otherwise. That's new leverage. Terminal Use makes deploying agents somewhat easier for teams already deploying agents. The filesystem forking and task-pinned versioning are clever engineering, but they don't unlock a qualitatively new capability. One engineer using Terminal Use produces roughly the same output as one engineer using E2B or raw containers with more DevOps. The Woz product -- agents in Slack -- gets closer to new leverage by letting non-technical team members interact with agents, but at $49-99/user/month SaaS pricing, it's a feature, not a platform.

The bull case I have to take seriously: agent infrastructure is genuinely early, the market is growing at 50% CAGR, and the specific niche of persistent filesystem agents for long-running background tasks may be underserved. If background agents become the dominant computing paradigm -- and the Gartner prediction of 40% enterprise apps embedding agents by 2026 suggests they might -- then the team that nails the developer experience for this specific workload pattern captures enormous value. The Palantir pedigree is real and rare among competitors. E2B focuses on ephemeral sandboxes; Terminal Use focuses on persistent, long-running agents -- these are genuinely different architectural bets. If the market bifurcates, Terminal Use could own the "stateful background agent" segment. And the founding team's deep relationship -- university together, Palantir together -- is the kind of trust foundation that survives the hardest years.

But I keep coming back to the competitive dynamics. The agent infrastructure space has the signature of a hot consensus market: too much capital, too many smart teams, and model providers with every incentive to bundle. OpenAI has already launched Frontier. Anthropic could ship hosted Claude Agent infrastructure tomorrow. When the model provider can say "deploy your agent here with one click," the value of a third-party orchestration layer compresses. Vercel succeeded because React and Next.js created a stable framework layer that cloud providers couldn't absorb -- but agent frameworks are in violent flux right now. Building infrastructure on top of a shifting foundation is a timing bet, and the timing bet here is that frameworks stabilize before model providers swallow the hosting layer. I don't see evidence that Terminal Use has the distribution, the community, or the technical moat to win that race against teams with 10-50x their capital. Competent founders, real market, wrong structure for asymmetric returns.
