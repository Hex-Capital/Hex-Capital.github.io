# Synthetic Sciences -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that jumps out about Synthetic Sciences is the competitive landscape. When I look at who else is building "AI co-scientists," I see Edison Scientific with $70M, Elicit with $31M and 400,000 monthly researchers, Sakana at $2.65B, and Google itself announcing an AI Co-Scientist product. This is the definition of a hot category where the consensus has already formed. I've said before that the most impressive companies I see are often not working on whatever everyone else is excited about. When every well-funded lab in the world is building the same thing, the upside for a $1.5M pre-seed company gets structurally compressed. The question becomes: what do these two founders know that Jeff Dean and Google's entire research division don't?

The organic origin story is real but complicated. Bansal has 15+ publications at ICML, NeurIPS, and ACL, with research stints at CMU, Harvard, and MIT CSAIL. Gangwani qualified for USACO Platinum and ranked at the International Olympiad in AI. These are not people who read about scientific research in TechCrunch — they've lived inside the fragmented workflow of Jupyter notebooks, Overleaf documents, and cloud GPU providers. That's genuine organic discovery, and it counts for something. But the trajectory tells a different story than the credentials. They started with InkVell, a focused tool for turning research into publication-ready documents. That's a narrow, specific product born from a narrow, specific pain — exactly the kind of deliberately contained fire I like to see. Then during the YC batch, they pivoted to "AI co-scientists that run research end-to-end" with four separate modes spanning biology, ML, writing, and model improvement. Going from document formatting to orchestrating swarms of autonomous research agents across GPU clusters is not a natural evolution of a product finding its users. It looks like a narrative expansion to match the ambition of the category, which is exactly the wrong direction.

The bull case is that these two are genuinely precocious. An 18-year-old with a prior exit and 15+ papers at top venues is unusual by any standard. If the research publications reflect real technical depth — not just co-authorship on large projects — then Bansal specifically has the kind of hacker-researcher profile I'd want building a tool for researchers. The data flywheel concept, where product usage generates process-level training data that makes the agents better, is theoretically sound and could create a moat that pure-play AI labs won't replicate because they're not building the workflow product. If two obsessive young researchers built a tool that other researchers genuinely preferred to their existing workflow — if ML grad students started using it organically because it was just better — that would be a signal strong enough to override the competitive concerns. Stripe also entered a space where incumbents existed, but the Collisons were building for developers and the incumbents weren't. The analogous bet here would be that these founders understand ML researchers so well that Google's generalist approach can't compete on workflow fit.

But I don't see that user pull materializing yet. There are no public user counts, no community, no social media presence, no organic sharing signals. The pricing page exists but there's no evidence anyone is paying. The benchmark claim — 92% on "BixBench Verified" — can't be independently verified, and the published BixBench results show frontier models at 17% on open-answer tasks, which makes me uneasy about what "Verified" means as a subset. When a company's primary traction signal is an unverifiable benchmark, that's optimizing for the pitch rather than the product. The founders should be out recruiting users one by one, not polishing benchmark numbers.

The scope problem is what concerns me most practically. A 2-person team is building: a research mode with hypothesis-to-manuscript pipelines, a biology mode with protein design and genomics, a flywheel mode with RL training infrastructure, a writing mode with citation verification, GPU orchestration across providers, universal credential syncing, sandboxed execution environments, and a CLI tool. Each of those is a substantial engineering project on its own. This isn't schlep blindness — where one ugly problem deters competition — it's scope ambition that exceeds what two people can credibly deliver. The founders who succeed at this stage are the ones who stay deliberately narrow. Facebook launched at Harvard only. Stripe launched for developers only. InkVell was the right instinct — a contained product solving one painful thing. The expansion to "end-to-end research" during the batch feels like a response to the category's gravitational pull rather than to what users were actually asking for.

I respect the founders' raw talent. Publishing at ICML as a teenager and achieving a prior exit at 18 are real signals, and I wouldn't bet against them in general. But the combination of a consensus-hot category, a massively well-funded competitive field, an unverifiable primary traction claim, and a product scope that requires a team five times this size to execute — that's too many headwinds for the organic-origin signal to overcome. If they narrowed back down to something specific, found ten researchers who couldn't live without it, and demonstrated that their product-generated training data actually made the agents meaningfully better, I'd look again.
