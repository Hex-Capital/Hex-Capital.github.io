# Corvera -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Corvera: this is a sane team building the most consensus product imaginable. "AI agents that automate CPG supply chain operations" is not an idea that provokes confusion or skepticism from anyone in 2026 — it's the default pitch at every enterprise AI demo day. CPG AI adoption jumped from 42% to 71% in a single year. Crisp literally launched an "AI Agent Studio" in late 2025. Every supply chain software vendor on earth is bolting agentic workflows onto their existing product. When I describe my "sane person, insane idea" filter, the critical failure mode is a sane person with a sane idea — because you get competent execution aimed at an opportunity where the returns are already fully priced in by the market. Christopher Kong scaled Better Nature across 5,000 retail locations in six countries. Dirk Breeuwer built multi-agent AI systems at Google. Matthew Collins has Princeton CS and product chops. These are serious operators. But the idea itself has zero non-consensus energy. Nobody looks at this and says "that's crazy." They say "of course."

Now run my AI kill test: would this business be good if AI didn't exist? The answer is unambiguously no. Remove the AI layer and Corvera is... a supply chain management dashboard. A less-funded Cin7. The entire value proposition IS the AI. This is the exact inverse of my "cherry on top" framework. When I invested in Craftwork, the insight was that a painting company has an operational moat that software amplifies but cannot replace — you need actual painters, trucks, quality control, scheduling. Corvera doesn't own warehouses. It doesn't run trucks. It doesn't employ logistics workers. It processes purchase orders with language models. That's software, and software moats are dissolving faster than anyone predicted. The dossier itself acknowledges the core workflows are "well-understood operational problems" and that the "AI agent layer relies on generally available LLM capabilities." When your own research memo flags commoditization risk, you should listen.

The competitive landscape tells the structural story. Crisp has $127M+ in funding and 7,000+ brands. Settle has $506M. Unioncrate has been doing CPG supply chain AI since 2016 with Founders Fund backing. These aren't hypothetical future competitors — they're well-capitalized incumbents with existing distribution into exactly the customer segment Corvera targets. And the encroachment direction is clear: it's far easier for a data analytics platform with 7,000 brand relationships to add autonomous order processing than for a four-person startup to build distribution from scratch. The "agentic" framing — AI that acts, not just informs — is a feature differentiator, not a structural one. Features get absorbed.

The bull case requires me to take seriously Kong's operator credibility as a genuine distribution wedge. He lived this pain. He ran the CPG supply chain across six countries. That's not fake domain expertise — it's the real thing, and it means his founder network into CPG operators is probably the most efficient initial GTM channel possible. If Corvera can embed deeply enough into 15-50 brands' order-to-cash workflows, switching costs could compound, and the operational data accumulated across diverse supply chains could genuinely improve forecasting accuracy beyond what a generic competitor offers. The Birchbox lesson I carry is that narrative without moat produces the worst outcomes — but the counter-lesson is that sometimes operator-founders build switching costs through operational depth that looks trivial from the outside. If Kong's intuition about where automation breaks in CPG supply chains is as sharp as his track record suggests, Corvera might build workflow lock-in that looks like SaaS but functions more like infrastructure. I don't think this is the likely path, but I'm not dismissing it.

What I keep coming back to is the primitive-versus-application test. Corvera isn't creating a new layer that other businesses build on. It's not establishing a protocol or a standard. It's an application running on top of LLMs and existing supply chain infrastructure — one of dozens pursuing the same category. There's no compounding network effect where Corvera's success makes other companies' products better. The "Agentic OS" branding gestures at platform-level ambition, but the actual product is three vertical workflows (orders, inventory, forecasting) for a specific industry segment. That's a solid business if you win, but it's not an infinity story in the way I look for at seed. The narrative ceiling is "we become the operating system for mid-market CPG brands," which is a real market but a capped one — and one that SAP, Oracle, and a dozen funded startups are all reaching for simultaneously.

I'm passing. The founders have genuine credibility, and I respect Kong's operator background more than I respect most technical founders pitching into this space. But the structural positioning violates almost everything I look for: AI is the product rather than the cherry on top, the business model is SaaS in a world where SaaS moats are evaporating, the idea is pure consensus, and there's no new primitive being created. This is a feature waiting to be absorbed by an incumbent with distribution — and there are multiple incumbents with hundreds of millions in funding already targeting this exact customer.
