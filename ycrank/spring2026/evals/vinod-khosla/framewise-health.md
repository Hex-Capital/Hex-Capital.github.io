# Framewise Health -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

$320 million. That's the entire CMS HRRP penalty pool across 2,273 hospitals in FY2023. Framewise Health has built its entire economic justification around helping hospitals avoid their share of this pool — David Cui himself frames it as "$1M/year in Medicare readmission penalties" per hospital. When I evaluate a company, I start with one question: if Framewise Health fully succeeds — captures every penalized hospital, eliminates every avoidable readmission driven by poor discharge education — what happens to the world? The answer is that hospitals save money on government fines and some patients understand their medication instructions better. That is a useful outcome. It is not a consequence that changes how millions of people live. The ceiling of this business is defined by a regulatory penalty, which means the best case is a moderately profitable healthcare SaaS company. I've seen this pattern hundreds of times — AI applied to a specific enterprise vertical, automating 40% of a workflow, anchored by a clear ROI conversation. The consequence of success is a successful company, not a different world.

Framewise Health's core technology — LLM-generated video plus FHIR-based EHR integration plus AI avatars — is assembled from increasingly commoditized components. The dossier itself states that "the defensibility rests more on clinical workflow integration and hospital sales relationships than on the underlying AI technology." VidifyAI already advertises HIPAA-compliant healthcare video generation with BAA and SOC 2 Type II. Synthesia sits at $146M ARR and a $4B valuation with a general-purpose AI video platform that could add healthcare workflows as a feature. Framewise Health is not building new rocket engines like Peter Beck did at Rocket Lab — it is brokering cheaper launches on existing rockets. There is no step-function technical improvement here, no proprietary science, no non-linear insight. The FHIR integration creates switching costs once deployed, which is a business moat, not a technology moat. When I distinguish between companies I fund and companies I pass on, the dividing line is whether the technical approach produces a 10x improvement or a 2x improvement. Framewise Health produces a workflow improvement measured in nurse-hours saved and penalty dollars avoided. That is arithmetic, not disruption.

Tane Kim dropping out of medical school to build Framewise Health is the single most interesting founder signal in this dossier. That decision shows willingness to abandon a conventional path when a better opportunity appears — exactly the kind of irreverence I look for. Kim's time at the Lundquist Institute at Harbor-UCLA and at Sarepta Therapeutics provides genuine clinical domain credibility, and David Cui covers the technical build with his Brown CS background. The founding team is competent and appropriately composed for a health tech product. But I have zero evidence of learning velocity — no pivots, no public articulation of what surprised them, no signal of first-principles reasoning beyond credentials. When I backed the 19-year-old at Mach Industries building weapons systems, the signal was not the resume — it was the audacity of the vision and the visible trajectory of capability growth. Framewise Health's founders have sensible backgrounds for the problem they chose. That is a necessary condition for a good company, not a sufficient condition for the kind of company I invest in.

The bull case an advocate would make: hospital readmissions cause real patient harm, non-English-speaking patients face dangerous comprehension gaps at discharge, and Framewise Health could expand beyond HRRP penalties into all patient education across all encounters — making this a wedge into the $7.59B patient engagement market, not a niche HRRP optimizer. Tane Kim's clinical background combined with David Cui's engineering creates the right founding DNA, and EHR integration creates genuine switching costs that prevent Synthesia from casually entering the space. I have funded healthcare companies with regulatory complexity before — Oscar Health required navigating state insurance commissioners, which I normally avoid. But Oscar was attempting to transform health insurance for millions of Americans through a technology-first approach to an entire industry. Framewise Health is optimizing a single discharge workflow within existing hospital operations. The Oscar exception required consequence magnitude that justified accepting regulatory risk. Framewise Health's consequence magnitude does not clear that bar. The company's pricing is literally "anchored to the hospital's readmission volume and CMS HRRP penalty" — the value proposition lives and dies with a government program that the dossier notes is "subject to ongoing policy revision," with $284M–$297M in penalties potentially being redistributed due to Medicare Advantage calculation issues. Building on regulatory sand is a risk I learned to avoid the hard way with KiOR and Range Fuels.

Framewise Health does not make healthcare radically cheaper for patients. It makes compliance cheaper for hospital CFOs. The patient still pays the same for their hospital stay. The nurse gets some time back. The hospital avoids a penalty that CMS might restructure next year. When I think about radical affordability — the kind that drives my deepest convictions — I think about Curai delivering AI primary care at near-zero marginal cost, or Impossible Foods making protein cheaper than animal agriculture. Framewise Health is redistributing existing institutional spend, not collapsing a cost structure. The company would need to demonstrate that its technology could replace the entire discharge education function and expand into continuous patient education across all conditions — essentially becoming the AI layer that makes patient comprehension of medical information universally accessible and free — to trigger my affordability thesis. Nothing in the current framing suggests that ambition.

**Pass.** Framewise Health is a competently constructed healthcare SaaS company addressing a real pain point with existing technology. The two dimensions most decisive for this verdict are consequence magnitude (the ceiling is a $320M penalty pool and workflow efficiency gains, not a changed world) and non-linear technical approach (commoditized components assembled into a workflow, not a technical discontinuity). The condition that would flip me to Invest: evidence that Framewise Health's technology produces a measurable, discontinuous improvement in patient outcomes — not penalty avoidance, but actual clinical outcomes — at a scale that suggests the discharge video is a wedge into replacing human-delivered patient education entirely across all of medicine. Show me that the technology makes expert medical communication effectively free for every patient in every language, and the consequence magnitude conversation changes completely.
