# arc-prize-foundation -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most analytically striking thing about ARC Prize Foundation is what it *isn't*: it's not a company. It's a nonprofit funded by donations, with no revenue model, no equity value to accrue, and no commercial capture mechanism. The co-founders simultaneously run Ndea, a for-profit AGI research lab that raised $43M from Coatue, Factorial Capital, and Quiet Capital. The commercial upside lives in Ndea. ARC Prize Foundation, the entity YC is backing, is the benchmark and the brand -- but not the business. When I invest my own money, I need a path to financial return. A nonprofit that funds itself through donations from AI labs and individuals, with $1M+ going out the door annually in prize pools, has the return profile of a charitable contribution, not an angel investment. This dual-entity structure is the central issue, and everything else is downstream of it.

The structural moment is real. I've been tracking AI capability curves since 2021, and the need for benchmarks that measure genuine reasoning -- not pattern matching, not memorization -- is acute. Every major AI lab is grappling with the fact that their existing benchmarks saturate within months of a new model release. ARC-AGI fills a legitimate gap: it tests the kind of fluid intelligence and generalization that current LLMs struggle with, which makes it the right benchmark at the right moment. If this were a for-profit company building AI evaluation infrastructure -- a "Moody's of AI" charging enterprise customers for model certification, or selling evaluation-as-a-service to labs and regulators -- I'd be deeply interested. The regulatory trajectory in the EU and increasingly in the US suggests mandatory AI evaluation is coming, and the team that owns the trusted standard could build an extraordinary distribution channel. But that company is either Ndea or something that doesn't exist yet. It's not ARC Prize Foundation.

The founder quality is undeniable and would be exceptional for almost any venture. François Chollet created Keras -- 63.9K GitHub stars, the most widely used deep learning framework for years -- and is one of the most cited researchers in AI with 71K+ citations. His thinking about intelligence measurement is genuinely original and influential in the field. Mike Knoop built Zapier to approximately $5 billion in valuation and $310 million in annual revenue, profitable since 2014 -- that's an extraordinary operating outcome, especially as a remote-first company before that was fashionable. Greg Kamradt created the Needle In A Haystack benchmark that became a standard evaluation tool across the industry. These are builders who have shipped consequential things. If Chollet and Knoop came to me with a for-profit AI evaluation infrastructure company, I'd take the meeting that day.

But the bull case requires stacking assumptions. For this to generate returns, you'd need: (1) the nonprofit to either convert to or spin off a commercial entity, (2) that entity to build a paid product layer on top of the benchmark's credibility, (3) the dual-entity governance with Ndea to resolve cleanly rather than creating IP and resource allocation conflicts, and (4) the commercial entity to compete with well-funded evaluation startups that are already emerging. That's four sequential dependencies -- it fails my single-miracle test by a wide margin. The benchmark itself is arguably already built and recognized. The miracle isn't technical. The miracle is commercial, and the nonprofit structure actively works against it. Ndea's existence as a separate for-profit entity compounds the problem -- it means the founders' commercial ambitions already have a different vehicle. ARC Prize Foundation isn't the thing that captures the value from this structural moment. It's the thing that creates the value that others -- including Ndea -- capture.

I have deep respect for what Chollet and Knoop are building for the AI research community. The timing within the AI evaluation cycle is genuinely strong -- we're at an inflection where benchmark quality determines whether the field can measure real progress versus synthetic improvements. But when I'm writing a check from my own fund, I'm looking for software-driven businesses with distribution moats, not research nonprofits with donation-based funding models. This is a "software-aware" organization in the sense I've written about -- AI evaluation is the tool, but the organizational model is a traditional research foundation, not a technology business that compounds. A smart toaster analogy doesn't quite apply, but the structural point holds: the entity's value doesn't compound through a commercial flywheel.
