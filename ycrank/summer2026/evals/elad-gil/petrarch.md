# Petrarch -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Petrarch is the inverse of the profile I write checks for: a real market catalyst (AI-lab spend on specialized training data, with **Scale AI** at $1.5B ARR growing 97% YoY, per Contrary Research) paired with the weakest possible execution signal (pre-launch "Coming 2026" landing page, two Harvard undergrads, one CTO who is a "college sophomore" per LinkedIn). Samuel Hahn is studying Economics & History, Sudhish Swain is a sophomore, and Ian Lee's shipping record is real estate construction at CBRE/DPR -- not data brokerage, not AI-lab commercial. That signature -- thematically adjacent to a hot market, structurally unqualified to execute the specific miracle the market requires -- is a shape I've seen fail more times than I can list.

On the catalyst dimension, the closest portfolio analog is **Decagon** (2023), where the LLM step-function opened a post-support-agent workflow layer that we funded because the *category catalyst was mechanically linked to the company's wedge*. Petrarch is trying to arbitrage two catalysts at once: buy-side (AI-lab training-data demand, real) and sell-side (record startup wind-downs releasing orphaned datasets, plausible but episodic). The load-bearing analogy breaks on the sell-side: Decagon's supply of "customer conversations" is infinite and recurring; Petrarch's supply of "distressed proprietary datasets" is one-shot, adversely selected, and legally encumbered. The dossier's own risk section flags "supply concentration and repeatability" -- when a pre-launch company's dossier concedes the supply is episodic and adversely selected, the catalyst is buy-side only, and buy-side-only catalysts are why **Scale AI** exists as a service layer rather than as a brokerage.

**Harvey** (2023) is the reference point on the revenue-velocity axis, and Petrarch is at the opposite pole of that ladder in the most literal way possible: Harvey ran "$3B → $5B → $8B in months" (TechCrunch, Nov 2025) on real ARR compounding, while Petrarch's public traction is a Business Operations Intern posting at $25/hr and a LinkedIn company page whose follower count is not retrievable. I said on Tim Ferriss #863 that "if you're an AI company and you're not seeing explosive growth quickly, something's fundamentally broken" -- and while pre-launch is not disqualifying at seed, the *absence of a single named counterparty* on either side of the marketplace (no signed AI-lab buyer, no exclusive dataset-acquisition LOI) is Petrarch telling me the two-sided cold-start hasn't been solved even at the level of two logos and a handshake.

The founder-shipping dimension is where I'd normally reach for the **Perplexity** (2022) template -- Aravind Srinivas pinged me on LinkedIn and showed up a week later with the thing we discussed built. Petrarch's founder set has no such artifact: Sudhish Swain's ISEF 2023 grand award for computational modeling of low-dose chemotherapy is a genuine research achievement but it is not a shipped product with users, and Samuel Hahn and Ian Lee have zero disclosed shipping history in software or data. Ian Lee built Google/Apple/UCSF campuses at DPR, which is real operational muscle for physical projects, and I don't dismiss that -- it may translate to sourcing conversations with bankruptcy trustees. But over 18 months, the compounding question is whether these three ship a licensing-compliant marketplace stack faster than the legal complexity compounds against them, and nothing in the dossier suggests the shipping rhythm exists.

On index position, the counterfactual is clean: for Petrarch to score in the range I gave **Stripe** (2010) on this axis, every AI lab would need to route through Petrarch's catalog the way every internet business routes through Stripe's API. That would require Petrarch to become the default provenance-cleared dataset registry -- and *that* would require exclusive supply at scale, which the dossier gives no evidence of. The mid-case is Petrarch becomes a specialty broker in a few verticals (healthcare imaging from failed telemedicine cos, say), which is a real business but not an index. Right now Petrarch is closer to **Datarade**'s $2.1M ARR bootstrapped self-serve model than to any infrastructure-index outcome.

The single-miracle test is where I usually stop. Petrarch requires four independent miracles to compound: (1) sourcing distressed datasets with clean chain-of-title from bankruptcy trustees, (2) anonymizing under GDPR/CCPA to a standard AI labs will accept, (3) matching adverse-selected supply to buyer specifications, (4) beating synthetic-data alternatives (**Gretel**, $50M Series B) and in-house labeling. That is the exact "compounding small odds" pattern NFX and I warned against -- Color Genomics worked because I *isolated* the miracle to a workflow layer on commodity sequencing; Petrarch has not isolated anything. The Known Exception I'd normally check -- Color as a bio play I made because I operated it as workflow-on-hard-thing -- doesn't rescue Petrarch, because Color had Othman Laraki with prior Twitter/Google shipping, and Petrarch has undergrads.

My 36-month forecast: Petrarch is either dead or acqui-hired for under $5M by a data-labeling incumbent (**Snorkel**, **Scale**, or **Defined.ai**) that wants the sourcing rolodex, with the two undergraduate co-founders returning to Harvard and Ian Lee going back to real estate or a strategy role. The mechanism is provenance liability: the first dataset Petrarch resells that contains improperly-transferred end-user data triggers a takedown, a lab customer churns, and the two-sided marketplace collapses before the take-rate covers the legal spend.

The strongest bear-case-against-my-forecast objection is the wind-down supply argument: 2024-2025 saw record startup shutdowns, and if Ian Lee's operational muscle from CBRE/DPR translates to structured bankruptcy-trustee sourcing, Petrarch could lock exclusive rights to 5-10 high-quality datasets before competitors notice the vertical exists -- a **Coinbase** (2013) style "basket of a category peers ignored" outcome. It's a real argument and I want to steel-man it. It dies on the legal miracle: bankruptcy trustees sell assets on an as-is basis, and end-user data transfer restrictions in the source companies' original ToS almost never allow onward AI-training licensing without notice-and-consent workflows that no wind-down has the operational capacity to run. Even **Scale AI**, with billions in funding, does not touch third-party dataset resale -- that's not oversight, that's the liability tail being longer than the take-rate. My verdict flips if Petrarch shows me, within 6 months, three signed exclusive dataset-acquisition agreements with clean chain-of-title opinions from a top-tier law firm on the cap table. Absent that, this is a Pass.
