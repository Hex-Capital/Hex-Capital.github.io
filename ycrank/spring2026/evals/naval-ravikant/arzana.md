# Arzana -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I look for is a technologist on the founding team. It's the technology business. If the core founding team doesn't include someone who built the thing, I have a structural problem with the investment. Both co-founders here are non-technical — Alexander studied Econ + CS at Stanford but has no engineering track record, Kools took "one coding class" and holds an MS in Management Science & Engineering. The lead founding engineer, Joe Politi, is not a co-founder. This means the two people with equity control, strategic direction, and customer relationships cannot independently evaluate whether their own product is well-architected. When I invested in Stack Overflow, it was built by developers for developers. When I backed Replit, Amjad Masad had been building coding tools for years. Arzana is asking me to fund a technology company where the technology judgment is delegated to an employee, not embedded in the founding DNA. That's not a timing issue you solve with the next hire — it's a structural gap that compounds.

The specific knowledge question is the one that matters most to me, and here I don't see it. Alexander is 21, from Okoboji, Iowa — describes himself as a "manufacturing nerd." Kools is 24, from Neenah, Wisconsin, a Fox Valley manufacturing hub. Growing up near factories gives you cultural proximity, not non-transferable expertise in how manufacturing front offices actually operate. Neither founder has worked in manufacturing operations, managed an ERP system, processed RFQs, or sat in the chair of the person they're building for. Compare that to any of my best investments — the founder had spent years inside the problem, developing insights that couldn't be acquired through six months of customer discovery. What do these founders know about manufacturing front-office workflows that a smart Stanford grad couldn't learn in a summer? The dossier doesn't answer that question, and the absence is loud.

The leverage architecture here is automation, not leverage creation. There's an important distinction. Reducing a 30-person office to 5 people is labor substitution — valuable, but it scales linearly with customer acquisition, especially when each deployment requires 1-3 months of implementation across heterogeneous ERP environments. Supporting 10+ ERP systems and 6+ CRMs at a team of four isn't a moat — it's a maintenance liability. Each ERP vendor update, each edge case in document parsing, each customer-specific workflow configuration demands engineering time that doesn't compound. Compare this to Notion, where every template created by a user makes the platform more valuable for every other user. Or Replit, where the platform gets better as more people code on it. Arzana's integrations get more fragile as they multiply. The $2,500-$7,000/month pricing with multi-month onboarding has the economic profile of a services business with a software wrapper.

The timing isn't contrarian — it's peak consensus. "AI for manufacturing" is a category where $170M (Conexiom), $111M (Rossum), and $51M (Paperless Parts) have already been deployed by serious investors. Endeavor AI raised $7M from Craft Ventures for nearly the identical thesis. When 98% of manufacturers say they're "exploring AI," the contrarian opportunity has already been priced. Nobody smart is dismissing this idea — everyone agrees manufacturing is ripe for automation. The real contrarian bet would be something most people think is wrong about this space, and the dossier reveals no such thesis from the founders.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. If those customer logos are real — Schneider National, Zeiss, Koike — then two young founders with no industry connections somehow persuaded Fortune 500 companies to adopt their product. That would be extraordinary sales talent, which is itself a form of specific knowledge. The breadth play could work if they're building the connective tissue between ERPs and AI agents that becomes the default office layer for mid-market manufacturers — the ERP vendors won't build cross-platform compatibility because it undermines their lock-in incentive. And if LLMs genuinely generalize across document formats in a way that prior template-based approaches couldn't, Arzana could accumulate workflow training data that improves agent accuracy with each deployment. But the dossier itself flags that these logos lack independent verification — "no independent press coverage, case studies with quantified outcomes, or third-party validation" — and the sole testimonial is from Tier One Technologies, not from Schneider National or Zeiss. Until I can verify those relationships are real, paying engagements rather than free pilots or logo permissions, the bull case rests on unconfirmed evidence.

What I see is two energetic, committed young founders — Kools paying himself $10,000 a year signals genuine conviction, not founder tourism — building a consensus automation product in a crowded market without the technical depth or domain-specific knowledge that my framework requires. The integrity and long-term orientation signals are positive. The determination is real. But determination without specific knowledge and technical founding capability puts you in an execution race against better-funded, more specialized competitors, and execution races are not where asymmetric returns come from.
