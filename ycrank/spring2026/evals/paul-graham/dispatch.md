# Dispatch -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that caught my eye immediately is the heat shield built in an apartment. When I hear that two engineers with real spacecraft experience built a full-scale reentry heat shield -- the single hardest component in atmospheric return -- for what they claim is 100x less than competitors, in a living space, that's the kind of story that makes me lean forward. It reminds me of what we saw with Stripe's first integration: the Collisons didn't wait for permission or funding to start solving the hard problem. They just started solving it. Case came from the Lunar Gateway thermal group at Aerospace Corporation and the CTO's office at Astranis. Mello was leading avionics at the same company. These aren't two MBA students who googled "space market size" -- they were literally building spacecraft for a living and presumably saw the return-from-orbit bottleneck firsthand.

But here's where my framework gets complicated. Schlep blindness is my most reliable signal for spotting opportunities others miss. The idea is that certain problems are so painful and tedious that your unconscious mind literally won't let you see them. Payments before Stripe. Trust between strangers before Airbnb. Reentry vehicles for in-space manufacturing is a genuine schlep -- dealing with thermal protection at Mach 20+, guidance systems, regulatory approvals, recovery operations. The schlep is real. The problem is: this particular schlep is not blind. Seven funded competitors are already working on it. Varda has $329M and five successful reentry missions. Inversion has $125M and a DoD contract. Reditus Space -- another YC company, one batch earlier -- is building essentially the same thing. When I count seven competitors with collective funding exceeding half a billion dollars, I'm not looking at a problem everyone unconsciously avoids. I'm looking at a problem everyone has noticed simultaneously. That's the opposite of schlep blindness. That's a hot category.

The picks-and-shovels positioning is the smartest part of Dispatch's approach. Instead of vertically integrating like Varda -- owning the manufacturing payload, the capsule, and the bus -- Dispatch is building the return logistics layer to serve multiple payload operators. I tend to favor infrastructure plays that serve other builders. Stripe didn't process payments for its own e-commerce store; it processed payments for everyone else's. Replit doesn't build apps; it gives developers the tools to build them. If in-space manufacturing takes off and dozens of companies need a way to get products back to Earth, the logistics provider that's cheapest and most reliable could become the Stripe of orbital return. That's the bull case, and it's a good one. If their 100x cost advantage on heat shield fabrication scales to full vehicle production, they become the default choice for every company that doesn't want to build its own reentry capability. And the Astranis background means both founders actually understand the disciplines required -- thermal protection on Case's side, avionics and embedded systems on Mello's side.

The strongest argument for investing is the combination of domain-native founders and demonstrated scrappiness. These two built a full-scale heat shield and validated it in the Mojave with rocket exhaust simulating reentry conditions. Mello's GitHub shows C++ embedded systems work and real-time face detection -- he's a builder, not a manager. A two-person team that has already completed hardware validation for a reentry vehicle represents remarkable output per capita. If the 100x cost claim holds and translates into 10x cheaper missions, the capital intensity problem partially solves itself -- they might reach first flight for a fraction of what competitors spend. That's the version of this story where Dispatch wins.

The problem is that I'm not sure any of those "ifs" resolve favorably. The 100x cost claim is unverified by anyone outside the company. A ground test with rocket exhaust, however clever, is not an orbital reentry -- Inversion Space's first actual reentry attempt failed due to propulsion issues, and they had $125M behind them. The gap between Mojave and Mach 20 from orbit is not a gap you bridge with resourcefulness alone. It requires $50-100M+ in capital, which means Dispatch needs to raise at least 100x their current funding before they can even attempt what they're promising. There are no customers, no LOIs, no revenue signals, no press coverage. The market is validated -- Varda proved it works -- but nothing in this dossier tells me anyone is pulling Dispatch specifically toward them. At pre-seed I don't demand traction, but I do want to see some signal that the world is reaching for this team's particular solution. I don't see it yet.

I like these founders. They remind me of the kind of people we'd fund at YC -- technical, domain-native, willing to build things with their hands rather than slide decks. If this were a less crowded space, or if they had a signed letter of intent from a pharma company that needs downmass, or if the 100x claim had independent validation, I'd be substantially more interested. But a pre-seed company entering a market where the leading competitor has $329M and proven operations, with six other funded players in between, needs to show me something more than a promising ground test and a smart positioning narrative. The founders found this problem organically, and the schlep is real, but the schlep isn't blind anymore. Everyone sees it now.
