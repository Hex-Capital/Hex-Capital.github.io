# Squid -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The most striking fact in this dossier is that National Grid DSO -- one of the most risk-averse institutions on the planet -- launched a public-facing product "powered by Squid" in February 2026. A regulated utility embedding a two-person pre-seed startup's software into customer-facing infrastructure is not a pilot, not a POC, not a "we're exploring options" conversation. That's a production deployment with reputational risk. For a company at this stage, that signal carries more weight than any revenue number they could cite. The mechanism behind it is equally notable: both founders previously co-founded VoltQuant serving these exact customers, then started Squid and brought the relationships with them. Customer trust that survives a company transition is earned, not manufactured.

The specific knowledge here is real but bounded. George Kolokotronis was Head of Technology at Octopus Energy -- not observing the grid planning problem from the outside, but responsible for the technical systems that interface with it. Cambridge engineering MPhil, AWS AI/Data experience, then building grid intelligence software across two startups. Conor Jones operated inside National Grid and Octopus before founding. They understand why planning teams drown in spreadsheets and conflicting model versions because they sat in those rooms. This is knowledge that takes years to accumulate -- you can't hire a smart generalist and have them replicate the understanding of how a distribution network operator actually makes capacity decisions, which regulatory frameworks constrain the workflow, and where the data breaks down between GIS exports and planning models. But I'd distinguish this from the kind of specific knowledge I look for in my best investments. Evan Williams at Twitter had a theory about human communication that predicted how people would want to express themselves. The Squid founders have deep operational knowledge of a broken workflow. One generates new categories; the other generates better tools for existing categories.

The leverage question is where this company falls short of my framework. Squid is enterprise SaaS sold to distribution network operators through direct sales. Each customer is a regulated utility with 6-18 month procurement cycles, compliance requirements, and customization needs. A two-person team with SOC 2 Type II and ISO 27001 certifications has done extraordinary work to clear the compliance barriers -- that's months of operational effort that signals genuine seriousness. But the distribution model is fundamentally labor-intensive. This isn't Replit, where millions of people self-serve into code leverage. It's not Alchemy, where every blockchain developer routes through your API. It's closer to a vertical SaaS company where each new utility deployment requires relationship-building, onboarding, and integration work that scales linearly with headcount. The versioned network model has some platform characteristics -- once a utility builds its single source of truth in Squid, switching costs are real -- but the path to getting them there is one-at-a-time enterprise sales.

The bull case I have to engage honestly: the energy transition is a physical infrastructure transformation that will touch every grid on every continent. Every distribution network operator needs to replan its grid for EVs, heat pumps, and distributed generation. If Squid becomes the default planning layer -- the way Figma became the default design layer -- the compound value is enormous. The browser-native, collaborative approach could create a wedge that desktop-bound incumbents like GE Vernova's PlanOS can't match without cannibalizing their own enterprise bundles. The structural unbundling argument is sound: incumbents sell planning tools as part of integrated hardware-and-software suites, meaning a standalone lightweight planning layer can be adopted without a full platform swap. And the founders have already demonstrated the hardest thing in this market -- getting a production deployment at a major utility. If they can replicate this pattern across five more DNOs in the next eighteen months, the company starts to look like infrastructure for grid modernization rather than a tool for grid planners.

But the timing is consensus, not contrarian. Grid modernization software is a recognized investment category with well-funded competitors: Envelio (E.ON-owned, serving Westnetz, Enel, Iberdrola), Camus Energy ($25M, 500% ARR growth), Plexigrid ($11.6M). Nobody smart is dismissing the need for better grid planning tools -- they're funding it from multiple directions. The browser-native angle is differentiated execution, but it's not a contrarian thesis about the world. When I invested in Uber, ride-sharing was illegal. When I bought Bitcoin, it was considered nerd money. Squid is building a better tool in a category everyone already agrees needs better tools. That's fine -- it can still be a good company -- but the pricing on consensus bets reflects optimism, and the asymmetric upside I hunt for comes from being right when others are wrong, not from being right when everyone else is also right.

I respect the founders' operational maturity and domain depth. The SOC 2 Type II certification alone, for a two-person team, tells me these are people who think in terms of decades, not months. The carried customer relationships from VoltQuant demonstrate integrity and compounding trust. But this is a vertical enterprise SaaS company in a competitive, consensus category with linear distribution mechanics -- it doesn't create a new form of leverage, it doesn't occupy a contrarian position, and the technology, while well-executed, could be replicated by a well-funded team with the right domain hires. It's a solid company for the right investor. It's not my kind of bet.
