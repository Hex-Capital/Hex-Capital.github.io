# Burt -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The most striking thing about Burt is not what the founders are building -- it's that HappyRobot, a YC S23 company doing essentially the same thing, has already raised $62 million, hit eight-figure revenue, and is valued at approximately $500 million. And there are two *other* YC W26 companies -- Lanesurf and Lighthouz AI -- targeting overlapping segments of the same freight brokerage customer base. When I see three companies in a single YC batch chasing the same buyer, plus a dominant incumbent three cohorts ahead with fifty times the capital, I'm not looking at a non-consensus opportunity. I'm looking at a consensus stampede. This is the opposite of my investment filter. The "insane idea" premium has been completely arbitraged away -- everyone sees that AI agents can automate freight brokerage back-office work. The only question is execution, and execution races against $62M-funded competitors with enterprise logos are exactly the kind of bet I don't want to make with my own money.

The founders are competent engineers -- UC Berkeley EECS, UC Irvine CS, both worked at Replo building coding agents and LLM tooling. That's the "sane" half. But the sane-person-insane-idea pairing requires BOTH components, and the idea here is completely legible to the market. Bobby Zhong's career pattern also gives me pause: Educado (seven months), Moonwish Studios (web development agency), Pirros (about a year), and now Burt, which itself pivoted from a general model fine-tuning platform to logistics AI teammates. That sequence reads more like searching for traction than pursuing a deep conviction. Neither founder has any documented experience in freight, logistics, or supply chain operations. In a business where domain credibility matters for sales -- freight brokers are relationship-driven, trust-intensive buyers who are skeptical of tech outsiders -- this is a real liability, not just a gap on a résumé.

My "cherry on top" test kills this one cleanly. Would Burt be a good business if AI didn't exist? No. There is no business without AI. The entire value proposition IS the AI layer -- LLM-powered agents that chase PODs, track shipments, validate invoices. Strip the AI away and nothing remains. This is exactly the category I've been publicly arguing against since 2024. When I backed Craftwork, the moat was the painting crews, the scheduling operations, the physical labor that software enhances but cannot replace. When I look at Burt, I see a pure software automation play with no physical-world anchor, no proprietary data asset, no protocol position. The dossier itself reports "No defensibility signals found in public sources" and explicitly states "The barrier to entry is low for well-resourced teams with logistics domain knowledge." That's not my inference -- that's the factual assessment.

The strongest bull case for Burt would be this: HappyRobot is chasing DHL, Werner, and Schneider -- the enterprise whales. The U.S. freight brokerage market has thousands of small-to-midsize players who need something lighter, more configurable, more affordable. If Burt could own the mid-market with a "follow your specific processes" approach and accumulate enough workflow data across hundreds of small brokerages, that data corpus could become a compounding moat. The TMS integration layer could create real switching costs. And the timing is genuinely good -- cyclical freight downturn is pressuring margins, LLMs just crossed the capability threshold for parsing messy logistics communications, and analysts project 15%+ of spot-market truckloads will be automatically tendered by 2026. For this to work, though, Burt would need to acquire customers faster than HappyRobot moves down-market, faster than Lanesurf and Lighthouz expand their surface area, and faster than incumbent TMS platforms bolt on AI features. With a two-person team, no logistics domain expertise, and no visible traction, the execution timeline is brutally compressed against well-capitalized competition.

The business model is almost certainly per-seat or per-task SaaS pricing -- the dossier infers this and the vertical software pattern confirms it. A subscription pricing model for software access in a category where the underlying AI capabilities are commoditizing quarterly is exactly the trap I keep warning about. Each new model release from OpenAI, Anthropic, or Google makes the core technology more accessible to every competitor and every incumbent simultaneously. Burt creates no new primitive that other businesses build on. It's an application layer sitting on top of someone else's LLMs and someone else's TMS platforms -- doubly dependent on infrastructure it doesn't control.

Pass. This is a sane person with a sane idea in a crowded market, building a pure AI software play with no structural moat, no domain expertise, no traction, and a dominant competitor fifty times better funded. Everything about this deal pattern is consensus, and consensus is where my money goes to generate mediocre returns.
