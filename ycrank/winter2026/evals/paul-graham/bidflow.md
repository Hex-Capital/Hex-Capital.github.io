# Bidflow -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I look at is how the founders found the problem. With Bidflow, the answer is transparent: they didn't find it through lived experience. Six months ago these same two founders were building QA agents for bug testing under the name Ghostship. That didn't work, so they went looking for a new market. Their email is still founders@tryghostship.dev. Electrical estimating isn't something that grew out of their own lives — it's something they selected. That's the pattern I described in "How to Get Startup Ideas" as the canonical failure mode: sitting down to think of startup ideas instead of noticing problems you already have. The problem they landed on is real. Counting electrical symbols across fifty pages of circuit diagrams while juggling legacy Accubid installations is genuinely painful work. But they arrived at it through a pivot search, not through years of frustration with the workflow. That matters because the deep knowledge of how electrical contractors actually think, bid, and lose money is what separates a tool that gets adopted from one that demos well.

What makes this evaluation harder than it should be is that these two are clearly exceptional hackers. Jesse Choe is USACO Platinum — top one percent of competitive programmers in the country — and was externing at Jane Street at eighteen. Gautham Ramachandran has three ICML preprints on reinforcement learning and bootstrapped something to $120K revenue at sixteen. They met in middle school coding class and have been building together for years, including the all-nighter Gautham pulled fixing bugs during a CerealCodes competition. That co-founder relationship is exactly what I look for — a friendship forged through shared work long before any company existed. And they got into YC twice, which means partners who know them well decided to back them again after a pivot. These are not ordinary pre-seed founders.

The problem with Bidflow isn't the founders' ability. It's the competitive landscape relative to their domain depth. Drawer AI is doing the identical thing — AI-powered electrical takeoff and estimating — with $5M in funding and founders who have twenty-five years of construction experience. In construction, domain knowledge isn't a nice-to-have. Electrical estimating involves NECA labor units, local material pricing, code compliance, and the relationship dynamics of how subcontractors actually win bids. Jesse and Gautham can build an AI model that counts symbols on drawings. Getting from 83.5% accuracy to the 95%+ that contractors need to trust the output requires the kind of domain-specific training data and feedback loops that come from deep industry relationships. Drawer AI's founders have those relationships. Bidflow's founders are building them from scratch while a better-funded competitor with a multi-year head start runs the same race.

The strongest bull case here is that technical ability is the actual bottleneck, not domain knowledge. Maybe the construction industry veterans at Drawer AI can't build AI models as well as two USACO Platinum programmers with ML research backgrounds. Maybe the path to 95%+ accuracy is more about model architecture and training methodology than about knowing which junction box goes where. And maybe these founders' speed of iteration — they built a working product with named customers in six months in a domain they'd never touched — is itself proof that domain expertise can be acquired faster than technical excellence. The Bass Electric testimonial is real: a contractor named Jeff Yee Jr. reporting a 5x reduction in estimate time. That's not nothing. If they can stack up more of those testimonials and improve accuracy quickly, the domain gap could close. Young technical founders sometimes win precisely because they approach old industries without the assumptions that constrain insiders.

But I keep coming back to the origin. When I funded Stripe, the Collisons had been building web applications and hitting the payment processing wall themselves, repeatedly, for years. When I funded Dropbox, Drew Houston had been forgetting his USB drive. The organic connection between founder and problem creates a kind of determination that manufactured discovery doesn't. These founders are determined — the pivot itself shows that. But they're determined to build a successful company, not determined to fix electrical estimating specifically. If Bidflow doesn't work, they'll pivot again. That's not wrong, but it's a different animal than a founder who's been living inside a problem and can't rest until it's solved. In a market where a domain-expert competitor already has capital and a head start, that difference in motivation could be decisive.
