# Compresr -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's a company building a better compression algorithm for a cost that's evaporating. LLM inference prices are deflating at roughly 10x per year. DeepSeek entered the market at 90% below incumbent pricing. Google already offers million-token context windows. Compresr's core value proposition -- save money on tokens -- is a race against a price curve that's heading toward zero with or without them. This reminds me of KiOR, but inverted: at KiOR, the technology worked but the economics didn't because oil prices fell instead of rising to $150 as I'd assumed. Compresr faces the same structural vulnerability -- their entire consequence-of-success calculation rests on token costs remaining high enough to justify a compression layer. And every data point says those costs are collapsing.

Let me ask the question I always ask first: if Compresr succeeds completely, what changes? Developers pay somewhat less for LLM inference. AI agents run marginally cheaper. The FinanceBench result shows a 2.2 percentage point accuracy improvement -- from 72.3% to 74.5% -- on SEC filing analysis. That's real engineering but it's not transformational. Nobody's industry gets disrupted. No physical system changes. No trillion-dollar market becomes obsolete. The consequence of maximum success is a modest infrastructure business in a crowded developer tools market. This is precisely the kind of company where the team has reduced every risk to the point where the outcome, even if everything works, doesn't justify the career investment. I would tell these founders: you are clearly smart enough to work on something that matters more.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. If agentic workloads grow 100x -- which the a16z data on inference workloads suggests is plausible -- then even at 10x lower per-token prices, total token spend balloons, and a compression layer captures real value. The LLM providers' per-token revenue model creates a genuine structural disincentive to cannibalize their own pricing by offering native compression. And the accuracy improvement story -- compression as a quality layer, not just a cost layer -- could become the wedge if it generalizes beyond one benchmark. If agents become the dominant compute workload and compression becomes the CDN equivalent for AI infrastructure, Compresr would be well-positioned with their open-source gateway and managed API. But I don't find this compelling. CDNs solved a physical bandwidth constraint that didn't deflate on its own. Token costs are computational and already in free fall. Google's ADK already documents context compaction. The "structural disincentive" argument assumes LLM providers won't figure out that enabling more agent usage at lower per-call cost generates more total revenue -- which is exactly what they're figuring out right now.

The founding team is technically strong -- four EPFL graduates who co-authored a peer-reviewed paper benchmarking 25 models on compression. Ivan Zakazov's PhD research is directly in LLM context compression. That's real domain depth. But it's also the pattern I'm most skeptical of: researchers who found a technical optimization in their research domain and productized it. There's nothing contrarian here. Everyone in the LLM infrastructure space knows context management is a challenge. The team is homogeneous -- all from the same institution, three from the same program, two from the same lab -- with no visible go-to-market capability, no enterprise sales experience, and no evidence of the kind of outsider thinking that produces category-defining companies. Microsoft Research has a free, open-source alternative in LLMLingua that's already integrated into LlamaIndex and Azure. The competitive moat is thin.

The timing trajectory is what kills this for me. In my best investments -- OpenAI in 2019, cleantech in 2004, food tech in 2010 -- I was positioned ahead of an accelerating curve. Compresr is positioned against a decelerating one. The cost problem they solve is getting smaller every quarter. The context window constraint they address is being solved by the platforms themselves expanding those windows. When I evaluate rate of change, I ask: will the conditions that make this company necessary be more true or less true in two years? For Compresr, every indicator points to less true. That's not where I want to place a bet.
