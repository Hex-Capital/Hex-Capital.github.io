# Inth -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Guillermo Rauch calling c15t "@shadcn for Consent Management" and listing Vercel as a customer is the most informative signal in Inth's dossier -- not because CEO endorsements are inherently meaningful, but because of what it reveals about layer positioning. Shadcn succeeded by converting a UI component category from opaque, vendor-locked packages into composable, developer-owned primitives. Inth is attempting the exact same architectural move against OneTrust's $500M revenue business: take consent management from external script injection sold to compliance officers and rebuild it as an embeddable SDK sold to engineering teams. I've seen this pattern produce outsized returns before. Gusto did it to ADP in payroll. Checkr did it to legacy background-check vendors. Infisical -- which is both a portfolio company of mine and a listed Inth customer -- did it to secrets management. The shared mechanism is always the same: boring, compliance-heavy category where the incumbents built for the wrong buyer persona, and a developer-first challenger rebuilds the infrastructure layer from scratch. Inth's 1.8M npm downloads and 388.3K monthly downloads in April 2026 suggest the developer community is already voting with its package.json files.

The market timing question for Inth is more nuanced than it appears on the surface. Privacy regulation isn't new -- GDPR was 2018, CCPA was 2020. Most investors hear "consent management" and think the market is mature with OneTrust dominating at $500M revenue. But the structural shift Inth is exploiting isn't regulatory expansion alone -- it's the convergence of three forces: Next.js and React becoming the dominant web frameworks (Next.js at 6M+ weekly npm downloads), Core Web Vitals becoming a Google SEO ranking signal that makes CMP performance a business metric, and the DMA enforcement in March 2024 expanding consent requirements to gatekeeper platforms. OneTrust's architecture -- 514ms of overhead from external scripts, 50-100KB bundle bloat -- was tolerable when compliance was a checkbox exercise. When CMP performance directly impacts search rankings and page experience, the entire architecture needs to be rebuilt. That's a legitimate structural window, but I want to be precise: this is a "boring infrastructure rebuilt for the modern stack" opportunity, not a "market that was dead and suddenly cracked open" opportunity like crypto in 2012 or legal AI before GPT-4. The nonobviousness is moderate -- most VCs will pass because "consent management" sounds boring, which is exactly why the competitive landscape for developer-first entrants is wide open.

Inth's infrastructure-layer positioning is where my conviction concentrates. Christopher Burns built c15t as a TypeScript-native consent engine with framework-specific SDKs for React, Next.js, Svelte, and Node.js. Once a development team integrates these SDKs into their application code, migrating to an alternative means rearchitecting consent handling across their entire stack -- that's compounding switching costs, the same dynamic that made Stripe's payments API so sticky. The 411 releases and contributors from Expo, Adobe, Vercel, and Google signal that c15t is becoming embedded in the developer infrastructure ecosystem. The BigCommerce Catalyst integration extends reach beyond the modern framework community into e-commerce. The three operational modes (hosted, offline, custom) map to the Hashicorp/Better Auth playbook: open-source core creates adoption, hosted tier captures revenue. Inth is building a platform other businesses integrate into, not an application sitting on someone else's infrastructure. That's the layer I want to own.

The single-miracle test is favorable for Inth. The technology already works -- 0.6KB total size, 30ms FCP versus 445-709ms for incumbents -- and the performance gap is architectural, not algorithmic, meaning incumbents can't close it with incremental optimization. Regulatory tailwinds are exogenous: India's DPDP Act, Canada's CPPA, and ongoing EU enforcement keep expanding the addressable market without Inth needing to lobby for them. The one miracle Inth needs is converting open-source adoption into paid revenue at scale. No pricing page exists as of April 2026, and there's zero public revenue data. That's the critical uncertainty. But this is a well-understood conversion challenge with proven playbooks -- Hashicorp, GitLab, Sentry all navigated it. The consent.io hosted dashboard exists, suggesting the monetization infrastructure is being built. One miracle, and it's a known category of miracle.

Christopher Burns is a second-time founder who built Everfund as CTO, processing payments across 33+ countries with multi-jurisdiction regulatory handling -- directly relevant domain experience for building a consent platform that must support GDPR, CCPA, IAB TCF 2.3, UK GDPR, Quebec Law 25, and DPDP. His 10 years of TypeScript experience and RedwoodJS contributions demonstrate the technical credibility needed for a developer tools company. But Everfund raised $2.15M and scaled to 7 people -- that's not the hypergrowth operator profile I look for. The YC page lists only Burns despite a team size of 3, and the other two team members are invisible in public sources. I don't need a co-founder structure to invest, but at a 3-person company the identities and capabilities of the other two people matter significantly. The Rauch relationship and Vercel ecosystem integration suggest Burns has the distribution instincts and network to grow Inth, even if he hasn't yet demonstrated the organizational scaling skills that the High Growth Handbook focuses on.

The bear case deserves direct engagement: the consent management market's developer-focused segment may be too small for venture returns, OneTrust could build an SDK offering if threatened, and open-source monetization is genuinely hard -- many developer tools companies with strong GitHub traction have failed to convert free users into paying customers. Inth's self-created CookieBench benchmark, while open-source, introduces a credibility question for enterprise buyers comparing CMP performance. And regulatory complexity at 3 people is a genuine resource constraint -- every new jurisdiction adds linear maintenance cost. Against this, the structural argument holds: Inth is building the infrastructure layer, not the application; regulatory complexity is a moat that deters replication more than it costs to maintain; and the open-source adoption curve (1.8M downloads, named customers like Vercel and Expo) is creating the kind of bottom-up distribution that enterprise sales teams can later harvest. If Inth publishes pricing and shows $50K+ in ARR within the next two quarters, that converts my moderate conviction to high. If npm download growth stalls or the Vercel ecosystem moves to build consent tooling natively, I'd downgrade to Pass.

Inth earns an Invest at the lower end of the range, driven primarily by infrastructure-layer positioning that maps directly to my highest-conviction pattern: boring, compliance-heavy category rebuilt as developer infrastructure, with regulatory complexity serving as a moat rather than just a cost. The market timing is real but only moderately nonobvious -- it's not a generational structural shift, but the "wrong buyer persona" dynamic creates a genuine opening that consensus undervalues. The absence of any revenue data or pricing is the binding constraint on a higher score; everything else in the framework aligns.
