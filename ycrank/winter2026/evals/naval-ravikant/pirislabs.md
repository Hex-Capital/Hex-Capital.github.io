# Piris Labs -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Ali Khalatpour built the first room-temperature terahertz semiconductor laser. He designed the optical engine for NASA's GUSTO mission. He spent a decade at MIT, Harvard, and Stanford working at the frontier of photonic physics — the science of moving information with light instead of electrons. This isn't domain expertise. This is the kind of specific knowledge that takes a lifetime to accumulate and cannot be replicated by hiring smart generalists. When I invested in Extropic, I was betting that Guillaume Verdon's understanding of thermodynamic computing — knowledge that exists in maybe fifty human brains — would compound into a technology moat no amount of capital could replicate. Khalatpour represents the same class of founder: someone whose knowledge isn't transferable because it was built through years of working at the physical limits of what's possible with light. Pair that with Keyvan Moghadam's experience building 0-to-1 AI infrastructure at Meta and X, and you have a founding team where the photonic physicist knows how to move data with light and the systems engineer knows how to serve inference at scale. That combination doesn't exist in a hiring pipeline.

The leverage architecture here is worth examining carefully. Most photonic interconnect companies — Lightmatter, Ayar Labs, the now-acquired Celestial AI — sell components. They're picks-and-shovels plays for the data center supply chain. Piris Labs is doing something structurally different: offering inference-as-a-service with proprietary photonic hardware underneath. This is the distinction between selling GPUs and selling compute. If the hardware works, every improvement flows through to every customer simultaneously — that's genuine leverage. One engineering advance multiplied across the entire customer base. It's also an infrastructure play, which is my revealed preference whether I articulate it or not. Alchemy over any individual DeFi app. Replit over any individual software product. Piris Labs over any individual AI application company that's renting GPU time.

The contrarian positioning is nuanced. Photonic interconnects themselves are no longer contrarian — NVIDIA dropped $4 billion into Lumentum and Coherent, Marvell acquired Celestial AI for $3.25 billion, NVIDIA bought Groq for $20 billion. The market has validated that copper is hitting physical limits. What IS contrarian is the belief that a two-person team with $500K can compete in a space where incumbents have raised $500M to $850M. Most smart investors would call that delusional. But at angel, I'm not betting on Piris Labs outspending Lightmatter. I'm betting on optionality — that world-class specific knowledge, combined with a differentiated full-stack approach, creates a path that doesn't require winning the capital arms race. The acquisition multiples in this space ($3.25B for Celestial AI, $20B for Groq) mean that even a modest success produces asymmetric returns on a small check.

The strongest case against is obvious and I won't pretend otherwise: hardware requires capital, and capital is a moat in semiconductors. Lightmatter's $850M, Ayar Labs' $500M Series E backed by NVIDIA and AMD — these aren't paper threats. They have fabrication relationships, supply chains, and production hardware. Piris Labs has performance claims that haven't been independently validated. The history of photonic startups is littered with brilliant physicists who couldn't cross the valley from lab demonstration to production-grade product. If the full-stack service model requires Piris to manufacture its own photonic hardware at scale, the capital requirements become prohibitive. For this investment to work, one of three things must be true: they demonstrate enough performance advantage to raise serious capital quickly, they find an ODM partnership model that outsources fabrication, or they get acquired early by someone who needs their specific technology. Their active recruitment of chip maker and ODM partners for beta suggests they understand this — the question is whether the technology is far enough along to attract those partners.

What I genuinely like: this is a bet on compute scarcity as the defining constraint of the AI era, which is where my conviction has been moving since 2022. The memory wall is real physics, not a market narrative. Trillion-parameter models cannot be served economically on standard GPU clusters — the interconnect bottleneck is getting worse, not better. The founders aren't building a wrapper or an incremental improvement. They're working at the physics layer where the actual constraint lives. The vertical integration of photonic hardware with inference-optimized software creates the potential for compounding technology — each iteration of the hardware-software co-design makes the system harder to replicate. At angel stage, I'm underwriting the specific knowledge, not the manufacturing plan. If Khalatpour's photonic interconnect design achieves even half the claimed performance improvements, the team will be able to raise real capital in a market that's paying multi-billion dollar premiums for inference infrastructure.

I'd write a small check. The specific knowledge is extraordinary. The leverage architecture is sound if unproven. The capital intensity risk is real but priced into the angel bet — I'm buying optionality on world-class physics knowledge at a time when the market is paying enormous premiums for exactly this type of technology. The same logic that led me to Extropic applies here: when the founder's knowledge is genuinely non-replicable and the physics problem they're solving is the binding constraint of an era, the asymmetry favors a bet.
