# Maquoketa Research -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Maquoketa Research's GitHub organization tells me more than their pitch ever could. Nine public repositories — every single one a fork. PX4-Autopilot, QGroundControl, MAVLink, HackRF, AM32 motor firmware. No original repos. No proprietary code visible to the world. When I backed Mach Industries — a teenager building weapons systems to fight Lockheed Martin — the irreverence was matched by a distinctive technical approach. Maquoketa Research claims vertical integration and autonomy-first architecture, but the public evidence shows assembly of open-source components, not a technology breakthrough. That distinction matters enormously in my framework. A systems integrator competing on execution in a market where Anduril is building a $1 billion factory for tens of thousands of units annually is not the same animal as a company with a proprietary technology wedge that incumbents cannot replicate.

Maquoketa Research operates in a category with genuine consequence magnitude. Ukraine proved the thesis — cheap autonomous drones neutralizing armored vehicles that cost 100x more is exactly the kind of cost asymmetry that reshapes how nations fight wars. The global loitering munition market reaching $13.26 billion by 2030 at 19.9% CAGR reflects a real doctrinal shift, not speculative demand. If Maquoketa Research succeeds, precision strike capability moves from expensive guided munitions at $100K+ per unit down to expendable autonomous systems at a fraction of that cost. Millions of soldiers get capabilities previously reserved for elite units with Tomahawk access. The consequence of the category is real. But the consequence of *this specific company* versus AeroVironment (scaling to 1,200 Switchblades per month, $990M Army contract) or Anduril ($6.9B raised, $30.5B valuation, Arsenal-1 factory coming online July 2026) is where the argument weakens. Maquoketa Research needs to show me what they do that those companies cannot.

Alejandro Hernandez is the most compelling signal in Maquoketa Research's dossier. Berkeley Wireless Research Center, specializing in phased array systems and RF design — this is not a generic software background. In contested electromagnetic environments where GPS is jammed and communications are denied, the open-source autopilot stack fails. If Hernandez has built proprietary EW-resilient navigation and targeting, that could be the non-linear technical wedge that makes Maquoketa Research fundamentally different from the dozens of FPV drone companies that emerged from Ukraine's conflict. But I see no public evidence of this — no papers, no original repos, no demonstrations. The YC page mentions the specialization; the GitHub shows forked HackRF software radio code. The gap between the claim and the visible evidence is where my conviction stalls. Evan Yeager's background — UC Berkeley EECS, SpiderRock, Stylis.AI — is competent but doesn't signal the kind of outsider genius or visible learning velocity I look for. Two of four co-founders (Muchow, Williams) have almost no public footprint.

The bull case for Maquoketa Research is structurally strong, and I want to steelman it properly. The defense primes — Lockheed, Raytheon, Northrop — face genuine business model cannibalization risk. Their revenue models depend on $100K+ precision munitions; aggressively competing in sub-$10K expendable drones cannibalizes their core economics. AeroVironment occupies the space but faces scaling constraints. Anduril is formidable but hasn't locked up the entire market. The U.S. Army's fiscal 2026 budget specifically proposes equipping infantry brigades with loitering munitions at scale. DoD wants multiple domestic suppliers — they learned from the Ukraine ammunition crisis that single-source dependency is a strategic vulnerability. Maquoketa Research's "built entirely in the USA" positioning, Elk Grove Village manufacturing corridor location, and ITAR-compliant supply chain hit exactly the right procurement notes. A 5-person team with YC backing, complementary technical founders spanning EECS, RF, and mechanical engineering, building in a market with urgent government demand and structural incumbent reluctance — this is a legitimate option worth pricing. My Mach Industries bet had similar characteristics at a similar stage.

But here is why Maquoketa Research does not clear my threshold: the non-linearity test fails on available evidence. FPV attack drones are widely commoditized — dozens of manufacturers in Ukraine produce similar platforms. The autonomy software stack is the key differentiator, but Maquoketa Research's public repos are forks of PX4 and ArduPilot-adjacent projects, not proprietary breakthroughs. Anduril and Shield AI are investing billions in autonomy with teams of hundreds of AI engineers. A 2x improvement in drone autonomy assembled from open-source components does not generate the asymmetric return I need — I want to either lose my money or make 50 times my money, and a company competing on integration quality against opponents with thousand-to-one resource advantages is designed to produce moderate outcomes or zero. The path to radical affordability is inherent in the expendable drone category itself, not in anything Maquoketa Research specifically enables. The defense procurement cycle risk — 18-36 months to first contract revenue with pre-seed funding — creates the same cash flow death valley that killed many of my cleantech bets when the technology wasn't differentiated enough to attract bridge capital.

Maquoketa Research could flip me with one verifiable condition: a demonstrated autonomous engagement capability in GPS-denied, EW-contested environments that meaningfully outperforms anything achievable with off-the-shelf PX4 and open-source computer vision. If Hernandez's phased array work produces proprietary navigation and targeting that works where competitors' systems fail, that is a genuine technology wedge — the kind of non-linear advantage that justifies competing against Anduril's billions. Without that evidence, Maquoketa Research is a competent team in a consequential market without a visible technical moat, and I have learned from too many cleantech failures that market tailwinds without technology differentiation produce casualties, not category winners.
