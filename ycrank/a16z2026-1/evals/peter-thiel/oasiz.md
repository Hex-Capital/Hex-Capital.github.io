# Oasiz -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Oasiz faces the most damning condition in my entire framework: multiple well-funded startups pursuing the identical thesis simultaneously. Rosebud AI has raised $15 million from a16z and Khosla Ventures for AI game creation. CodeWisp and Playabl.ai are both YC-backed and building text-to-game tools. When I see four companies converging on the same insight — "AI can now generate games instantly, so someone should build the distribution platform" — I do not see a competitive landscape. I see the absence of a secret. A genuine secret makes knowledgeable people uncomfortable or dismissive. "AI-generated games need a TikTok-style feed" makes every investor in the room nod. That is consensus dressed in product language, and consensus has never been the foundation of a monopoly.

The structural comparison that clarifies Oasiz's position is Wish — our most expensive portfolio lesson. Wish entered e-commerce with a slight twist (discovery-based discount shopping) against Amazon. Oasiz enters UGC gaming with a slight twist (AI-generated casual content plus social feed) against Roblox's 380 million monthly active users and $4.8 billion in projected 2025 revenue. The dossier itself identifies the lethal dynamic: Roblox could absorb Oasiz's value proposition by adding AI-assisted casual creation to its existing ecosystem of 3.5 million active creators and $1 billion+ in annual developer payouts. When the incumbent can add your differentiation as a feature while you cannot replicate their network, you are not building a monopoly — you are building a feature demo. The AI game generation layer compounds this vulnerability, as the dossier explicitly acknowledges it relies on "generally available LLMs and generative models." A moat built on commodity inputs is no moat at all.

Abel Dagne's distribution instincts are genuine — a prior app reaching #1 on App Store social charts with 500,000+ downloads is not an accident, and 6.5 million organic TikTok views before meaningful marketing spend demonstrates the kind of consumer pull I look for. Oasiz's 230,000+ plays and 5,000+ playable experiences in closed beta show organic engagement, not manufactured metrics. If I were Paul Graham, this founder signal alone might carry the investment. But my framework subordinates founder capability to monopoly structure. A brilliant, determined founder pursuing a competitive market is still a loser — and the AI gaming creation space, with at least four funded entrants and one public incumbent, is definitionally competitive. Dagne's talent would be better deployed against a problem where being right creates structural dominance rather than a slightly better product in a crowded field.

The strongest bull case for Oasiz requires believing it is creating an entirely new category — "playable social content" as distinct from UGC gaming the way TikTok created short-form video as distinct from YouTube. If interactive AI-generated games become a native social medium, the first platform to achieve network density in that medium could establish defensible winner-take-all dynamics. The Atari IP interest, if real, suggests content partners see this potential. For this to work, three things must be simultaneously true: the creation-distribution bundle must be genuinely harder to replicate than it appears, Roblox must be structurally unable to pivot downmarket into casual AI content (the cannibalization argument in the dossier), and TikTok must choose not to add playable mini-games despite their obvious fit with the format. Each of these is plausible individually; requiring all three simultaneously makes the monopoly path fragile rather than structural.

Oasiz is a pure software consumer platform built on commodity AI models, with no physical-world technology, no government customer pathway, no proprietary data asset, and no technical discontinuity that makes the existing approach obsolete. Jonathan Dinh's Berkeley AI research background (ROAR AI lab) provides technical competence but not a proprietary technological edge — the same foundation models are available to every competitor. The "Jemz" virtual currency introduced in v1.3 signals monetization experimentation rather than a validated business model, and no revenue data exists. The company occupies the precise category I warn against: an "AI-powered" version of an existing software category, entered because the category is hot rather than because the founders possess a specific non-obvious insight about it. The two dimensions that lock this verdict are the absence of a monopoly secret — evidenced by four simultaneous competitors pursuing identical theses — and the commodity software architecture with no physical-world or infrastructure barriers to replication.
