# qomplement -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

$200,000 to train a proprietary CUA model sounds like conviction until you look at who else is building the same capability. OpenAI, Anthropic, and BrowserUse (50,000+ GitHub stars, $17M seed from TechCrunch March 2025) are all shipping computer-use agents that click, type, and navigate desktop applications. qomplement's core automation mechanism -- an agent that mimics human desktop actions -- is not a product category they invented. It's a technology layer that frontier labs treat as a feature. When I see a startup whose primary technical asset is a capability that Google, Microsoft, and Anthropic are racing to commoditize, I apply the same test I use for every "AI wrapper" company: if the foundation model provider ships this natively, does qomplement still exist? The local-first, OS-level execution is a real architectural choice that partially survives that test. But "partially" is not the word that precedes outlier returns.

Kerim Taray and Andres Garza Garcia are both described as "former CTOs from high-growth startups" by Semilla Ventures, while Milenio describes the CTO as a "recently graduated engineer." qomplement's founders attended Tec de Monterrey together -- Mexico's strongest engineering school -- which suggests an organic co-founder relationship, not a startup-weekend pairing. But I cannot locate the specific knowledge. Where are the years of obsessive engagement with legacy enterprise systems? Where is the domain expertise so deep it would take competitors years to replicate? The founders identified a real pain point in Latin American enterprises stuck on old software -- five thousand invoices processed monthly by hand at one logistics customer (Expansion, September 2025) -- but identifying a pain point through market observation is not the same as possessing non-replicable knowledge about how to solve it. Ev Williams didn't observe that public communication needed a platform; he had built Blogger and lived inside the problem for years. qomplement's founders show no equivalent depth in document processing, CUA architecture, or legacy system automation that I can verify from public evidence.

qomplement's leverage architecture deserves a more careful audit than most pre-seed companies receive, because it contains a genuine contradiction. The product runs locally on machines with 8-16 GB RAM -- that's code leverage deployed at the edge, with privacy guarantees that regulated healthcare and financial services customers actually need. The claimed 15x cost advantage over cloud-based alternatives (Expansion, September 2025) could be structurally real if the local model performs adequately. But qomplement simultaneously spends $60,000 per month on AWS for model training and maintenance. That's not zero-marginal-cost scaling -- that's capital leverage masquerading as code leverage. At pre-seed, with YC's standard $500K, qomplement's AWS bill alone consumes the runway in roughly eight months. The economics need to improve dramatically for the leverage thesis to hold, and the dossier provides no evidence of a path to that improvement.

The bull case an advocate would make: qomplement occupies a wedge that US-focused competitors structurally cannot serve. Rossum ($114.5M raised, $44.9M ARR) and Extend AI ($17M, cash-flow positive) are cloud-native platforms requiring API integrations -- precisely what legacy Latin American enterprises cannot provide. Fifty percent of Mexican companies use AI agents but only seventeen percent have them deeply integrated (Google Cloud, cited in Expansion). qomplement's local-first, no-API-required approach is the only product architecture that reaches these customers where they actually are. The transportation customer automating 10,000+ invoices monthly and saving $15,000 per month is real traction at pre-seed -- most YC companies have less. The founders' LatAm networks (AWS GAIA 2025, Semilla Ventures) give them distribution that San Francisco startups would take years to build. This is a legitimate access democratization play: giving companies trapped in legacy software their first path to AI automation. If qomplement can lock in regulated industry verticals before the frontier labs figure out enterprise distribution in Latin America, the switching costs from recorded and automated workflows create a defensible position.

Here is why that bull case does not clear my threshold. The geographic wedge is real but it is not specific knowledge -- any competent engineering team from Monterrey with enterprise connections could replicate this positioning. qomplement's product descriptions vary across every source: "OS-level AI agent for legacy software automation" (Semilla Ventures), "AI Agents for Document Filling" (YC page), "Turn invoices and POs into system-ready data" (X/Twitter). Their website contains no product information at all. This positioning ambiguity at a company with four people and real customer deployments signals that qomplement has not yet found the sentence that explains what they are. Compare this to Perplexity, where Aravind Srinivas could articulate "AI-native search" from day one because his specific knowledge made the product obvious. qomplement's product is searching for its own identity, which means the founders' knowledge has not yet crystallized into a productized insight that compounds. The condition that would flip me: if Kerim Taray can demonstrate that qomplement's proprietary model achieves measurably higher accuracy on legacy LatAm enterprise workflows than GPT-5 or Claude's computer use -- not 10% better, but structurally better because of training data no one else possesses -- that would constitute specific knowledge I could not find in this dossier.

qomplement's team-to-value ratio is genuinely lean at four people shipping a deployed product with real enterprise customers. That earns respect. But the $60,000 monthly infrastructure burn, the commoditization pressure from BrowserUse and frontier labs, and the absence of identifiable specific knowledge in either founder's background create a profile I have seen many times: competent builders in a real market, building on a technology layer they do not control. The access democratization angle for LatAm legacy systems is the strongest signal in qomplement's favor -- it maps to the pattern that produced AngelList and CoinList. But AngelList worked because I had spent a decade accumulating specific knowledge about what angel investors needed. qomplement's founders have not demonstrated equivalent depth. The two dimensions that lock this verdict are specific knowledge productization, where I find no evidence of non-replicable expertise, and permissionless leverage architecture, where the core technology is being commoditized underneath them.
