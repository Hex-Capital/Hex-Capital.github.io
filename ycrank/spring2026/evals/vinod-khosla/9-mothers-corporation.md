# 9 Mothers -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Roman Khomenko spent eight years building production ML pipelines at Rainforest QA, won first place in a Kaggle iMaterialist challenge, and deployed YOLO-based computer vision systems at scale. He is also Ukrainian. Ukraine is the exact battlefield where $500 FPV suicide drones are destroying $10M armored vehicles and rewriting the rules of modern warfare. When I see a founder with world-class ML credentials who has existential proximity to the problem — not market research proximity, not "I read about it" proximity, but "this is happening to my country" proximity — that is a founder-market fit signal most investors would miss entirely. 9 Mothers is built on this combination: Khomenko's direct CV/ML expertise (PyTorch, YOLO, OpenCV, Kaggle wins) applied to the specific tracking-and-engagement problem that Ukraine has proven is the defining military challenge of this decade.

The Mach Industries parallel here is structural, not cosmetic. I backed a 19-year-old MIT dropout building weapons systems to compete with Lockheed Martin because the irreverence and naivety mattered more than defense industry credentials. 9 Mothers presents a different version of the same thesis: Russell Smith is a QA software entrepreneur who scaled Rainforest QA (YC S12) to $41.2M raised and $25.7M revenue over a decade, and he's now building autonomous weapon turrets to compete with Anduril at $60B and Epirus at $550M+ raised. Smith is not naive in the way I typically celebrate — he's proven — but he IS an outsider to defense, attacking it with software-first economics in a hardware-dominated procurement ecosystem. The 8-year working relationship between Smith and Khomenko is a genuine "gene pool" signal: complementary skills (startup operations + deep ML), battle-tested collaboration, not a team assembled for a pitch deck.

The consequence question for 9 Mothers is real but bounded. If EDDA works — a portable, low-cost, AI-driven counter-drone turret that any infantry squad or vehicle can carry — it changes the tactical calculus of modern warfare. The DoD has committed $1.86B in counter-drone spending. The C-UAS market is projected to hit $20.31B by 2030 at 25.1% CAGR. FPV suicide drones are the IED of this generation — cheap, asymmetric, lethal — and there is no ubiquitous portable defense today. 9 Mothers is attempting to be the answer. But I need to be honest: this consequence, while urgent, is military-specific. It saves soldiers' lives and protects assets. It doesn't collapse the cost of a basic human need for billions of people in the way that fusion energy or AI healthcare would. The civilian counter-drone applications (airports, stadiums — the $115M DHS funding for America250 and FIFA 2026) could expand the consequence envelope, but the dossier frames 9 Mothers primarily as a defense play.

The real non-linearity in 9 Mothers' approach is the cost curve, not the underlying science. A Stinger missile costs $38,000 per shot. Epirus's directed energy systems cost millions per installation. A shotgun shell costs single-digit dollars. If AI can reliably detect and track a fast-moving FPV drone — and Khomenko's CV expertise suggests this is achievable — then 9 Mothers' kinetic approach creates a three-orders-of-magnitude cost reduction per engagement. That is a genuine step-function. Fortem Technologies uses interceptor drones ($69M raised, $28.5M revenue) — you need a drone to kill a drone, an expensive match. D-Fend Solutions uses RF takeover ($73M raised) — which fails against autonomous drones with no communications link. 9 Mothers' shotgun turret defeats the target regardless of its communications protocol, at negligible marginal cost. The components themselves (computer vision, edge AI, mechanical turret) are not novel individually, but the integration into a portable, autonomous kinetic system at this price point is a defensible architectural choice. The job postings — 14 open positions spanning acoustic ML, DSP, robotics, and AI engineering at $150K-$250K — suggest the technical integration challenge is being attacked with serious engineering depth.

The bear case is competitive annihilation. Anduril just secured a $20B Army framework contract and operates Lattice, a full-stack software platform that can integrate any sensor or effector. They could build a cheap turret and bundle it into their ecosystem tomorrow. Epirus has $550M+ and purpose-built directed energy for C-UAS. 9 Mothers has YC funding, a handful of angels (Pioneer Fund, Theory Forge, Rsquared, Sterling Road), and no public revenue. The ITAR and security clearance constraints are non-trivial — two of three founders have Ukrainian/international backgrounds, which can slow facility clearances and limit classified program access. Long DoD procurement cycles could starve the company before revenue arrives. And "no public data found" for moat signals means I'm investing on thesis fit and founder quality, not proven defensibility. These are legitimate risks, and the competitive landscape is the most funded I've seen for a YC pre-seed company's target market.

But here's why the bear case doesn't kill the deal. Anduril's $20B contract is for large-scale integrated defense systems — they are becoming Lockheed Martin, not building $50K portable turrets. This is the innovator's dilemma in real time: the incumbents' business models drive them toward high-ASP enterprise contracts, leaving the low-cost portable segment underserved. 9 Mothers occupies the segment gap that every large defense prime will ignore because the unit economics don't justify their overhead structure. Smith demonstrated at Rainforest QA that he can build and scale a company through multi-year sales cycles. Khomenko's ML expertise is directly, specifically applicable — not adjacent, not analogous, but the exact skill set required to make autonomous drone tracking work in real-time on edge hardware. The condition that would flip me to Pass: if Anduril or another well-funded competitor ships a comparable portable C-UAS turret at similar price points within 12 months, the segment gap thesis collapses. The condition that keeps me in: evidence that 9 Mothers has live field testing data or a DoD rapid acquisition pathway (DIU, SOCOM) that validates the product before the primes react.

9 Mothers earns an Invest on the strength of two dimensions: the radical affordability of kinetic-vs-missile cost economics (shotgun shells vs. Stingers is not incremental, it's a category shift) and the founder-market fit of Khomenko's ML expertise combined with direct proximity to the defining battlefield of modern drone warfare. The consequence magnitude is real but bounded to military applications, which keeps this from the upper register. The technical approach is non-linear on cost but not on underlying science, which is the honest assessment. This is a binary option I would price: either the segment gap is real and 9 Mothers captures it before the primes notice, or competitive pressure crushes them. That's exactly the payoff structure I look for.
