# Kinro -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's what I see when I look at Kinro: an AI sales agent for insurance. That sentence should already tell you where this lands for me. Half the companies I meet right now are pitching "AI agent for [regulated industry]" and the pitch always sounds the same -- vertical-specific compliance, carrier integrations, domain expertise the horizontal platforms won't build. And you know what? They're not wrong that the opportunity exists. But the opportunity existing and the opportunity being mispriced are two completely different things. Harper raised $46.8M. COVU has ~$50M. FurtherAI pulled $30M from a16z. YC itself has Strada from S23 and Panta from W26 in adjacent insurance AI lanes. When I'm looking at a 3-person pre-seed team in a category where multiple competitors already have $30-50M in the bank, I'm not seeing asymmetric risk/reward. I'm seeing a fully priced category where the upside is already baked in for the winners, and a 3-person team isn't obviously the winner.

The positioning choice -- enabling brokers and carriers rather than replacing them -- is the most interesting strategic decision here. Harper and Panta want to be the brokerage. Kinro wants to be the infrastructure layer that makes existing brokerages AI-native. That's a more Stripe-like positioning, and I respect it. It avoids channel conflict, which matters enormously in insurance where relationships are everything and carriers will not work with companies that compete with their distribution partners. WTW showing up as a client logo at pre-seed is genuinely impressive -- that's one of the largest brokerages on Earth. But being a vendor to WTW and being a category-defining company are different things entirely. Kinro is making insurance distribution incrementally better. It's not creating a behavior that didn't exist before. Nobody's going to say "Kinro me a policy" the way people say "Venmo me." This is automation of existing workflows, not invention of new ones.

The bull case deserves serious engagement. What if ChatGPT and Gemini become the primary surface where consumers buy insurance? WTW dropped 12% when the ChatGPT insurance app news hit. That's the market telling you something real is happening. If AI platforms become the dominant distribution channel for personal lines insurance -- and J.P. Morgan is literally publishing scenarios where this happens -- then someone needs to build the regulated, compliant, carrier-integrated infrastructure that connects insurers to these platforms. Kinro is positioning for exactly that. Pierre-Alexandre came from DeepMind where he worked on Gemini for financial services -- that's about as direct a founder-market fit as you can get for this specific play. If the AI-platform-as-distribution-channel thesis is correct, Kinro could be building genuinely important plumbing. That's the scenario where I'm wrong to pass.

But here's why I still pass: the structural durability question cuts both ways. Yes, insurance regulation creates barriers that protect Kinro from generic AI wrappers. SOC 2 Type II, state-by-state compliance, carrier pricing integrations -- this is real infrastructure work that takes time. But who benefits most from that infrastructure? The incumbents. When OpenAI decides it wants insurance distribution on ChatGPT, they'll partner with WTW or Aon directly, not with a 3-person startup. Google has been in insurance comparison for years through Google Compare (which they shut down and could reopen). The platforms control the distribution, and in my experience, platform dependency is a death sentence. Kinro is building compliant insurance AI agents that live on someone else's platform -- and that someone else can change the rules, increase the take rate, or build the feature themselves at any moment. This is the AI application-layer trap I keep warning people about, dressed up in regulatory clothing.

The founder team has strong individual credentials -- DeepMind researcher, Zoox ML infrastructure engineer, first employee at an insurance startup. Competent people. But nothing about this idea is insane. No one at a dinner party would say "you're doing what? That's crazy." AI agents for insurance sales is exactly the kind of thing McKinsey puts on slide 47 of their digital transformation deck. Sane people, sane idea. That ratio doesn't get me out of my chair. And the co-founders don't appear to have deep pre-existing relationships -- no shared employer, no shared university, no visible history of working through hard problems together. I don't know the origin story here, but the absence of visible shared history is something I notice.

I'd rather be early to something no one believes in than on time to something everyone's already funding. Kinro is on time.
