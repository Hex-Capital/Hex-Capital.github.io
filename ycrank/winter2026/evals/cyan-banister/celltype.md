# CellType -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that strikes me about CellType isn't the science — it's that Sundar Pichai publicly endorsed the technology, Google DeepMind co-developed the core model, and the company claims 100% inbound deal flow from Top 10 pharma. Those are remarkable signals for any pre-seed company. They're also exactly the kind of consensus validation that makes me pull back. When the CEO of Google is publicly championing your work and pharmaceutical giants are calling you, you are not an unpopular company that's difficult to assess. You are the opposite. The most promising companies in my experience are the ones nobody wants to touch — the ones where I'm writing the first check because everyone else thinks the founder is out of their mind. CellType's problem isn't a lack of credibility. It's that the credibility might be the wrong kind of signal for my framework.

If I could sit across from David van Dijk, my first question would be: tell me how you got here — not the lab, not the papers, not the Google collaboration. I want to know what drives you personally to this problem. And the dossier gives me nothing to work with on that front. I see a distinguished academic career — PhD from Amsterdam and Weizmann, postdoc at Columbia, associate professorship at Yale, over eleven thousand citations. That's genuinely world-class. But it's not the same thing as a founder whose life story IS the company's mission. The founders who persist through the darkest moments of building a company are usually the ones who can't walk away because the problem is personal. When I backed Travis Kalanick, his obsession with the taxi medallion racket wasn't academic — it was visceral. When I championed Contraline through Founders Fund, the founder had spent years in the trenches of a problem nobody else cared about. Van Dijk turned down Google to build CellType, which shows real conviction, but I'd need to hear much more about why this particular problem has a hold on him that goes beyond intellectual fascination.

AI drug discovery is one of the most heavily invested categories in biotech right now. Recursion has raised over $460 million. Insilico has drugs in clinical trials. Isomorphic Labs — a Google DeepMind spinout — just raised $600 million. This is not a space where VCs are scared to write checks. The Cell2Sentence methodology itself is genuinely novel — converting gene expression data into language for LLMs to process is a creative insight that I find intellectually compelling. But the methodology is published at ICML under an Apache 2.0 license with 815 GitHub stars. The collaborator who helped build it has virtually unlimited resources and their own drug discovery subsidiary. When your core innovation is open-source and your most powerful partner can become your most dangerous competitor overnight, your moat depends entirely on what you build on top — proprietary data from pharma partnerships, the agentic orchestration layer, validated predictions. None of that is publicly demonstrated as differentiated yet.

Here's the bull case I genuinely wrestled with: this could be an inflection-point infrastructure play, the kind of bet I made on SpaceX or Crusoe Energy. If Cell2Sentence becomes the foundational layer for computational biology the way AlphaFold became the standard for protein structure, CellType could be defining a new category rather than competing in an existing one. The Senhwa Biosciences MOU is real traction — a signed partnership with a six-month pilot, not just a handshake. And if pharma partnerships generate proprietary validation data that continuously improves the models, the open-source base matters less over time because the trained models and accumulated predictions become the real asset. Van Dijk's academic network and published credibility are genuine distribution advantages that money can't easily buy. If I'm wrong about this company, it's because I'm underweighting the possibility that the science is so fundamentally right that it overwhelms every structural concern.

But I keep coming back to who this company serves and how. CellType makes drug discovery faster and cheaper for pharmaceutical companies — some of the wealthiest organizations on Earth. That's valuable, but it doesn't create economic access for underserved populations or make an invisible system visible. The path from "computational screening platform" to "affordable medicine for people who need it" runs through the very pharma pricing structures that keep drugs inaccessible. I invest in companies where the impact is direct — Uber didn't need an intermediary to create income for drivers, Affirm didn't need permission from banks to make credit transparent. The professor-student co-founder dynamic between van Dijk and Vrkic also gives me pause. Academic hierarchies don't always translate into the kind of equal partnership that survives the brutal pressure of commercialization, and I've seen too many companies crack along fault lines that were baked in from day one.

This is a company built by genuinely brilliant people working on important science in a space I respect. But through my lens, it reads as a well-validated academic spinout in a consensus category — exactly the kind of deal that every institutional investor will compete for, which means the price will be wrong and the weirdness has already been arbitraged away. I'm not the right investor for this one.
