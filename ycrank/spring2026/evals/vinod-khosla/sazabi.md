# Sazabi -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Sazabi: the company is entering a market that's already consolidating beneath its feet. Langfuse was acquired by ClickHouse within two years. Arize raised $70M. LangSmith hit a $1.25B valuation. Datadog, a $50B behemoth with $2.1B in annual revenue, is bolting AI observability onto a platform that already has distribution to every engineering team on Earth. Sazabi has a waitlist and a manifesto. When I see a startup entering a market where incumbents are already adapting and adjacent startups are being absorbed, I don't ask "can they compete?" — I ask whether the consequence of their success justifies the option price. And that's where this falls apart for me.

If Sazabi fully succeeds — captures the AI-native observability market, proves the "logs are all you need" thesis, displaces Datadog for AI-forward teams — what happens to the world? Software engineers have better dashboards. They debug AI-generated code faster. That's useful. It's not consequential. Nobody's life fundamentally changes. Healthcare doesn't get cheaper. Energy doesn't become abundant. Education doesn't reach a billion more people. The ceiling here is a very good B2B SaaS company — maybe a $5-10B outcome in a favorable scenario — and that's competing for existing observability budgets, not creating new demand. This is redistribution of enterprise spend, not the kind of consequence explosion that makes me lean forward.

The technical thesis is the most interesting piece, and I want to give it honest weight. "Logs are all you need" — the claim that AI can reconstruct metrics and traces from logs alone, collapsing three pillars of observability into one — is genuinely contrarian. If it works at production scale, it's not a 2x improvement; it's a structural simplification that could cut observability costs by two-thirds or more. That's the kind of technical bet I respect. But it's an unvalidated claim on a waitlist-only product. No public customers, no usage data, no evidence it works beyond the manifesto. The technical ambition is there in words, but I need to see it working before I call it non-linear. A bold slide is not the same as a bold capability.

Callaway has earned his stripes. Built observability infrastructure at Brex. Founded Opkit, got it through YC, sold it to 11x within a year. Then led development on Alice — described as one of the largest agentic systems in production. He's lived inside both sides of the problem Sazabi targets: the observability side and the AI agent side. That's real founder-market fit, and the angel roster (Anthropic, Vercel, LangChain, Replit, Brex leadership) confirms the ecosystem respects him. But here's my tension: in my framework, domain expertise is a weaker signal than learning rate and outsider perspective. Callaway is the informed insider, not the naive outsider who doesn't know what can't be done. He's building exactly what his resume predicts he would build. That's competent. It's not surprising. The strongest founders I've backed — Peter Beck building rockets in a New Zealand garage, a 19-year-old competing with Lockheed — had no business being in their industries. Callaway has every reason to be in his. That limits the upside of the bet, because domain experts tend to build within the boundaries of what they already understand.

Let me steelman the bull case, because it deserves it. If AI coding agents cross a threshold in the next 18 months — if 50%+ of production code at fast-moving companies is AI-generated — then every one of those companies has a new category of failure that Datadog wasn't designed to detect. Agent decision errors, hallucinated code paths, cascading bugs from AI commits that no human reviewed. In that world, Sazabi's "agent decisions as first-class telemetry" isn't a feature; it's a category. The SOC 2 and ISO certifications are real investment for a 10-person team, and they signal enterprise seriousness. If the logs-only thesis actually works, the cost advantage could be a wedge into Datadog's install base. And the timing of YC S26 means Callaway has 12-18 months to build the product before the adoption curve inflects. I can see the scenario where this is a $5B+ outcome. But a $5B outcome at a pre-seed valuation is a fine return — it's not the kind of consequence that justifies accepting the competitive risk of Datadog adding this as a feature line in their next quarterly release. I want to either lose my money or make 50 times my money. This company is designed to produce a solid 10-20x, which in my framework means someone else should fund it.

The core issue is simple: Sazabi is a better mousetrap in a market that already has excellent mousetraps and well-funded mousetrap factories. The ambition is to be the best observability platform for a new era, not to make something expensive free or to do something the world thinks is impossible. That's a perfectly valid company for most investors. It's not my kind of bet.
