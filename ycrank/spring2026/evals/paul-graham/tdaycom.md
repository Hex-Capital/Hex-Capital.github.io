# tday.com -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Cyrus Kelly graduated from Adelaide with a Mathematical and Computer Sciences degree at 18 and is now on his third shipped product with Shyamsundar Shrestha — communities.one, then markup.one, now tday.com — and that serial-iteration pair is the single strongest signal in this dossier, while the absence of any GitHub handle, Twitter account, or Hacker News footprint for either of them is the single weakest. tday.com is a persistent two-person Adelaide team without a hacker-tribal trail, pitching into a creative-AI category that already contains Arcade, AdCreative.ai, Omneky, Pencil, and Lapis — the kind of company where I keep wanting to like the founders more than the dossier lets me.

The **Airbnb** (2009) cereal-box moment is what I'm looking for on animal resourcefulness, and tday.com's closest equivalent is the markup.one → tday.com rebrand layered on top of an earlier communities.one — three products from the same Adelaide pair, the youngest of whom finished his degree at 18 and mentored 300+ student founders as Chief Student Entrepreneur. That is real shipping behavior, more than most pre-seed dossiers contain. Where tday.com diverges from the **Airbnb** (2009) rung is that there is no stunt — no equivalent of selling 1,000 boxes of cereal at $40 each, no Brian Armstrong-style post-rejection prototype, no unscalable manual move named in the dossier. Kelly and Shrestha look like diligent, repeat-shipping founders; they do not yet look like animals. Between the **Coinbase** (2012) mid-rung and the MBA-pass-example floor, closer to the middle.

The **Stripe** (2010) failure mode for hacker genealogy is the inverse of what made the Collisons obvious — Patrick had won Ireland's Young Scientist for Lisp work at 16, Auctomatic existed, the code was visible. The pattern that worries me is when the dossier says "Bachelor of Mathematical and Computer Sciences" and stops there. tday.com fits that worrying pattern: Kelly and Shrestha both have BMaCS from Adelaide, no GitHub repos surface, no Twitter or X presence, no open-source artifact, no competitive-programming result, no Hacker News thread. Shrestha's claim about "fully deterministic" code-based design is the kind of architectural sentence a hacker would write, but I cannot verify it from the outside, and the dossier's note that "no third-party benchmarks, customer count, or public output samples" exist makes it a marketing claim until proven otherwise. Hackers leave GitHub trails. These two haven't.

The organic-idea question is whether Kelly and Shrestha personally felt the pain of shipping launch content for their own product before they built tday.com. The mechanism that makes this dimension compound is brutal: founders who use their own product test it ten times a day before any customer does, which is how **Dropbox** (2007) found its UX a year ahead of competitors. tday.com's prior shipped products — communities.one and markup.one — both would have needed launch creative, social posts, captioned walkthroughs. So the pain is plausibly real, but the dossier never says Kelly or Shrestha spent hours per week making launch videos and hated it. The story I want — "we kept making the same Instagram graphic for our own product changes" — is not in the dossier. Without it I'm guessing.

Arcade is the relevant single anchor for the schlep dimension, and it is unfortunately on the wrong side: Arcade raised ~$11.5M and is already targeting the exact "product launch video" use case tday.com names on its homepage. The schlep test asks whether mainstream founders are unconsciously routing around the work; in this case other YC-grade founders are running *into* it. The deterministic-design pipeline and the 10-channel reformatting (Instagram, TikTok, YouTube, X, Facebook, LinkedIn, Pinterest, Threads, Bluesky, Google Business) is real per-channel grind, but it is software grind, not a **Coinbase** (2012)-style regulatory moat. tday.com sits in a crowded software category, not a schlep zone.

The counterfactual for cofounder configuration is what would have to be true for me to score this higher: McDonough — the COO listed on the YC page but absent from the Adelaide article that profiled Kelly and Shrestha — would need a documented prior relationship with the other two. Right now Kelly and Shrestha have the **Reddit** (2005)-grade dorm-roommate pattern from ThincLab, communities.one, and the markup.one→tday.com lineage, which is a genuinely strong pair signal. McDonough reads as a later add with no public trail. A strong pair plus an unverified third is better than solo and worse than the **Airbnb** (2009) trio where all three lived together.

My 36-month forecast for tday.com: pivots away from the multi-channel launch-content positioning toward a narrower brand-asset-API for software companies under pressure from Arcade and AdCreative.ai, fails to reach $1M ARR, and either quiet acqui-hires into Canva/Adobe/Figma or quietly winds down. The mechanism is competitive density — five funded competitors, one (Arcade) directly on the same use case — combined with a founding team that has shipping discipline but no observable hacker-distribution edge to outrun them on velocity.

The **Twitch** (2007) precedent is the bull case that stings, and I should say it out loud: Kan and Shear came back from **Kiko** (2005) and I re-funded them, and tday.com is on its third iteration of the same founding pair — communities.one, then markup.one, then tday.com — which is structurally the Kiko→Justin.tv→Twitch shape of a team learning across attempts. The dismantling fact is that markup.one→tday.com is a rebrand of the same product, not a failure-then-comeback; the Adelaide article describes markup.one in language substantively identical to tday.com's homepage. Kan sold Kiko on eBay and came back with a new thesis. Kelly and Shrestha renamed the URL. The verifiable condition that would flip my verdict: a single customer from the Relay/Drift/Slate/Forge/Inkwell logo wall confirmed as paying, using tday.com weekly for actual launch content, with a named contact at that customer willing to say so — that is the Drew-Houston-Hacker-News-demo moment for this company, and the dossier does not yet contain it.
