# EigenPal -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The first thing I notice about EigenPal is the competitive landscape, and it tells me everything I need to know about the absence of a secret. Rossum has raised $114.5 million. Hyperscience has raised roughly $300 million. Nanonets has $29 million. Google, Amazon, and Microsoft each offer document extraction APIs bundled into cloud contracts that enterprises already pay for. And now EigenPal enters this market at pre-seed with the thesis that AI can process documents without templates. This is not a contrarian insight -- it is the unanimous consensus of every player in the intelligent document processing space. When I ask "what important truth do very few people agree with you on?" and the answer is something that Rossum, Hyperscience, Nanonets, Google Document AI, AWS Textract, and Azure Document Intelligence all also believe, there is no secret. There is only competition. And competition, as I have said many times, destroys value for everyone involved.

The technology compounds this problem. LLM-based document extraction is becoming a commodity capability. Any developer can call GPT-4 or Claude with a document image and extract structured data with reasonable accuracy. EigenPal's differentiators -- an "evaluation-first" workflow, natural language configuration, observability dashboards, on-premises deployment -- are features layered on top of increasingly generic infrastructure. Features are not proprietary technology. A workflow builder is not a 10x improvement over anything; it is a marginally better interface for a capability that foundation model providers are rapidly making trivial. The claim of "99% accuracy" on their homepage is precisely the kind of metric that every IDP vendor advertises and none can sustain across the full diversity of enterprise document types. Nothing here represents the kind of technological discontinuity that makes the prior approach structurally obsolete.

The founders are competent but do not display the pattern I look for. Blaszyk's engineering background at Elastic and his open-source document editor demonstrate relevant technical skill. Novak's consulting stints at McKinsey and Accenture, combined with his prior B2B AI company Assetario, show enterprise awareness. But consulting internships are the opposite of the deep technical conviction I value -- they train people to serve client preferences, not to hold a singular vision against consensus pressure. More concerning: Assetario appears still active according to multiple directories, and the dossier notes the relationship between that venture and full-time commitment to EigenPal is publicly unclear. The founders may well be dedicated, but the observable signal is ambiguity rather than the kind of burn-the-boats commitment that Zuckerberg showed when he told his board "we're obviously not going to sell."

The strongest bull case would require something specific to be true: that regulated European financial institutions represent a beachhead where compliance requirements (SOC II Type 2, GDPR) and on-premises deployment needs create genuine switching costs that larger competitors cannot easily replicate. If EigenPal could monopolize loan document processing for, say, the top 50 European banks -- a narrow, compliance-heavy vertical where procurement cycles are slow and vendor changes are painful -- that initial dominance could theoretically expand into insurance claims, then manufacturing documents, then healthcare records. The two European bank relationships suggest this path exists. But even this optimistic scenario collides with Rossum, which is itself a European company (Prague-based) with seven years of enterprise penetration and $44.9 million in revenue already targeting the same customers. The beachhead is already contested.

I keep returning to the structural question: what does EigenPal know that Rossum, with $114.5 million in capital and $44.9 million in annual revenue, does not? The dossier reveals no answer. The Girardian dynamic here is textbook -- multiple companies imitating each other's approach (template-free AI extraction), each claiming slightly different feature combinations (human-in-the-loop vs. evaluation-first vs. developer-API-first), all converging on the same customers with the same value proposition. This is a market where everyone has the same thesis, and when everyone has the same thesis, nobody has a secret. I pass.
