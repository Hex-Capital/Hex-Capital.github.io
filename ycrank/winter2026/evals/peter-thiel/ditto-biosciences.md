# Ditto Biosciences -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The most interesting thing about Ditto Bio is what happened before they existed. Coronado Biosciences spent years and tens of millions of dollars trying to treat Crohn's disease by having patients swallow live pig whipworms. It failed Phase 2 trials in 2013, and the entire paradigm of parasite-derived therapy was declared dead. The consensus conclusion was clear: parasites don't work as medicine. But that conclusion was wrong in a precise way -- what failed was the delivery mechanism (live organisms with uncontrollable biology), not the underlying insight (that parasites have evolved sophisticated molecular machinery to modulate human immunity). Ditto's founders recognized this distinction, and that recognition is the closest thing to a secret this company possesses. When the crowd runs from a domain because of one dramatic failure, the gap between what actually happened and what people conclude happened is sometimes where genuine opportunities hide. The question is whether this gap is large enough to build a monopoly inside.

The secret, stated plainly, would be something like: "Evolution has already conducted a billion-year drug discovery program for autoimmune disease, and nobody is mining the results because a crude previous attempt poisoned the well." I find this partially compelling. The claim that over 98% of viral and parasite proteins remain uncharacterized suggests genuinely unexplored territory -- a vast chemical library that conventional pharma has ignored. And the early binding affinity data (1-2 nM against validated human targets) from their computational screen of over one million parasite-derived proteins suggests this isn't merely theoretical. But I want to be precise about what kind of secret this is. It is not a truth that most intelligent biologists would actively argue against -- many would likely agree that parasite-derived proteins are scientifically interesting as potential therapeutics. It is more accurately an unexplored space that nobody has commercially organized around, which is a weaker form of contrarian insight. The strongest secrets are beliefs that provoke genuine disagreement from experts, not merely neglected observations. This distinction matters because the degree of contrarianism predicts the degree of competitive insulation.

The founding team is scientifically exceptional by any standard. Three PhDs from UCSF, UC Berkeley, and programs touching Harvard, with fifty-plus publications, a patent on anti-CRISPR proteins, and relevant stints at Illumina, DuPont, and Arcadia Science. More importantly, they worked together for three years at Arcadia before founding Ditto -- they are not strangers assembled around a pitch deck. I have always valued pre-existing working relationships; the PayPal team's ability to collaborate under pressure came from having built together before, and that pattern repeats in the strongest founding teams. Borges's training in host-pathogen immune evasion at UCSF and her Miller Fellowship at Berkeley, Sun's computational genomics expertise, and Weiss's high-throughput screening background from Illumina create genuine interdisciplinary coverage across exactly the domains this approach requires. The team possesses what I would call deep specific knowledge -- they are not MBAs who read a trend report about AI drug discovery and decided to start a company. They are scientists who lived inside the biology and saw something others missed.

Where I struggle is with the monopoly question. In technology companies, monopolies arise through network effects, economies of scale, proprietary technology with compounding advantages, or brand. Ditto's defensibility runs through two channels: a proprietary database of characterized parasite proteins (which compounds as they add data) and eventual composition-of-matter patents on specific drug candidates. The database advantage is real but replicable -- a well-funded competitor with computational biology talent could mount a parallel screening effort within two to three years. Composition-of-matter patents are pharma's traditional moat, but they are time-limited by design and their value depends entirely on clinical success, which remains deeply uncertain. There are no network effects here. There are no scale economies that widen over time in the way SpaceX's learning curve compounds with each launch. The monopoly path, if one exists, runs through the standard pharmaceutical playbook: patent a molecule, prove it works in trials, license or sell to big pharma. This is a perfectly legitimate business model, but it is not the kind of structural monopoly that produces the extreme power-law returns I seek. Biotech exits tend to be acquisitions in the low billions, not independent market dominance generating decades of monopoly profit.

The bull case deserves articulation. If Ditto's computational platform can systematically and rapidly identify parasite-derived proteins that hit validated immune targets -- and if even one of those candidates succeeds in clinical development -- the returns would be substantial. Blockbuster autoimmune drugs generate five to ten billion dollars annually. The platform nature of the approach means multiple shots on goal across different indications: rheumatoid arthritis, IBD, lupus, MS. The AI timing is genuine -- systematic screening of millions of proteins was computationally impractical even recently, which explains why this approach wasn't attempted at scale before. And the immunogenicity work (the tissue biobank mapping immune memory to parasite proteins) could become a proprietary asset that de-risks the entire pipeline. If you believe that evolution is the most powerful optimizer available, and that parasite genomes represent a trillion-dollar R&D library that the pharmaceutical industry has irrationally abandoned, then Ditto at pre-seed valuations represents an asymmetric bet. The team is credible enough to execute. The science is plausible enough to attract follow-on funding.

But I keep returning to the structural question. Drug development requires hundreds of millions of dollars and a decade of work, during which the company will raise multiple rounds and suffer significant dilution. The binary nature of clinical trials -- a Phase 2 failure can destroy ninety percent of a company's value overnight -- makes this a fundamentally different risk profile from technology investments where products can iterate and compound. I don't see the kind of definite, specific plan that distinguishes a Zuckerberg or a Musk -- the founders are running what amounts to a sophisticated scientific search through an enormous protein space, hoping to find candidates that survive clinical development. This is closer to the "lean" methodology I am skeptical of than to definite optimism, even though the underlying biology is rigorous. The market positioning -- targeting the $125 billion autoimmune therapeutics market broadly rather than identifying a specific beachhead indication they can own completely -- reinforces this concern. A company that lists seven different diseases as potential targets is a company that hasn't yet made the hard choices about where to concentrate force. This is a competent team with a genuine scientific insight in an important domain, but it lacks the structural path to monopoly and the definite founder vision that would make it my kind of bet.
