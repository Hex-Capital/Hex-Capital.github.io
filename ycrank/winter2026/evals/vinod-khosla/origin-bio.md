# Origin -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Two CS undergrads from UIUC built a model that outperforms Google DeepMind's AlphaGenome at predicting regulatory element binding activity. A four-person team beating one of the best-resourced AI labs on the planet at a specific biology task -- that gets my attention. This is the outsider pattern I've seen produce enormous outcomes. Peter Beck didn't know what aerospace engineers considered impossible, so he built a rocket. These founders don't come from biology, which may be exactly why they built a unified generation-prediction architecture that nobody in genomics thought to build as a single model. The 6.7% improvement on prediction and 9x enrichment on targeted transcription factor motifs are real technical signals, not marketing vapor.

The consequence of success here is substantial but requires careful framing. Gene therapies are limited today primarily by a safety problem: you can deliver a gene, but you can't precisely control where it turns on. Toxicity, off-target effects, lack of cell-type specificity -- these are what keep gene therapies from scaling to the hundreds of diseases they could theoretically treat. If Origin solves expression control through AI-designed regulatory DNA, they don't just build a tool -- they remove the bottleneck that constrains a market projected to reach $100-230 billion within a decade. That said, this is enabling infrastructure, not the therapy itself. Origin would be like Juniper building the routers, not the applications running on the internet. Enabling infrastructure can produce transformational returns -- Juniper returned 2,500x -- but it requires becoming the indispensable layer, not merely a nice-to-have component. Whether Origin becomes indispensable hinges entirely on the proprietary wet-lab dataset they claim to be building.

And that dataset is both the company's greatest promise and its most glaring vulnerability. The Axis model is trained on publicly available ENCODE V4 data using standard transformer architectures -- any well-funded lab could reproduce the architecture. Origin's stated moat is "the largest proprietary dataset of synthetic regulatory sequences across diverse cell-states," validated experimentally. But this dataset is aspirational, not realized. With four people. No disclosed lab infrastructure, no disclosed dataset scale, no disclosed validation throughput. Building this kind of compounding data flywheel -- design sequences computationally, test them in the wet lab, feed results back into the model -- is exactly the right strategy. It's also a strategy that requires either massive capital or brilliant partnerships, and I see no evidence of either yet. Asimov has $209 million and operates adjacently. DeepMind has functionally unlimited resources. The window for Origin to build a defensible dataset lead is real but narrow.

Let me articulate what would make this a great investment, because the bull case is genuine. Gene therapy is on an exponential adoption curve -- multiple FDA approvals in 2023-2025, a market growing at 18-24% CAGR, and the safety problem is consensus-acknowledged but unsolved. Origin is positioned at the exact right layer at the exact right moment: AI capabilities have crossed the threshold to design regulatory elements (validated by Nature Genetics 2025 publications), but no commercial player owns this niche. If Yash and Malhar secure one major pharma partnership -- a Novartis or Roche that needs custom regulatory elements for a specific therapeutic program -- that single deal could fund the wet-lab infrastructure, generate proprietary data, and create a reference customer that makes the next deal easier. The advisory board (Kellis at MIT/Broad, Paulk at UCSF/Siren Bio) has the pharma connections to make this happen. If I saw evidence that a deal like this was in negotiation, my conviction would shift dramatically. The rate of change in both AI capability and gene therapy adoption is clearly accelerating, and Origin is positioned ahead of the curve.

What holds me back is the gap between ambition and current operational reality. The technology risk from in silico benchmark to in vivo therapeutic safety is enormous -- years of biological validation, animal studies, regulatory processes. The founding team is all computational with no biology operator. Advisors are not co-founders. The free API with no pricing signals they haven't yet found a customer willing to pay, which at this stage is understandable but means I'm investing purely on the problem and the model performance, with no demand validation whatsoever. And the specific niche -- regulatory DNA design -- is narrow enough that it could become a feature inside a larger platform like Asimov rather than a standalone company. I need to see whether these founders are building toward category-defining infrastructure or a clever tool that gets absorbed.

The timing is right, the outsider angle is real, and the problem matters. But the consequence of success, while meaningful, doesn't reach the threshold where I'd invest before the company exists, the way I did with Impossible Foods. This is a company I want to track closely -- if they secure a pharma partnership and demonstrate wet-lab validation throughput, the picture changes entirely.
