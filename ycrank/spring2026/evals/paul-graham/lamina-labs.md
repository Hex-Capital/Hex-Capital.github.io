# Lamina Labs -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Knowlify raised $3M in its seed round, landed teams at Google and Amazon as customers, and graduated from YC S25 -- all building AI-generated explainer videos from prompts and documents. TMA.live attacks the same 3Blue1Brown-style animation with Manim plus LLMs. Leap built an MCP tool for it. MathMatrixMovies exists. When I count four or five teams converging on the same idea at the same time, I know I'm looking at a consensus-good idea. And I wrote an entire essay about why those are exactly the bets I avoid. The sweet spot for startups is the intersection of "seems like a bad idea" and "is a good idea." Lamina Labs lives in the quadrant where everyone nods along -- "AI that creates educational animations? Of course that should exist." Nobody reacts with confusion or skepticism. The 3Blue1Brown YouTube channel has 6M+ subscribers proving the demand, and multiple well-funded teams are already chasing the supply side. That tells me the return premium has already been competed away before Lamina Labs even opens its waitlist.

Kartikesh Mishra (MIT BS '24, MEng '25, CSAIL research under Daniela Rus) and Sudip Rokaya (MIT CS & Math, on leave, prior co-founder at Harmonic) are clearly technically capable. MIT CSAIL work on drone natural-language command-following, 3D shape generation from 2D images, teaching ML and computer vision -- these are exactly the skills you'd want for building an animation engine. But when I evaluate founders, I'm looking for stories, not credentials. I want to hear about the time they were blocked and hacked their way through. The dossier gives me degrees and research papers. Sudip had a prior startup called Harmonic -- what happened? Did it fail? What did he learn? That's the X-ray I use to see the skeleton beneath the presentation, and here the X-ray is blank. Sudip leaving MIT is a commitment signal, but leaving school to start a company has become so normalized in our world that it no longer distinguishes. Lamina Labs' founders may have the animal quality, but nothing in the available record demonstrates it.

The origin story for Lamina Labs reads as a thesis, not a frustration. "The only way to learn a difficult thing is to watch someone else draw it" is a pedagogical belief -- a reasonable one -- but it's not "I kept forgetting my USB drive" (Dropbox) or "I couldn't find electronic parts online" (Octopart). Kartikesh's teaching assistant work and high school mentoring through Momentum AI place him adjacent to the problem, which is better than discovering it from a McKinsey deck. But adjacent isn't the same as organic. An MIT math student who watched 3Blue1Brown videos and thought "what if anyone could make these?" is having the most obvious possible insight for someone in that position. The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas -- it's to notice what's missing in your daily work. Lamina Labs' problem discovery feels more like "this market obviously exists" than "I couldn't solve this problem any other way."

The 11-second generation speed that @aaronsiim praised on X, compared to 15+ minutes for competitors, is the single most interesting technical signal in the dossier. A 100x speed difference doesn't come from incremental optimization -- it implies Lamina Labs built a fundamentally different rendering pipeline rather than wrapping Manim with an LLM. That's real engineering work. Combined with their emphasis on "deterministic, mathematically precise" output versus the accuracy problems Hacker News commenters flagged in TMA.live (93 upvotes, 46 comments, January 2025), there's a plausible case that Lamina Labs has a genuine technical edge. The bull case writes itself: young MIT CS founders with directly relevant ML and vision research, 100x speed advantage suggesting proprietary architecture, a proven massive market (3Blue1Brown's audience), and a specific accuracy-first approach that addresses the known failure mode of competitors. If I compare this to my investment in Replit -- hackers building tools to make creation more accessible, discovered through a technical demo that showed obvious differentiation -- there's a surface parallel. But Replit was building something that didn't have four funded competitors when I found it on Hacker News in 2017. Lamina Labs is entering a market where Knowlify already has enterprise customers and $3M in funding.

The product rename from Pictor to Simi and the shifting tagline show Lamina Labs is iterating, which is mildly positive for learning rate. But the company remains waitlist-gated with no public user counts, no revenue, and no growth metrics. I can't evaluate the 5-7% weekly growth compass that I rely on because there's no data to compute it from. For learning rate, I'm left with the indirect signal of the rename and the speed improvement claim -- evidence of technical iteration, but not the kind of user-driven learning that separates startups from research projects. The founders' earnestness reads as genuine: Kartikesh's teaching and mentoring background, Sudip sacrificing his MIT degree, the educational philosophy driving the product -- these suggest people building for the right reasons rather than chasing a hot market. But at pre-seed, earnestness without demonstrated resourcefulness is an incomplete signal.

Lamina Labs' most critical weakness is the absence of schlep. Compare this to Stripe, where the Collison brothers waded into PCI compliance, bank integrations, and regulatory complexity that physically repelled other developers from the payments space. Or Bindwell, where two teenagers dove into pesticide chemistry and regulatory approval pathways. Prompt-to-educational-animation is intellectually interesting and technically demanding, but it doesn't involve the kind of painful, unglamorous complexity that creates natural competitive moats. The barrier here is pure technical execution speed and accuracy -- and those advantages erode as LLMs improve. If GPT-5 or Claude's next generation can reliably generate correct Manim code, the 100x speed advantage narrows. Lamina Labs' moat depends on staying ahead of foundation model capabilities, which is a bet against the strongest force in technology right now. One verifiable condition that would flip me to Invest: evidence that Lamina Labs has built a proprietary animation engine that does NOT depend on LLM-generated code -- a fundamentally different architecture that improves with scale in ways that Manim wrappers cannot replicate, demonstrated by accuracy benchmarks on mathematically complex content where LLM approaches fail. That would transform the defensibility picture entirely.

The dimension that most decisively places Lamina Labs in Pass territory is Idea That Repels Before It Attracts. When Knowlify, TMA.live, Leap, and others are all building variations on the same concept, and when the pitch generates immediate enthusiasm from everyone who hears it, I'm looking at an idea in the wrong quadrant of Thiel's diagram. The founders' absence of demonstrated resourcefulness stories compounds this -- if Lamina Labs were tackling a problem that repelled everyone else, I'd be more patient about waiting for the founder signal to emerge. But in a crowded, consensus-validated space, you need either extraordinary founder evidence or extraordinary technical moats to justify a bet. Lamina Labs has neither -- yet.
