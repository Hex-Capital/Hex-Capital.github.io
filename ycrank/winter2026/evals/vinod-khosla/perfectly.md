# Perfectly -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's what tells me everything I need to know about Perfectly: Moonhub, an AI recruiting company I backed through Khosla Ventures, was acquired by Salesforce in June 2025. Juicebox raised $30M from Sequoia in September 2025. Paraform raised $20M from Felicis. Perfect raised $23M from Hanaco. When Sequoia, Felicis, and Salesforce are all converging on the same category at the same time, you're not skating to where the puck will be -- you're arriving at a rink that's already crowded. My best investments were in spaces where the incumbents told me the idea was laughable. Cisco's CTO told me TCP/IP routing would never happen. The food industry mocked plant-based meat. Every VC on Sand Hill Road abandoned cleantech. Nobody is mocking AI recruiting. The consensus has already formed that this is a valid category, which means the consequence of success, for a late entrant with $500K, is fighting for scraps among well-capitalized competitors -- not building something category-defining.

The consequence magnitude question settles this quickly. If Perfectly fully succeeds -- completely automates the contingency recruiting agency -- what changes? Companies pay less for hires. Recruiting agencies lose margin. The hiring process gets faster. That's a real business. It's not a trivial market at $189 billion in US staffing. But compare the ambition: Impossible Foods was about reclaiming half the world's arable land. OpenAI was about building general intelligence. Commonwealth Fusion is about unlimited clean energy. Perfectly is about making it cheaper to hire software engineers at startups. I'm not dismissing the problem -- hiring is genuinely broken. But when I ask "if this works, does the world operate differently?" the answer is no. The world operates the same way, just with lower transaction costs on one intermediary service. That's the kind of outcome where the consequence of success, even at maximum execution, doesn't justify the magnitude of effort a founding team should devote to their life's work.

Three ML engineers from TikTok building a recommendation engine for recruiting is the opposite of the outsider pattern I look for. This team is applying their exact domain expertise -- recommendation systems -- to an adjacent matching problem. They know precisely what's possible and what's not, which is the bias I warn about. Victor Luo sat through 800 bad agency interviews at TikTok and built a tool to fix that pain. That's a legitimate founder origin story, and the "2x founder" signal adds some weight, but it's the story of ten thousand B2B SaaS companies. Where is the thesis that the recruiting industry's fundamental structure is wrong in a way nobody else sees? The team has deep ML chops but zero diversity of perspective -- no one from the recruiting industry to understand candidate psychology, no go-to-market founder to navigate enterprise sales, no one who brings the outsider naivety that lets you attempt what the experts say can't be done. It's a technically capable team executing a technically obvious playbook.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. Recruiting agencies face a genuine innovator's dilemma: their business model depends on human recruiters billing at 20-25% margins, so automating the recruiter would cannibalize their own economics. That's a real structural advantage for a software-first entrant. And the compounding data flywheel -- where each placement improves matching quality and candidate screens become reusable -- could create defensibility over time. If Perfectly executes faster than Juicebox, Paraform, and the dozen other entrants, and if the end-to-end integration (intake through delivery) proves genuinely difficult for point solutions to replicate, they could build a durable franchise. The TikTok recommendation expertise is directly transferable. Success-based pricing eliminates adoption friction. For this to be a great investment, you'd need to believe that workflow integration and data compounding create a winner-take-most dynamic in a fragmented market, and that this team executes better than competitors with 50-70x more capital. It's possible. But it's a bet on execution superiority in a recognized category, not a bet on a technology trajectory or a problem magnitude that justifies extraordinary risk.

The technology itself doesn't create structural obsolescence -- it applies generally available LLM capabilities (candidate matching, automated outreach, conversational screening) to an existing workflow. The dossier acknowledges this directly: "the core technical components are accessible to well-funded competitors." What Perfectly is building is an integration layer, not a technological breakthrough. When I backed Square, Jack Dorsey wasn't just making payments cheaper -- he was giving millions of merchants a capability they literally couldn't access before. Perfectly isn't giving hiring managers a new capability. It's giving them the same capability (sourced and screened candidates) through a cheaper channel. That's a business model innovation, not a technology disruption. And business model innovations without technology moats get competed away by better-funded teams.

I pass. These founders are too talented to spend their careers making recruiting agencies 50% cheaper. The problem is real but the ambition is calibrated to a pain point, not to a transformation. Go find a problem where the experts tell you it's impossible and the consequence of getting it right reshapes how an entire system works.
