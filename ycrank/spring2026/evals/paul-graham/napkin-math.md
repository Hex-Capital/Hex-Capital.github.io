# Napkin Math -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Paper Star's website lists three simultaneous consumer products — Napkin Math, "Pooping With," and "Stampede: Bay Scouts" — built by three people. That's the first thing I notice, and it's hard to get past. A three-person team building three consumer apps isn't scrappy resourcefulness; it's the absence of conviction about which problem actually matters. Consumer products are brutally hard. They require the kind of obsessive, single-minded focus where you're thinking about onboarding flows in the shower and retention curves at dinner. Napkin Math needs a team that wakes up every morning thinking about nothing but why people abandon food journals after two weeks. Instead, it has a team that also wakes up thinking about gut health tracking and location-based social exploration in San Francisco. Every hour spent on "Pooping With" is an hour not spent understanding why Napkin Math's TestFlight users do or don't come back tomorrow.

Manuel Castro (MIT, Verkada, Sonos), Claire Nord (MIT MEng, Apple OS security engineer), and Jynnie Tang (MIT, Retool, game design) form a technically credentialed team with overlapping MIT connections and shared time at Samsara. Good résumés. But résumés aren't the signal I'm looking for. When I evaluate Napkin Math's founders, I'm looking for stories — moments where they were blocked and found a creative way through. The dossier contains none. No prior exits, no narratives of scrappy workarounds, no evidence they personally performed the service their software would automate. The multi-product studio model is actually anti-evidence: instead of doing things that don't scale on one product, they're doing things that scale to zero on three products simultaneously. Jynnie Tang's portfolio of social side projects (multiplayer trivia with 12 GitHub stars, a majiang platform with 8 stars, collaborative pixel art) does show a genuine builder instinct oriented toward social consumer experiences, and Claire Nord's Rust spinlock implementation reveals real systems depth. These are honest signals of people who build for the love of building. But building side projects and building a company require different kinds of intensity.

"Strava for food" is the kind of formulation that worries me. Drew Houston didn't build Dropbox because he thought "what if we applied Strava's model to file storage?" He built it because he kept forgetting his USB drive on the train to New York. The origin story was a specific, personal frustration. Napkin Math's origin story, as far as the dossier reveals, is an analogy: take Strava's social habit-tracking model and apply it to food logging. None of the three Napkin Math founders have backgrounds in nutrition, food science, dietetics, or even consumer health. Castro comes from hardware product management. Nord comes from OS security. Tang comes from game design. Where is the personal frustration with food tracking that led them to build this? The dossier doesn't contain one, and that absence is informative — founders who discovered their problem organically almost always have a compelling story they can't stop telling. Napkin Math's team page says "stop counting calories," but I want to know which of them was counting calories, hit a wall, and built something better. The multi-product approach reinforces this concern: when you're solving your own burning problem, you don't hedge by simultaneously building two unrelated apps.

The bull case for Napkin Math is real and I should be honest about it. Cal AI built a $40M-revenue business on AI food photo recognition, then got acquired by MyFitnessPal in December 2025, removing the leading independent player from the market. Napkin Math's anti-calorie-counting positioning is genuinely contrarian — every funded competitor (Cal AI, Foodvisor, PlateLens, Noom) doubles down on precision tracking, while Napkin Math explicitly rejects it. That's the Venn diagram I like: the incumbents' entire infrastructure and brand equity are built around calorie databases and macro precision, making it structurally difficult for them to copy a journal-first approach without undermining their core product. Vision-language models crossed a quality threshold in 2024-2025 that makes food recognition accessible without purpose-built computer vision, which is a legitimate "why now." And Jynnie Tang's career arc — game design, multiplayer social apps, Comparative Media Studies — is exactly the background you'd want for someone building a social consumer experience. If Napkin Math achieves the social viral loop its "share your table" feature promises, network effects could compound quickly. ClassDojo proved that a consumer product in a sector VCs consider unsexy can reach unicorn status when execution quality is high enough.

But Napkin Math isn't ClassDojo. ClassDojo's founders were deeply embedded in the K-12 education world and building for a problem they understood from the inside. Napkin Math's founders are MIT-trained engineers and designers building a food journaling app from the outside — and simultaneously building two other apps from the outside. The anti-calorie positioning is contrarian, but "social food journal" isn't terrifying or repulsive; it's the kind of idea that makes people say "hm, that's cute" rather than "that will never work." The concept has just enough appeal that it sounds plausible without being genuinely offensive to conventional wisdom. And the behavioral adoption challenge is severe: Napkin Math must prove that an engagement mechanism without numerical goals (calories, macros, weight targets) can drive daily retention competitive with the quantified feedback loops that power MyFitnessPal and Cal AI's proven engagement models. The name collision with Every's well-known "Napkin Math" newsletter and the napkinmath.fm podcast compounds Napkin Math's organic discovery problem — a team fully obsessed with this product would have caught and resolved the SEO conflict before launching.

One verifiable condition would flip me to Invest: if Napkin Math shut down the other two Paper Star products, showed me 500+ weekly active TestFlight users with 60%+ day-30 retention, and the founders could tell me the specific personal food-tracking frustration that started this — the concrete moment, not the market thesis — I'd reconsider. The team has the technical chops and the builder instinct. What's missing is evidence that Napkin Math is the thing they were put on this earth to build, rather than one of three ideas they're testing in parallel. The dimensions that most determined this verdict are Organic Problem Discovery, where the analogy-based framing and absent origin story score well below my threshold, and Founder Resourcefulness Under Constraint, where the multi-product dilution actively contradicts the single-minded determination I need to see.
