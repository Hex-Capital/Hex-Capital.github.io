# Kimpton AI -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

AlphaSense has raised $1.63 billion and generates $500M+ in annual recurring revenue building AI-powered market intelligence for the buy-side. Hebbia has raised $161 million. Microsoft acquired Fintool in January 2026 specifically to embed agentic financial AI into Office 365. Kimpton AI is entering a space where every serious venture firm is already writing checks and competing for deals — and that's the first thing that tells me this isn't my kind of bet. When I think about my biggest wins, the common thread is that other investors were actively avoiding the space. Three hundred AngelList investors passed on Uber before I wrote my check. VCs wouldn't touch Anduril because defense tech was politically toxic. Flock Safety made civil liberties advocates nervous. Kimpton AI makes nobody nervous. "AI for portfolio managers" is the kind of thesis you can articulate at a dinner party and get enthusiastic nods from every GP in the room. That absence of discomfort is, for me, a strong negative signal.

Jack Zumwalt and Mauricio Ortiz met at a Goldman Sachs intern event roughly seven years before founding Level III Capital, their systematic digital asset trading firm that ran four years with $10M AUM. Adrian Del Bosque brought infrastructure experience from SpaceX and Vistra Corp. I want to be clear: this is real domain expertise. Four years operating a hedge fund and building internal tooling for yourself is meaningfully different from a team that read a McKinsey report and decided to build "AI for finance." Kimpton AI's founders were the user, and that matters. But when I ask my first question — tell me their story, not the business — what I hear is a career arc: Goldman Sachs analyst, Oxford computer science, hedge fund operator, productized the internal tools. That's a professional progression, not a biographical forge. Nobody here was broken and rebuilt. Nobody encountered an extractive system as a teenager and spent decades circling back to dismantle it. The problem didn't find these founders through lived adversity; they identified a market gap from inside a privileged perch. That's competent entrepreneurship. It's just not the signal I invest behind.

Kimpton AI's "Build vs. Buy" positioning frames the value proposition as saving PMs the $1–3M annual cost of hiring quants and data engineers to build comparable tooling in-house. That's a cost optimization argument — and it's a perfectly valid one for the buyers. But it's the opposite of what I look for. When I backed Affirm, the thesis was exposing credit card opacity that extracted value from consumers who didn't understand the terms. When I backed Ownwell, it was attacking the property tax system that overcharges homeowners who lack the expertise to appeal. Kimpton AI serves hedge fund portfolio managers at firms with $40B+ in assets under management — professionals who already pay for FactSet terminals, Bloomberg subscriptions, and teams of analysts. These are the most information-rich, best-compensated people in the economy. There is no hidden tax being exposed here, no invisible system being made visible, no information asymmetry being resolved for people who lack power. Kimpton AI is productivity software for the already-privileged, and that's a structural mismatch with how I allocate my own capital.

FactSet provides Kimpton AI's pricing, fundamentals, estimates, transcripts, ownership, and M&A data across 120K+ assets. Plaid connects portfolios across 2,500+ brokerages. The mandate.md and strategy.md personalization files create some switching cost. But the dossier's own Key Facts table lists moat signals as "No public data found," and the commoditization risk section states plainly that "data vendor integrations are licensable by any competitor with capital." Kimpton AI's FactSet dependency is a structural vulnerability — FactSet controls the data, could raise licensing costs, restrict API access, or build competing agentic features themselves. I've been burned by businesses without structural moats: HQ Trivia had the right product and the right timing but no infrastructure beneath the surface. Kimpton AI's 27 financial tools and four years of workflow refinement give it more depth than a typical wrapper — Lee Jacobs and I talk about rejecting "B.S. SaaS companies or AI wrappers," and Kimpton AI sits in that uncomfortable zone where the domain expertise is genuine but the structural moat is thin. Compare this to Carta, where every new cap table on the platform made switching costlier and the data network more valuable. Kimpton AI's mandate files are plain text. A PM can port their strategy to any competitor that offers a better agentic layer.

The strongest bull case an advocate would make goes like this: Kimpton AI's founders spent four years as the user and built exactly the tool they needed — this is the Slack pattern, where internal tooling at a small company becomes a category-defining product. Microsoft's acquisition of Fintool at $440K revenue validates that large platforms will pay premium multiples for agentic financial AI. The PM testimonials from a $40B+ sustainable equity fund suggest real engagement with sophisticated buyers. The team has both Goldman Sachs engineering pedigree and operational hedge fund experience, and they're in YC P26 with Aaron Epstein as partner. The AI-in-asset-management TAM is projected to reach $38.94B by 2034 at 23.76% CAGR. I take this case seriously — the founder-market fit is real, the timing is strong, and the Fintool acquisition proves acquirer interest. But I keep returning to the same structural problem: AlphaSense at $500M+ ARR has the resources, the data relationships, and the distribution to build or acquire this capability. Kimpton AI is racing to build a moat while standing on someone else's data layer, in a space where every well-funded competitor is running the same direction. The condition that would flip me to Invest: evidence that Kimpton AI has built or acquired a proprietary data asset — not licensed from FactSet but generated through its own platform usage — that creates compounding defensibility with each new PM onboarded. A network effect, not just a tool.

Kimpton AI is a competent team with genuine domain expertise building in a consensus-attractive market for already-privileged users, with licensable data integrations as the core infrastructure. The two dimensions that lock this into a Pass are Contrarian Conviction (this is the opposite of a space that makes investors uncomfortable — it's one of the most actively funded categories in venture) and Invisible System Made Visible (serving hedge fund PMs with cost optimization is not disrupting an opaque, extractive structure). I wish Jack Zumwalt, Mauricio Ortiz, and Adrian Del Bosque well — they clearly know the buy-side workflow deeply. But this isn't a Long Journey bet. My money goes to the founders whose lives left them unable to look away from something broken, building in spaces that make other investors flinch.
