# Andustry -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Andustry is that it's operating in one of the most popular investment categories of the moment. AI procurement has attracted serious capital in the last eighteen months -- LightSource Labs raised $33 million, Matchory closed a EUR 6 million seed, Cavela pulled in $8.6 million, and there are more I probably haven't seen yet. When I look at a company and see that kind of capital already deployed in adjacent spaces, my instinct is to walk the other direction. The most promising companies are not popular. They are difficult to assess. AI-powered procurement in 2026 is neither of those things. Every VC I know has seen some version of this pitch. That doesn't mean nobody wins the market, but it means the weirdness has already been priced out -- and I don't find my best investments in consensus categories.

What I'd want to understand before anything else is the founders' stories -- not the business, just how they got here. Johann Sturken grew up in a family that trades industrial goods. That's a real biographical thread, and I take it seriously. He didn't read about this problem in a McKinsey deck; he watched it at the dinner table. His mechanical engineering degrees from RWTH Aachen and TUM, his Airbus and McKinsey aerospace work, and winning a NASA design challenge -- these are impressive credentials that demonstrate ability. But I'm listening for something different than credentials. I'm listening for the specific hardship that makes someone unable to quit when everything falls apart. Het Dave is a Heinrich-Hertz scholar with SaaS transformation experience -- again, competent and smart. But nothing in either biography tells me these are the scrappy outsiders who will grind through the brutal sales cycles of industrial procurement when a well-funded competitor tries to eat their lunch. They look like exactly the kind of polished, credentialed founders that do well at Demo Day and attract a lot of investor interest -- which is, paradoxically, what makes me less interested.

The bull case is worth engaging with honestly, because there's a real insight buried here. The distinction between off-the-shelf industrial machinery and custom manufacturing is specific and potentially correct. Xometry built a $2 billion public company around custom parts with instant quoting -- but that's a fundamentally different buying motion than sourcing standard equipment. Thomasnet is a directory, not a transactional platform. If Andustry is right that nobody has built the full discovery-to-quote workflow for catalog industrial goods, and if their EUR 1M+ in beta transaction volume is real and repeatable, they could be carving out a niche that the bigger players aren't structurally positioned to serve. Johann's family proximity to this workflow means he probably understands the actual pain points -- the faxes, the three-week wait for a quote on a standard CNC machine, the opacity of pricing across borders. That's the kind of invisible system I look for, one where buyers and suppliers operate in mutual darkness and a platform could bring transparency. If they leaned hard into connecting small manufacturers in developing countries with international buyers, there's an economic access story here that would genuinely move me. But the German-language supplier examples and Munich base suggest they're starting with established European industrial trade, not democratizing access.

My core concern is defensibility. The software layer -- LLM-based RFQ generation, supplier matching, quote comparison -- is reproducible by any well-funded team. The 500K+ verified supplier claim is the thing that could matter as a moat, but nobody has verified it, and if it's aggregated from public directories like Kompass or Europages, competitors can build the same database in months. The real moat in a marketplace like this is liquidity -- getting enough buyers generating enough orders that suppliers check Andustry first. That takes years and enormous capital to build, and they're a two-person team going up against companies with ten to fifty times their resources. I keep thinking about the industrial sales cycles too: mid-size machinery is a high-consideration purchase with procurement compliance, relationship trust, and evaluation committees. That's a brutal environment for founder-led sales with no enterprise infrastructure.

I respect the domain knowledge Johann brings and the specificity of the niche they've identified. In a different market environment -- if nobody else was building AI procurement tools, if this were 2019 and they were the weird ones -- I'd be more drawn in. But this feels like a well-reasoned bet in a crowded category, built by credentialed founders from elite programs, and that pattern is almost exactly what I've learned to avoid. I need the founder's story and the company's mission to be inseparable. I need the idea to make other investors uncomfortable. I need to feel that jolt. I don't feel it here.
