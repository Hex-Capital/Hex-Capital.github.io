# InLoop Robotics -- Paul Graham Evaluation

InLoop Robotics is what happens when three robotics researchers ship hardware into a real schlep before they have any organic reason to be there -- Stepan Feduniak has been doing robot-learning since age 18, Zakariea Sharfeddine ran ML at Bosch and BMW, Pasha Rizali came out of TUM's RoboTUM, and none of them has ever picked an order off a shelf for $14/hour. The high mark on this dossier is the schlep itself; the low mark is that the founders found the schlep through a literature review, not through their own lives. That pairing is the entire analytical signature.

On the schlep dimension, the right anchor is **Coinbase** (2012) -- Brian Armstrong didn't win because bitcoin was clever; he won because nobody else wanted to eat the compliance grind, and the grind became the moat. InLoop Robotics is running toward the same kind of unpleasant work in a different costume: bimanual hardware on customer floors, 24/7 teleoperator fallback through their Safety Module, "multiple deployed robots" with zero breakage, 300+ picks/hour in paid pilots. That is not a pure-software AI-wrapper batchmate; that is somebody who agreed to show up at a warehouse at 3am when the gripper jams. Where InLoop Robotics diverges from the Armstrong analogy is that the schlep here is partly already paid for -- Dexterity has raised $291M at a $1.65B valuation chasing the same physical-world grind, so the question isn't "will anyone do this work" (Coinbase's question) but "will the well-funded version of this work crush the $500K version." Different schlep economics. Still a schlep.

The organic-idea dimension is where InLoop Robotics looks most like my **Pass Example**. The MBA-AI-wrapper team I described never personally suffered the problem they were solving; they spotted it. Drew Houston at **Dropbox** (2007) kept forgetting his USB stick at MIT -- the product was his own annoyance compiled into software. Read the InLoop Robotics dossier and the founder-pain origin is missing. The "Problem" line cites warehouse turnover of 49% annually and >100% at peak sites, which is a Fortune Business Insights paragraph, not a Feduniak-at-3am paragraph. Three robotics researchers picked warehouses because foundation models crossed a manipulation threshold around 2024-2025, not because Pasha Rizali ever packed a box. That's "spotted a market opportunity" -- the exact bottom-quartile anchor in my own rubric. It doesn't kill the bet, but it's the gap I'd press on in the interview.

Hacker genealogy compounds over 18 months only if there's a public trail to compound on. The InLoop Robotics dossier says explicitly: "No public repos found" for Sharfeddine, "No public repos found" for Feduniak, "No public repos found" for Rizali. No Twitter accounts. Feduniak's robot-learning-from-age-18 line is real hacker tell if it survives interview pressure, and Nvidia Inception is at least an institutional vouch on the ML stack. But Patrick Collison had a 41st Young Scientist prize visible in public record before **Stripe** (2010); Aaron Swartz had the RSS spec before **Reddit** (2005); the trail existed. InLoop Robotics's trail is private. Over 18 months this matters because hacker-recruiting compounds off public artifacts, and a robotics company that can't pull the top RL kids out of TUM and Berkeley loses to Dexterity's $291M paycheck.

Counterfactually, what would have to be true to flip InLoop Robotics's organic-idea score a full tier? One of three founders would need to have spent a year on a warehouse floor or running a fulfillment operation -- not researching it, doing it. That would convert the manipulation-stack pedigree into the **Dropbox** (2007)-shaped origin story instead of a market-opportunity story. The dossier does not contain that fact. If Tom Blomfield surfaces it in the interview, the score moves; absent it, I assume absence.

The cofounder configuration is the cleanest part of InLoop Robotics's dossier in single-anchor terms: three technical founders, Sharfeddine and Rizali both routed through the TUM/RoboTUM ecosystem, company founded in Germany before relocating to SF. That is structurally closer to the **Airbnb** (2009) trio -- people who were already in a shared scene before the startup gave them a reason to incorporate -- than to the hackathon-pairing red flag I avoid. I don't know the exact tenure of the relationships, and the dossier doesn't pin it, but the RoboTUM and MIRMI overlap is a real institutional substrate. I'd take this configuration at par.

Forecast, 36 months: InLoop Robotics is alive at $2-4M ARR with 6-12 deployed stations, has raised a difficult Series A in the $8-15M range from a hardware-tolerant fund, and is in active acquisition conversation with Nvidia or a tier-2 logistics integrator at a valuation under $200M -- the **Covariant** outcome ($380M Amazon acqui-hire below the prior $625M mark) is the genre, not the **Stripe** outcome. The mechanism producing that forecast is the dimensional signature: a real schlep being attacked by a team without the organic pain that produces decade-of-stubbornness, in a category where Dexterity's $291M war chest sets the floor on what "winning" costs.

The strongest bear-on-the-bull case I can make for InLoop Robotics is the **Airbnb** (2009) Known Exception -- I funded a team whose idea I had silently rejected because the founders passed the animal test via the cereal-box stunt. Could InLoop Robotics be the same shape? The bull mechanism would be: foundation-model manipulation crossed a generalization threshold in 2024-25 that the $291M incumbents trained against the old paradigm and can't cheaply re-tool; InLoop Robotics's "every failure becomes training data" flywheel from live deployments beats Dexterity's larger but staler dataset; the per-month RaaS pricing is structurally incompatible with Symbotic's multi-million-dollar fixed-DC sale, opening a real wedge. That argument stings. What dismantles it for me is the absent cereal-box equivalent -- there is no pre-funding stunt in the InLoop Robotics dossier, no Armstrong-style hand-built artifact that overrode a rejection, no organic founder-pain origin. The flip condition is specific and verifiable: if InLoop Robotics shows three signed multi-station expansion contracts (not pilots, expansions) with named Fortune-500 logistics customers inside the next six months at disclosed per-station MRR, I revise to Invest. Absent that, Neutral.
