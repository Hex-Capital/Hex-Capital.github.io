# Oxus -- Paul Graham Evaluation

SOX compliance is a genuine schlep. Every CFO at every public company knows it's painful and overpriced. Every consultant at the Big Four knows the work is tedious document review that humans do slowly and inconsistently. The annual cost is $300K to $1M+ per company, and the incumbents -- AuditBoard, Workiva, TeamMate+ -- are workflow managers, not work-doers. They organize the suffering; they don't eliminate it. That's the kind of gap where schlep blindness operates: the problem has been obvious for years, but nobody has automated the substantive audit work because it requires navigating regulatory liability, professional standards, and the conservative buying habits of Chief Audit Executives. This is a real schlep, and I respect that Oxus is walking toward it rather than away from it.

But when I ask the first question I always ask -- how did the founders find this problem? -- the answer isn't obvious. Janet Liu was at D.E. Shaw and the Federal Reserve doing CBDC research. Melinda Liu was at JPMorgan doing finance. These are finance-adjacent roles, not audit roles. Nobody on this team, as far as I can tell, has sat in a conference room reviewing SOX control documentation at 11 PM, wondering why they're manually cross-referencing evidence files that a machine should be reading. The problem is real, but the discovery path looks more like smart people scanning for large, AI-applicable markets than founders who stumbled into a personal frustration they couldn't ignore. That's the pattern I described in "How to Get Startup Ideas" -- when the idea could have been conceived by anyone with a TechCrunch subscription and an LLM, the motivation structure is different from someone who's been living inside the problem for years.

The strongest thing about this team is the co-founder relationship. Janet and Melinda are sisters who've been collaborating since high school in Florida -- co-founding a nonprofit, co-authoring economics books published by Scholastic, both attending MIT, both studying CS and economics. That's not a co-founder match made at a startup mixer. That's a partnership with years of shared context and trust, which is exactly what I mean when I say cofounders are for a startup what location is for real estate. Adding Christine Watts with an MIT AI degree and experience building ML systems at startups gives the team legitimate technical depth. All three are MIT-trained, all technically capable. The credentials are strong.

What would have to be true for this to work? The bull case is that SOX compliance automation is where payment processing was before Stripe -- an obvious problem that nobody has solved because the regulatory and enterprise-sales schlep scares away hackers, and the technical capability to automate evidence review only became possible with LLMs in the last two years. If Oxus can get even a handful of enterprise pilots, the switching costs in audit workflows are high, the pricing headroom against $200-$800/hour outsourcing is enormous, and the incumbents are structurally slow because AuditBoard and the Big Four both profit from the current manual regime. In that world, three MIT graduates with finance backgrounds and AI chops are exactly the right team to build the picks-and-shovels layer for a $4.5B market. The sister dynamic gives them founder durability that most pre-seed teams lack.

The concern that keeps me from getting excited is the "AI for X" pattern. In 2025 and 2026 I've seen dozens of companies that work like this: find an expensive, document-heavy professional workflow, apply LLMs to it, call it "AI-powered automation for [industry]." Some of these will work. But the ones that win will be the ones where the founders have domain depth that lets them encode the actual judgment calls -- not just the document processing. In audit, that means understanding which controls are material, how to interpret evidence ambiguity, what auditors actually worry about when they sign off on workpapers. I don't see evidence that this team has that depth yet. No public GitHub repos, no demo I can evaluate, no customers or pilots mentioned, no signal from actual auditors saying "I need this." The 2,290 YC Launch upvotes tell me the YC community thinks the idea sounds good. That's not the same as a Chief Audit Executive saying "when can I start using this?"

I like the schlep. I like the sister co-founder bond. I worry the idea was manufactured rather than discovered, and I see no evidence of user pull from the people who would actually buy this. At pre-seed, that's not fatal -- but combined with the lack of a visible organic origin story, it leaves me without enough conviction to write a check.
