# Clara -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

George Favvas spent ten years scaling Circle Medical (YC S15) to a $100M ARR telehealth practice, and Clara is the same operator running the same play with an LLM layered on top — a category-incumbent's second act, where the consequence ambition is genuinely large but the founder profile is the exact "domain-expert continuity" pattern my TED Talk warned against. Clara's highest-loading dimension is consequence magnitude, because "AI primary care doctor" is at least adjacent to the $15T labor displacement frame I told Fortune about in March 2026. Clara's lowest-loading dimension is founder-defies-expert-consensus, because Favvas, Caitlin Swift (ex-VP Clinical Ops at Circle Medical), and Zeeshan Ahmed (ex-Meta, ex-Circle Medical) are not contradicting their field — they are extending it.

**Impossible Foods (2011)** is the right HIGH rung for consequence: the success-state was animal protein production collapsing as a category, not "better veggie burgers." Clara gestures at the same shape — primary care visits at Circle Medical, One Medical, K Health collapsing into an AI-resident layer — and the dossier even names the incumbents Clara would have to displace. Where Clara diverges from the Impossible analog is the architecture itself: "Clara drafts, clinician signs" on every prescription refill and lab order, which structurally caps the displacement at clinician-assist rather than clinician-replacement. Impossible did not require a butcher to sign off on the patty. Until Clara demonstrates an unsupervised mode, the success-state at maximum ambition is "faster, cheaper primary care visits," not "primary care visits, as currently constructed, end."

The pass-example pattern from my reference voice — "eight years at Salesforce, now applying the same playbook with AI" — is exactly the Clara founder dossier with Circle Medical substituted for Salesforce. Favvas ran multi-state telehealth primary care for a decade; Swift ran clinical ops for the same multi-state telehealth primary care practice; Ahmed designed it. They have not publicly contradicted telehealth orthodoxy, have not pivoted from an adjacent expertise, and have not staked a "too important not to do" position visible in askclara.com. The clinical staff trained at Georgetown, UCLA, and Thomas Jefferson — paradigmatic credential signals. The UC Berkeley 28,000-prediction study I keep citing applies here: the experts who built Circle Medical are the ones telling me what AI primary care will look like, which is the prior I systematically discount.

On technical/capital risk that repels consensus VCs (mechanism analysis): Clara's $12M pre-seed from YC and A.Capital with Joe Montana's Liquid 2, SV Angel, and Immad Akhund is precisely the round any Tier-1 generalist writes in 2026 — there is no science miracle in the dossier that would have caused Sequoia or a16z to flinch. The capital compounds wrong over 18 months too: clinician labor scales linearly with encounter volume per the website's mandatory sign-off architecture, so each dollar of revenue drags a dollar of licensed-clinician cost, which is the inverse of the gross-margin curve I underwrote at **OpenAI (2019)** when I wrote $50M into a no-revenue company.

On founder learning rate (counterfactual): the **Slash (2024)** $100M Series C cleared my bar because the founder had a publicly told pivot story — the Kanye West moment — that demonstrated thesis-update under pressure. For Clara to flip from a 7/15 to an 11/15 on this dimension, the dossier would need a specific moment where Favvas publicly updated against his Circle Medical priors — a published essay arguing the visit-based model is structurally dead, a documented kill of a feature his Circle Medical team would have shipped, a pivot inside Clara's nine-month history. The dossier is silent. Ten years of linear category execution is not a learning-rate signal; it is a competence signal, which is a different axis.

Compared with **Cognition (2024)** on required-capital fit: Cognition's roadmap visibly required >$200M to train and serve a software-engineering agent at frontier capability, which is why my Series B participation made sense at the check size I write. Clara's roadmap as disclosed — 50-state licensure achieved, LegitScript certification achieved, EHR connectivity via what is almost certainly Health Gorilla or Particle — needs maybe $30-50M to reach meaningful ARR, and the two open roles (Data Engineer, Product Engineer) confirm a product-build cadence, not a foundation-model training run. I am over-resourced for the deal Clara is actually running.

My 36-month forecast: Clara reaches $40-90M ARR as a profitable D2C AI-primary-care subscription and is acquired by **Hims & Hers** (2026 guidance $2.7-2.9B revenue, already shipped Labs AI in 2026) or by **Amazon** folding it into One Medical, for $400M-$900M. The mechanism is that Favvas' Circle Medical operating muscle plus the LegitScript-plus-50-state regulatory footprint is a clean tuck-in for an incumbent that wants the AI-primary-care SKU without building the licensure stack — a respectable consumer-health exit, not a category collapse, and not a return profile that fits my fund math.

The strongest bull case sits on **Hippocratic AI** ($404M raised, $3.5B valuation per Fierce Healthcare Nov 2025) as proof the labor-displacement narrative in clinical AI is being priced civilizationally already, and on the **DoorDash (2013)** Known Exception which lets capital-light consumer plays into my portfolio when the consequence and operator quality align. The bull argues Clara is the D2C version of Hippocratic at one-thirtieth the entry valuation, run by the team that already proved they can scale a 50-state telehealth practice to $100M, and that the clinician-sign-off architecture is a temporary regulatory necessity that lifts the moment state medical boards greenlight autonomous AI prescribing. That case stings. It loses to one fact: the gross-margin advantage I need for the consequence to compound — fewer clinicians per patient over time — is currently architected against, not toward. **Verifiable flip condition**: if within 18 months Clara publishes encounter-level data showing >60% of visits resolved without clinician escalation while operating in at least one state under an autonomous-practice authorization, the unit-economics ceiling lifts and Clara becomes a labor-displacement bet rather than a labor-augmentation bet — at that point I revisit.
