# Sazabi -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's my fundamental problem with Sazabi: this is a SaaS company building software to observe software in an era where I've been telling anyone who will listen that software moats are dissolving. The "AI-native observability" category isn't some frontier bet that other investors are too scared to touch -- Arize just raised $70 million, LangSmith hit a $1.25 billion valuation, Langfuse got scooped up by ClickHouse, and Laminar came out of YC two batches ago. When I look at a deal and every serious firm in developer tools is already funding a competitor, the "insane idea" premium has been fully arbitraged. You're buying consensus at market price. Sane person? Absolutely. Insane idea? Not even close.

Sherwood Callaway is the strongest element of this entire pitch. He built the observability infrastructure at Brex -- he literally lived inside the problem. He founded Opkit, got it acquired by 11x, then helped build one of the largest agentic AI systems in production. That's a founder who has shipped, exited, and operated at the exact intersection of observability and AI agents. The a16z scout role gives him deal-flow-grade network access. The angel list reads like a who's who of the AI developer tooling ecosystem -- people from Anthropic, Vercel, LangChain, Replit. These are people endorsing Callaway personally, and that endorsement is credible. If I were scoring founders in isolation, he'd be in the top quartile of YC applicants I see. But the founder isn't the issue.

The issue is that Sazabi is walking directly into the teeth of my thesis about AI killing software businesses. The conversational debugging interface? That's a feature, not a moat -- every observability platform will ship natural language querying within a year. The "logs are all you need" thesis is intellectually elegant, but it's a software insight that Datadog's two-thousand-person engineering organization can replicate in a quarter if it threatens their business. And the real killer: the AI coding agent platforms themselves -- Cursor, Replit, Claude Code -- have the distribution AND the telemetry data to build observability directly into their products. This is exactly the distribution advantage I keep hammering on CNBC. Apple, Google, and the platforms with existing users will capture the AI value, not startups building features on top of someone else's ecosystem. Sazabi's dependency on the AI coding agent adoption curve cuts both ways -- if those platforms succeed, they have every incentive and capability to own their own observability layer.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. If Callaway can define the telemetry schema for how AI agent decisions get logged and that schema becomes a de facto standard, Sazabi transitions from application to protocol -- from dashboard provider to infrastructure primitive. That's the Airtable exception in my framework: something that looks like SaaS on the surface but functions as a new primitive that other tools build on. The SOC 2 Type I & II, ISO 27017, and ISO 27001 certifications for a ten-person team signal enterprise seriousness that most pre-seed companies lack. And the timing argument has some force: traditional observability pricing models (per-host, per-trace) create genuine business model inertia for incumbents trying to pivot to a logs-first approach. The market structural shift is real. But "the market is shifting" and "this startup will capture the shift" are very different claims. Langfuse had 20,000 GitHub stars and 2,000 paying customers and still ended up getting acquired rather than becoming the category winner. The pattern in this market is consolidation, not independent breakouts.

When I think about where my money goes, I keep coming back to my Birchbox lesson: narrative magnitude without structural moat produces the worst possible venture outcome. Sazabi has a real narrative -- AI-generated code needs new observability tools -- but that narrative belongs to the entire category, not to Sazabi specifically. There's no physical-world operation here, no protocol lock-in, no asset that gets harder to replicate over time. The "Perfect Memory" data flywheel is the kind of thing every SaaS company claims will create switching costs, and it almost never does at the scale that matters. I'd rather fund someone running painting crews with a scheduling algorithm than someone building the fifteenth entrant into a hot developer tools category. This is a pass -- not because Callaway isn't talented, but because the structural game he's playing is the one I believe AI is actively destroying.
