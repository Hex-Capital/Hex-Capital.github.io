# Oxus -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Oxus is the exact company I use as my hypothetical when explaining why AI is a bad startup investment. An AI-powered tool that automates SOX compliance workflows, sold as enterprise software, competing against incumbents with massive distribution. I don't need to construct a thought experiment here — this IS the thought experiment. The entire value proposition is "LLMs got good enough to read audit documents and generate workpapers." Which means every competitor's "why now" is identical, every incumbent's roadmap includes this feature, and the moat dissolves the moment AuditBoard ships their next product update. AuditBoard has $200M+ in ARR, was acquired for over $3 billion, and is already marketing "AI-driven insights." Workiva, a public company, has launched "Workiva AI." When I say on CNBC that software companies with moats are learning they aren't moats anymore, I'm describing the incumbents — now imagine being the startup whose only moat IS the software.

The founder-idea pairing here inverts my primary filter in the worst way. Janet and Melinda Liu are credible — MIT, Federal Reserve CBDC research, D.E. Shaw, JPMorgan. Christine Watts has an MIT AI degree and BCG experience. These are sane people. But the idea isn't insane — it's one of the most consensus plays in enterprise AI right now. "AI for compliance" is on every VC's wish list. Nobody hears this pitch and thinks it's crazy. They think it's obvious. And obvious means the opportunity has already been priced in. When I backed Coinbase, people thought Brian Armstrong was building infrastructure for fake internet money. When I backed Helium, people thought decentralized IoT hotspots were a joke. The "insane" component is what creates the non-consensus premium that produces outsized returns. Oxus has no insanity premium — it has a TAM slide and a market report showing 15.4% CAGR.

I'll give the founders one structural credit: Janet and Melinda are sisters who co-founded a nonprofit together, co-authored Scholastic books together, and attended the same high school and university. That's the kind of deep relational foundation I believe survives adversity — my own first investment was co-led with my father. But relational depth in the founding team doesn't fix the business model problem. The "cherry on top" test is binary: would this business be good if AI didn't exist? If AI vanished tomorrow, Oxus literally ceases to exist. The AI isn't on top of anything — it IS the sundae, the cherry, the bowl, and the spoon. This is the structural vulnerability I now screen for obsessively after Birchbox taught me that narrative without moat produces the worst venture outcome.

The bull case, which I want to take seriously: SOX compliance is regulated, high-stakes, and deeply specialized. Auditors face personal liability under Sections 302 and 906. This creates trust barriers that generic AI tools can't easily overcome, and a startup that earns auditor trust could build genuine switching costs. The domain expertise required to encode control testing methodologies and evidence interpretation correctly is non-trivial — you need people who understand both ML systems and PCAOB standards, which is a rare intersection. The pricing headroom is enormous ($300K–$1M+ in annual outsourcing costs), and the Big Four face a structural conflict that prevents them from building this themselves because it cannibalizes their own billable hours. If Oxus could establish itself as the trusted AI layer for audit before incumbents catch up, the regulatory complexity could actually function as a moat. That's a real argument. But it's an argument about domain specificity slowing commoditization, not preventing it. AuditBoard doesn't need to build this from scratch — they can acquire it, partner for it, or hire three auditors and five ML engineers and ship it in a year. The regulatory complexity that might slow adoption also slows Oxus's own sales cycle, and a three-person team running enterprise sales to Chief Audit Executives at public companies is a bandwidth nightmare.

There's no new primitive being created here. Oxus isn't building a protocol that other companies integrate with or a layer that generates compounding network effects. It's an application sitting on top of LLM infrastructure built by others, selling into a market defined by others, competing with tools built by others. The narrative can expand — SOX to all internal audit to all compliance — but that expansion path is the exact journey AuditBoard already completed, and they did it with workflow management before AI even existed. The ceiling is visible and the path is congested. I'd rather fund a painting company with a tech layer than a tech company whose entire identity is the layer that's about to become free.
