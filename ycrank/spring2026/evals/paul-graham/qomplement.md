# qomplement -- Paul Graham Evaluation

$200,000 spent training a proprietary language model at pre-seed, plus $60,000 per month in AWS infrastructure — that's the first number that jumps out about qomplement. At a stage where most founders are scraping by on ramen and free-tier cloud credits, Kerim Taray and Andrés Garza García are burning capital at a rate that would consume a standard YC deal in eight months on infrastructure alone. This is either a sign of deep technical conviction — founders who know exactly what they need to build and are racing to build it — or a sign of misallocated ambition, betting the company on a proprietary model that OpenAI and Anthropic might commoditize as a feature drop. I can't tell which from this dossier, and that inability to tell is itself the problem. When founders are genuinely relentlessly resourceful, the stories leak out — the Airbnb cereal boxes, the Stripe brothers personally migrating early users' payment code. qomplement's dossier gives me job titles ("former CTO from high-growth startups") and market statistics ("50% of Mexican companies use AI agents"), but no stories. The unnamed high-growth startups, the absent GitHub profiles, the placeholder website with only a company name — these aren't disqualifying at pre-seed, but they leave me with nothing to anchor a founder assessment on.

Kerim Taray describes himself as "engineer, techno-optimist, contrarian" on his personal website. I've learned to be wary of founders who self-identify as contrarian — genuinely contrarian people don't usually need to announce it; they're too busy building the thing everyone told them was stupid. qomplement's idea — AI agents that automate document processing in legacy enterprise software — is the opposite of contrarian right now. BrowserUse has 50,000+ GitHub stars building browser-level automation agents. Rossum has $114.5 million and $44.9 million ARR doing cloud document processing. Extend AI is cash-flow positive at $17 million raised. "AI agents for documents" could appear verbatim in a TechCrunch trend piece about 2025's hottest enterprise AI categories, and nobody would blink. qomplement's specific angle — OS-level rather than browser-level, local-first rather than cloud-first — is a genuine technical differentiation, but the core category is crowded consensus.

qomplement's origin story, as best I can reconstruct from Expansión and Startups Latam coverage, starts with a market observation: Latin American enterprises run on legacy systems that weren't designed for AI integration, and routine document tasks create cost and error burdens. This is accurate — and it reads like the output of a strategy analysis, not a personal frustration. The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas; it's to look for problems you have yourself. Both founders attended Tecnológico de Monterrey and have backgrounds in LatAm tech, which gives them proximity to the problem space. But proximity and lived experience are different things. Drew Houston didn't analyze the portable storage market — he kept forgetting his USB drive. I want to know: did Kerim or Andrés personally spend months entering invoices into SAP, cursing at the interface? Or did they observe others doing it and recognize an opportunity? The dossier's framing — citing Google Cloud statistics about AI adoption rates — suggests the latter. That's the tell that worries me.

The strongest bull case for qomplement centers on schlep blindness. Legacy enterprise software automation in Latin American regulated industries — transportation, healthcare, financial services — is genuinely unglamorous work that most YC founders would never touch. The sales cycles are long, the customers are conservative, the systems are ancient. This is the kind of work that makes polished Stanford CS grads recoil, which is exactly what creates natural moats. qomplement has at least one real customer: a transportation company automating 10,000+ monthly invoices with $15,000/month in documented savings (Expansión, September 2025). That's not nothing — it's a concrete deployment in a concrete industry. And the local-first architecture, running on 8-16 GB RAM machines without cloud data transfer, is a legitimately contrarian architectural choice in an era when every competitor assumes cloud connectivity. For regulated industries that can't send documents to external servers, qomplement is solving a real constraint. If I could sit down with these founders for ten minutes and hear them tell me how they landed that first logistics customer — the specific sequence of calls, demos, objections, and workarounds — and if I heard the running-back quality in those stories, the creative path-finding around obstacles, I might write a check.

But I can't sit down with them, and the dossier doesn't give me those stories. qomplement's product positioning has shifted across sources — "OS-level AI agent for legacy software automation" on Semilla Ventures, "AI Agents for Document Filling" on the YC page, "Turn invoices and POs into system-ready data" on Twitter. This might represent genuine learning and iteration, which would be a positive signal. Or it might represent a team that hasn't yet found the real problem underneath their technology. At pre-seed I don't penalize this heavily, but I note it. qomplement's $60K/month infrastructure burn against undisclosed revenue, combined with well-funded competitors like Extend AI (cash-flow positive) and BrowserUse ($17M seed, YC-backed), creates a capital efficiency challenge that requires either rapid revenue growth or additional fundraising within months of the YC batch ending.

The condition that would flip me to Invest is specific: I need to hear qomplement's founders tell detailed stories of creative workarounds — not what they built, but how they navigated the obstacles of deploying an AI agent inside a Latin American logistics company's legacy accounting system. If Kerim Taray can describe the moment their agent first failed on a real invoice and what they did in the next four hours, that tells me more than any market analysis. The decisive dimensions are Founder Resourcefulness Under Constraint, where the dossier provides almost no narrative evidence of the animal quality I look for, and Idea That Repels Before It Attracts, where qomplement is operating in one of the most consensus AI categories of 2025-2026. The schlep of the customer segment is real, but the technology approach is trending, and the combination doesn't create the kind of asymmetric bet I'm looking for with my own money.
