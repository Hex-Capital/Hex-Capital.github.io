# Apollo Atomics, Inc. -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Apollo Atomics, Inc. is a **Glean-exception-shaped consequence bet wearing an expert-continuity founder profile** — highest on consequence magnitude because a truck-transportable compact PWR at the company's published 3¢/kWh would collapse the gas-peaker regime that is currently the binding constraint on hyperscaler AI buildout, and lowest on founder-defies-consensus because Assil Halimi's 2021–2025 MIT PhD under Prof. Koroush Shirvan is the paradigmatic "researcher commercializes their advisor's lab" path that my TED Talk warned about. The right question on Apollo Atomics is not "is Halimi unconventional," it is "what economic mechanism breaks if a 10MWe reactor ships from a factory on a truck by 2030."

**Commonwealth Fusion** (2019) is the load-bearing analogy for Apollo Atomics on consequence: I led the $115M Series A when fusion was investor-toxic precisely because the success-state was the displacement of fossil baseload, not a 30% efficiency gain on top of it. Apollo Atomics inherits the same shape — a 10MWe → 50MWe → 300MWe roadmap that, if it lands, removes the diesel/gas peaker as the marginal unit behind every AI training cluster. Where Apollo Atomics diverges from Commonwealth Fusion is honesty about the physics ceiling: pressurized water reactors are not a thermodynamic miracle, they are an engineering and regulatory compression problem with 15,000 reactor-years of operating history as the base case. That makes the consequence slightly smaller than fusion (PWR fuel cycles persist; uranium logistics persist) but the probability meaningfully higher — which is the right kind of asymmetry to underwrite, not the wrong kind.

**Glean** is the failure-mode I have to stare at on Apollo Atomics's lowest dimension: Arvind Jain was ex-Google search building enterprise search — pure domain-expert continuity, which my framework normally rejects. The pattern in Apollo Atomics's dossier rhymes — Halimi did his MIT PhD on "compact iPWR steam generator + forced-circulation" design and then founded a company to commercialize compact iPWR steam generators. That is expert-extrapolating-the-past in its purest form. Two things rescue Apollo Atomics from the Glean concern: first, the underlying *thesis* — that a steam generator can be 40x smaller than the conventional unit — is a position the existing nuclear industry (Westinghouse, GE-Hitachi) actively rejects because it cannibalizes their large-PWR product lines; second, co-founder Drew Walker pivoted out of Blue Innovations Group (electric boats) and the White House into compact fission, which is the inverse of expert continuity. The Glean override applies, weakly.

Technical and capital risk on Apollo Atomics compounds over the next 18 months along one mechanism: the MIT NSE research collaboration announced April 16, 2026 either confirms two-phase flow performance in the compact steam generator at PWR conditions or it does not, and that single experimental result determines whether Apollo Atomics can credibly file an NRC construction permit application by the stated 2028 target. If the heat-transfer numbers hold, Apollo Atomics becomes fundable at $50–100M Series A scale on the **Commonwealth Fusion** template; if they wobble, the design retreats to a paper exercise and the 2028 pilot slips to 2030. Consensus VCs cannot underwrite either branch, which is exactly why the deal belongs in my book and not at a generalist Tier-1.

Counterfactually, founder-consensus-defiance would flip from a low score to a mid score if Halimi had publicly committed to a position the NRC or the nuclear establishment had rejected — e.g., advocating Part 53 risk-informed licensing for a PWR class historically licensed under Part 50, or publishing a thermal-hydraulics result that the Westinghouse/EPRI consensus called impossible. The dossier shows neither. What it does show is Halimi's MIT News profile framing nuclear as competitive against renewables on cost — which is contrarian within the climate-funding establishment but conventional within the nuclear academic community he came from. That is a half-instigator, not a full instigator in the sense of my TED Talk "12 Good Instigators" framing.

On capital profile, Apollo Atomics is a near-exact match for the check sizes I write: Aalo Atomics has absorbed $127M+ and X-energy more than $700M for comparable demonstration scope, which establishes the deployment curve. Apollo Atomics's seed cap table — Genesis Fund, Intuition, New Era, Paperjet, NSF — is a placeholder, not a constraint, and the Series A is the meaningful entry point. A $40–80M lead at Series A puts Apollo Atomics on the **Commonwealth Fusion** (2019) capital arc, not the DoorDash (2013) capital arc, which is the correct match.

My 36-month forecast on Apollo Atomics: by mid-2029, Apollo Atomics raises a $50–100M Series A led by a strategics-heavy syndicate (likely with a hyperscaler anchor — Microsoft, Google, or Amazon, given the Aalo precedent of co-locating microreactor + data center), publishes a successful MIT-collaboration heat-transfer result, but **slips the NRC construction permit application from 2028 to 2030** and has not generated reactor revenue. Not dead, not pivoted, not exited — capital-extended on the strength of the science result while the licensing clock drags. This is the **Commonwealth Fusion**-shaped path, where the consequence-of-success is large enough that capital recycles even when the timeline disappoints.

The strongest objection to my Invest lean is that Apollo Atomics is structurally a **KiOR** (~2008) repeat with the regulatory body instead of the catalyst chemistry as the failing miracle. KiOR's three required miracles — catalyst yield, feedstock economics, refinery integration — map cleanly onto Apollo Atomics's three: steam-generator thermal-hydraulics, NRC Part 50/53 licensing on a novel-architecture PWR, and capital adequacy through a 5–7 year pre-revenue window where competitors with $700M (X-energy) and demonstration sites at INL (Aalo Atomics's July 2026 cold criticality) are ahead. The bear case stings because I have lived it publicly and written the 2014 CBS letter to defend it. What dismantles the bear case is that Apollo Atomics's underlying physics is not novel — PWRs work, they have 15,000 reactor-years of operating data, and the licensing precedent exists. KiOR was three physics miracles; Apollo Atomics is one engineering miracle (the 40x compact steam generator) plus a regulatory compression. **The verifiable condition that flips my verdict to Pass: the MIT NSE collaboration publishes a heat-transfer or two-phase-flow result by end of 2027 that falls more than 25% short of the design target at representative PWR conditions.** That single data point kills the thesis; nothing else does.
