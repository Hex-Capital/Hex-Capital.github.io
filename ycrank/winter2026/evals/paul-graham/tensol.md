# Tensol -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Tensol is the direction of the pivot. They entered YC as Solstis, building AI agents for manufacturing ERPs -- a vertical problem where at least one founder (Pratik, with Rivian and Magna on his resume) had genuine domain exposure. Then during the batch they pivoted to become a managed hosting layer for OpenClaw, the open-source agent framework that happened to be the hottest project on GitHub. That pivot direction bothers me. They moved away from a specific, schlep-heavy domain toward a generic infrastructure play built on someone else's technology. In my experience, the best pivots go from broad to narrow -- Justin.tv started as lifecasting everything before narrowing to game streaming and becoming Twitch. Tensol went the other way: from a narrow domain they actually knew toward a broad opportunity they spotted because it was trending.

How did they find this problem? The honest answer seems to be: OpenClaw hit 265,000 stars and they saw a hosting opportunity. That's not organic discovery. Nobody on this team was personally struggling to deploy OpenClaw agents and thought "I wish someone would just host this for me." The idea was manufactured from market observation -- the startup equivalent of reading TechCrunch and seeing what's hot. Compare this to Stripe: the Collisons didn't read a report about payment processing being broken. They were hackers who felt the pain every time they tried to accept money online. The difference matters because manufactured ideas don't carry the same founder conviction through the years of painful execution ahead.

The schlep question is where this really falls apart for me. Managed hosting is a real schlep -- security, compliance, credential management, SOC 2 -- but it's not the *right kind* of schlep. The schlep that creates moats is the kind that structurally deters competition: Stripe dealing with banks, Airbnb dealing with trust between strangers. The schlep of managed hosting is operational infrastructure that every cloud provider already knows how to do. AWS, GCP, and Azure could offer managed OpenClaw hosting as a checkbox feature. And with OpenClaw's creator joining OpenAI and the project moving to a foundation, the governance of Tensol's entire technical substrate is now outside their control. Building your company on a dependency whose leadership just departed is a risk I'd want to see addressed with much stronger founder-problem fit than exists here.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement. OpenClaw is growing explosively -- 265K stars in roughly four months. If Tensol captures even a fraction of that community's enterprise conversion, the numbers work. The Heroku analogy is real: Heroku didn't need to own Ruby on Rails to build a billion-dollar business on top of it. ClawHub with 13,000+ skills could create ecosystem switching costs. And the founders aren't incompetent -- Oliviero shipped enterprise workflow features at Stacksync that were "used by Fortune 500s," Pratik has a CMU AI degree, and they won $50K at a serious venture competition. They can build. The question isn't whether they're capable but whether this is the right thing for them to be building. If I squint, I can see a world where Tensol becomes the default enterprise deployment layer for the dominant open-source agent framework. But that world requires OpenClaw to maintain its trajectory, no cloud provider to offer managed hosting, and Tensol to outrun multiple other managed OpenClaw implementations already launching (Kimi Claw, TrustClaw, Perplexity Computer). That's at least two miracles, maybe three.

The user pull evidence is particularly thin even for pre-seed. Fifty reviews at 4.8 stars from an unspecified source. Two customer testimonials, one of which (Stacksync) is Oliviero's former employer -- that's not independent validation. No revenue figures. No user counts. The product in its current form is weeks to months old. I'm not penalizing them for being early, but I need at least one signal of genuine organic pull -- users who found the product without being asked, who'd be upset if it disappeared. I don't see that here. What I see is a well-executed landing page for a product that might have users, in a category where the NBER found 90% of firms report zero measurable productivity impact from AI agents. That's a headwind the whole category faces, not just Tensol.

The founders can code, which matters. Oliviero started programming in 2017, attended École 42, and built production features at a YC company. Pratik has formal AI training from Carnegie Mellon. But their GitHub profiles are modest -- 22 and 11 public repos respectively, none with significant traction. They're builders, not hackers in the sense I mean when I use that word. The distinction matters because when your product is a hosting wrapper around an open-source project, the technical differentiation has to come from somewhere. The enterprise features (audit trails, credential injection, sandboxed VMs) are necessary but not sufficient -- they're the kind of features that a competent infrastructure team at a larger company builds in a quarter.

I keep coming back to the same question: would these founders have built this product if OpenClaw hadn't gone viral? The answer feels clearly no. And that tells me this is an idea that was found through opportunity recognition rather than lived experience. Opportunity recognition can work -- plenty of successful companies were built by founders who saw a gap in the market. But it's not how the best companies I've funded got started, and the competition here is fierce enough that you need every advantage you can get.
