# Asimov -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The competitive dynamics here are the most striking signal. micro1 has $100M+ ARR and just shipped Ray-Ban glasses for near-identical crowdsourced robotics data collection. General Intuition raised a $134M seed for spatial reasoning from video. Scale AI at $16.4B+ could enter this niche any quarter. Asimov is a pre-seed company with $500K and a three-person team trying to build a data marketplace against extraordinarily well-capitalized competitors already pursuing overlapping approaches. When I invested in PagerDuty, no one else wanted to fund operations alerting — that's what made it a "too boring" market. Robotics training data is no longer boring or non-obvious. The capital is already flowing, and the window for a small startup to build a defensible position is narrowing fast.

The structural inflection itself is real — I want to be clear about that. Billions flowed into humanoid robotics in 2024-2025 (Physical Intelligence, Figure, 1X, Skild AI), and the data bottleneck is becoming the binding constraint. This follows a pattern I've seen before: language models needed internet-scale text, vision models needed billions of images, and now physical AI models need internet-scale human motion data. The "why now" is specific and evidence-based — frontier robotics labs have the architectures and capital to consume massive training data, but the supply infrastructure doesn't exist at the scale they need. The downstream humanoid robot market at 39.2% CAGR creates genuine demand pull. If I'd seen this company 12 months earlier with no competitors, I'd be much more excited. The inflection point exists, but the opportunity has been recognized — and when I see a $134M seed round for a comparable approach and a $500M-valued company pivoting into the same space, that's a market that has moved from non-obvious to consensus faster than most.

What concerns me most is the buyer concentration. The addressable customer set today is maybe 10-15 frontier robotics labs. Compare this to Gusto, where the customer base is every small business that employs people, or PagerDuty, where every company running software needs alerting. When your entire revenue depends on a handful of well-funded labs, any single customer building internal data capabilities or switching to simulation-first training (NVIDIA Cosmos) can eliminate a significant percentage of your market. Customer concentration in a small buyer pool creates structural fragility that operational excellence can't fully offset. The long-term bet is that humanoid robots proliferate to hundreds or thousands of companies — and that's probably directionally correct — but the path from 15 buyers to 1,500 buyers may take five or more years.

The bull case deserves genuine consideration. If humanoid robotics achieves the scale that optimists project, Asimov could become critical infrastructure — the Checkr of physical AI, where a specialized data layer seems niche until suddenly every robotics company depends on it. The 5,000+ contributor network across three continents is real operational execution for a pre-seed company; that's not a slide deck, that's deployed logistics. Anshul Verma's Scale AI experience gives the team direct pattern-matching on data marketplace operations, and the BAIR research connection provides technical credibility. If the data moat compounds faster than competitors can replicate — if environmental diversity and annotation quality create meaningful switching costs for robotics teams — then early positioning matters. The Harvey parallel is instructive: building ahead of the capability curve can work when you're capturing market share before the wave fully arrives. But Harvey's miracle was being delivered externally by OpenAI's model improvements. Asimov's miracle — building and maintaining a global contributor network that produces superior data quality while outrunning micro1's $35M war chest — is one they must deliver themselves, and it's really two miracles: operational scale and competitive differentiation simultaneously.

The product-to-distribution trajectory is where the investment case weakens most for me. Stripe's payment API created a developer relationship that naturally channeled Atlas, Billing, and Treasury. What does Asimov distribute beyond datasets? Training-as-a-service, model evaluation, simulation environments — these are plausible extensions but don't flow naturally from a data purchasing relationship. The "marketplace" framing obscures what this functionally is: a data collection services business with per-hour contributor costs and per-dataset sales. True marketplaces have reinforcing network effects between supply and demand. Here, contributors get paid regardless of how many robotics teams buy data. The business model is closer to staffing with a data processing layer than to a platform that compounds.

I'd lean toward watching this one rather than investing. The structural timing is genuinely good, the founders show real operational velocity in building the contributor network, and the downstream market could be enormous. But the competitive landscape has already intensified beyond what I'd want to see for a pre-seed entry, the buyer pool creates concentration risk that won't resolve quickly, and I don't see a clear path from single-product data sales to a platform with distribution leverage. If the founders can demonstrate named enterprise contracts and measurably superior data quality relative to micro1's approach in the next six months, that changes the calculus.
