# 9 Mothers -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The first thing I notice about 9 Mothers is the competitive landscape, and it tells me nearly everything I need to know. Allen Control Systems has $30M, a U.S. Army xTechOverwatch win, a SOCOM maritime contract, and tripled its Austin operations -- building an autonomous kinetic C-UAS turret. Sentradel is building a nearly identical concept. Anduril has a $642M USMC contract for its interceptor approach. Epirus has $550M for directed energy. DroneShield is publicly traded with AU$216M in revenue doing electronic warfare. When I see five funded companies converging on the same battlefield problem with overlapping solutions, I see a Girardian nightmare unfolding in real time. Everyone watched the same Ukrainian drone footage, drew the same conclusion, and is now imitating each other into a contested market. Where is the secret? That cheap drones are dangerous? The Pentagon published that in its budget request. That kinetic kill is needed because autonomous drones can't be jammed? ACS and Sentradel already agree. The thesis here is consensus dressed in contrarian clothing -- "we're different because we're smaller and cheaper" is a positioning choice, not a secret about the world.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement, because the sector is one I care deeply about. Defense technology is civilizationally important -- I co-founded Palantir, I backed Anduril with the largest single check in Founders Fund history, I sit on General Matter's board. The problem 9 Mothers addresses is real and urgent. If their secret is that a truly soldier-portable kinetic system -- not a vehicle-mounted turret integrating heavy weapons like ACS's Bullfrog, but something a dismounted soldier carries -- constitutes a different category entirely, that would be interesting. A system that works at the squad level rather than the platform level would address a gap that neither ACS nor Anduril currently fills. Roman Khomenko's Ukrainian background and his "Builder of Thinking Machines" framing suggest personal motivation rooted in lived experience with the drone threat, which matters. And the team has eight years of working together at Rainforest QA, which is a genuinely positive signal -- co-founder relationships forged through years of shared execution are more durable than pitch-day partnerships. If they could demonstrate a working prototype with kill performance that ACS cannot match at a fraction of the size and cost, and if they could secure an SBIR before ACS locks up the program-of-record pathways, there would be something here.

But the evidence doesn't support that scenario today. The founders are making a dramatic domain transition -- from QA testing software to autonomous weapon systems. Russell Smith built Rainforest QA to $41M raised and $25M revenue, which demonstrates he can build and scale a company, but that company appears to still be operating under a different CEO, which raises questions about the nature of his departure. More fundamentally, none of the three founders have publicly documented experience in defense contracting, weapons engineering, military systems integration, or the specific regulatory and procurement landscape they now need to navigate. When I backed Anduril, Palmer Luckey had already sold Oculus to Facebook for $2 billion and had the kind of extreme conviction that comes from building revolutionary hardware as a teenager. Trae Stephens was a Founders Fund partner with deep defense-tech relationships. The 9 Mothers team has strong software and AI credentials -- Khomenko's computer vision work is directly relevant to the detection and tracking core -- but the gap between production ML pipelines for screenshot segmentation and autonomous weapons engagement in contested electromagnetic environments is measured in engineering lifetimes, not product iterations.

The technology question reinforces my concern. EDDA claims to be smaller, lower power, and lower cost than the Bullfrog, but there is no public evidence of a working prototype, no demonstration video, no test data, no government evaluation. The core capability -- AI-guided kinetic engagement of small, fast-moving drones -- is genuine engineering, but it is not obviously a 10x improvement over what ACS already fields. It is a positioning along the same technology vector: slightly different form factor, slightly different weapon integration, targeting the same Group 1 threat. This is the definition of 1-to-n work -- making a variation on something that already exists. The company that creates a genuinely new category of counter-drone capability -- perhaps one that makes the turret concept itself obsolete -- would have a secret. A cheaper turret does not.

The durability picture compounds these concerns. Government defense contracts can create extraordinary moats -- Palantir's integration depth with intelligence agencies makes displacement structurally impossible after a decade of deployment. But that moat requires being the incumbent, and ACS is building that incumbency now with Army and SOCOM contracts. 9 Mothers has no publicly disclosed government relationships, no SBIR awards, no testing partnerships. In defense procurement, the company that gets to the program of record first often becomes the last mover by default, because the switching costs of qualifying, testing, and integrating a new weapons system are enormous. If ACS is already inside the building, 9 Mothers may be arriving at a locked door.

I would want to be wrong about this company, because the problem it addresses matters at the civilizational level, and I have built my career backing defense technology that the consensus dismissed. But backing defense tech requires either a genuine technological discontinuity that makes existing approaches obsolete, or a founder with the kind of extreme conviction and domain mastery that bends procurement bureaucracies to their will. I see neither signal here. What I see is a competent software team entering a market where the thesis is consensus, the competition is funded and ahead, and the path to monopoly runs through a procurement system they have no demonstrated experience navigating. The mimetic dynamics are already visible -- multiple companies imitating each other toward the same solution for the same customer. This is a market, not a monopoly. I pass.
