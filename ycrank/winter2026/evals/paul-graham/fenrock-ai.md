# Fenrock AI -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Fenrock AI is what I don't see: any connection between the founders and the problem. Charu Sharma built a healthtech company that reached 6 million patients across Latin America, then built an HR tech mentoring platform that served Fortune 500 companies, then closed it. Now she's building AI agents for anti-money laundering compliance. Three different domains in sequence. That pattern tells me something. It tells me someone is searching for a market, not someone who can't stop thinking about a problem. When Drew Houston kept forgetting his USB drive, he didn't choose file sync from a menu of options. When the Collisons built Stripe, they'd been dealing with payment APIs as programmers for years. The organic backstory matters because it predicts whether the founders will still be grinding on this problem three years from now when the AI hype cycle moves on and the hard regulatory work remains.

The schlep in financial crime compliance is real — navigating BSA/AML frameworks, OFAC sanctions, EU directives, building audit trails that regulators will accept, earning trust from compliance officers whose careers depend on getting this right. That's genuinely painful work that scares people away. But here's the thing: it's not scaring people away anymore. Bretton AI just raised $75 million and has completed 1.2 million investigations. Hawk AI has $134 million in funding and 80+ bank customers. ComplyAdvantage has 3,000 enterprises across 75 countries. Flagright, also YC-backed, is already in market. This isn't schlep blindness — this is a consensus category. When I said the most impressive YC companies aren't working on AI, part of what I meant was that the best founders don't chase categories where everyone with a TechCrunch subscription can see the opportunity. Financial crime compliance + AI agents is the definition of a category that looks obvious from market research.

The strongest argument for Fenrock is Charu Sharma herself. Scaling Osana Salud to $26.5 million raised and 100 employees is not trivial. Getting Forbes 30 Under 30 and EiR at SignalFire's $2 billion fund shows she can operate in high-stakes environments. The fact that she closed NextPlay.ai and came back to start again — that's a signal I normally value. I funded Emmett Shear and Justin Kan after Kiko died. Founders who fail and return are often stronger. But there's an important distinction: Emmett and Justin came back and built something in the same neighborhood as what they'd learned from. They understood live video. Sharma is entering a domain where her prior experience offers no visible edge. Financial crime compliance buyers are notoriously conservative — they need to trust that you understand their regulatory world. A first-time entrant in AML, competing against teams with deep compliance DNA and nine-figure war chests, is fighting uphill on credibility alone.

The technical picture concerns me independently. I see no GitHub profile for the CEO, whose background is in economics, enterprise sales, and business school. The second co-founder is essentially a ghost — "Michael M" appears in some search results but has no public profile, no listed background, no title. For a company whose core value proposition is AI agents that automate compliance workflows, I want to see who's actually building the AI. When I look at YC's best companies, the pattern is overwhelming: the founders wrote the code. If neither founder can build the core product, this is a pitch deck, not a startup. Maybe Michael M is a world-class ML engineer — but the absence of any public signal is itself a signal.

For this to work, several things would need to be true simultaneously. Sharma would need to have developed deep compliance domain knowledge through channels not visible in public sources. The mystery co-founder would need to be an exceptional AI engineer. The product would need to be differentiated enough to win against Bretton AI's $75M head start. And regulated banks would need to trust a pre-seed startup with mission-critical compliance workflows where penalties for failure are severe. That's too many miracles. One miracle I can underwrite. Four is a portfolio of prayers.
