# RealPact -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

RealPact is what happens when clean-shipping Dartmouth builders -- Ranvir Deshmukh and Erik Peterson, the CourseMe team that hit 90% of one campus -- aim their next swing at single-state vertical SaaS for New Hampshire brokerages. The dimensional signature is unmistakable: highest mark on the 10-year-partner trio, lowest on network mechanics, and that pairing tells you exactly what kind of company this is. RealPact is a competent labor-automation tool sold one brokerage at a time, and the 4 named NH customers including Four Seasons Sotheby's are the proof and the ceiling at once.

Twitter, 2007 is the leverage-architecture rung I rank against, and RealPact is nowhere near it. Twitter's distribution was media at zero marginal cost -- a tweet replicates without permission from anyone. RealPact's distribution is Ranvir flying to a brokerage in Hanover, demoing Deed Agent and Tax & Vision Agent, signing a per-seat contract, then re-mapping county-registry pipelines for the next state. That is capital-and-labor leverage end-to-end. The "AI agent" framing on realpact.ai does not change what the GTM actually is -- the four logos are direct enterprise sales, not viral adoption, and the founders are not shipping an open-source library that brokerages discover. When the dossier names Dotloop (Zillow Group, ~50% share) and SkySlope (400K+ professionals) as the incumbents, what it is telling me is that the distribution game in this category is won by whoever already owns the seat -- and RealPact has to wedge into that seat through permissioned channels.

The single-sided SaaS problem is the one that should have ended this conversation before the founder section loaded. Brokermint at $400-800/mo is the rung RealPact actually sits on -- each brokerage customer is economically independent of the next, the Nth brokerage does not make the (N-1)th's experience better, and there is no compounding user-added value on the product surface. Compare to Uber 2010, where every new driver made every rider's wait shorter and every new rider made driving more economic; that is the asymmetric math that underwrote the 4,000x. RealPact's path is per-seat ARR multiplied by seat count, capped by the brokerage TAM, with no network mechanic to bend the curve. The dossier is honest about this -- no patents, no proprietary dataset, public-records scraping plus LLM autofill is "buildable by Dotloop, SkySlope, Brokermint, DocuSign."

On specific knowledge: Stack Overflow's Atwood and Spolsky were developers writing for developers. Ranvir and Erik are Dartmouth undergrads who shipped a course advisor for their own campus and now ship contract automation for an industry neither has operated inside. Founder-product-market fit at RealPact is consumer-software-for-college-kids transposed onto real estate compliance -- the dossier flags "no disclosed real estate or brokerage operating background," and the New Hampshire beachhead is Hanover geography, not domain insight. CourseMe being acquired is a builder-credibility signal, not a domain-knowledge signal. This is the "ex-FAANG PM identified market opportunity" pattern in undergraduate dress.

The pivot-room math: real estate transaction management is one vertical, and the dossier's own risk section says each new state requires re-mapping county-registry data sources and state-specific contract forms. That is the inverse of pivot-room -- it is a tax on every adjacent move. The TAM cited ($2.1B-$11B) is the wrong frame; what matters is whether RealPact can pivot twice inside seed runway when the first idea does not work, and the answer is no without rebuilding the data pipeline. Niche markets do not work because the first idea never works, and RealPact's market is shaped to punish exactly the pivot the seed thesis depends on.

The trio reads clean. Deshmukh and Peterson shipped CourseMe together and got it acquired, won the $20K Magnuson, and are not advertising their honesty -- they are just executing. I would happily spend ten years with these two. The problem is not who they are; it is what they have chosen to build. This is the inverse of the Pinterest miss -- there the architecture passed and I let aesthetic taste override the framework; here the founders pass and the architecture is wrong, and I cannot let founder-likability override that.

RealPact, 36 months out: $2-5M ARR concentrated in the Northeast, acquired by Dotloop/Zillow Group or SkySlope for $25-50M as a feature add-on, or pivoted to a horizontal AI document parser sold into title insurance or mortgage origination where the same county-registry pipeline finds a second use. The mechanism producing this forecast is the single-sided SaaS ceiling intersecting with the incumbent-distribution moat: RealPact grows linearly to a credible logo count, runs into Dotloop shipping the same AI autofill on top of 50% market share, and the exit is acqui-hire-priced rather than category-defining.

The bull case that should sting: brokers ARE builders -- they assemble deals -- and RealPact is a builder-tool for them, which would put it in the same unstated sleeve as AngelList, Notion, Replit. Three of five Perplexity gates cleared and I waived the network criterion; could I do the same here? No, and the reason is that Perplexity passed criterion #1 emphatically (API-distributed, code-leverage native) and #3 emphatically (Aravind Srinivas, Berkeley retrieval PhD). RealPact passes neither. The exception precedent requires the waived criterion to be the only failing gate, not the third. The condition that flips the verdict: RealPact ships an open public-records API that third-party developers build on without permission, turning the data pipeline into code leverage rather than a labor-served moat. Until then, pass.
