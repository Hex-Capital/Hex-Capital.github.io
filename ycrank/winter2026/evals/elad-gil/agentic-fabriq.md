# Agentic Fabriq -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The competitive landscape here tells you everything you need to know about whether this is a non-obvious market. Arcade.dev -- founded by a former Okta executive with $12M in seed funding -- is literally building the same "Okta for Agents" thesis with someone who helped build the original Okta. OASIS Security has $75M from Sequoia and Accel. Composio has $29M led by Lightspeed. Opti has $20M. SailPoint, a public company, has already shipped Agent Identity Security as a product. That's over $136M in competitive funding deployed against a $500K pre-seed company with two first-time founders. When I invested in PagerDuty, nobody else wanted to fund operations alerting -- that's what made it a non-obvious market. When I backed Harvey, most investors thought legal AI was a science project. Agent identity governance in 2026 is the opposite of that: it's a consensus-hot category where every enterprise security investor has already placed bets. The "why now" is real -- agent adoption is creating a genuine new security surface -- but it's obvious to everyone, which means the asymmetric return profile I look for isn't here.

The structural moment itself is legitimate. The shift from AI-as-chat to AI-as-agent genuinely creates a new class of non-human actors that need identity and authorization. The stat that NHIs grew 44% year-over-year and 90% of agents are over-permissioned signals an acute, growing problem. If I were evaluating agent identity as a *category*, I'd be interested -- and I have been, which is why I track it. But the question isn't whether the category will produce a big company. The question is whether this specific company has a structural advantage within the category. And I don't see one. The dossier itself acknowledges that "the barrier to building a functionally similar product is moderate" -- the underlying components (OAuth flows, RBAC, token management) are commodity. Differentiation comes down to product abstraction: admin-facing control plane versus developer-facing SDK versus security monitoring. That's a UX positioning bet, not a technology moat.

The bull case I'd want to stress-test: if legacy IAM systems are architecturally unable to handle real-time, per-action agent authorization at machine speed, then purpose-built startups have a structural window before incumbents can re-architect. Agentic Fabriq's admin-facing positioning could become the control plane that enterprises standardize on -- similar to how Okta displaced on-premises Active Directory because cloud identity required a fundamentally different architecture. If the founders execute fast enough to build integration density (connectors to enterprise tools), the switching costs compound and you get the "boring but critical infrastructure" pattern I've seen with Gusto and PagerDuty. The product-to-distribution potential exists too: a centralized agent governance layer could expand into compliance reporting, threat detection, cost management, and agent lifecycle management -- the same way Okta expanded from SSO into a full identity platform. For this bull case to work, though, two things would need to be true: (1) the architectural gap is real and wide enough that incumbents can't just add a feature, and (2) this team can win enterprise deals against Arcade.dev's former Okta exec and OASIS's $75M war chest. I don't have evidence for either.

That second condition is what ultimately pushes me to pass. Enterprise IT and security buyers are the target customer here, and those are among the hardest buyers to reach as a young, unknown team. Both founders are MIT AI researchers who dropped out before their sophomore year -- strong technical backgrounds, relevant to the agent infrastructure problem, but no documented enterprise sales experience, no prior startup exits, no advisors with enterprise distribution networks beyond YC. When I compare this to Arcade.dev's Alex Salazar, who literally built at Okta and carries the credibility that enterprise CISOs require, the founder-market fit gap is significant. This isn't a developer-adoption play like Stripe where the product spreads bottom-up; it's a top-down enterprise security sale that requires trust, compliance certifications, and reference customers. The "Okta for Agents" analogy is also structurally risky -- it names the incumbent that will compete with you. Stripe succeeded not by calling itself "PayPal for developers" but by building something architecturally distinct.

The technology cycle positioning is correct -- agent identity is a real problem at the right time. But correct timing without a differentiated structural advantage in a crowded field doesn't meet my threshold. I need to see either a non-obvious market that other investors dismiss, or a structural moat that makes this team uniquely positioned. Agent governance in 2026 is neither non-obvious nor does this team have a clear structural edge. Pass.
