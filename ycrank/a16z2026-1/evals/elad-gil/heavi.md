# Heavi -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Sanjay Dasari scaled WayCool Foods to $250M+ ARR in agricultural supply chain logistics in India, raised $366M across 28 rounds, and then went and got an ASE T8 Diesel Mechanic certification. I've backed hundreds of companies and I can count on one hand the number of founders who've built a $750M supply chain marketplace AND can personally diagnose a Cummins ISX engine. That founder profile is structurally similar to what I saw with Parker Conrad at Rippling -- an operator who already navigated the 1-to-100 transition at a prior company, now applying that scaling playbook to a new domain they understand at a practitioner level. Dasari's father ran Ashok Leyland, one of India's largest commercial vehicle manufacturers, which gives him network access into the heavy-duty vehicle ecosystem that no cold-calling founder can replicate. Michael Holkesvik brings the technical execution side -- he built NCX's satellite imagery and ML marketplace as CTO, scaling to 15,000 members. The NCX trajectory (Verra declined certification, 40% layoffs) is worth noting but doesn't diminish his marketplace-building chops. At pre-seed, this is an exceptionally strong founding pair.

The market structure question: what's changed? Two things are converging. First, the diesel technician shortage has hit a structural inflection -- 65.5% of shops reported being understaffed in 2025, with a 67,000-technician shortfall projected by 2026 per ATRI data. This isn't cyclical; training program completions (under 11,000 annually) structurally cannot fill 28,500 annual openings. Second, LLM capability crossed the threshold in 2024-2025 where agentic workflows -- multi-source search, cross-referencing fitment databases, autonomous vendor communication -- became reliable enough to deploy in production. Two years ago you could not build an agent that navigates the fragmented heavy-duty parts ecosystem with sufficient accuracy. The convergence of labor crisis plus AI capability threshold is real. Where I'm less certain: is this *nonobvious*? Heavy-duty truck repair is definitely not where prestige-seeking AI founders or consensus investors are looking -- they're chasing legal, healthcare, and finance verticals. That absence of competition is a positive signal in my framework, the same dynamic I saw with Gusto in payroll and Checkr in background checks. But the labor shortage itself is well-documented within the industry; the nonobviousness is more about Silicon Valley's blindness to blue-collar operational infrastructure than about a hidden structural catalyst.

Heavi's positioning sits in an interesting middle ground on my infrastructure-layer test. The "marketplace" label and the end-to-end procurement agent architecture suggest this could evolve into a platform layer -- if Heavi becomes the default procurement channel connecting thousands of repair shops to parts vendors, that's infrastructure with compounding network effects and switching costs. Each parts search generates proprietary data on pricing, availability, fitment accuracy, and vendor reliability. Over time that data advantage compounds, similar to how Stripe's transaction data became a moat. But right now, Heavi doesn't own the underlying data layer -- it searches across OE systems, aftermarket catalogs, and ecommerce sites it doesn't control. The parts data access dependency is a real structural risk. If FleetPride or TruckPro decide to restrict access, or if Fullbay (5,000+ shops, $19.4M revenue, fresh Pitstop AI acquisition) builds procurement into their existing ERP, Heavi faces a feature-vs-company challenge. The defensibility depends entirely on speed of data accumulation and depth of vendor integrations -- domain complexity is the moat, not proprietary infrastructure.

The single-miracle test is favorable. The core technology miracle -- AI capable of reliable agentic parts procurement across fragmented databases -- is happening exogenously as foundation models improve. Heavi's execution challenge is domain-specific application: building accurate fitment matching, cross-referencing thousands of OEM part numbers, and automating vendor communication. That's hard engineering but not a breakthrough requiring novel research. Distribution into fragmented, traditionally tech-averse repair shops is a concern, but $100,000 in contracted revenue from 5 design partners with reported 30% employee time savings and ~40% margin improvement suggests the willingness-to-pay miracle has already been demonstrated at small scale. One primary miracle remains: does the AI work reliably enough at scale that shops trust it for procurement decisions where the wrong part means costly vehicle downtime? The margin architecture is speculative at pre-seed -- no pricing page exists -- but the marketplace model implies a take-rate on parts transactions that could scale with GMV rather than headcount, which aligns with the "seats to cognition" pricing evolution I look for.

The strongest bear case: Fullbay's March 2026 acquisition of Pitstop signals that the incumbent is moving toward AI-embedded shop operations. With 5,000+ shops already on its platform, Fullbay has the distribution Heavi lacks and could build procurement AI as a feature of its existing ERP. If Heavi is competing against Fullbay's distribution advantage with only 5 design partners, the window may be narrow. My counter: Fullbay is architecturally a workflow management company. Building an autonomous procurement agent that searches across fragmented data sources, contacts vendors, and coordinates ordering is a fundamentally different technical problem than managing invoices and scheduling. Bolt-on AI features rarely match purpose-built agentic systems -- the same reason Harvey beat legal AI features from existing legal tech companies. Heavi's agent-first architecture should allow faster iteration on the core procurement problem. But I'd want to see Heavi expand from 5 to 50 shops within six months to confirm the distribution challenge is surmountable before writing a follow-on check.

The dimensions that lock my verdict are Operator-Scaler Founder Profile and Nonobvious Market Timing. Dasari's combination of $250M+ ARR scaling experience and ASE T8 domain certification is the single strongest founder signal in this evaluation -- it directly maps to my pattern of operator-founders who experienced hypergrowth and are now solving problems they understand at a practitioner level. The market timing is solid but not exceptional: the AI capability threshold and labor crisis convergence is real, but the nonobviousness is more about Silicon Valley's neglect of blue-collar infrastructure than about a hidden structural shift. Heavi fits my "boring but critical" infrastructure pattern -- the Gusto parallel -- where absence of prestige-seeking competition is itself a competitive advantage. At $100K contracted from 5 partners, there's enough early signal to write a check, but infrastructure-layer positioning needs to be proven as Heavi scales.
