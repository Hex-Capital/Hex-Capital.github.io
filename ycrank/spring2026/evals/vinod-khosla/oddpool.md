# Oddpool -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Oddpool is a two-engineer data broker normalizing Kalshi and Polymarket feeds at $30-$100/month, and that single fact is the whole signature: competent infrastructure pedigree (Avi Arora out of Microsoft Prompt Shields, Ritesh Malpani out of Bloomberg) pointed at a narrow institutional data niche that opened up because Polymarket acquired Dome in early 2026. The highest dimension here is the founders' engineering credibility; the lowest is the ambition itself. That pairing tells me what kind of bet Oddpool is — a market-structure arbitrage, not a consequence explosion. Bloomberg-for-X is a fine business; it is not a Khosla business.

Consequence Magnitude is where Oddpool falls hardest against my framework. The HIGH rung on this ladder is OpenAI — I wrote a $50M check and an apology letter to my LPs because if AGI works, civilization changes. The MID rung is DoorDash, where the consequence was food access for hundreds of millions, planetary in scale. The LOW rung is the YC company building an AI copilot for accountants from my Pass Example — useful, incremental, substitutable. Oddpool sits between LOW and MID and closer to LOW: even in a fully-successful scenario where Oddpool becomes the definitive cross-venue prediction-market data layer, the population whose lives change is quant traders and macro desks at prop shops — measured in thousands, not millions. The dossier itself anchors the user base in "institutional flow" and "Kalshi ~40% institutional" — that's the ceiling, not the floor. If Oddpool fully works, the world looks identical except a few dozen trading firms have better dashboards.

Improbable Ambition Level is the other floor. The HIGH rung is Commonwealth Fusion Systems — I funded fusion because the world said it was twenty years away and the founders said no. The MID rung is Rocket Lab, where Peter Beck attempted small-sat launch when consensus said SpaceX had won. The LOW rung is the YC company solving a known problem with a well-understood approach. Oddpool's stated end-state — pm.wiki literally calls it "the Bloomberg for prediction markets" — is the opposite of improbable. No expert in market-data infrastructure looks at Oddpool and says "that can't be done." They say "Dome already did it, and Polymarket bought them." That's not improbable ambition; that's filling a vendor gap. The pricing page itself — $30/mo Pro, $100/mo Premium — telegraphs a SaaS metering business, not a category-defining swing.

Non-Linear Technical Approach. The HIGH rung is Rocket Lab's Rutherford engine — electric turbopumps and 3D-printed combustion chambers, a step-function on cost. The MID rung is QuantumScape's solid-state cell — real science risk, not just engineering. The LOW rung is the LLM wrapper company. Oddpool is normalizing two public REST/FIX APIs and storing the orderbook history. The 1-5 minute granularity archive back to March 2026 is a real compounding asset — once accumulated, no later entrant can reconstruct it — but that's a data-moat, not a technical leap. FinFeedAPI and pmxt and Prediction Hunt v2 already exist; the dossier flags Prediction Hunt v2 covers five venues to Oddpool's two. Oddpool sits firmly between LOW and MID, on the LOW side.

Founder as Learning Machine is Oddpool's highest dimension and the one place I lean in. The HIGH rung is the 19-year-old Mach Industries dropout building weapons systems — pure outsider velocity. The MID rung is Peter Beck, self-taught New Zealand engineer. The LOW rung is the founder citing a ten-year resume with scripted answers. Avi Arora and Ritesh Malpani are competent engineers — Bloomberg + Amazon trading infra at 100K TPS, Microsoft Prompt Shields at 20B classifications/year, both Georgia Tech — but the more interesting signal is that the dossier flags Oddpool as a recent pivot from Benchspan inside the same P26 batch. A pivot inside YC is a learning signal; that's exactly the rate-of-change I look for in a three-month window. But they pivoted from one narrow B2B tool (prompt-injection detection) to another narrow B2B tool (prediction-market data normalization). Pivot velocity, yes; pivot ambition, no. They sit MID-LOW.

Path to Radical Affordability is where Oddpool actively contradicts my deepest unstated pattern. The HIGH rung is Impossible Foods making protein cheaper than animal agriculture, or Curai delivering AI primary care at near-zero marginal cost. The MID rung is DoorDash compressing delivery economics. The LOW rung is enterprise SaaS pricing as value-capture rather than cost-collapse. Oddpool's pricing page is exactly that — $30, $100, "Enterprise custom." It is selling expensive data to wealthy traders. Nothing about Oddpool makes a basic human necessity 10x cheaper. The affordability axis is pointed the wrong direction.

My 36-month forecast: Oddpool gets acquired by an exchange (Kalshi being the most natural buyer) or a financial data vendor (Bloomberg, Refinitiv, or a CoinAPI-style consolidator) within 24 months at $5-40M, or settles into a $500K-$2M ARR profitable data-vendor lifestyle business. The mechanism is precisely the precedent the dossier names: Polymarket bought Dome to absorb the neutral aggregator; Kalshi has equal incentive to do the same to Oddpool, and the founders' Bloomberg/Microsoft pedigrees make the acqui-hire trivially priced. This is not a binary $50x option. It is a $1-3x option dressed up as a startup.

The bull case worth steel-manning: prediction markets are a Tetlock-scale civilizational forecasting layer in waiting. If $44B in 2025 becomes $500B by 2030 and event contracts replace polling, news, and meaningful slices of insurance underwriting, then the neutral data layer underneath that becomes Bloomberg-scale — and Bloomberg is a $60B revenue business. Under that scenario Oddpool's compounding orderbook archive from March 2026 is the moat, and the Dome acquisition cleared the field. I don't dismiss this — prediction markets as decision infrastructure is a thesis I take seriously. But the bull case still routes through "Oddpool becomes a great data vendor," not "Oddpool changes how a billion people live." Even at Bloomberg-scale the consequence magnitude tops out at financial-industry productivity, which is exactly the share-fight-in-existing-budget pattern my Red Flags call out. The flip condition is concrete: a signed enterprise contract with a tier-1 quant fund (Citadel, Jane Street, Two Sigma) at $250K+ ARR by month 12, plus coverage expansion to five-plus venues demonstrating Oddpool is building category infrastructure rather than a Kalshi/Polymarket point tool. Until then, this is a pass.
