# matforge -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Akash Ramdas co-authored a paper in *Science* in January 2025 on niobium phosphide as an ultrathin conductor — 38 citations and counting — and the YC page claims materials he discovered during his Stanford PhD have been adopted into Intel and TSMC interconnect roadmaps. If that second claim is real, matforge has something almost no pre-seed company has: a founder whose work product has already been validated by the two most important semiconductor manufacturers on earth. That's not a pitch deck claim about "potential partnerships." That's a PhD student whose research actually changed what Intel and TSMC plan to build. The claim is unverified outside the YC page, which I note, but if true it represents exactly the kind of founder-domain relationship I look for — not someone who studied a market, but someone who was already producing the output the market consumes.

Matforge's origin story has the structure I trust most. Akash spent years in Stanford's Materials Computation and Theory Group, published 8 papers (101 citations, h-index 5 per Google Scholar), and screened 15,000+ materials for interconnect applications in his Wiley Small first-author paper. He didn't sit down to brainstorm startup ideas. He lived inside the 10-year discovery timeline, experienced its slowness viscerally, and is now building the tool that compresses it. This is the Dropbox pattern — Drew Houston forgot his USB drive, Akash spent a PhD watching computational materials science crawl — except matforge's version has even more domain depth behind it. Advaith Sridhar complements this with production AI engineering: founding applied scientist at Persona AI building autonomous agents, company acquired by Luma Labs, CMU's AI program, 377 GitHub stars on WhatsApp-Llama. The pairing — deep domain scientist plus shipped AI engineer, best friends from IIT Madras — covers both halves of what matforge needs to build. Neither founder chose this problem from a trend report.

Matforge's semiconductor focus is the right kind of narrow. CuspAI raised $100M at a $520M valuation building a broad "search engine for molecules" — that phrase alone tells me they designed the company to sound good in a pitch. Orbital Materials raised $200M at $1.2B for cleantech sorbents and catalysts. Both are well-funded and credentialed. But matforge chose the harder, less pitch-friendly path: only semiconductors, only datacenter and fab applications. Selling novel materials to TSMC means multi-year qualification cycles, PCI-level regulatory scrutiny, and customers who will test your material to failure before considering adoption. Most founders hear "multi-year enterprise sales cycle with 5 potential customers" and their unconscious steers them toward something easier. That's schlep blindness operating as intended — the difficulty repels competitors, including the billion-dollar ones who would need to hire matforge-level domain experts to pivot into semiconductors. The risk is real though: CuspAI nearing $1B valuation with 100-500x more capital could decide to enter semiconductors by acqui-hiring a materials science team. Matforge's defense is that semiconductor materials discovery requires more than model architecture — it requires fabrication-compatibility constraints, multi-objective optimization under physics constraints, and relationships with fab process engineers. Akash's claimed Intel/TSMC roadmap contributions, if verified, represent exactly this kind of hard-to-replicate domain integration.

The bull case for matforge echoes Bindwell — my investment in two teenagers using AI for pesticide discovery, where I personally advised them to pivot from selling tools to owning the molecules. Matforge has deeper credentials (Stanford PhD and Science paper versus high school students), a larger addressable market ($72-81B semiconductor materials versus pesticides), and the same schlep pattern: an unsexy, compliance-heavy domain where AI-first approaches can compress decade-long timelines. If I backed Bindwell's teen founders, the argument goes, matforge's more experienced team tackling a bigger market should be a stronger bet. Machine-learned interatomic potentials reaching benchmark quality (Nature Machine Intelligence, 2025) creates a genuine "why now." The convergence of foundation models, AI chip power consumption doubling annually, and advanced packaging materials growing at 11.8% CAGR gives matforge real demand-side urgency. Advaith has shipped before — Persona AI was acquired — and Akash's research has reached production roadmaps. These are builders, not just researchers.

What holds me back from a full Invest conviction is that I have no stories. Matforge's dossier gives me credentials and publications but zero behavioral evidence of the "animal" quality — no account of hacking through obstacles, no creative workaround when blocked, no do-things-that-don't-scale narrative of personally performing the service before automating it. Resourcefulness under constraint is my most heavily weighted signal at 30 points, and matforge provides nothing to score it on beyond the baseline inference that someone who gets a Science paper published and influences fab roadmaps is probably not passive. That's something, but it's not the cereal-box story. I also have no traction — no revenue, no LOIs, no users, no prototype demo, no public presence beyond the YC page. At pre-seed I don't penalize this as a stage default, but it means I'm evaluating matforge almost entirely on founder credentials and problem-domain fit. Those are strong, but they're not sufficient for me to write a check based on this dossier alone. The condition that would flip me to Invest: a single verified pilot or LOI with a named semiconductor manufacturer, or a concrete story from the founders about how they acquired their first design partner through some creative, unscalable hustle. Either would confirm the behavioral quality that the credentials currently only suggest.

Matforge scores highest on organic problem discovery — Akash's PhD is as pure an example of "live in the future, then build what's missing" as I've seen at pre-seed — and lowest on demonstrated resourcefulness, where the dossier simply gives me nothing to evaluate beyond a modestly positive prior. The company sits in the territory where the domain fit is genuinely compelling but the founder-behavior evidence is insufficient to push past Neutral. Semiconductor materials is exactly the kind of schlep-heavy, relationship-intensive domain that creates natural moats, and matforge's founders have the right technical depth to operate in it. But depth of knowledge and depth of determination are different things, and at this stage I can only verify the first.
