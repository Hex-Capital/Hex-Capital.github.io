# Gojiberry AI -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Pierre-Eliott Lallemant is co-founding two YC Spring 2026 companies simultaneously -- Gojiberry AI and Standout. That fact alone tells you something about commitment density. I've said repeatedly that the best founders are missionaries, not mercenaries, and splitting yourself across two venture-backed companies during the same batch is the opposite of missionary. But let me set that aside because the structural problem with Gojiberry AI is much bigger than founder attention. The structural problem is that this is the single most consensus category in B2B SaaS right now. AI SDR tools. LinkedIn outreach automation. Intent-based lead scoring. Every demo day I attend, I see five of these. Artisan raised $46.1M doing essentially the same thing. Amplemarket raised $12M. Salesforge, Valley, Persana -- the dossier lists five direct competitors and I'd bet there are fifty more. When every investor in the room is nodding along, the upside is already in the price. There is zero mispricing here. The risk is not baked into the price -- it's hidden behind consensus enthusiasm.

Gojiberry AI at $99/month with 1,000+ paying customers and $1.4M ARR in nine months -- I'm not going to pretend that's not real execution. It is. Bootstrapped, no less. That's the kind of capital discipline I respect. The CoCo AI exit, Edusign's 600K+ users -- these are founders who've built revenue before without needing a VC to hold their hand. If you told me Pierre-Eliott, Román, and Dylan were building something in a mispriced category with structural durability, I'd lean in hard. But capital discipline is necessary, not sufficient. You can bootstrap your way to $1.4M ARR selling something that has no moat and still end up dead. The question isn't "can they execute?" It's "does execution matter when LinkedIn can build this feature next quarter?"

Gojiberry AI fails the AI kill test more completely than almost any company I've seen this year. The entire product monitors LinkedIn signals and auto-generates personalized LinkedIn messages. That's it. That's the product. LinkedIn itself could add intent-based outreach scoring tomorrow -- they have all the data, they own the platform, and they have every incentive to monetize it directly rather than let third-party tools arbitrage their API. When I backed Metropolis at seed, the thesis was that AI amplifies the parking infrastructure but cannot replace it. Physical garages don't get commoditized by a better model. Gojiberry AI has the exact opposite structure -- it's pure software sitting on someone else's platform, wrapping someone else's data, using models that are getting cheaper by the month. Salesforce, HubSpot, ZoomInfo -- any incumbent with a sales product and an AI team is looking at Gojiberry AI's feature set and thinking "we should add that." The Salesforge review even notes Reddit complaints about spam behavior, which means LinkedIn policy enforcement is a live existential risk.

Gojiberry AI is not creating a new primitive. "Monitor intent signals and send personalized outreach" is a workflow optimization, not a new behavior. Nobody is going to say "Gojiberry me" the way they say "Venmo me." Venmo created peer-to-peer mobile payments as a behavioral category that didn't exist before. Gojiberry AI is making an existing behavior -- LinkedIn prospecting -- somewhat more efficient. That's a feature, not a company. The 30+ signal taxonomy and ICP scoring are execution differentiators, not category creation. Five years ago you could have described exactly what Gojiberry AI does; the only thing that changed is that LLMs made the message generation cheaper. When the enabling technology gets cheaper, the application layer commoditizes. Protocols compound. Applications compress.

The bull case an advocate would make: three repeat founders with exits, $1.4M ARR bootstrapped in nine months, 44% month-over-month growth, 1,000+ paying customers, #1 Product of the Day on Product Hunt, strong GTM engine with multi-channel distribution. They'd point to the focused LinkedIn-native positioning versus Artisan's broader multi-channel approach and argue that depth beats breadth in early markets. They'd note the compounding optimization flywheel -- customer-specific models that improve with usage create switching costs. And they'd argue that the small-team focus ($99/month) is a wedge the well-funded competitors are ignoring because it's too downmarket for their economics. I've checked my Known Exceptions: Slack overrode consensus pricing because it was genuinely creating a new behavioral primitive. Gojiberry AI is not creating a new primitive -- it's optimizing an existing workflow in a crowded category. The exception doesn't apply. The traction is real but the traction reflects market demand for the category, not a durable moat for this specific company. At $99/month with SMB customers, churn will be brutal, and the next LinkedIn policy change could crater the business overnight.

The one condition that would flip me: if Gojiberry AI pivoted to owning the data layer -- building a proprietary intent graph from cross-platform signals that isn't dependent on LinkedIn's API, creating something that looks more like a protocol than an application. That would change the structural durability calculus entirely. But based on what's in front of me, Gojiberry AI is the archetype of what I pass on: a consensus-hot AI application-layer tool, pure software with no physical or protocol moat, sitting on someone else's platform, in a category where the incumbents will eat the value. The capital discipline is genuinely impressive and the founders clearly know how to build. Wrong company for my framework. Pass.
