# Andustry -- Elad Gil Evaluation

€1M+ in transaction volume during beta, achieved by a two-person team across 100+ countries -- that's the number that made me look twice at Andustry. For a pre-seed company, that's real throughput, not a demo. But the number that actually matters for my framework is different: Xometry generated $687M in revenue in FY2025, Zip has raised hundreds of millions for AI procurement, Fairmarkit was acquired by Coupa, and Leverage AI is actively funded. "AI agents for B2B procurement" is not a market that consensus has missed -- it's a market where consensus arrived two years ago. When I backed Harvey in early 2022, legal AI had been a graveyard for a decade, and the structural shift (GPT capability crossing the accuracy threshold for legal precision) was invisible to most investors still burned by prior failures. Andustry's structural catalyst -- LLMs enabling autonomous supplier discovery and quote normalization -- is real, but it's the same catalyst powering a dozen well-funded competitors. The cross-border SME focus adds geographic differentiation, but targeting European SMEs rather than North American enterprises doesn't constitute a nonobvious market window; it's a geographic segmentation of an obvious trend.

Johann Stürken's profile deserves specific attention: mechanical engineering from RWTH Aachen, McKinsey consulting, Airbus operations experience, and a family background in trading goods and supplier sourcing. That's genuine domain density -- the kind of founder-market fit where the problem was discovered through lived experience, not market research. Het Dave provides the CS/engineering complement from IIT Jodhpur. Andustry's founding team mirrors the pattern I've seen work in vertical B2B: one founder who has personally felt the procurement pain, one who can build the technical system. But neither founder has operated at a company during its scaling phase. Johann's McKinsey and Airbus stints were advisory and corporate, not startup scaling from 10 to 1,000 employees. At pre-seed I don't penalize this heavily -- I've seen plenty of first-time founders execute well when the market is strong enough -- but the absence of scaling experience combined with the ambition of operating across 100+ countries with cross-border compliance raises an execution flag that domain expertise alone doesn't resolve.

Andustry's positioning against Xometry reveals a genuine structural wedge that a generic analyst might overlook: Xometry's business model as manufacturer-of-record creates channel conflict that prevents them from easily replicating a pure sourcing marketplace. Andustry matches buyers to independent suppliers rather than manufacturing the parts itself, which is architecturally incompatible with how Xometry generates revenue. I've seen this dynamic before -- incumbent channel conflict is one of the most reliable structural advantages a startup can exploit. But here's where my infrastructure-layer test matters: Andustry isn't building a platform that other businesses integrate into their core workflows via API. It's a transactional marketplace. Stripe became the payments API that thousands of companies built their checkout flows on, creating compounding switching costs. Gusto embedded so deeply into payroll workflows that ripping it out means rebuilding your entire HR operations stack. Andustry's marketplace creates transaction-level value, not workflow-level dependency. The 500k+ verified supplier network could evolve into proprietary data infrastructure -- better matching accuracy with each transaction -- but today that claim is unverified. How many of those 500k companies are actively responding to RFQs versus passively listed from a scraped database?

Andustry's single-miracle assessment lands in an uncomfortable middle zone. The technology miracle -- LLMs capable of autonomous supplier matching and quote negotiation -- is happening exogenously, which is favorable (the same dynamic that made Harvey's technology miracle already underway). But Andustry also needs marketplace liquidity: sufficient active supplier engagement density across enough industrial categories and geographies to make the buyer experience consistently better than emailing five known suppliers directly. That's a second, independent miracle. Two-sided marketplaces are inherently multi-miracle businesses at the cold-start phase. And operating across 100+ countries for physical goods adds regulatory and logistics complexity -- import/export compliance, tariffs, product liability -- that compounds the execution surface area well beyond what a two-person team can manage systematically.

The margin architecture is where Andustry most clearly diverges from the companies in my portfolio that I'm most excited about. Andustry monetizes via a take-rate on completed physical goods transactions -- no subscriptions, no listing fees, pay-on-delivery only. Industrial parts procurement operates on margins of 5-15% typically. If Andustry's take-rate is 3-5%, that represents a substantial chunk of the supplier's profit, creating immediate pressure to disintermediate the platform once a buyer-supplier relationship is established. Compare this to my investments in SaaS infrastructure: Gusto charges subscription fees for payroll software with 70%+ gross margins and negative net revenue churn because employers add employees over time. Andustry's model looks structurally more like Wish/ContextLogic -- a marketplace for physical goods with thin take-rates, cross-border fulfillment complexity, and no pricing power to raise rates without losing transaction volume. Wish collapsed 99% post-IPO precisely because marketplace take-rates on physical goods provide no structural protection when growth velocity declines.

The strongest bull case an advocate would construct: Andustry attacks a genuinely boring, unglamorous category (industrial parts sourcing for SMEs) where prestige-seeking founders don't compete -- my Gusto/Checkr pattern. Johann Stürken's family trading background gives him authentic domain knowledge that most AI-procurement founders lack. The €1M+ beta transaction volume proves real buyer willingness. Xometry's channel conflict creates structural space. And European SME procurement is legitimately underserved compared to North American solutions. If Andustry's supplier network achieves genuine liquidity and the AI matching improves with transaction data, it could become the default sourcing layer for mid-market European manufacturers -- a $50B+ addressable market with a first-mover data advantage. I've checked my Known Exceptions: the Anduril override (overwhelming market structure overrides multi-miracle concerns) doesn't apply because the market timing here isn't a generational structural shift -- it's a consensus AI application wave. The condition that would flip me to Invest: evidence that Andustry has achieved measurable supplier response rates (not just listings) above 40% on buyer RFQs across at least three industrial categories, combined with a demonstrated path to 15%+ gross take-rate that suppliers accept because the AI-driven demand generation justifies the fee. That would prove both marketplace liquidity and margin viability simultaneously.

Andustry lands at Pass, driven primarily by two dimensions. Nonobvious Market Timing scores low because "AI agents for procurement" is consensus, not contrarian -- the structural shift is real but already recognized and crowded with funded competitors including public companies. Margin Architecture scores low because a physical-goods marketplace take-rate in a thin-margin industry lacks the pricing power and retention dynamics I require -- there's no mechanism for Andustry to raise prices without losing transactions, and buyer-supplier relationships naturally disintermediate once established. The founders have genuine domain depth and the transaction volume is promising, but promising execution in a consensus market with structurally limited margins isn't where I deploy capital from my own fund.
