# Oximy -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The core technology is a fork of mitmproxy. That fact alone tells me most of what I need to know about defensibility. When your "sensor" — the component that does the actual work of intercepting network traffic and identifying AI tools — is an open-source proxy that any competent engineer can deploy, you don't have difficult technology compounding over time. You have a product wrapper around commodity infrastructure. CrowdStrike already has agents on hundreds of millions of endpoints monitoring network connections. Zscaler sits inline on enterprise traffic. For either of them, adding an AI tool detection taxonomy is a sprint, not a product. When I invested in Stack Overflow, the moat was a knowledge graph that got richer with every question and answer — something that couldn't be replicated by adding a feature to an existing product. Oximy's 3,500-tool taxonomy is a spreadsheet, not a knowledge graph. It's maintainable by a team of interns reading Product Hunt.

The specific knowledge question is where this falls apart for me. Naman Ambavi is clearly high-energy — multiple startups by 25, Google coding competitions, a COVID tracker that reached 10M users. But energy without specific knowledge is execution speed in the wrong direction. His exposure to enterprise AI governance came from being a founding member at Induced AI, where he was building AI tools, not governing them. That's adjacent knowledge. He didn't spend years as a CISO watching employees leak sensitive data into ChatGPT. He didn't build DLP systems at Symantec. He didn't write the endpoint agent architecture at CrowdStrike. The insight that "enterprises have dozens of AI tools with zero visibility" is available to anyone who reads a Gartner report. When I backed Evan Williams, he understood something about human communication that couldn't be extracted from market research. What does Ambavi understand about enterprise security procurement, about the psychology of CISOs, about the politics of endpoint agent deployment, that a smart generalist couldn't learn in six months? The dossier doesn't surface anything.

The timing is consensus, which is the opposite of where I make money. Enterprise AI governance is the category everyone agrees is important right now. Nudge Security raised $22.5M in November 2025 for exactly this problem. Market research firms project 39.4% CAGRs. Seventy-five percent of large enterprises plan to adopt AI governance platforms by 2026. When every analyst and every CISO agrees that something is needed, the pricing already reflects optimism. My best returns — Twitter, Uber, Bitcoin — came from betting when smart people thought the idea was foolish. Nobody thinks enterprise AI governance is foolish. They think it's inevitable. Inevitable is a different word for "priced in."

The bull case would require two things to be true simultaneously: (1) that the desktop agent approach represents an architectural wedge that agentless competitors structurally cannot replicate, and (2) that the data generated from monitoring enterprise AI usage across many customers creates a proprietary intelligence layer — aggregate benchmarks, anomaly detection, cross-enterprise usage patterns — that compounds into something defensible. If Oximy became the Plaid of AI usage data, sitting at the intersection of every enterprise's AI stack and providing intelligence that no individual deployment could generate, that would be a leverage architecture worth funding. The claim of "processing millions of requests daily for enterprises in financial services and healthcare" suggests real deployment. But the path from monitoring tool to intelligence platform is long, and CrowdStrike can walk that path with a much larger dataset from day one. The solo founder trying to simultaneously build enterprise security product, run MDM deployments at healthcare companies, achieve SOC 2 compliance, and execute enterprise sales cycles — that's a structural challenge, not a temporary gap.

I'd pass. The founder has energy and execution velocity, but the specific knowledge bar hasn't been met for this particular domain. The technology is commodity. The timing is consensus. The leverage is linear — per-seat, per-endpoint, enterprise sales motion with long procurement cycles. And the competitive moat is a catalog, not a compounding system. This is a feature that will live inside CrowdStrike or Zscaler within eighteen months, not a platform that creates a new form of leverage that didn't exist before.
