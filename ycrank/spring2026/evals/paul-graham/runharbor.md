# Harbor -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that jumped out at me about Harbor isn't what you'd expect. It's the schlep. Clinical trial EDC systems — electronic data capture for FDA-regulated studies — might be the single most compliance-burdened software category I've encountered. You need 21 CFR Part 11 validation, immutable audit trails, HIPAA, GDPR, eSignatures, role-based access controls. Every feature ships with a documentation burden most engineers have never experienced. And two people built this. That's the signal. Not the AI angle — everyone is doing AI. The fact that Albert and Nathan chose to build in a domain where the regulatory overhead alone would make 95% of YC applicants physically recoil. This is what I mean by schlep blindness operating in reverse: the founders who can see the opportunity through the compliance fog have a structural advantage, because their competition is fighting their own unconscious aversion to unglamorous work.

Albert Cai's path to this company is almost exactly the origin story I look for. He spent four years at Biolinq, a biosensor startup, doing clinical trial operations and regulatory strategy. He helped take their product from prototype through first-in-human studies to FDA De Novo authorization. He sat in research sites, watched coordinators manually enter data into systems designed in the 1990s, and experienced the full absurdity of eight-week EDC build timelines. He didn't identify this opportunity through market research — he lived inside the problem until it became unbearable. This is the Dropbox pattern: Drew Houston didn't study the file synchronization market, he kept forgetting his USB drive. Albert didn't study the EDC market, he spent years trapped inside its worst workflows. And then he declined medical school to fix them. That last detail matters. Medical school is the safe, high-status path for someone with his background. Choosing the startup over the credential is the kind of revealed preference that's hard to fake.

Nathan Leung complements this well. Employee #1 at Jupiter (YC S19), which means he's done the zero-to-one thing — built production systems from nothing at an early-stage company. Math at UCLA, CS at Michigan, internships at Google and Ramp, 74 public GitHub repos. He's a genuine builder, not someone who learned to code to found a startup. The university connection between them (both Michigan) suggests a real relationship with shared context, not a co-founder-matching-service pairing. I always worry about co-founders who met at a networking event and decided to start a company together. Two people who went through the same engineering program and then independently ended up in startup land before reuniting — that's a different foundation.

Now for what concerns me. "AI for clinical trials" doesn't sound like a bad idea. It sounds like an obviously good idea. And when something sounds obviously good, I get nervous, because it means other people see it too. Medidata is launching AI features. Veeva announced AI Agents rolling out across R&D in 2026. Castor and Clinion are both pushing AI capabilities. The competitive landscape is getting crowded from both directions — incumbents adding intelligence and startups like Harbor building from scratch. The saving grace is the Innovator's Dilemma angle: Medidata and Veeva's revenue depends on per-subject, per-site, per-CRF pricing and expensive professional services for database builds. Harbor's flat monthly fee and automated protocol-to-CRF generation directly cannibalize those revenue streams. The incumbents would have to destroy their own business model to match Harbor's approach, and public companies with $2.36 billion in annual revenue don't do that willingly. This is the dynamic that let Stripe eat enterprise payment processors even after they started noticing.

The early traction is meaningful for pre-seed. Five live clinical studies. Six named customers. A 1,600-subject randomized trial configured in one week versus the 10-12 week timelines competitors quoted. Customer testimonials that describe specific time compression — "two months to two days." These aren't vanity metrics or sign-up counts; they're regulated medical studies with real patients and real FDA oversight. Getting a clinical sponsor to trust a two-person startup with their trial data is itself evidence of something — either remarkable sales ability or a product so obviously superior that it overcomes institutional risk aversion. Probably both.

The bull case that would make this exceptional: if Harbor's regulatory compliance infrastructure and domain-specific extraction accuracy compound into a genuine data moat while incumbents are still debating how to reorganize their AI teams, Harbor could become the default EDC for the next generation of clinical trials. The flat pricing model accelerates adoption among smaller sponsors and CROs who are currently priced out of Medidata/Veeva, and switching costs lock them in for the duration of each study. If they can string together enough studies, the flywheel of validated AI extraction across therapeutic areas becomes a barrier that general-purpose LLMs can't replicate without the same regulatory investment. That's a path to something real. The question is whether two people can execute it fast enough before the incumbents' AI features become "good enough" — and in regulated industries, "good enough" from a trusted vendor usually beats "better" from an unknown startup. But then again, Stripe faced the exact same objection.
