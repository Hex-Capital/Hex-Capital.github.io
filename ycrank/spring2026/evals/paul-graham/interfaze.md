# Interfaze -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Yoeven D Khemlani built Stayr to a claimed $1M ARR in its first year and $3M in year two, then walked away from prop-tech in Singapore, moved to the US in 2025, and started building AI developer infrastructure from scratch. That trajectory is the most interesting thing about Interfaze — not the hybrid model architecture, not the IEEE CAI 2026 paper, but the fact that the founder has already shipped a revenue-generating product, possibly exited it, and chose to start over in a harder domain. I've seen this pattern work. Patrick Collison sold Auctomatic before Stripe. The repeat builder who demonstrates they can make something people pay for, then tackles something more ambitious — that's a real signal. But the Stripe parallel only goes so far. I knew Patrick for years before writing that $15K check. With Yoeven, I have a Peerlist claim and a podcast appearance. The Stayr numbers aren't independently confirmed, and I have no concrete stories of how Interfaze's founders navigated setbacks — no cereal-box moment, no weekend-prototype-that-became-the-product narrative. The cross-continental move from Singapore to San Francisco shows commitment, but commitment alone doesn't distinguish the top quartile from the merely dedicated.

Interfaze sits squarely in the "hackers building for hackers" category that dominates my portfolio — Stripe, Replit, Retool, all developer infrastructure companies. The OpenAI-compatible API, the consumption-based pricing at $1.50/$3.50 per million tokens, the Vercel AI Accelerator participation, the Groq partnership — this is the right ecosystem. Harsha Vardhan Khurdula brings genuine ML research depth from Purdue with publications in CV and NLP, and the two co-authored the architecture paper (arXiv:2602.04101) together, which tells me the technical work isn't outsourced. Interfaze bundles OCR, web scraping with a custom bot-circumvention engine, speech-to-text, and document classification behind a single model that routes through specialized DNN/CNN modules before touching an LLM. The architectural idea is sound — shift computation from expensive transformers to cheaper specialized components. That's real engineering, not a wrapper. But I notice the dossier reveals no shared history between Yoeven (Imperial College, Singapore career) and Harsha (Purdue). How these two connected matters. Co-founder conflict is one of the top startup killers, and the absence of a visible prior relationship is a gap I'd want to close in a 10-minute interview.

Interfaze's problem discovery is where my enthusiasm dims. The framing — "developers building pipelines for OCR, web scraping, document classification, and speech-to-text face a choice between general-purpose LLMs and standalone specialized models" — reads like market analysis, not lived frustration. The strongest origin stories start with "I was trying to do X and it was terrible." Dropbox started because Drew Houston forgot his USB drive on a bus. Where's Interfaze's bus? The JigsawStack origin (Jul 2024) suggests Yoeven was building an API aggregation platform and evolved toward building his own model, which is closer to organic discovery — he was living in the developer tools world and noticed the fragmentation firsthand. But the dossier doesn't make this narrative explicit, and the rebrand from JigsawStack to Interfaze in late 2025 signals strategic uncertainty more than deepening conviction. The email still comes from jigsawstack.com. When you're pivoting from API aggregator to model company, that's a significant identity shift, and the incomplete transition suggests Interfaze is still figuring out what it is.

The idea itself doesn't repel anyone, and that's my biggest concern about Interfaze. "Unified AI API for deterministic developer tasks" — who disagrees with that? Firecrawl raised $16.2M for just the scraping piece. Apify did $13.3M in revenue in 2024 on broad scraping automation. Google Document AI and Amazon Textract sell these capabilities to enterprises. The market is validated from every angle. When I look for companies in the sweet spot of Peter Thiel's Venn diagram — "seems like a bad idea" intersecting with "is a good idea" — Interfaze lands entirely on the "obviously good" side. That means the returns have been competed away before the race starts. Interfaze's differentiation is architectural (hybrid routing vs. monolithic LLMs), but the architecture is documented in a public arXiv paper. Publishing demonstrates intellectual seriousness and got them an IEEE acceptance, but it also hands the blueprint to any well-resourced team that wants to replicate the approach. Competing simultaneously against specialized tools in four domains — each with dedicated teams and communities — requires maintaining parity across all of them, and breadth is the enemy of excellence at this stage.

The bull case for Interfaze is genuine: repeat founder (Stayr), real technical depth (IEEE CAI 2026 paper), strong ecosystem positioning (Vercel, Groq), and the compound schlep of unifying perception tasks behind one API creates integration complexity that discourages lazy competitors. The Stripe analogy — bundling payment complexity behind a clean developer interface — is structurally valid. And the learning rate signal is real: evolving from JigsawStack's API aggregation layer to publishing a peer-reviewed hybrid architecture within roughly 18 months shows the team is getting technically deeper, not just adding features. The 173 GitHub stars on the deep-research repo and 39 repositories in the org show prolific output. But Stripe worked because payments were a schlep so painful that developers assumed it was unsolvable — the contrarian edge was in the problem, not just the solution. OCR and scraping being fragmented is annoying, not terrifying. Nobody says "unified perception API will never work." They say "oh yeah, someone should build that." And "someone should build that" is exactly the kind of idea that attracts twenty well-funded competitors simultaneously.

Interfaze scores above the generic pre-seed baseline because of Yoeven's prior shipping history and the team's genuine technical depth, but it falls short of my investment threshold because the problem discovery lacks the organic origin story I weight most heavily, and the idea generates consensus approval rather than the skepticism that predicts outsized returns. The one condition that would shift me: if Yoeven could show me a specific customer who's using Interfaze obsessively in its current form — "a small number of people want a large amount" — with a story about how they tried stitching together Firecrawl plus Surya plus Whisper and it was so painful they switched to Interfaze despite it being a two-person product they'd never heard of. That evidence of desperate early adoption would override my concern about the idea being too obviously good. Without it, Interfaze is a competent team building something reasonable in a competitive space — which is exactly the profile that looks promising on Demo Day and underperforms the portfolio.
