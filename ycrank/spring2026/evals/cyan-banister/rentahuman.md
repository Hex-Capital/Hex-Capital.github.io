# RentAHuman -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

700,000 registered workers and 70 connected AI agents. That ratio — roughly 10,000 humans waiting for every autonomous agent with a task to post — tells you everything about where RentAHuman sits right now. I've seen this shape before, and it didn't end well. HQ Trivia exploded with millions of players in weeks, viral press everywhere, and the world felt like it was witnessing a new paradigm. RentAHuman's trajectory has the same signature: 130 workers on Day 1, 145,000 by Day 3, Forbes and Wired writing it up, hundreds of millions of social media views. But attention is not a business. The dossier reports 11,300 active bounties against ~590,000 workers — a 50:1 supply-demand imbalance — and only 5,500 jobs actually completed. When Futurism checked in early February, they found 83 browsable profiles despite 70,000+ claimed registrations. I learned the hard way with IRL that the gap between signup numbers and verifiable engagement is where the real story hides.

Alexander Liteplo is 26, graduated from UBC in 2024, built across LayerZero, UMA Protocol, and Across Protocol — solid crypto infrastructure engineering. Patricia Tani is a self-taught developer who built and sunsetted Lemon AI, turned down Vercel. When I sit down with founders, my first question is always: tell me your story, not the business. What I hear from RentAHuman's dossier is that Liteplo conceived the idea "during travels in Japan, inspired by human-leasing services." That's a tourist observation, not a biographical forge. Neither founder appears to have lived inside the problem of AI agents needing physical-world labor, or experienced the gig economy's failures from the worker side, or built autonomous systems that hit the wall of physical-world limitations. Tani's resourcefulness — art student to self-taught engineer, startup founder who knew when to shut down — registers as resilience. But resilience and biographical inevitability are different things. I don't see evidence that this problem found these founders rather than the other way around.

RentAHuman's concept — "marketplace for AI agents to hire humans" — is genuinely the kind of thing that makes a room full of VCs shift in their chairs. The phrase alone inverts the narrative everyone's been telling about AI replacing humans, and the MCP server integration positions RentAHuman at a real technical inflection point, since Model Context Protocol only standardized in late 2024-2025. I feel the pull of that weirdness. But I need to separate memetic weirdness from structural contrarianism. Anduril was contrarian because defense tech was politically toxic and the DOD procurement system was a genuine barrier that kept out all but the most committed founders. Contraline was contrarian because male birth control was stigmatized at a biological level. RentAHuman operates at the intersection of two of the hottest consensus categories in venture — AI agents ($7.84B market growing at 46% CAGR) and gig economy platforms ($36.7B). The weirdness is conceptual, not structural. Nobody is refusing to invest in AI agent infrastructure because it's politically uncomfortable; they're lining up for it.

Here's where RentAHuman fails my most personal test — the invisible system. When I backed Affirm, the thesis was transparent lending terms against credit card opacity. When I backed Ownwell, it was exposing the property tax system that extracts value from people who don't understand it. My entire framework comes from having been fifteen and homeless, navigating structures designed to keep people like me confused and compliant. RentAHuman isn't exposing an opaque system — it's building one. The dossier is explicit: "Agents can anonymously post tasks; workers cannot confirm the real identity of the AI employer." Tasks structured as competitions where only one worker gets paid, leaving others' labor uncompensated. Cryptocurrency payments that are irreversible. No formal dispute resolution mechanism. Reported scams requiring manual intervention. Only 13% of registered users even managed to connect a crypto wallet, which means the payment infrastructure excludes 87% of the people who showed up to work. This isn't a hidden tax being revealed — it's a new information asymmetry being constructed, with AI agents holding the power and human workers bearing the risk. That's the opposite of what I invest in.

The bull case deserves honest construction. RentAHuman hit $20,000 MRR within two weeks of launch — real revenue, not vaporware. The MCP server creates genuine switching costs for AI agents that integrate it into their workflows. If I run the ten-year question, autonomous AI agents will absolutely need physical-world capabilities, and the company building the standard protocol for that becomes infrastructure. Liteplo's crypto engineering background at LayerZero and UMA is directly relevant to the escrow and payment layer. And my own RTFKT exception proves I can invest in culturally weird companies without infrastructure moats when the timing window is narrow. But RTFKT had Nike as an identifiable acquirer in a category (digital fashion) where timing was genuinely unrepeatable. RentAHuman's API layer is — per the dossier's own commoditization analysis — reproducible, and "any gig platform could add an AI agent API layer." TaskRabbit (owned by IKEA) or Amazon Mechanical Turk could ship this integration in a quarter if demand materializes. The 70 connected AI agents represent thin demand-side lock-in that doesn't yet constitute a moat.

One verifiable condition would flip me: if RentAHuman's demand side showed organic, repeating AI agent usage — not 70 agents but hundreds, with measurable task completion growth and worker retention above 30% — AND if either founder could articulate a personal story connecting them to labor exploitation or gig worker vulnerability, I'd revisit. The infrastructure-at-inflection pattern is real, and I've been early to things that looked like gimmicks before (Crusoe mining Bitcoin with flare gas). But Crusoe's founders were infrastructure people who happened to start with Bitcoin as their first workload. RentAHuman's founders are crypto engineers who happened to build a labor marketplace as their first product. The arrow of causation matters. The dimensions that lock this verdict are the absent biographical forge — when the founding story is "I saw this in Japan," there's no gravitational pull keeping these founders on this problem through the inevitable valley — and the inverted invisible-system signal, where the company is creating new opacity rather than destroying existing opacity. Those two together move RentAHuman outside my framework.
