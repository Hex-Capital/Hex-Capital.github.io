# Zatanna -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The pivot trail here tells a story, and it's not the one the founders would want me to read. Search engine caches show this company described as "a lawtech company focused on jury trials," then "Adaptive Healthcare Admin Agents," and now "Request Based Scraping Infrastructure." Three different products during or around a single YC batch. I've seen founders pivot successfully -- Justin.tv became Twitch, after all -- but there's a difference between a pivot born from learning something deep about your users and a pivot born from not having users at all. When Emmett Shear pivoted from lifecasting to game streaming, he'd spent years watching traffic patterns and understood where the pull was. Here, the trail looks more like a team trying doors until one opens, which is the opposite of organic problem discovery.

The one thing that saves this from being a clear manufactured-idea situation is Alex Blackwell. He worked at Pikkit specifically on anti-bot bypass technology. That's not market research -- that's lived technical experience in the exact domain they're now building for. When someone has spent their career reverse-engineering browser fingerprints and authentication flows, building scraping infrastructure that operates at the protocol level rather than spinning up headless Chrome instances is a natural extension of what they already know. The schlep here is also real: custom reverse-engineering per target site, maintaining integrations as sites change their TLS configurations and API structures, navigating legal gray areas around the CFAA. Most engineers look at that maintenance burden and unconsciously opt for the general-purpose browser approach. Zatanna is embracing the tedious per-site work. That's the right instinct. But embracing a schlep because your CTO happens to know how to do it is weaker than discovering a schlep through your own pain as a user.

The problem is that this doesn't look like an idea that was hiding in plain sight. Web scraping is a billion-dollar market with well-funded players everywhere. Firecrawl just raised $14.5M. Bright Data does $300M in annual revenue. Apify pulled in $13.3M last year. This is not a problem the world has been unconsciously avoiding -- it's a problem dozens of companies are actively attacking. The protocol-level approach is a genuine technical differentiator, but it's a technique, not a secret. Sophisticated in-house teams at data brokers already do this. Any well-resourced competitor could hire reverse-engineering talent and replicate individual integrations. The aggregate library of integrations compounds over time, which is good, but each integration also creates a maintenance liability that scales linearly. That's a services-shaped cost curve dressed up as infrastructure.

The bull case would require believing that the AI/LLM data revolution creates a step-change in demand for fast, cheap scraping that browser-based tools fundamentally cannot serve. If AI agents need to pull structured data from hundreds of sites in real time, sub-500ms response times and 1MB memory footprints would be transformatively better than 3-10 second browser renders at 200MB each. In that world, Zatanna's approach becomes the picks-and-shovels play for the entire AI agent ecosystem, and the per-integration maintenance burden becomes a moat rather than a cost center because no one else has done the tedious reverse-engineering work. Alex's domain expertise would be the perfect foundation for that company. But I'd need to see evidence that this is actually happening -- that real users are pulling this product because browser-based tools aren't fast enough. And I see nothing. No users, no revenue, no waitlist, no community, not even a self-serve sign-up. Just a Calendly link.

I'm also bothered by the co-founder dynamics. Rithvik went to Michigan, Alex went to Simon Fraser, Tarun is at Wisconsin-Madison. No shared school, no shared employer in the public record. I've watched this pattern enough times to be cautious about it -- nine companies in one YC batch added co-founders they didn't know well, and all nine fell apart within a year. Maybe these three have a deep relationship I can't see from public data, but the absence of any visible shared history is a flag I can't ignore. The team has technical talent -- Alex's GitHub shows real work, Rithvik's Coinbase experience adds credibility -- but I don't know if this is a team forged through shared adversity or assembled for an application.

The core issue is that three pivots deep with zero visible traction, in a crowded market, I don't see the organic founder-problem fit that makes me want to write a check. Alex's anti-bot expertise is a genuine asset, and the schlep is real. But those aren't enough when the origin of the idea looks more like "what can we build with Alex's skills?" than "we've been living with this problem and we can't not solve it."
