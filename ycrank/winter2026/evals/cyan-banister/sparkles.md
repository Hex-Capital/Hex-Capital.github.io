# Sparkles -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's my problem with Sparkles: this is a consensus category dressed up in a non-consensus outfit. Vibe coding is one of the most crowded, most competed, most capital-saturated spaces in tech right now. Lovable just raised at $6.6 billion. Cursor is at $29.3 billion. Bolt, Replit, Emergent — billions more. The positioning as "Lovable for existing projects" is clever, genuinely clever, but it's a feature differentiation in a market where the biggest players have war chests measured in hundreds of millions and engineering teams in the hundreds. When I think about defensibility and moats — one of the first things I look for — I see a product feature that any of these companies could replicate with a quarter's worth of engineering effort. "Import existing repo" is not a moat. It's a roadmap item at Lovable.

I wish I knew more about Daniil's story. He's twenty, Ukrainian, dropped out of UCL, and named himself after the word for moon in his father's language — there's something there that makes me curious. I'd want to sit with him and ask how he got here, not the business, but the real story. What I can see from the dossier is a talented young engineer who was a Cursor Ambassador, worked at a YC company, interned at a startup founded by Monzo's ex-CTO. That's a smart resume for twenty. But I can't find the thread I always look for — the biographical connection between this person's life and this specific problem. Why does a twenty-year-old engineer care deeply about making marketers more productive in existing codebases? Is there a personal frustration, a lived experience of watching someone he cares about struggle with git? Maybe there is, but it's not visible, and that absence matters to how I evaluate conviction. The founders who persist through the inevitable crises — and in this market, the crisis of a well-funded incumbent copying your feature — are the ones who are solving a problem that's personal to them, not just a gap they spotted in the landscape.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. The gap between "build new apps from scratch" and "developer IDEs" is real and observable today. If LLMs keep improving at code generation within existing codebases, the person who owns the non-technical user experience for that workflow could build something important. There's a version of the future where every marketing team, every design team, every ops team at every software company is making small changes to production code through a tool like this, and the company that nailed the UX and the GitHub PR safety gate first becomes the category. Daniil has the right background to build it — he's lived inside Cursor, he understands what these tools do and don't do. And the angel backing from people at OpenAI, xAI, and a16z suggests smart technical people see the wedge. If this were a less competitive space, or if the founder had a deeper biographical stake in the problem, I'd be leaning differently.

But I keep coming back to what I don't see. I don't see this creating economic access for people who lack it — this makes life marginally easier for people at tech companies who already have good jobs. It doesn't make an invisible system visible or transparent. Carta took the opaque world of cap tables and opened it up. Affirm took hidden credit card fees and made them honest. Flock Safety brought public safety data to communities that had none. Sparkles lets a marketing manager change button colors without filing a Jira ticket. That's useful, but it doesn't create the kind of real-world impact that gets me excited about writing a check with my own money.

The solo founder question is worth noting but not dispositive — plenty of great companies started with one person, and at pre-seed, solo is a common and understandable state. What concerns me more is solo-plus-commoditization. A solo founder building in a space where five well-funded companies could each ship a competing feature within months needs either an unfair technical advantage or an unfair distribution advantage, and I don't see either yet. The 161 YC Launch upvotes and 220 Twitter followers are fine for pre-seed but don't suggest breakout organic pull.

I'm passing on this one. It's a smart observation about a real gap, built by a clearly talented young engineer. But it sits squarely in consensus territory, lacks the biographical conviction signal I weight most heavily, and doesn't create the kind of economic access or systemic transparency that draws me to a company. The best ideas never look obvious at the start — and this one already looks pretty obvious to a lot of very well-capitalized people.
