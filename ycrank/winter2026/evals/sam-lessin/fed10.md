# Fed10 -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Fed10 is the precise company I use as my pass example when people ask what I avoid. An AI agent that replaces expensive professional services -- in this case, $500/hour policy consultants -- is not an insane idea. It is the single most consensus application of large language models that exists in 2026. Every pitch deck at every demo day features some version of "AI replaces expensive human experts in regulated domain X." When the idea provokes zero skepticism from any investor in the room, the non-consensus premium has been fully arbitraged away. You're buying at market price for the narrative, and the structural economics don't support it.

The founders are the one signal worth pausing on. Armand Iorgulescu, Zayan Islam, and Winston Wei aren't CS graduates who discovered policy exists -- they passed legislation together before they could vote. Armand was the youngest gubernatorial appointee in his state, drafted recommendations that became law, and studied government at Harvard. That's the kind of practitioner credibility I look for. The pre-existing working relationship predates the company, which matters -- these aren't co-founders who met at a hackathon. The "sane" part of my equation is genuinely present. But a sane person with a sane idea is a fundamentally different bet than a sane person with an insane idea. It's the difference between Solana at seed -- where Anatoly's Qualcomm systems engineering background met the genuinely unhinged proposition of building another Layer 1 blockchain during crypto winter -- and building a better Salesforce plugin. One produces asymmetric returns; the other produces a competitive slog.

Run the AI kill test and the structural problem becomes obvious. Remove the AI from Fed10 and the company ceases to exist. There is no painting crew, no parking infrastructure, no physical-world operation that software amplifies but cannot replace. The legislative data is public. The LLM analysis layer is available to any team with competent engineers and API access. FiscalNote already launched PolicyNote in January 2025. Quorum, with $61 million in revenue and 50% of the Fortune 100 as customers, is integrating AI features into their existing platform with their existing distribution. When I say "software companies that had moats are learning they aren't moats anymore," I'm describing what will happen to Fed10's value proposition over the next 18 months -- except Fed10 hasn't even built the moat yet. The incumbents have distribution and customer relationships; Fed10 has a demo page. The founders' lobbying networks provide warm introductions, not structural lock-in.

For the bull case to work, you'd need to believe three things simultaneously: (1) that policy analysis requires such deep domain-specific judgment that general-purpose AI tools and incumbent add-ons cannot match Fed10's quality, (2) that FiscalNote's organizational distress (stock down 97%, CEO transition) and Quorum's enterprise bloat create a durable window rather than a temporary one, and (3) that the founders can convert their lobbying relationships into a customer base fast enough to build proprietary training data before competitors close the gap. If all three are true, there's a real wedge here -- a domain-expert team building the definitive AI-native policy intelligence platform while incumbents stumble. The $500/hour consultant replacement creates genuine value for buyers, and the cost arbitrage is dramatic. But I've watched this movie before. The Birchbox lesson haunts me here: domain credibility plus obvious value proposition minus structural moat equals a company that creates real value for customers while capturing almost none of it for investors. When twenty teams can build an equivalent product in six months, the margins compress to zero regardless of who got there first.

The infinity story test reveals the ceiling. "AI-powered legislative intelligence" could theoretically expand to all regulatory compliance, all government interaction, but that narrative expansion runs directly into the same problem at every level -- the AI capability that enables the expansion is available to everyone else too. This isn't a new primitive that others build on. It's an application sitting on top of public data and public AI APIs. There's no protocol-layer compounding, no network effect where each customer makes the platform more valuable for the next. Each customer is a standalone sale requiring proportional effort, which is exactly the linear growth model I screen against.

The founding team deserves credit -- they're building in a domain they genuinely understand, which is more than most AI startups can claim. But founder-market fit is necessary, not sufficient. The structural positioning is wrong on nearly every dimension I care about: AI IS the product rather than a cherry on top, the business model is SaaS subscription in a world where I've argued the SaaS era is over, and the competitive moat depends on execution speed rather than structural lock-in. I'd be more interested if these founders were building something that leveraged their legislative expertise in a structurally novel way -- a lobbying operations company with a tech layer, a new compliance primitive, something that would be a good business even if AI never happened. As configured, this is a feature waiting to be absorbed by an incumbent with distribution.
