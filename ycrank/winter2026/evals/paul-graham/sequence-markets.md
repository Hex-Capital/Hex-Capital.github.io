# Sequence Markets -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Sequence Markets is the competitive landscape, and it tells me something important about the idea itself. Talos has raised $196M at a $1.5B valuation. FalconX has raised $430M at $8B. CoinRoutes has been building SOR for crypto since 2017. 1inch raised ~$190M for DEX aggregation. When I see a pre-seed company entering a market where incumbents have collectively raised over $800M to solve the same core problem, my question isn't "is the problem real?" — obviously it is. My question is: why hasn't this already been solved? And the answer is that it largely has been, at least for the customers willing to pay the most for it. This is the opposite of schlep blindness. Nobody is unconsciously avoiding this problem. Hundreds of engineers at well-funded companies are working on it right now.

Peter Bai's background at TMX Group and University Pension Plan Ontario gives him genuine exposure to market microstructure — that's real, and it matters. He understands order routing and execution quality from traditional finance. But the path from "I worked in market structure and noticed crypto is fragmented" to "let me build SOR for crypto" is professional pattern recognition, not organic discovery. It's the kind of idea you'd generate if you sat a smart finance person down and asked them what's broken in crypto markets. Contrast this with how Stripe happened: the Collisons were programmers who personally experienced the pain of accepting payments online, and the schlep of dealing with banks and fraud deterred everyone else for a decade. Crypto SOR doesn't have that structure. The problem is visible, the solution is understood, and well-capitalized teams are already executing on it.

The bull case would go something like this: the incumbents are all enterprise-focused with high minimums and long sales cycles, leaving a gap for active retail and mid-market traders. If Sequence nails execution quality for this underserved segment, they grow into institutional business from below — the way Stripe started with indie hackers and expanded to enterprise. And Peter Bai's traditional finance expertise gives Sequence more sophisticated routing than crypto-native competitors. If that's true, there's a real wedge. But the Stripe analogy actually cuts against this: Stripe worked because nobody else was building payment infrastructure for small developers. Here, multiple well-funded companies could move downmarket with a pricing change and a lighter onboarding flow. There's no structural barrier preventing that. The margins at the small end of financial infrastructure are worse, which is precisely why incumbents haven't bothered yet — but "incumbents haven't bothered" is different from "incumbents can't."

What concerns me most is the absence of visible technical depth. No GitHub repos for either founder. Muhammad Awan's public presence is essentially a YC tagline that says "I like building cool projects" — which tells me nothing about whether he can build the kind of low-latency financial infrastructure this company claims to have. The website claims sub-millisecond execution and AI-powered routing, but I can't find any evidence that the founding team has actually built these systems. For a company whose entire value proposition is execution quality, I need to see that at least one founder is the kind of programmer who stays up all night optimizing latency. Peter Bai's background is finance and business school, not engineering. If the core technology depends on engineers not yet visible, this is closer to a pitch deck than a startup.

There's also zero evidence of user pull. No revenue, no user counts, no organic community, no waitlist signals. The "$15B+ aggregated liquidity" claim describes venue connectivity, not demand. At pre-seed I don't require traction, but I do look for any signal that someone out there is pulling the product toward them — a trader who found the beta and won't stop using it, a fund that signed an LOI, anything. The absence of all such signals, combined with a crowded competitive field, suggests the founders may be building toward a market rather than responding to one that's reaching for them.

This is a competent team with relevant domain knowledge entering a consensus market where the problem is well-understood and well-funded competitors already exist. The idea wasn't discovered through organic pain — it was derived from professional adjacency. The schlep isn't hidden — it's a race being run by teams with hundred-million-dollar head starts. I'd want to see evidence that the founders have personally built something technically remarkable, that traders are already choosing Sequence over alternatives, or that there's some angle on this problem that genuinely nobody else sees. Right now I don't see any of those things.
