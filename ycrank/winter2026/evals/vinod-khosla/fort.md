# Fort -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Fort: Whoop spent real money acquiring Push Band in 2021 -- the closest prior art to what Fort is building -- and then discontinued the hardware entirely. The charitable interpretation is that Whoop's subscription-optimized business model couldn't accommodate a niche strength product, creating a structural opening. The uncharitable interpretation is that a well-funded team with the acquired IP and engineering talent concluded the market wasn't worth pursuing. Either way, Fort is walking into a space where the most resourced adjacent player already explored the terrain and walked away. That's not automatically disqualifying -- Cisco walked away from TCP/IP routing, and I made 2,500x on Juniper. But Cisco dismissed a technology that was about to reshape all of telecommunications. The question for Fort is whether strength tracking is analogous -- a transformational capability that the incumbent failed to recognize -- or whether Whoop correctly identified it as a feature, not a category.

I keep coming back to consequence magnitude, because that's where Fort loses me. If Fort executes perfectly -- ships on time, nails accuracy, captures the strength training wearable market -- what changes? Gym-goers get better data about their reps. That's a real product serving real demand. But nobody's life fundamentally changes. No trillion-dollar system gets displaced. Compare this to what I look for: Impossible Foods could eliminate animal agriculture. Commonwealth Fusion could replace fossil fuels. Guardant Health replaced surgical biopsies with a blood draw. Fort replaces manual workout logging with automatic workout logging. The fitness tracker market is $63 billion, but the strength-specific wearable subsegment is a fraction of that, and even capturing it entirely produces a business, not a disruption. The consequence of success is a $1-3 billion consumer electronics company at the upper bound. I don't write checks for upper bounds that look like Whoop's $3.6 billion valuation ceiling -- I write checks where the upper bound is unmeasurable because the category didn't previously exist.

The team deserves better than this problem. Miranda Nover designed 4680 battery cells for Cybertruck. Paul Schneider architected autonomy systems for Tesla Semi and Robotaxi. Zac Valles engineered structures at SpaceX and powertrains at Rivian. These are people who have shipped hardware at civilization-scale -- the kind of engineers I'd want building fusion reactors or orbital launch systems. Instead, they're building a gym accessory. This is not an outsider team attacking an entrenched industry with naive audacity; it's an insider hardware team applying Tesla-grade execution to a consumer electronics niche. Their competence is undeniable, but competence applied to an insufficiently ambitious problem is precisely what I warn against. They've assembled a team optimized for hardware execution risk -- which is the right risk to address -- but all three share the same background (Tesla, mechanical/electrical engineering), leaving gaps in ML, consumer brand building, and software that matter enormously for a data-driven consumer product.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. Strength training is experiencing genuine secular acceleration -- 122% YoY growth on Whoop's platform, Garmin's fitness segment growing 33% YoY, the entire longevity movement creating a cultural tailwind. The timing is arguably the strongest dimension here. If Fort establishes itself as the category-defining strength wearable before Whoop rebuilds Push or Garmin invests seriously in the weight room, the data moat from millions of per-rep kinematics could compound into something defensible. The multi-placement form factor (wrist, magnetic equipment mount, body strap) is a genuinely clever hardware design that captures richer motion data than any wrist-only competitor. And the pricing ($319 device + $80/year) could produce healthy unit economics compared to Whoop's $30/month subscription. If I believed the strength tracking wearable market was analogous to how the running watch market bootstrapped Garmin into a $40 billion public company, this team has the hardware chops to win it. But Garmin succeeded by being the essential tool for a massive existing behavior (outdoor navigation, then running). Strength tracking is an optimization layer for people who already lift -- it doesn't create or transform the behavior itself.

The technology itself is incremental, not disruptive. IMU-based exercise detection from wrist motion has been demonstrated by Push Band, RepOne, and multiple academic papers. The dossier explicitly notes the core technology "is not patented in a way that prevents replication" and "a well-funded wearable company with ML expertise could build comparable exercise detection." Fort hasn't published independent accuracy validation, which is concerning for a product whose entire value proposition rests on measurement precision. There's no 10x cost advantage, no fundamentally new capability, no structural reason why a Whoop or Garmin couldn't add equivalent features to their existing platforms. This is a better feature set, not a technology that makes the incumbent approach obsolete. I've learned -- painfully, from KiOR -- that technology achievement without structural economic advantage is a trap. Fort's technology may work, but it doesn't create the kind of economic displacement that builds durable companies.

I'd tell these founders what I tell every talented team pursuing an undersized problem: you're building toward a ceiling, not toward infinity. Go find something where the consequence of success terrifies the incumbents and transforms how the world works. You have the hardware skills. Apply them to a problem whose scale matches your capability.
