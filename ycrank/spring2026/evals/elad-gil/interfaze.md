# Interfaze -- Elad Gil Evaluation

arXiv:2602.04101 is both Interfaze's strongest credential and its most revealing vulnerability. Publishing a peer-reviewed architecture paper at IEEE CAI 2026 demonstrates real technical depth — routing perception tasks through specialized DNN/CNN modules rather than burning full transformer inference is a genuinely clever engineering insight. But the paper is public. The architectural blueprint is now available to every well-funded team in the space, including Firecrawl ($16.2M raised), Google Document AI, and Amazon Textract. When I invested in Stripe, the moat wasn't a paper — it was years of accumulated payment network relationships, regulatory compliance across jurisdictions, and developer workflow integration that compounded daily. Interfaze's technical contribution is impressive for a five-person pre-seed team, but a published architecture is a research contribution, not a defensible infrastructure layer.

Interfaze's market timing thesis rests on the claim that specialized models now match or exceed foundation models on perception tasks at lower cost. This is true — but it's not nonobvious. The "compound AI systems" framework has been mainstream since Berkeley's widely-cited 2024 paper. Every major AI lab is building mixture-of-experts and routing architectures. Every developer podcast discusses specialized vs. monolithic model tradeoffs. When I wrote the seed check for Perplexity, AI-powered search was a failed category that investors had written off after a decade of attempts. When I backed Coinbase, crypto infrastructure was actively dismissed by mainstream VCs. Interfaze is entering a market — unified developer APIs for perception tasks — where the structural thesis is consensus, not contrarian. The $14.66B Document AI market and $10.2B web scraping market are real, but they're also well-mapped by incumbents and well-understood by every growth investor in the ecosystem.

Interfaze faces a foundational platform risk that triggers one of my clearest red flags: building an application on a contested infrastructure layer. GPT-4o already handles OCR, vision, and speech natively. Claude processes documents. Gemini handles multimodal perception. Foundation model API costs are dropping roughly 10x per year — GPT-4-level inference is already 95% cheaper than at launch. Interfaze's cost advantage from routing to specialized models is real today but depends on foundation model pricing NOT collapsing further, which contradicts the observable trend. The company needs the specialized-model cost advantage to persist AND developers to adopt a new unified API over existing solutions — that's two independent bets, not one. My single-miracle framework penalizes this: when the technology miracle isn't in your control (it depends on how fast OpenAI and Anthropic drop prices), the distribution miracle has to do more work, and Interfaze's 654 Twitter followers and 239 Discord members don't signal the kind of organic developer pull that compensates.

Yoeven Khemlani brings a meaningful credential that distinguishes Interfaze from a median pre-seed team: Stayr reportedly hit $1M ARR in year one and $3M in year two before an apparent acquisition. That's real operational evidence, not just a LinkedIn headline. Harsha Vardhan Khurdula's Purdue M.Sc. and published CV/NLP research credentials are well-matched to building the hybrid perception architecture. The founder-market fit is credible — an infrastructure engineer plus an ML researcher is the right composition for this product. But neither founder has operated inside a company during hypergrowth (the 10-to-1,000 employee transition), and the co-founder relationship has no visible shared history. Yoeven's prior experience at UOB and Noah as Technical Lead is solid engineering background but not the operator-scaler profile I see in founders like Parker Conrad rebuilding after Zenefits or the Brex team who had been operating businesses since their teens.

The strongest bull case for Interfaze: developers genuinely hate stitching together Firecrawl for scraping, Tesseract for OCR, Whisper for STT, and separate classification models — each with its own API, pricing, and failure modes. The OpenAI-compatible API is the right interface choice for drop-in adoption. Consumption-based pricing at $1.50/$3.50 per million tokens positions well below foundation model rates for perception-heavy workloads. The Vercel AI Accelerator participation and Groq partnership signal real ecosystem positioning. If foundation model costs plateau for multimodal perception tasks — or if accuracy requirements for deterministic outputs keep specialized models relevant — Interfaze could become the default perception API layer, analogous to how Twilio aggregated telecom primitives during the mobile platform shift. I've checked my known exceptions: Anduril's override was a generational structural shift in defense — there's no equivalent magnitude shift here. The bull case is plausible but requires the cost/accuracy thesis to hold for three or more years against the most well-resourced AI labs on Earth, and I don't see evidence that the window is structurally protected.

Interfaze is a competent team with a published technical contribution, building a product that solves a real developer pain point. But the absence of a nonobvious market window is decisive for my framework. The unified API for deterministic perception tasks is useful — it's not structurally differentiated. The one verifiable condition that would flip me to Invest: if Interfaze showed 50+ paying customers with negative net revenue churn over six months, that would demonstrate that the bundled perception API creates genuine workflow lock-in that individual point solutions don't — proving the infrastructure thesis with retention data rather than architecture diagrams. Without that signal, the market timing is consensus, the platform layer is contested, and the architecture is replicable — three conditions that collectively place Interfaze below my investment threshold despite legitimate founder credentials and a clever technical approach.
