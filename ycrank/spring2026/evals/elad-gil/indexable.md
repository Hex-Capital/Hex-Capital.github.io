# Indexable -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most striking thing about Indexable is the collision between extraordinary technical depth and a market that's already crowded and consensus. A custom VMM on KVM with 26ms full-environment forking including databases, a custom Linux 6.19 kernel with upstream-unsupported patches, a content-addressable storage engine with tiered caching, RDMA-capable transport -- this is genuinely deep systems engineering, the kind of work that takes years to replicate. And yet the market they're entering has six funded competitors, three major cloud providers launching sandbox products in early 2026, and E2B claiming 88% of Fortune 100 signed up. When Cloudflare, Vercel, and Fly.io all launch sandbox products in the same quarter, you're not entering a nonobvious market. You're entering an obvious one.

The market timing question I always start with lands awkwardly here. The structural shift is real -- AI agents writing code, modifying databases, running shell commands need isolated environments, and the explosion of Claude Code, Codex, and Devin in 2024-2025 created genuine demand for this infrastructure. But that shift is visible to everyone. The more interesting timing question is whether *stateful* forking -- forking an entire running environment including databases, processes, and memory as a single atomic unit -- represents a capability threshold that the market hasn't yet recognized as critical. E2B does ephemeral sessions with no DB forking. Daytona uses Docker containers rather than a custom VMM. Most competitors are optimized for "spin up a container, execute code, tear it down." Indexable is betting that the next generation of autonomous agents will need to fork entire running states, explore branches, and merge back. That's a subtler thesis -- essentially that the agentic workflow paradigm is about to cross a statefulness threshold that current sandbox infrastructure can't serve. There's a version of this argument that's compelling, but the dossier doesn't show evidence that anyone's buying it yet. Zero public customers, zero revenue signals, a website returning HTTP 403.

Where Indexable genuinely excels is infrastructure-layer positioning. This isn't a wrapper around Firecracker or a Docker orchestration layer -- it's a custom VMM built from the kernel up. That's the deepest possible pick-and-shovels play within the AI agent infrastructure stack. If agent platforms integrate fork/snapshot APIs into their core workflows, ripping them out requires re-architecting state management logic. That's the compounding switching cost dynamic I look for. The content-addressable storage engine with cross-tenant deduplication has a potential data-driven moat: more tenants means better deduplication ratios means lower costs, which creates a flywheel. This reminds me structurally of what made Stripe defensible -- not just a better product but an API layer that became progressively harder to replace as more business logic depended on it. The problem is that Stripe's positioning was validated by developers actually building on it. Indexable has zero public repos and zero identifiable customers.

Andrew Gazelka's technical credentials map unusually well to this problem. Hyperion -- a Minecraft engine in Rust handling 10,000+ concurrent players in one world -- is a direct analog to the challenge of managing massive concurrent stateful environments with low-latency forking. The xAI stint adds credibility in high-performance compute. But I see a single named founder with a 3-person team maintaining a custom VMM, a custom kernel, a Rust-based Nix reimplementation, and an RDMA transport layer. That's an enormous surface area of custom low-level infrastructure for a team this small. Competitors using Firecracker (AWS-maintained, battle-tested) carry fundamentally less maintenance overhead. My "single miracle" framework raises a concern here: you need the technology to work at scale AND you need to win distribution against E2B ($32M+ raised, Fortune 100 adoption), Daytona ($31M raised, $1M ARR in two months), and Modal ($50M ARR). Technical superiority alone doesn't win when "good enough" alternatives have a 12-18 month head start in enterprise relationships. The performance gap between 26ms and 90-150ms may matter enormously for some workloads and not at all for others -- and if the answer is "not at all" for 80% of initial use cases, the technical moat becomes academic.

The strongest bull case: Indexable is building the right abstraction layer at the wrong moment of visibility. If autonomous agents become substantially more stateful over the next 12-18 months -- managing persistent databases, running multi-step workflows with rollback, branching and merging complex system states -- then the ephemeral container model that E2B and others are optimized for becomes insufficient. Indexable's custom VMM, designed from day one for atomic state forking, would be the only infrastructure capable of serving this workload without a ground-up rebuild. In that scenario, E2B's Fortune 100 adoption becomes a liability rather than an asset -- they've scaled an architecture that doesn't extend to the problem that actually matters. This is the kind of structural bet that occasionally produces breakout infrastructure companies. But it requires the statefulness thesis to be right, and it requires Indexable to survive long enough for the market to catch up to their technical architecture. With YC-level funding against competitors with 20-40x more capital, that survival window is tight.

The margin architecture for infrastructure of this type should be solid in theory -- usage-based pricing on compute-intensive operations tends toward 70%+ gross margins once the infrastructure amortizes. But maintaining a custom VMM and kernel likely compresses those margins versus competitors running on AWS-maintained Firecracker. No pricing data exists to evaluate this, so I'm applying standard pre-seed assumptions.
