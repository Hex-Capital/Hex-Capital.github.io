# Keyframe Labs -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Parth Radia spent five years at Ericsson building the exact product Keyframe Labs now sells. Not "something related" — the actual thing: photorealistic digital humans with real-time voice and video synthesis. He started a team, grew it to 30 people, built a human scanning rig in LA, and shipped Ericsson's first generative AI product (Ericsson blog, July 2023). That kind of founder-market fit reminds me of what I saw with Patrick Collison and Stripe — someone who already proved the product works inside a big, slow institution, then left to build it at startup speed. When Patrick built Auctomatic as a teenager, I wasn't betting on the company; I was filing away the information that this person could ship. Parth Radia has a similar filing: five years of accumulated model architecture knowledge, training data intuition, and production deployment scars that can't be replicated by reading papers. Keyframe Labs inherits all of that institutional knowledge in a two-person company.

Kaahan Radia brings the complementary half — five years building camera-based perception systems for Zipline's autonomous drones, with publications at CVPR and JAMA. The Keyframe Labs co-founding team covers the full technical stack: generative video models on one side, real-time computer vision and inference on the other. The shared surname suggests they're siblings, which introduces a particular co-founder dynamic — trust comes pre-built, but the difficult conversations about equity, roles, and what happens when things get hard can be even harder to force between family. I see no evidence either way on whether they've had those conversations, so I treat it as neutral. What I do see is two people who each chose to spend half a decade on hard, unglamorous technical problems (telecom AI infrastructure, drone perception) rather than chasing whatever was trending. That's a character signal.

Keyframe Labs is tackling a genuine schlep — real-time generative video at 500ms latency, hundreds of simultaneous connections, photorealistic emotional expression, all at $0.06 per minute. The compute optimization alone requires deep expertise that most AI founders lack. But here's where the Stripe parallel breaks down: when I invested in Stripe, no well-funded competitor was doing the same thing. Online payments were broken for every developer, and no one was fixing it because the regulatory and integration complexity repelled everyone. Keyframe Labs faces Tavus, which raised $64.2M including Sequoia and Scale (BusinessWire, November 2025), has shipped its Conversational Video Interface product, and has a three-year head start in the exact same real-time segment. The schlep isn't blind anymore — Tavus saw it too. Behind Tavus, Synthesia sits at $146M ARR with $530M+ raised (Sacra, September 2025), and HeyGen at $100M ARR (Sacra, October 2025). Both could add real-time capabilities with their next funding round. Keyframe Labs is entering a space where everyone agrees the future involves AI video avatars, and multiple well-funded teams are already building it.

That consensus is my primary concern. The best startup ideas initially seem like bad ideas — that's not just a heuristic, it's a structural prediction about returns. If everyone agrees that real-time AI avatars are valuable, the returns from being right about the market have already been competed away. What remains is pure execution risk: can Keyframe Labs build a better model, at lower cost, with a two-person team, against Tavus's $64.2M? Maybe. Parth's Ericsson experience is a real advantage in model architecture. The $0.06/min price point, if sustainable at reasonable margins, could be a wedge — but I have no data on Keyframe Labs' GPU costs or gross margins, and pricing below competitors is a strategy that works until you run out of money. The nine testimonial categories on Keyframe Labs' website (company website, April 2026) show breadth of potential use cases, but no named customers, no quantified metrics, no evidence of the small group of obsessive early users I look for. Zero GitHub stars, one GitHub follower, no Product Hunt launch, no press coverage. I can't find the signal of desperate early adoption anywhere in this dossier.

The bull case an advocate would make is strong: Parth Radia is one of the most qualified people on earth to build this specific product; the Ericsson intrapreneurship proves he can ship through bureaucratic resistance; sibling co-founders with complementary technical depth (generative models + perception engineering) spanning the full product stack; consumption-based pricing at $0.06/min creates a developer-friendly on-ramp that mirrors Stripe's API-first distribution; and the real-time conversational segment is architecturally distinct from async video generation, meaning Synthesia and HeyGen would need fundamentally different inference pipelines to compete. I've made exceptions before for companies outside my typical developer-tools pattern when the schlep is severe enough — ClassDojo succeeded in K-12 education, which every VC considered too unsexy to fund. But ClassDojo's schlep was selling to school districts, a distribution problem that repelled competitors permanently. Keyframe Labs' schlep is a technical one — model quality and inference speed — and technical schleps erode as compute gets cheaper and models improve. Tavus is already on the other side of that schlep.

What I'm missing — and what would flip me to Invest — is evidence of the animal quality in these founders operating under startup constraints specifically. Show me a customer who switched from Tavus to Keyframe Labs because the model quality was noticeably better. Show me a week-over-week usage growth curve. Show me the founders doing something scrappy and creative to acquire their first ten customers, something that reveals the running-back mentality I can't assess from résumés alone. Parth Radia's Ericsson track record tells me he can build the product; it doesn't tell me he can acquire customers with no brand, no budget, and no sales team. Keyframe Labs has the technical foundation but hasn't yet demonstrated the startup-specific resourcefulness that separates a strong engineering team from a fundable company.

Keyframe Labs lands in the neutral zone for me. The founder-market fit is genuinely exceptional — Parth Radia's five years building this exact product category is the strongest single signal in this dossier, and it lifts the organic problem discovery score well above default. But that signal is substantially offset by the consensus nature of the market (multiple well-funded competitors validate the opportunity, which means the Black Swan premium is gone) and the absence of any evidence of scrappy founder resourcefulness under real constraint. The two dimensions that most determine this tier are Organic Problem Discovery, where Keyframe Labs scores well on the strength of Parth's direct domain experience, and Idea That Repels Before It Attracts, where Keyframe Labs scores below default because Tavus's $64.2M, Synthesia's $4B valuation, and HeyGen's $100M ARR collectively prove this idea sounds good to everyone.
