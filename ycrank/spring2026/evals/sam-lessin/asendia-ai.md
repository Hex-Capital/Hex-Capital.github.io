# Asendia AI -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Paradox raised $253M and got acquired by Workday for north of a billion. HireVue does $89M in annual revenue. Humanly just closed a $25M Series B. Sense HQ hit a $500M valuation. And now Asendia AI walks in and says "we built voice AI agents for staffing agencies" — in a category where every sophisticated investor on the planet is already writing checks. My first question is always "what if the market is wrong?" But here the market isn't wrong. The market has priced AI recruiting as valuable, funded it aggressively, and consolidated it through M&A. Asendia AI isn't a contrarian bet where the risk is baked into the price — it's a late entrant into the most consensus AI application category in venture right now. When I look at my best returns — Solana at four cents, Eaze when every fund avoided cannabis — the entire thesis was that smart people had dismissed the category and I could buy the opportunity cheap. With Asendia AI, the opportunity has already been bid up by everyone else.

Rihab Lajmi leaving Google at 27, Badis Zormati's six years at Infineon building AI systems — these are clearly competent, driven people. Rihab's personal story of leaving Tunisia at 18, working her way through Microsoft Munich and Google Cloud, conducting 35+ recruiter interviews to understand the problem — that's real grit. But competent people pitching a sensible idea in a validated market is exactly the combination that doesn't excite me. My framework requires a sane person pitching something insane. "AI voice agents that screen candidates for staffing agencies" isn't insane — it's the single most obvious thing you could build with voice AI in 2026. Every product person at every big company has this on a roadmap somewhere. Asendia AI's idea would make a great feature inside Workday, inside Bullhorn, inside LinkedIn Talent Solutions. That's the problem.

Asendia AI's dossier makes the bear case for me: "Voice AI screening agents can be built on top of commodity LLMs and telephony APIs (Twilio, etc.). The core technical components — speech-to-text, LLM-based evaluation, text-to-speech — are widely available." That's the AI kill test, and Asendia AI fails it. Workday didn't buy Paradox for $1.5B to sit on their hands — they're going to integrate Olivia into every module, and the moment voice screening becomes table-stakes for ATS platforms, Asendia AI's entire product is a feature that Bullhorn or Greenhouse ships natively. The 50+ ATS integrations are real work and create some friction, but integrations are not a moat — they're a feature checklist that any well-funded competitor can match. Compare this to Metropolis, where the physical parking infrastructure can't be replicated by an API call. Asendia AI has no physical-world asset, no protocol-level lock-in, no network effect that gets stronger with scale. It's pure software wrapping commodity AI on commodity telephony rails.

Here's the bull case an advocate would make, and I want to be honest about it: the $600B global staffing industry has under 1% AI penetration. Workday's acquisition of Paradox actually creates a structural gap — Workday will prioritize enterprise HCM customers, not the thousands of independent staffing agencies running Bullhorn. Asendia AI's ~10 paying clients across US and European markets, their SOC 2/GDPR/HIPAA compliance stack, their SIA corporate membership — these are real signals of a team that understands enterprise sales to staffing buyers. The claimed "$6M higher revenue from placements" for a customer, if real, suggests meaningful ROI. And the vertical-specific angle (healthcare, IT, blue-collar) could build domain knowledge that horizontally-focused competitors lack. But here's why it doesn't flip me: even granting all of this, the structural problem remains. Every ATS vendor — Bullhorn, Greenhouse, Lever, Ashby — will build or buy voice screening capability within 18 months. Asendia AI's domain expertise is real but not defensible. This is "better CRM" territory — competing on execution in an existing market where the upside is capped. No Known Exception in my framework applies here because Asendia AI isn't creating a new primitive (the Slack exception) or a new social behavior (the Clubhouse exception). It's automating an existing workflow more efficiently.

Asendia AI's capital position is the one dimension where I don't have strong signal either way. Three-person team, prior European funding at an undisclosed amount, founding engineer posting at $150-180K — that's rational pre-seed economics. But the competitive set has raised $90M+ collectively and Asendia AI will need to raise aggressively to compete on sales and integrations, which means this company's future depends entirely on the fundraising conveyor belt I think is dying. The B2C-to-B2B pivot from "FAANG" career coaching to staffing agency voice AI is also a signal I weigh negatively — not because pivots are bad (Slack was a pivot), but because all the Product Hunt traction and web traffic growth metrics belong to a different product. Asendia AI's actual B2B business is ~10 clients old.

One condition would flip me: if Asendia AI showed me that their screening data was generating a proprietary matching algorithm that materially outperformed generic LLM-based screening — a real data flywheel with measurable placement-outcome improvement that compounds over time and can't be replicated by bolting GPT onto an ATS. That would be a primitive, not a feature. Right now, no evidence of that exists. Pass.
