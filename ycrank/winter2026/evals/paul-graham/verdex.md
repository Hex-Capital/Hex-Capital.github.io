# Verdex -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing that strikes me about Verdex is the gap between the claim and the claimants. "Insuring over 11% of all American farmland" and "live with one of the largest US crop insurers" — that's a bold statement from two people who graduated from Dartmouth last year with no documented experience in either insurance or agriculture. Evan Rankin's engineering capstone was about diagnosing autism spectrum disorder. Jad Bousselham ran a "stealth startup" of unspecified nature. Somewhere between graduation and YC, they decided crop insurance verification was their problem to solve. When I encounter that kind of disconnect, my first question is always the same: how did you find this?

And the dossier gives me no answer. That's the core issue. The problem itself is legitimate — $5 billion a year spent sending humans to physically stare at fields that satellites can see better. That's the kind of obvious inefficiency that should have been solved already. The schlep is real too: USDA/FCIC regulation, conservative buyers who move slowly, the need to earn regulatory trust for a verification method that replaces boots on the ground. Insurance companies don't adopt new claims workflows on a whim. This is exactly the kind of painful, tedious, regulatory problem that causes schlep blindness. But here's the thing — the schlep has to be discovered, not selected from a menu. Stripe worked because the Collisons had personally experienced the pain of integrating payment processing as developers. They didn't read a McKinsey report about payment infrastructure inefficiency. I see no equivalent story here. Two smart engineers appear to have identified an attractive market and built toward it. That's not disqualifying, but it removes my strongest positive signal.

The traction claim deserves serious scrutiny. If it's real — genuinely processing satellite verification for 100+ million acres through a major carrier — that's remarkable execution for a pre-seed company, and it would demonstrate the kind of hustle that can compensate for a manufactured origin story. But single-customer traction is a pilot, not product-market fit. One large insurer trying your product is an experiment; three insurers refusing to stop using it is evidence of wanting. And the concentration risk is severe — if this carrier decides to build in-house (RCIS is already piloting similar tech with satellite data and AI), Verdex loses everything. There's no public revenue data, no evidence of a multi-customer pipeline, and no organic distribution signal like word-of-mouth or inbound interest from other carriers.

The strongest bull case goes like this: the timing is genuinely right (405 EO satellites launched since 2022, imagery costs plummeting), the regulatory schlep creates a real moat for whoever gets through first, and these founders have somehow already convinced one of America's largest crop insurers to trust them — which means they're either more connected or more persuasive than their resumes suggest. If they can convert this single relationship into a reference customer that opens doors to the handful of other major AIPs, the market structure actually works in their favor: crop insurance is concentrated among a small number of carriers, so you don't need viral growth — you need five to ten enterprise sales. And the data advantage compounds: every acre you monitor gives you better ground-truth for training models that the next entrant can't replicate. If Verdex turns out to be the company that makes USDA comfortable with satellite-based verification as a regulatory standard, they could own this category.

But I don't have enough evidence to bet on that scenario with my own money. The technical question is unresolved — neither founder has public GitHub repos, and I can't evaluate whether they've built genuine computer vision models for crop loss assessment or assembled existing satellite analytics tools into a workflow wrapper. Evan's ML background is relevant but thin (one capstone project). The domain expertise gap worries me specifically because selling to insurance carriers requires speaking their language — actuarial concepts, regulatory compliance, claims adjustment workflows — and learning that language takes years, not months. The founders may be brilliant and may have learned fast, but the dossier gives me no evidence of it beyond the traction claim itself, which I can't independently verify.

This is a real problem worth solving, approached by smart people who chose it rather than discovered it. The schlep exists but the organic connection doesn't. One customer is a start, not a story. I'd want to see these founders in person and ask them the question directly: when did you first encounter this problem, and why couldn't you stop thinking about it?
