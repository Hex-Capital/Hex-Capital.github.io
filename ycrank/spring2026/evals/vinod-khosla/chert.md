# Chert -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Chert is an AI SDR that swapped the channel from email to iMessage. That is the entire wedge. Two undergraduates at NYU and Penn looked at a category where 11x.ai raised $74M and reportedly lost 70-80% of customers within months (TechCrunch via dossier), and concluded the fix is to message you on the same thread your mother uses. When I read that the trychert.com landing pitch is "10x response rates" because the inbox is less crowded, I am not looking at a technology bet. I am looking at channel arbitrage on a personal medium that Apple has explicitly never opened to business — and the moment Apple closes that door, the company is gone. This is the most distinctive thing about Chert and it is also the bear case in one sentence.

Gary Gao and Ian Fong — start with the consequence question, because that is where I always start. If Chert fully succeeds, what happens to the world? Some B2B sales teams book 1-5 more meetings per week on iMessage instead of LinkedIn, and a few SDR salaries shift into a Chert retainer. Nobody's healthcare gets cheaper. No expensive thing becomes free. No new market is created — this is share redistribution out of the email/LinkedIn outbound budget into iMessage outbound budget. The dossier even cites the AI SDR TAM at $4.12B growing to $15B; that is the size of the existing SDR-tooling pie Chert is fighting Artisan, AiSDR and 11x for. My OpenAI bet required an apology letter to LPs because the consequence of success was civilizational. The consequence of Chert succeeding is a moderately profitable managed-service business and a lot of irritated iPhone users. That is not the option I buy.

The non-linear test is where Chert really fails for me. Sendblue (also YC) and Project Blue already sell the iMessage primitives — the hard part, the Mac/relay infrastructure that gets around Apple's lack of a business API, is a commodity input Chert is consuming, not building. The "AI agent" layer on top is GPT-class wrappers booking calendar slots. There is no Rutherford engine here, no 3D-printed combustion chamber, no novel architecture that produces a 10x cost or capability step. The dossier is honest about this: "any AI-SDR competitor (11x, Artisan, AiSDR) could layer iMessage on top of those APIs." Chert's wedge is a distribution channel that any competitor with a credit card to Sendblue can replicate in a quarter. When I read that, I read a 2x improvement on response rates, not a discontinuity.

Gary Gao's Penn Circuits Lab work with Marc Miskin on oscillator-based physical learning networks is genuinely interesting — that is a first-principles brain doing real research on energy-efficient AI hardware, exactly the kind of mind I look for. But he is not building that company. He is building an iMessage SDR. And Ian Fong's LinkedIn lists "incoming @ jpmc" as a credential — a JPMorgan internship pending while you are running a YC company is a focus signal I cannot ignore. Eight hackathon wins demonstrate raw technical velocity, which I credit, but there is zero evidence in the dossier of the founders pivoting, articulating what surprised them, or showing the learning trajectory I look for over the YC batch. The "rate of change in three months" question — I have nothing to answer it with.

Bull case for Chert, stated as honestly as I can: the consequence of success is larger than I am giving credit for if you believe iMessage outbound becomes the dominant B2B channel of the next decade, the way email did in 1999. The DoorDash exception is the relevant precedent — Tony Xu had no novel technology, just an execution wedge on logistics, and the consequence was a $72B company because the channel itself was generational. If Chert is the first mover on what becomes the next generational outbound channel and Apple looks the other way, this is a $5B company. Gary Gao's research credentials and Ian Fong's competition track record are above the typical YC team baseline. The 75% reply rate Gao quotes on his personal site, if it survives third-party verification, is genuinely anomalous. I take that bull case seriously enough to write the next paragraph.

Apple is the unsigned variable that ends this. The DoorDash analogy breaks because food delivery did not depend on a single platform owner deciding whether the entire business model was permitted. Chert depends on Apple — a $3T platform with no business iMessage API, an explicit consumer-trust posture, and a history of killing relay-based workarounds when they get noisy. The 11x precedent in the dossier — 70-80% customer churn at $10M ARR — tells me the AI SDR category itself has a retention problem, and bolting on a more invasive channel will accelerate the backlash, not solve it. TCPA exposure on automated outreach to mobile numbers is the kind of regulatory drag that turns enterprise sales cycles into legal review hell. None of these are technology risks I can engineer down. They are platform-owner and policy risks — the exact category my 2025 essay says I now filter out.

Pass. The condition that flips me to Invest: Chert demonstrates that the lead-sourcing layer — verified phone numbers matched to ICP — is a defensible data asset producing meeting bookings independent of the iMessage channel, such that if Apple closes the door tomorrow Chert can route the same agents through SMS, WhatsApp, or whatever channel opens next. That would convert this from a channel-arbitrage bet into an outbound-AI-platform bet, and I would re-engage. The two dimensions that locked the tier are Consequence Magnitude (a managed-service SDR replacement is a redistribution play, not a new market) and Non-Linear Technical Approach (Sendblue and Project Blue have already commoditized the only piece of infrastructure that matters).
