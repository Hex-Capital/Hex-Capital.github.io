# Axis -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Let me be direct: this is an AI-first company where AI IS the product. That's my single biggest avoidance category, and Axis sits squarely in the center of it. When I run my "cherry on top" test -- would this business be good if AI didn't exist? -- the answer isn't just no, it's that the business literally wouldn't exist. There is no physical operation, no protocol layer, no data asset, no network effect underneath the AI layer. Strip away the LLM and you have two recent graduates with an interest in commodity markets and nothing to sell. That's a feature, not a company. And features get absorbed.

The sane-person-insane-idea filter fails on both sides here. On the "sane" axis: Eric Zhu appears to have interned as a quant trader at TransMarket Group, and Ian Wang is described as "interested in trading" from Yale '25. These are smart young guys, but a quant trading internship is not the same as having run a physical commodity desk at Trafigura or having built risk infrastructure at Vitol. The deep insider credibility that makes me trust a founder to navigate an operationally complex domain -- the kind of thing I saw with the Teamshares team understanding small business operations from the inside -- isn't demonstrably present. On the "insane" axis: "AI for trading desks" is possibly the single most consensus application of LLMs in all of fintech. Every VC I talk to has seen twenty of these pitches. When the idea doesn't make anyone uncomfortable, you're buying narrative at market price. This is a sane idea from founders whose sanity credentials in this specific domain are still forming.

The structural moat question is where this falls apart most severely. The dossier itself acknowledges "no defensibility signals found in public sources" and every potential moat is marked as "[Inferred]" and "unproven." The competitive landscape tells the real story: Kpler has $200M in funding and Insight Partners backing with existing commodity data relationships. Vortexa has Morgan Stanley money and satellite-derived proprietary data assets. Enverus was acquired by Blackstone and does $854M in revenue. S&P Global Platts owns the commodity pricing infrastructure. These are not sleepy incumbents waiting to be disrupted -- they are well-capitalized data companies that can bolt AI analytical layers onto their existing proprietary datasets faster than Axis can build the data relationships from scratch. And that's before we get to the most obvious threat: the commodity trading houses themselves. Glencore, Vitol, and Trafigura have enormous in-house technology teams and every incentive to build proprietary AI tools using the same APIs Axis would use, trained on their own vastly superior proprietary trading data. Why would a trading desk buy an external AI analytical tool when the analytical edge comes from the data they already possess internally?

The strongest bull case I can construct: commodity trading is genuinely specialized, the unstructured data problem (broker commentary, geopolitical analysis, refinery reports) is real and underserved by existing structured data providers, and if Axis can build a proprietary training dataset from early institutional customers, the flywheel could compound into something defensible. The narrow market might actually be too small for Kpler or Vortexa to prioritize, creating a protected niche. And there's a version of this where Axis becomes the analytical protocol layer that sits between data providers and trading desks -- a new primitive for commodities intelligence. For that to work, the founders would need to demonstrate commodity-specific domain depth far beyond what's visible today, build proprietary data pipelines that can't be replicated with off-the-shelf LLMs, and convince me that the product is infrastructure rather than an application. None of those conditions are evidenced in the current dossier, and the website returning nothing but a page title suggests the product itself may still be conceptual.

The narrative ceiling is also constrained in a way that concerns me. CTRM software is a $112M market. Even the generous estimate of $13.8B by 2031 doesn't create an infinity story. You could argue expansion from commodities to all institutional trading, but that path runs directly into Bloomberg Terminal, which is about as entrenched as infrastructure gets. The regatta here doesn't have open water -- every direction you sail, there's a well-funded fleet already there. This is an application on other people's platforms (LLM providers, data providers, trading infrastructure) with no visible path to becoming a layer that others build on.
