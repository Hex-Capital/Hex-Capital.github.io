# Caretta -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The competitive structure here tells you everything before you even reach the founders. Gong just raised $250M at a $7.25B valuation, has $332M in revenue, owns the customer relationships, and real-time call assistance is the most obvious product extension they could build. Salesken, Balto, and Cresta are already shipping real-time features from different angles. When I ask "what important thing does the market currently get wrong about this problem?" the answer comes back empty. Nobody disagrees that real-time AI during sales calls is valuable. The entire conversation intelligence industry is converging on it. That's not a contrarian bet -- that's a feature roadmap item for four different well-funded companies. The best asymmetric returns come from being right when the crowd is wrong. Here, the crowd already agrees with the thesis.

The specific knowledge question is where this falls apart for me. Three TU Delft CS graduates built a Discord bot that scaled to 30K users, then pivoted into voice AI, then into sales call assistance. That's a journey toward a problem, not a journey from deep inside one. What do these founders know about B2B sales that a senior product manager at Gong doesn't? What non-obvious insight do they have about how enterprise sales reps actually think, fail, and recover during live calls? The dossier shows competent engineers iterating toward an application domain -- but the domain knowledge runs in the wrong direction. They understand real-time systems. They don't appear to understand sales. When I backed Stack Overflow, Joel Spolsky and Jeff Atwood weren't just building developer tools -- they had spent years living inside the developer knowledge problem, writing about it, understanding its texture at a level that made their product decisions non-obvious to outsiders. Caretta's founders are technically capable generalists applying engineering skill to a domain they've studied from the outside. That's teachable knowledge, not specific knowledge.

The leverage architecture is linear SaaS -- per-seat subscriptions for sales teams. Each new customer requires onboarding, integration, Slack setup, knowledge base population. The "compounding organizational advantage" the company describes is real but narrow: it's customer-specific data accumulation that creates switching costs, not platform-level leverage that compounds across the network. One customer's accumulated call data doesn't make the product better for another customer. Compare this to how Notion works: every template a user creates is potentially useful to millions of other users. Every developer answer on Stack Overflow compounds the value for every future developer. Caretta's data flywheel is siloed per customer -- it's a retention mechanism, not a leverage mechanism. That's the difference between a moat and a wall.

The strongest bull case would require believing three things simultaneously: first, that Gong's $7.25B architecture is sufficiently rigid that adding real-time features would require a ground-up rebuild rather than an incremental extension. Second, that the founders' iteration from VoysAI to Caretta has given them latent product intuition about sales workflows that hasn't surfaced publicly yet. Third, that the enterprise sales cycle problem -- four people selling against established vendor relationships -- can be solved through bottom-up Slack adoption before incumbents close the gap. If all three were true, you'd have a genuine timing window. But "if three unlikely things are simultaneously true" is not the basis for an asymmetric bet. The timing window for real-time call AI is open to everyone with LLM access, not specifically to Caretta.

What I look for in a technology bet is compounding difficulty -- the kind where each year of operation makes replication harder, not just switching more expensive. Caretta's GitHub wraps NVIDIA's Parakeet model for transcription and uses standard RAG for knowledge retrieval. These are commodity capabilities. The engineering challenge is real -- sub-second latency in a live call is hard -- but it's engineering difficulty, not scientific difficulty. A well-resourced team at Gong or Cresta could replicate the technical approach in months. The founders are building a feature, not a platform. Features get absorbed by incumbents. Platforms create new categories of leverage that didn't exist before.

I respect the team's technical competence and their persistence through the VoysAI pivot. Three technical co-founders building together from university is a healthy foundation. But I invest in founders whose specific knowledge makes them the only people who could build this particular thing -- and I invest in companies that create new forms of leverage, not better versions of existing tools. Caretta is a competent team building an application-layer product in a consensus category against well-funded incumbents, with no visible domain advantage and no platform dynamics. That's a pass.
