# Nine Fives -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Six gigahertz. That's the ceiling on both of Nine Fives' shipping products — the POE-ATTEN-6G and the POE-SWITCH-6G. In a world where 5G mmWave testing demands 24–40 GHz and satellite systems push well beyond that, Nine Fives has built instruments that can't serve the most transformative segments of the RF test market. This isn't a temporary limitation — it reflects a product architecture scoped for a comfortable niche, not for capturing a discontinuity. When I backed Rocket Lab, Peter Beck wasn't building a rocket that could only reach low Earth orbit if you squinted; he built the Rutherford engine with electric turbopumps and 3D-printed combustion chambers, a fundamentally novel approach to cost reduction that created a new market for dedicated small-sat launch. Nine Fives has built an attenuator with an Ethernet port. The engineering is competent. The ambition is not.

Andrew Kurtz and Noah Levy both came from SpaceX — Kurtz on the Falcon 9 payloads team, Levy as a Senior RF Engineer on satellite hardware development. Both MIT and Cornell educated, roughly a decade each in aerospace. I respect the pedigree, but it triggers one of my core concerns: these are domain experts executing a predictable playbook. They experienced RF test bench frustration at SpaceX, and they built the tool they wished they'd had. That's a fine origin story for a lifestyle hardware business. It's not the origin story of a company that changes how millions of people live. I've said repeatedly that when I have to choose between domain expertise and better thinking, I pick better thinking. Nine Fives shows no evidence of the outsider irreverence I funded at Mach Industries, where a 19-year-old dropout attacked Lockheed Martin's monopoly precisely because he didn't know what couldn't be done. Kurtz and Levy know exactly what can be done — and that's exactly what they're doing.

Nine Fives' bull case deserves honest construction. The Innovator's Dilemma argument is real: Keysight pulls $5.4B in annual revenue from a full-stack test platform that depends on proprietary software licenses, and Mini-Circuits manages 60,000+ catalog products with structurally deprioritized per-product software investment. Nine Fives' zero-install, REST-API-first architecture directly cannibalizes those software margins — exactly the kind of business model conflict that prevents incumbents from responding. The DevOps-ification of hardware test, with headless Linux CI/CD pipelines and containerized test orchestration, is a genuine secular shift that creates demand for instruments with native API access. If Nine Fives executes well and expands from two products to a full portfolio of networked instruments — signal generators, spectrum analyzers, power meters — they could become the platform for the modern automated test bench. Mini-Circuits at $111M–$400M estimated revenue demonstrates there's meaningful money in RF components without venture scale. But I checked my Known Exceptions, and none apply. DoorDash overrode my technical novelty requirement because the consequence magnitude of transforming food access was planetary-scale. Nine Fives' consequence magnitude, even in the most generous reading, is "RF engineers in aerospace and telecom have a better day at work." That's a $500–$5,000 instrument sale, not a world-changing outcome.

The dossier's own assessment names the core vulnerability plainly: "Adding a REST API to an existing product line is an engineering project, not a fundamental barrier." Nine Fives' differentiation versus Mini-Circuits' RCDAT series and Vaunix Lab Brick devices is a better software interface on comparable hardware. That's a 2x improvement in setup experience, not a 10x leap in capability or cost. The TAM for the broader RF test equipment market is $4B growing to $8.5B, but Nine Fives' serviceable market — networked programmable attenuators and switches — is characterized in the dossier as "likely in the low hundreds of millions." Even capturing a dominant share of a low-hundreds-of-millions niche doesn't produce the asymmetric returns I require. I don't do investments where success means a moderate outcome. I want to either lose my money or make 50 times my money. Nine Fives is designed, by its very architecture, to produce neither.

Nine Fives does not make anything expensive free. RF test equipment that's easier to configure is not the same as RF testing that costs a tenth of what it does today. My most passionate investments — Impossible Foods making protein without animal agriculture, Commonwealth Fusion Systems pursuing limitless energy, Curai Health delivering AI primary care at marginal cost — all converge on collapsing the cost of a basic human need. Nine Fives collapses the cost of driver installation. The one verifiable condition that would flip me to Invest: if Nine Fives demonstrated a technical architecture that could deliver AI-driven automated RF test characterization — eliminating the $200K/year RF test engineer entirely, not just giving them a nicer API — I would reconsider. That would be a consequence worth funding. What they've shown me is a hardware product company with a narrow moat, domain-expert founders following an incremental playbook, and a ceiling both literally (6 GHz) and figuratively.
