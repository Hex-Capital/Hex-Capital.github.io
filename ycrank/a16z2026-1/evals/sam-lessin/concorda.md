# Concorda -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Legal AI is the most consensus-hot category in venture capital right now. Full stop. Legaltech funding hit $4.3B in 2025, up 54% year-over-year. Harvey just closed at an $11B valuation with $190M in ARR. EvenUp is north of $2B. Thomson Reuters paid $650M for Casetext and already has the federal judiciary contract. AI adoption among legal professionals went from 19% to 79% in two years. Every sophisticated investor on Sand Hill Road is tripping over themselves to fund legal AI. So when Concorda walks in pitching "the AI operating system for trial lawyers," the first question isn't whether it's a good product — the $204K annualized revenue in three months suggests it might be. The question is whether there's any mispricing left in this category. And the answer is obviously no. The upside is already in the price. Every dollar of expected value in legal AI has been bid up by Harvey's $11B and EvenUp's $2B. Concorda is buying into a market where the consensus is already correct, which means even if they execute perfectly, the risk-reward is symmetric at best.

The primitive test is where Concorda really loses me. What new behavior is being created here? AI-assisted document drafting for litigation — Case Command, Motions & Briefs, Discovery modules — is an application of existing LLM capabilities to existing legal workflows. Samuel Oh says he "spent years drafting motions knowing there had to be a better way." That's a product insight, not a category invention. Venmo created a verb. Solana created a new consensus mechanism. Concorda creates... faster briefs? That's "better CRM" energy. Harvey, CoCounsel, EvenUp, Supio, Crimson — they're all building variations of "apply AI to legal work." Concorda's litigation-specific focus is positioning, not primitive creation. Nobody is going to say "the Concorda of X" because the category already has a name: legal AI.

Samuel Oh's Harvard CS plus UChicago Law background and Ke Ma's Northwestern Law JD plus McKinsey stint make them credentialed and clearly smart. But smart people building a sensible product in a validated market is the definition of boring in my framework. The sane-insane ratio requires insanity in the idea — something that makes other investors say "that's fucking crazy." Nobody hears "AI for litigation" and thinks it's crazy. They think it's obvious. That's the problem. A sane person with a sane idea in a consensus-hot market is the least interesting configuration possible. If Oh and Ma were building, say, an AI system that autonomously files and prosecutes patent cases without human attorneys — something that would make the bar association lose their minds — then we'd be talking. Instead, they're building what every legal tech founder is building, just with more litigation specificity.

Concorda fails the AI kill test decisively. Harvey has relationships with 8 of the top 10 US law firms and $190M in ARR. Adding litigation-specific execution modules to Harvey's platform is a product sprint, not a strategic pivot. Thomson Reuters already owns Westlaw and CoCounsel. The dossier itself acknowledges "no structural barrier (business model cannibalization, regulatory moat) prevents them from building one." LLM-based document generation gets cheaper and more accessible every quarter. The firm-specific voice models create mild switching costs, but "we fine-tuned the LLM for your firm's tone" is a feature any platform can offer. The dual services/SaaS model is the one structurally interesting move — actually practicing law creates regulatory complexity that pure software companies avoid — but it also exposes Concorda to malpractice liability and state bar requirements that could strangle them before they scale. It's a moat that might also be a trap.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. $204K annualized revenue in three months from a two-person team is real. That's not vaporware — someone is paying for this. The services-plus-SaaS hybrid means Concorda embeds in actual case workflows rather than sitting as a tool on top, which creates stickier relationships than Harvey's platform approach. Litigation is genuinely underserved compared to contract review and legal research, and the depth of Concorda's modules — especially the USPTO integration for patent litigation — could create a niche Harvey is too horizontal to fill well. Ma's prosecutorial experience at the Cook County State's Attorney's Office gives real courtroom credibility that most legal AI founders lack. If Harvey stumbles on litigation depth, if the services model builds irreplaceable client relationships, and if the litigation vertical is large enough to sustain a standalone company, Concorda could build a meaningful business. But "could build a meaningful business" in a consensus category is not the same as "represents asymmetric risk-reward." The downside scenario — Harvey adds litigation modules, Crimson and three other YC companies compete for the same niche, LLMs commoditize document generation further — is at least as likely and isn't priced into the deal.

Capital discipline is actually where Concorda looks best through my lens. Two people, $204K annualized revenue, three months in. They're generating cash from the services component, not burning through venture money waiting for product-market fit. That's the kind of capital efficiency I respect — build something, charge for it, survive. If this were the last money they ever raised, the services revenue could keep the lights on while they build the platform. But capital discipline alone doesn't make a deal when the category is consensus, the product isn't a primitive, and an $11B incumbent can replicate the offering. The most capital-efficient company in a commoditizing category still loses.

Concorda is a competent team building a reasonable product in the most consensus-hot category in venture. That's exactly backward for my framework. The two dimensions that kill this deal are Consensus Mispricing — legal AI has zero mispricing left with Harvey at $11B — and Primitive Creation — AI-assisted litigation is an application, not a new category. No Known Exception applies because Concorda isn't creating a new behavioral primitive (unlike Slack) or a new social behavior (unlike Clubhouse). Pass.
