# Wealor -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Wealor is the 2026 archetype of my Pass Example -- "AI-native operating system for wealth managers" is the exact construction Zero to One was written against -- and the only thing keeping it off the floor is that Karim Bouri and Pierre Depuydt are credentialed Polytechnique/Berkeley operators rather than first-batch undergraduates. The high-low signature is brutal: founder pedigree slightly above the YC median, contrarian secret functionally absent. That pairing names the company. Wealor is a competent French team executing a consensus pitch into the most crowded advisor-AI bracket of the cycle.

Stripe in 2010 is the HIGH rung on contrarian secret -- Patrick Collison named a specific incumbent (PayPal), a specific architectural failure (developer experience built for merchants, not engineers), and a thesis the market had actively rejected for half a decade. Wealor's positioning is the inverse mechanism: an "AI-native operating system" that "embeds with wealth management firms to fix workflow bottlenecks between their tools." That is consensus restated as product. Wealor's dossier names Jump ($105M raised), Zocks ($65M), Vise ($124.5M, $24M revenue), Orion's Denali AI (Feb 2026), Addepar's Addison (Mar 2026), Altruist's Hazel ($60/seat/mo via the Thyme acquisition), and Anthropic's own RIA plug-ins -- seven competitors converging on the same advisor desktop in the same quarter -- and Wealor's differentiation is "horizontal across the stack." When the answer to "what truth do very few people agree with you on" is "advisors want fewer tabs open," there is no secret, only mimesis.

The Pass Example failure mechanism is moat-terminating-at-"we have a better model" -- when the durability story collapses into model quality, any competitor closes the gap in nine months. Trace this through Wealor: the dossier's moat section literally reads "No defensibility signals found in public sources beyond founder domain experience." Orion sits on $5.8T AUM across 8M accounts. Addepar sits on $9T across 1,400+ firms in 60 countries. Wealor proposes to sit *across* both of them as a horizontal layer -- which means at the moment Wealor matters, Orion and Addepar will cannibalize their own per-module licensing rather than let a Series A startup arbitrage their stacks. The Anthropic RIA plug-in release in April 2026 ratifies the commoditization vector: foundation-model vendors are shipping the workhorse capability directly into the channel Wealor needs to own. There is no pricing-power story here on a five-year horizon.

Definite plan -- mechanism analysis. Wealor's eighteen-month compounding path requires (a) signing a lighthouse RIA against Jump/Zocks's multi-year runway, (b) building integrations into Orion and Addepar APIs while those vendors are actively launching competing native AI, and (c) defending against Altruist's $60/seat custodian-bundled pricing. The dossier surfaces zero named technical milestones, zero disclosed pilots, zero customer logos. "Forward deployed engineers for RIAs and family offices" is the language of services revenue, not definite-optimism roadmap. Wealor will iterate to PMF -- which is the diagnostic phrase, not a strategy.

Founder lineage -- counterfactual. For Wealor's lineage score to flip a tier, Karim Bouri would need to have shipped a product to scale -- not "ML work at CANAL+" and "structured private investments for wealth managers," which is buyer-side exposure plus IC engineering, not operator pedigree. Pierre Depuydt's profile -- Tiffany & Co. finance, BCG strategy, French military deputy section chief -- is the impeccable European generalist resume that Founders Fund systematically passes on because none of it is a shipped-to-scale signal. Compare to the Asana override: Moskovitz had built Facebook. Wealor's founders have built nothing the dossier can name. The Polytechnique-Berkeley overlap is real co-founder chemistry; it is not lineage in the Levchin/Luckey sense.

Atoms-heavy / regulated -- single-anchor parallel. Palantir is the obvious comparator because both target a regulated customer base, but Palantir's moat is integration depth into classified workflows over fifteen years and a customer (USG/IC) Silicon Valley refused to touch. RIAs are SEC-regulated under the Investment Advisers Act, which is real -- recordkeeping, fiduciary boundary, custody rules -- but Wealor's dossier discloses no compliance posture, no SOC2, no fiduciary-aware architecture. The regulatory surface is present as risk, absent as moat. Software-only into a saturated buyer with no certification depth is the de-weighted profile.

The 36-month forecast: Wealor is acqui-hired by Altruist, Orion, or a mid-tier RIA tech vendor for under $15M in talent-and-IP terms, or pivots to a forward-deployed-engineering services boutique serving three to five family offices at sub-$3M ARR. The mechanism producing this is the seven-named-competitor convergence plus the Anthropic-plug-in commoditization plus the absence of any named secret -- Wealor cannot outspend Jump's $105M into the same buyer, cannot out-integrate Orion's installed base, and cannot out-price Altruist's custodian bundle, so the exit vector is talent absorption.

The strongest bull case: Karim's "forward deployed engineers" language is not throwaway -- it is the Palantir playbook applied to RIAs, and the dossier's note that Wealor "embeds with wealth management firms" suggests a deliberate services-led wedge that compounds into the integration-depth moat I claim is absent. Palantir looked like consulting for a decade before the software moat became visible. The dismantling: Palantir's wedge worked because the USG had no alternative vendor with comparable trust and clearance; RIAs have Jump, Zocks, Vise, Orion, Addepar, Altruist, and Anthropic all offering parallel wedges with vastly more capital. The structural underservedness that justified Palantir's services-as-moat does not exist in wealth-tech in 2026. One verifiable condition would flip me: Wealor signs a top-50 RIA (>$10B AUM) on a >$250K ACV multi-year contract within twelve months, with the founders naming the specific Orion or Addepar workflow failure mode the customer is paying to escape. Absent that, this is the canonical pass.
