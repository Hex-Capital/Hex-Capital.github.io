# Silmaril -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Five AI security startups acquired for a combined $1.4B+ in eighteen months -- Protect AI by Palo Alto Networks for $500M, Robust Intelligence by Cisco for $400M, Prompt Security by SentinelOne for $180M, CalypsoAI by F5 for $180M, Lakera by Check Point -- and that acquisition wave is the most structurally interesting signal in Silmaril's dossier. Incumbents buying rather than building tells you the technical complexity of real-time prompt injection defense is high enough that build-vs-buy favors buy. But it also means those capabilities are now bundled into enterprise security suites with existing sales channels and millions of customers. The question I ask about Silmaril is whether this consolidation marks the end of the market or the end of the first generation of it. My read is somewhere in between: the agentic AI shift -- multi-step tool-calling agents, MCP connectors, confused-deputy attack patterns -- creates genuinely new attack surfaces that the acquired chatbot-era guardrails were not designed for. Lakera's 98%+ detection on input-pattern matching doesn't help when the attack vector is an indirect injection flowing through a tool call result in a multi-agent chain. Silmaril's framing -- "does this execution lead to a harmful outcome?" rather than "does this input look malicious?" -- is architecturally correct for this shift. But the market isn't being ignored. Straiker AI raised $21M from Lightspeed and Bain Capital Ventures for a dual attack-sim and runtime defense product. When that kind of capital flows in at seed, you're past the nonobvious window and into recognized-but-early territory.

Silmaril's infrastructure-layer positioning has the right shape but unproven depth. The 5-line SDK integration into LangGraph, coverage across inputs, tool calls, MCP, and internal agents, and self-hosted deployment option all point toward middleware that embeds in agentic workflows rather than a point solution layered on top. The data flywheel claim -- autonomous red-teaming agents generating proprietary attack data per deployment, training a compact classifier that blocks novel patterns "in under an hour" -- is the mechanism that could create compounding switching costs over time. If Silmaril's accumulated threat intelligence from diverse deployments becomes the moat, you get the network-effect dynamic I look for in infrastructure investments: more customers make the product better for all customers. This is structurally analogous to how I think about Stripe's compounding advantage -- the payment data across millions of merchants creates fraud detection capabilities that individual merchants can't replicate. But there's a critical difference: Stripe embedded in business-critical payment flows where switching means re-plumbing revenue infrastructure. Silmaril's 5-line integration is lightweight in both directions. At pre-seed, the flywheel is entirely theoretical with no public evidence of production deployments generating this data.

Aum Upadhyay's claim of building the security and privacy framework at AWS that "prevented over $1.8B in damages" maps directly to the problem space, and Eduardo Velasco's low-latency ML experience at Amazon -- "models generating $400M in annual revenue" -- is precisely the skill required to deliver 20ms classifier overhead in production. Silmaril's founders have domain expertise at the intersection of security and low-latency ML from operating at Amazon-scale, which is the right background for this specific technical challenge. What I don't see is startup scaling experience. Building frameworks at AWS is fundamentally different from navigating the organizational and go-to-market challenges of scaling a startup from two people to two hundred. No prior exits, no evidence of previous startup founding. The shared Amazon tenure provides a working relationship, and both founders have clear technical credibility, but the scaling gap is a real factor -- especially in enterprise security sales, where the founder-led Cal.com booking page will need to evolve into a structured sales motion to compete with Palo Alto's and SentinelOne's existing enterprise sales forces.

Silmaril's single-miracle test is where my concern concentrates. The designed-for miracle is the technology: does the multihead classifier plus autonomous red-teaming actually deliver 99% detection at 20ms latency? Security professionals in the YC LinkedIn post comments raised valid concerns about "validation methodology and deterministic reproducibility," and the 99% vs. 50% comparison lacks independent verification. If the technology works as claimed, that's one miracle -- and the founders' backgrounds suggest they can build it. But Silmaril also needs enterprises to choose a standalone, developer-first security product over bundled capabilities from their existing cybersecurity vendor. That's a distribution challenge layered on top. And there's platform risk: if Anthropic or OpenAI ship effective native prompt injection defense -- and both are investing in safety layers -- the need for a third-party guardrail diminishes, particularly for single-model deployments. The multi-model, multi-framework argument partially mitigates this, but it's not a complete answer. I count roughly 1.5 to 2 miracles, which pushes past my comfort zone. For comparison, when I invested in Harvey, the technology miracle was happening exogenously (foundation model capability improving on its own curve), so Harvey needed zero technology miracles of its own -- just execution. Silmaril needs its own technology to work AND to win a distribution battle against incumbents that already own the customer relationship.

The bull case an advocate would make: Silmaril sits at the exact intersection of my "boring but critical" and "regulated market moat" patterns. Security is unglamorous, operationally essential, and creates compliance-driven switching costs once integrated. The acquisition wave validates market demand while creating a gap for a developer-first, agentic-native solution that post-acquisition products will be slow to build. Silmaril's RL-based self-healing approach is technically differentiated from the static guardrails that the incumbents acquired, and the data flywheel creates a compounding moat that widens with each deployment. The customer testimonial -- "prevented 8 breaches, blocking $28M in damages" in two months -- suggests real production impact. And the TAM is massive: $8.65B in generative AI cybersecurity today, growing at 26.5% CAGR to $35.5B by 2031. At pre-seed, this has the structural elements that warrant conviction. That bull case has merit -- I've seen "boring infrastructure in compliance-heavy markets" produce exceptional returns with Gusto and Checkr. But the distinguishing factor in those cases was a clear absence of well-funded competition. Silmaril faces Palo Alto Networks ($500M acquisition), Cisco ($400M acquisition), and SentinelOne ($180M acquisition) -- three of the most formidable enterprise security sales forces on the planet now holding AI security products. The condition that would flip me to Invest: independent third-party validation of the 99% detection claim at 20ms latency, combined with three named enterprise customers generating recurring revenue. That would convert the technology miracle from theoretical to proven and demonstrate that developers are choosing Silmaril over bundled incumbent solutions.

Silmaril scores highest on market timing -- the agentic AI shift is a real structural catalyst, even if it's no longer fully nonobvious -- and lowest on margin architecture, where no public evidence exists. The decisive dimensions are Nonobvious Market Timing (real but recognized structural shift, not the kind of dismissed market where I find highest alpha) and Single-Miracle Viability (technology risk plus distribution risk against $1.4B+ in acquired incumbent capabilities). The founders have the right domain background but need to prove they can build and scale a company, not just build a framework inside one.
