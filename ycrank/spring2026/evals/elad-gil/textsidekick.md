# Sidekick -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Yourco raised a $6M Series A in March 2026 -- one month before Sidekick entered this YC batch -- and already operates SMS infrastructure with an established frontline worker customer base. TeamSense has ~$9M in funding with the same SMS delivery channel. Beekeeper has raised $100M+. When I see a pre-seed company entering a market where multiple funded competitors already own the distribution channel and the customer relationships, I have to ask what structural shift makes Sidekick's timing different from simply being late. The dossier's "why now" case rests on LLM capabilities crossing a quality threshold for RAG-based Q&A -- and that's real, but it's also the most widely understood technology shift in venture right now. Every SMS platform serving frontline workers can add an AI-powered Q&A feature as an incremental product extension. Sidekick's SMS delivery is a distribution choice, not a structural moat. Compare this to Harvey entering legal tech: that market had been a graveyard for a decade because prior AI couldn't meet the precision bar. Frontline worker communication has been *actively funded* throughout this period. The window Sidekick is targeting is open, but it's visible to everyone -- including competitors with more capital and existing customer bases.

Sidekick's architecture sits on top of two infrastructure layers it doesn't control: LLM providers and SMS carrier networks. The core stack -- RAG over uploaded documents, delivered via Twilio or a similar aggregator -- is reproducible with commodity components. A2P 10DLC registration requirements, per-message costs, and carrier filtering policies all represent external dependencies that constrain margins and scale. This is the opposite of the infrastructure-layer positioning I look for. Stripe became the payments API that thousands of companies built on; Gusto became the payroll infrastructure with deep regulatory integration. Sidekick is an application consuming two infrastructure layers without owning either. The per-customer knowledge base creates switching costs -- once a manufacturing facility has encoded its institutional knowledge through Sidekick's escalation-learning loop, migrating is painful -- but these are per-deployment switching costs, not the compounding network effects that infrastructure companies generate. Each new Sidekick customer starts their data flywheel from zero.

Justin So's family connection to manufacturing -- his father owns a facility that inspired the product -- provides an authentic entry point and likely explains the 19-location, 400+ worker footprint. That's real pre-seed traction for a solo founder with a UPenn MS in CS. But the bull case requires me to look past several structural concerns. The strongest argument for Sidekick: the SMS channel choice is genuinely contrarian in a market dominated by app-based platforms, eliminating the adoption barrier that plagues Beekeeper and Axonify for workers without smartphones or data plans. The 12+ language support and multimodal input (photos, voice memos) are specific to industrial environments. The escalation-learning loop -- unanswered questions routed to managers, then learned from -- creates a compounding per-customer data advantage. If Sidekick can capture enough manufacturing facilities before Yourco and TeamSense ship competing AI features, the accumulated institutional knowledge becomes genuinely sticky. I've seen "boring but critical" infrastructure in unglamorous categories -- the Checkrs and Gustos -- generate defensible businesses precisely because prestige-seeking founders avoid these markets.

Here's why the bull case doesn't override my structural concerns. Justin So is a solo founder with a finance background at Arm and no public evidence of prior startup experience or scaling operations. Sidekick's team size is 1, and the company is not currently hiring. Building an AI product requiring NLP engineering, SMS infrastructure management, multilingual support, enterprise sales, and customer success simultaneously is an enormous scope for one person. I don't require every founder to have scaled a company from 90 to 1,500 employees, but I've seen enough companies at this stage to know that a solo founder without startup or scaling experience, entering a market with multiple funded competitors, faces compounding execution challenges. The single-miracle test is mixed: the technology miracle (LLMs good enough for industrial Q&A) is happening exogenously, which is favorable, but Sidekick still needs a distribution miracle against established competitors AND sufficient AI accuracy for safety-adjacent industrial contexts. That's closer to two miracles than one.

Sidekick occupies a real market with real pain -- 2.7 billion deskless workers globally, 83% reporting missed information due to inaccessible communication channels -- but real markets with obvious pain attract competition. The one verifiable condition that would flip me to Invest: $10K+ MRR with 90%+ logo retention at six months, demonstrating that the escalation-learning data flywheel creates stickiness Yourco and TeamSense can't replicate by bolting on AI Q&A. Without that signal, Sidekick is an application-layer company in a market that isn't nonobvious, built by a solo founder without scaling experience, on infrastructure it doesn't own. The two dimensions that lock this into a Pass are Nonobvious Market Timing (the market is active and funded, not stuck) and Infrastructure-Layer Positioning (Sidekick consumes commodity infrastructure rather than building a layer others depend on).
