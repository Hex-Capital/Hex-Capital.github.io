# Robocurve -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Jay Chooi is a top-7% contributor to UK AISI's Inspect Evals repo, and that single line is the reason Robocurve is not the consensus YC AI-agent pass I rehearsed. This is a founder who *shipped the framework* the entire safety-evals world runs on, then decided the same primitive belongs one abstraction below the model — on the robot. The lowest signal, though, is that I cannot ride a Robocurve, I cannot photograph one at a Four Seasons café, and I cannot hand the WorldEvals PyPI package to a friend at dinner. Robocurve is a lived-engineering founder pointed at a B2B abstraction only Physical Intelligence, Skild, 1X, and Figure will ever touch — a Hanke-shaped biography aimed at a market that never leaves the lab.

The load-bearing analogy for Robocurve is **Niantic** — John Hanke ~2015 mattered because he had already shipped Keyhole into Google Earth, so location-data-as-play was not a memo he wrote, it was a decade of muscle memory. Jay Chooi's Inspect Evals contributions are the compressed version of that pattern: the safety-evals framework he helped build for UK AISI is exactly the thing he is now re-instantiating for robots as "Inspect Robots" v1. Where Robocurve diverges from the Niantic rung is that Hanke had *end users* — I could open Ingress on my phone in 2012 — while Chooi's users are four frontier labs whose CEOs I could name on one hand. The lived-experience signal is real but it lives in framework fluency, not in the buyer's shoes.

**The consensus YC AI-agent pass** fails because the product is pure software abstraction I cannot install, photograph, or hand to a friend. Robocurve inherits the same failure mode with one asterisk — the framework is open-source and pip-installable, so a robotics researcher can `pip install worldevals` tonight, which is more physical-world verifiability than a closed API. But a "normal human" per my own criterion cannot touch this. The dossier confirms only one pilot on a real robot; no video, no public leaderboard, no artifact I could show at a dinner table. Flock Safety I found because the founder's Wi-Fi name was visible at a café; Robocurve would require me to already be inside a frontier robotics lab to observe the product doing its job.

Robocurve's non-consensus score compounds or fails on one mechanism over 18 months: whether "trusted third-party score" becomes a *procurement* line item the way SOC2 became one for SaaS. If Physical Intelligence's next raise (multi-billion per CB Insights) forces LPs to ask "who verified the demo videos," Robocurve owns that question. If not, ManipulationNet's free academic consortium (Rice/NIST/MIT/Berkeley/CMU) caps pricing at zero and Robocurve is a beautifully engineered public good. The PBC registration — "Forecasting Physical Automation for Societal Preparedness" in the footer — actively telegraphs that Chooi has partially accepted the second outcome.

For Robocurve's CEO-authority score to flip a tier upward, one of two things has to be true: either Chooi's Rhodes-plus-CEO stack comes with a documented operating agreement giving him tiebreaker authority over Zhu, or Zhu's GPC paper (IEEE RAL 2025) so clearly makes him the technical lead that the split is unambiguous by function. Neither is in the dossier. Two Harvard co-founders with clean CS/robotics division-of-labor is the *good* version of the two-founder red flag I invoked after HQ Trivia, but I've watched clean title splits at Series A become custody battles at Series B when the money gets real.

The single-anchor parallel for Robocurve's capital-intensity signal is **Anduril** — Palmer Luckey chose to operate physical hardware in a regulated category most software VCs refused to touch, and the moat compounded because every deployed drone made the next one cheaper to build. Robocurve is on the same axis at a smaller magnitude: operating physical robot hardware and real-world task setups is exactly the "too hard or too unsexy" work software-only YC teams will not do. The dossier is honest that hardware operating cost per benchmark run is undisclosed, which is the exact question Anduril had to answer round after round.

My 36-month forecast: Robocurve gets acqui-hired by Physical Intelligence or Skild in the $15-40M range as an internal evals team, or pivots to selling to enterprise robotics buyers (Amazon Robotics, Symbotic, warehouse integrators) who need independent verification before committing capex. Neither outcome is venture-scale for a Founders-Fund-sized check, and the PBC wrapper is Chooi telling me in advance that he has priced the ceiling.

**The bull case that stings**: MLPerf became a $100M+ mindshare franchise inside the LLM boom despite starting as an academic exercise, and if Chooi's Inspect Evals lineage lets Robocurve become the MLPerf of embodied AI right as the 2028 general-purpose-robotics money floods in, the labs themselves will *demand* to be scored to fundraise. This is the polymath override that made DeepMind work despite the credentialed-founder red flag — Chooi's AI-safety-plus-astronomy-olympiad-plus-Rhodes-plus-Inspect-Evals stack is exactly the cross-domain compounding I described in the Generalist piece. The rebuttal that wins: MLPerf had NVIDIA as a paying anchor customer who *wanted* its chips scored; Robocurve's putative buyers are the labs themselves, whose incentive is to self-report favorably, and the dossier shows zero paying pilots. **The verifiable condition that flips me to Invest**: one signed paid contract, $250K+, with a named frontier lab (PI, Skild, 1X, Figure) or a named enterprise buyer (Amazon Robotics, Symbotic) inside the next nine months. Until then this is a magnificent open-source contribution wearing the costume of a company.
