# Isengard Industries Inc -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Francisco Serra-Martins and James Tennant already built and scaled Terminal Autonomy to $60M in revenue selling combat-deployed strike drones to the US Army, NATO, and the Ukrainian Armed Forces — and now they are doing it again at Isengard Industries with six platforms, an in-house TJ-100 turbojet at TRL 9, and 18,200 sq ft of distributed sovereign manufacturing across St. Petersburg and Warsaw. The dimensional signature here is unusual: Isengard Industries scores highest on schlep-zone exposure — Ukrainian front-line manufacturing, ITAR, allied-MoD procurement cycles — and lowest on hacker genealogy, because neither Serra-Martins nor Tennant has a GitHub trail and the @FranciscoSerraM X account has 101 followers. Isengard Industries is not a Lisp-hacker company; it is an operator-founder company in a part of the world that smells like the **Coinbase** (2012) compliance grind and looks nothing like **Reddit** (2005).

The HIGH rung on schlep blindness is **Coinbase** (2012), where Brian Armstrong's appetite for bitcoin compliance — KYC, banking partnerships, state-by-state money transmitter licenses — was the moat the rest of the YC batch routed around. Isengard Industries sits closer to that HIGH rung than any defense-adjacent company I've looked at this batch: ITAR/EAR export controls, dual-use munitions classification, Ukrainian frontline logistics, distributed manufacturing in a NATO frontline state (Warsaw), and the program-of-record sales cycle that takes years before the first big check lands. The divergence from **Coinbase** (2012) is that Armstrong was a solo-ish founder grinding into a regulatory frontier nobody had touched; Isengard Industries' founders have already been through this grind once at Terminal Autonomy ($60M revenue, customers cited on Tennant's LinkedIn), which means the schlep is not hypothetical — it has been priced in human-hours and validated in revenue.

The LOW rung on hacker genealogy is the polished AI-wrapper team I described in my pass example — no shipped artifact, no open-source footprint, credentials-by-degree. Isengard Industries is not that — Serra-Martins is a combat engineer and Tennant is an Army officer with a private-credit background — but it is not **Stripe** (2010) either, where Patrick Collison's Young Scientist Lisp prize at age 16 was the visible hacker fingerprint before the company existed. Searching Isengard Industries' dossier for the equivalent yields nothing: no GitHub for either founder, no open-source contributions, no Hacker News posting history, no demo code. The pattern I am watching for here is whether the company is engineering-led at the founder level or whether the engineering is being delegated. In hardware-defense companies this matters less than it does for **Dropbox** (2007), because the artifact is a turbojet not a deduplication algorithm, but it is the one dimension where Isengard Industries does not give me what I usually want to see.

Animal resourcefulness at Isengard Industries does not arrive in the form I am used to — there is no cereal-box stunt in this dossier — but it arrives in a form that is arguably stronger: Serra-Martins and Tennant already converted ADF combat-engineering experience into a $60M-revenue defense company with frontline-Ukraine production before applying to Isengard Industries' YC batch. That is not a pre-funding demo; that is a pre-funding exit-grade operating record. Over the next 18 months the mechanism compounds — every drone hour logged on the Russian-Ukraine front is a feedback loop competitors at Anduril ($61B) cannot get from Pendleton, and the founders' DoD/NATO procurement relationships from Terminal Autonomy collapse the 36-month sales cycle that kills most defense startups into something closer to 12.

Counterfactually, the organic-idea dimension at Isengard Industries would jump a tier if the dossier showed that Serra-Martins or Tennant had personally lost a unit, lost a friend, or had a specific operational failure in ADF service that the AQV 114 Manhack or AQ 101 Deleter is designed to solve — the way Drew Houston's lost USB stick made **Dropbox** (2007)'s origin a single sentence. The dossier instead shows ADF combat-engineering service and a prior $60M company in the same category, which is strong founder-market fit but slightly less than the surgically organic founder-pain story I score top-quartile on. It is closer to **Stripe** (2010) — Collisons had been hacking commerce since **Auctomatic** (2007) — than to the USB-stick anecdote, and that is sufficient.

The cofounder configuration at Isengard Industries parallels the **Stripe** (2010) brother-pair on one axis that matters more than people credit: Serra-Martins and Tennant already shipped a hard thing together at Terminal Autonomy and did not break up. Two additional co-founders are undisclosed on the website, which is a minor flag but not the **Kiko** (2005)-era solo-founder problem; the load-bearing pair has documented two-company tenure, University of Sydney overlap, and complementary coverage (Serra-Martins technical/military, Tennant finance/military). This is the configuration that survives the bad month.

My 36-month forecast for Isengard Industries: alive, not acquired, $40-90M revenue selling munitions and counter-UAS to Poland, the Baltics, Ukraine, and one or two US program-of-record line items via the founders' prior NATO relationships, raising a Series A in the $250-500M valuation range — squeezed on the top by Anduril ($61B, May 2026) and Helsing ($18B, May 2026) on the exquisite end and by Ukrainian/Chinese workshops on the commodity-FPV end, surviving in the middle as the sovereign-manufacturing specialist. The mechanism that produces this outcome is the schlep moat compounding faster than the hacker-genealogy gap matters, because at this stage in defense the binding constraint is procurement access and frontline iteration, not Lisp.

The strongest objection to investing in Isengard Industries is the Terminal Autonomy overlap — Serra-Martins and Tennant ran a company with the AQ-400 Scythe in Ukraine, and Isengard Industries' AQ/XQ line and Ukrainian manufacturing footprint look like the same business under a new cap table. Why a new company? IP entanglement, non-compete exposure, customer-contract assignment, and the **Kiko** (2005)-flavored question of whether this is a real second swing or a structural rearrangement. The dossier evidence that wins against this objection: the founders have explicitly expanded scope into counter-UAS and US/EU production, which Terminal Autonomy did not have, and YC plus Serra-de Estrela plus Balmoral (Tennant's own fund) all wrote checks into Isengard Industries — Tennant putting his own prior-fund money in is the closest thing to a cereal-box signal this dossier offers. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict to pass: if a Terminal Autonomy entanglement surfaces in diligence — pending litigation, IP assignment dispute, or unresolved customer-contract overlap — I do not write the check, because **Auctomatic** (2007) → **Stripe** (2010) worked precisely because the Collisons cleanly exited before the second swing.
