# Smart Bricks -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Mohamed Mohamed's career reads like a masterclass in agent-mode optimization: Blackstone, Goldman Sachs, McKinsey's AI practice, BCG, Atomico, Greycroft. Six accelerator programs in under two years — a16z Speedrun, Techstars, 500 Global, Google AI Accelerator, NVIDIA Inception, Microsoft GrowthX. Forbes 30 Under 30. This is someone who has spent a decade learning how to navigate institutions and collect institutional validation. That skill is real, and it's valuable — but it is the opposite of specific knowledge. Specific knowledge feels like play and looks like work to others. Collecting accelerator stamps feels like work and looks like work. The question Smart Bricks forces me to ask is whether Mohamed's institutional investing experience at Blackstone and Goldman constitutes non-replicable expertise that IS the company — or whether it's transferable process knowledge that any competent ex-Blackstone associate could deploy. I lean toward the latter. He didn't spend years building real estate tools because he couldn't stop. He left BCG in July 2024 and identified an opportunity. That's opportunity recognition, not obsessive domain immersion.

The leverage audit reveals a structural problem. Smart Bricks claims to automate "99% of the investment workflow" across Dubai, London, New York, and Miami. Real estate transactions, however, resist zero-marginal-cost replication in ways that pure software products do not. Every closed deal requires legal review, title transfer, regulatory compliance, counterparty negotiation — these are jurisdiction-specific, human-mediated processes. Smart Bricks can use AI to compress the analysis phase, but the transaction phase remains labor-intensive across four distinct regulatory regimes simultaneously. Compare this to Perplexity, where every additional query costs fractions of a cent with zero human involvement. Or Notion, where a new workspace requires zero marginal effort from the team. Smart Bricks' revenue, if transaction-based, scales with deals closed — and deals require people. EliseAI needed $392M and reached $100M+ ARR focusing on leasing automation in a single country. The idea that Smart Bricks will achieve comparable AI leverage across four countries with $5M and 20 people requires a leap of faith the dossier doesn't support.

The principal-agent diagnostic is where Smart Bricks most concerns me. The pattern I see — consultant at McKinsey, growth investor at Blackstone, angel investor in 30+ startups, advisor to a sovereign wealth fund — is the profile of someone who has been an exceptional agent across many contexts. The transition from agent to principal is possible but rare, and the evidence here tilts toward agent behavior. A principal would have been building in real estate before deciding to start a company. A principal would have a GitHub profile, or at minimum a technical co-founder whose engineering conviction matches their domain conviction. Smart Bricks has neither — no co-founder identified, no CTO named publicly, no technical leadership visible in any source. A team of 20 with a sole business-background founder means the entire technical stack is built by hired agents, not principals. "Learn to sell. Learn to build. If you can do both, you will be unstoppable." Mohamed can sell. The evidence for building is absent.

The strongest case for Smart Bricks is the democratization angle. Making institutional-grade real estate analysis accessible to individual investors who currently operate with "PDFs, WhatsApp threads and incomplete data" — that's a genuine gatekeeper removal. This fits the pattern of my AngelList, CoinList, and Polymarket investments: giving millions access to capabilities previously restricted to insiders. The $84,000 average capital gains per client, if real, demonstrates tangible value transfer to a previously underserved population. If Smart Bricks can compress three-to-six-month acquisition cycles to minutes for individual investors across global markets, the democratization is structural, not incremental. I also believe the "why now" is genuine — frontier LLM capabilities enabling agentic reasoning over unstructured real estate data is a real inflection, and declining inference costs make real-time analysis of a million data feeds economically viable for the first time.

The self-reported ~$12M revenue run rate demands examination. A company founded in July 2024 claiming $12M in revenue eighteen months later, having raised only $5M pre-seed, would be exceptional — so exceptional that the absence of any independent verification is itself a signal. The metric's definition is unspecified: is this gross transaction value, platform fees, or ARR? If it's genuine platform revenue, Smart Bricks would be one of the fastest-growing pre-seed companies in proptech history, and the $5M raise would be irrationally modest. The more parsimonious explanation is that this figure represents something other than recurring platform revenue — possibly gross transaction value or a projection. I've learned from the Wish experience that leverage criteria alone, without verified specific knowledge, produce unreliable outcomes. Wish had marketplace network effects and zero-marginal-cost digital matching — and still collapsed 95% from its IPO because the specific knowledge moat was absent. Smart Bricks rhymes with that pattern: real leverage potential, genuine market opportunity, but no non-replicable founder expertise anchoring the whole structure.

Smart Bricks lands in a zone where the access democratization thesis is real but the founder-company fit lacks the density I need to write a check. The two dimensions that lock this evaluation are specific knowledge productization — Mohamed's institutional finance experience is relevant but transferable, not the kind of non-replicable expertise that survives competitive pressure — and team-to-value leverage, where 20 people at pre-seed with a sole business-background founder across four countries represents the structural opposite of my shrinking-company thesis. If Mohamed had spent five years building real estate analysis tools as an obsessive side project, or if the team were four engineers shipping what twenty people currently do, my conviction would shift materially. Pass.
