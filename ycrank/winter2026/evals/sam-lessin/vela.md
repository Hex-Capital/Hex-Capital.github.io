# Vela -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the thing about AI scheduling assistants: they are the "hello world" of AI startups. If you asked a thousand people to brainstorm the most obvious AI application, half of them would say "an AI that schedules my meetings for me." Clara Labs tried this in 2014. x.ai tried it. Microsoft shipped Cortana with scheduling features in 2015. The category is so consensus that its history is littered with well-funded corpses. Vela is a sane team building the sanest possible idea, which in my framework is the single worst founder-idea combination. There is no non-consensus premium to capture here. Every VC on Sand Hill Road would nod along to this pitch, and that's precisely the problem -- if the idea doesn't provoke confusion or skepticism, the market has already priced it.

The founders are genuinely impressive. Gobhanu left Wharton's Huntsman program summa cum laude and has a legitimately unusual operational résumé -- founding a microfinance nonprofit at 14 that disbursed $400K+ across 77 countries shows real execution ability, not just academic credentials. Saatvik completed CMU's CS dual degree in two years with highest honors and built on AWS's ML Supercompute team. They're siblings with a long history of building together, which I value enormously -- speed-dating co-founder matches lack the relational resilience these two clearly have. The "sane" half of the equation is solid. But the "insane" half is completely absent. An AI scheduling agent for enterprise recruiters is incrementally better than existing tools, not categorically different from how the world currently works. Brian Armstrong proposing a regulated crypto exchange when everyone dismissed Bitcoin -- that's insane. Building a better Calendly is not.

My AI kill test is devastating here. Would this business be good if AI didn't exist? The answer is no -- without LLMs, there is literally no product. The AI doesn't sit on top of an independently valuable business; it constitutes the entire value proposition. This is the exact opposite of what I look for. Craftwork would be a painting company without AI. Metropolis would still have parking infrastructure. Vela without AI is an empty scheduling link page, which is just Calendly. And the incumbent threat is as severe as it gets in any category I evaluate: Google has Gemini plus Google Calendar plus every enterprise's email. Microsoft has Copilot plus Outlook plus Teams. Both already have the calendar data, the communication channels, and the distribution that Vela is trying to build from scratch. When I said on CNBC that "the winner of the AI race depends on consumer distribution," I was describing exactly this scenario. The company's multi-channel integration across email, SMS, WhatsApp, Slack, Teams, and phone represents real engineering work, but it's engineering that Google and Microsoft can replicate with a fraction of the effort because they already own half those channels.

Let me steelman this, because the strongest version of the bull case deserves genuine engagement. You could argue that Vela is building a new coordination primitive -- that "scheduling negotiation" is a fundamentally different product category from "booking links" or "calendar optimization," and that becoming the default cc: address for any complex scheduling conversation creates protocol-like lock-in. The Paradox AI acquisition by Workday opens a window in enterprise recruiting. The enterprise logos are real -- Netflix, LinkedIn, AWS, Scale AI aren't signing up for toys. If scheduling context data genuinely compounds into organizational intelligence that makes Vela increasingly irreplaceable, there's a data flywheel story. And the team's operational DNA -- coordinating microfinance across 77 countries is logistically closer to multi-party scheduling than most VC-backed founders' prior experience. If all of that were true, you'd have a company that looks less like Clara Labs and more like an early Venmo -- a new primitive for coordination that becomes invisible infrastructure. But I don't buy it. Scheduling preferences are thin data compared to payment graphs or social networks. The compounding dynamics aren't there. And Birchbox taught me the cost of narrative magnitude without structural moat: the subscription box concept was also real, also generating real revenue, also serving real customers -- and also trivially replicable by anyone with a mailing address and a credit card processor.

The narrative ceiling is constrained in a way that matters. Even the most generous expansion path -- from recruiting scheduling to sales scheduling to universal AI coordination -- runs directly into Google and Microsoft's roadmap. This isn't an infinity story; it's a feature story with a known expiration date tied to the next Copilot or Gemini release cycle. The per-seat SaaS model that's almost certainly the monetization path is precisely the business model architecture I've argued is dying. There are real usage signals -- "thousands of interviews each week" and enterprise adoption -- which at pre-seed is meaningful traction. But traction in a category where the structural dynamics are against you just means you're building faster toward the moment an incumbent absorbs your functionality. I'm passing.
