# Samora AI -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice is that this is a hot category with a lot of money already in it. Bland AI has $65M. Synthflow has $30M. Vapi has $20M and is YC-backed. When I see a company entering a space where four well-funded competitors are already building overlapping products, my default instinct is to walk away. I wrote in 2025 that the most impressive YC companies I'd seen weren't working on AI, and the reason is exactly this dynamic: consensus markets attract capital faster than differentiation can compound. Voice AI agents for call centers is not an idea that was hiding in plain sight because of schlep blindness. It's an idea that everyone already sees and everyone is already funding.

But then I look at how these particular founders found this problem, and the story is different from the typical "we noticed voice AI is trending" origin. These three people spent years together at I-Stem, building accessibility technology for multilingual populations with disabilities across 80+ countries. Kartik Sawhney -- who is blind, who was the first blind student to study science in his country, who went to Stanford for AI and then worked on Microsoft's Cortana team -- didn't sit down and brainstorm startup ideas in a hot space. He spent years learning that reaching diverse, multilingual populations through voice interaction is brutally hard. The multilingual capability isn't a feature they bolted on because market research said "differentiate on languages." It's where their entire working lives have been. Shakul worked at Sarvam, which focuses specifically on Indian language AI. The path from I-Stem to Samora is organic in a way that most voice AI startups' origin stories are not.

What I find genuinely interesting is the specific schlep they've chosen within this crowded market. Most voice AI companies build for English, add a few European languages, and call themselves multilingual. Real multilingual support -- dialects, code-switching mid-sentence, speakers from regions with limited connectivity -- is the kind of tedious complexity that developer-first platforms like Vapi and Bland structurally don't want to deal with. Their customers want clean API calls in English, not the messy reality of a UNICEF program reaching youth in multiple African languages. This is where I-Stem's experience creates a genuine advantage that looks small from the outside but compounds in deployment. The managed-service model for non-technical buyers (UNICEF, Government of India) reinforces this -- these are organizations that will never hire an engineering team to orchestrate voice AI, and they need it to work across languages that aren't commercially prioritized by the developer-first platforms.

Kartik's personal story is among the most compelling I've encountered at this stage. Getting from blind student denied science education in India to Stanford CS to Microsoft AI Research to building a nonprofit reaching half a million people -- that sequence demonstrates a kind of relentless determination that's hard to fake. When someone has overcome obstacles of that magnitude to get to the starting line, you're not betting on whether they'll quit when fundraising gets hard or a customer churns. The question is whether that personal resilience translates into the specific business-building resourcefulness a startup needs. I don't have a cereal-box story here -- no evidence of a creative pivot or a moment where the original plan failed and they invented something lateral. But the team of eight processing over a million calls with UNICEF and Signify (Philips) as named customers suggests they can execute.

The traction signals are meaningful if real. A million calls processed is not nothing. UNICEF, the Government of India, and Signify as customers are organizations with genuine operational needs, not logo farming. The VP at Signify describing how they used Samora to speak with "thousands of stakeholders instantly" sounds like real economic dependency, not a pilot. The $1.1M revenue figure is from Getlatka, which makes it unreliable, but even discounted it suggests the company is generating real money before YC -- at pre-seed, that matters.

The risk I can't dismiss is platform dependency and competitive compression. The entire stack is OpenAI: Whisper for transcription, GPT for reasoning, their TTS for voice output. That's not like Stripe building on banking infrastructure, where the banks were never going to build Stripe. OpenAI is actively developing voice agent capabilities. As foundation models get better at multilingual processing, the specific multilingual schlep that differentiates Samora could erode. The managed-service and institutional-customer positioning creates a moat that's more about relationships and deployment expertise than technology -- which is defensible but not the kind of compounding technical advantage I prefer.

If this company wins, it will be because the multilingual institutional market is structurally underserved by developer-first platforms, and Samora's I-Stem DNA gives them a permanent head start in understanding how to deploy voice technology to the organizations that need it most but are least equipped to build it themselves. That's a real thesis. It's not the biggest outcome in voice AI, but it could be a meaningful company serving a segment that the well-funded competitors are structurally misaligned to pursue.
