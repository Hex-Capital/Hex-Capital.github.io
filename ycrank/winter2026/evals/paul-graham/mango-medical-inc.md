# Mango Medical -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Mango Medical was founded in 2020. It's entering YC in Winter 2026. That's five or six years of existence before a pre-seed accelerator. For most YC companies, this would be a disqualifying signal -- either the founders couldn't gain traction, or the problem is so technically deep that it requires years of R&D before reaching market. In Mango Medical's case, both might be true. The question is whether the delay reflects a legitimate deep-tech timeline (building and validating AI for clinical-grade surgical planning is genuinely hard) or an inability to find product-market fit. The dossier doesn't resolve this. What I do see is a company that has been through XPRENEURS, Plug and Play, Stanford Ignite, and now YC -- a lot of programs for a company with no revenue and no FDA clearance. That pattern can mean the founders are resourceful about extracting value from every available support structure, or it can mean they're more comfortable in incubator environments than in the market. I can't tell which.

The first thing I evaluate is how founders found their problem. Adrian Kilian is a dentist. Not an orthopedic surgeon. Dentistry is adjacent -- it involves imaging, preoperative planning, and procedural precision -- but it's not the same domain. The company explicitly compares itself to Invisalign, which is a dental analogy, not an orthopedic one. This tells me the idea likely grew from Kilian observing how digital planning transformed dentistry and wondering if the same approach could work for orthopedic fractures. That's a reasonable intellectual transfer, but it's not the same as a surgeon who spent years frustrated by the manual CT-to-plan workflow and finally decided to fix it. The origin appears to be academic research at the Technical University of Munich -- the company spun off from that ecosystem. Academic spinoffs can work, but they carry a specific risk: the founders are solving a problem they studied rather than one they lived with. The schlep component is genuinely large -- FDA clearance, clinical validation, hospital procurement cycles, HIPAA, integration with existing surgical workflows. Nobody tackles that casually. But the problem wasn't hiding in plain sight from these specific founders; it was presented to them through an academic program. That's a meaningful distinction.

The $10M ARR LOI is the headline number, and it demands scrutiny. A letter of intent from a "top orthopedic company" sounds extraordinary for a pre-seed startup without FDA clearance. But LOIs in medtech are qualitatively different from LOIs in software. They typically say: "If you get FDA clearance, and if clinical validation meets our standards, and if you integrate with our implant portfolio, we intend to purchase." Every "if" is a potential multi-year delay or outright failure point. Formus Labs -- a direct competitor with deeper funding, actual FDA clearance since 2023, a Zimmer Biomet partnership, and $2.9M in real 2024 revenue -- took seven years from founding to clearance. The LOI tells me an orthopedic company has seen the technology and found it promising, which is a signal worth noting. But promising and purchasable are separated by a regulatory canyon that this company hasn't yet entered, let alone crossed.

What would have to be true for this to be a great investment? The bull case is that fracture reconstruction is genuinely underserved -- most AI competitors (Formus Labs, Enhatch) focus on joint replacement, which is more standardized and higher volume. Fracture surgery is messier, more variable, and harder to automate, which means the schlep deters competition exactly the way I'd want it to. If Mango Medical's agentic AI genuinely produces clinical-grade plans in minutes for complex fracture cases, and if they obtain FDA clearance, they'd own a niche that the bigger players haven't prioritized. The TU Munich research foundation could provide IP depth that's hard to replicate. And the orthopedic device companies have a proven acquisition pattern -- Brainlab bought TraumaCad, Materialise bought OrthoView -- which means a clear exit path exists. If the LOI converts, this company is worth multiples of what I'd pay today.

But the team composition gives me pause. The CEO's GitHub shows one public repository. The technical co-founder has no visible public repos. For a company building an AI pipeline that processes CT scans and generates 3D surgical plans, I want to see evidence that the founders are the ones writing the core algorithms -- not managing a research team that does. The TU Munich origin suggests there may be deep technical work underneath, but I can't verify it from what's available. A company whose core value proposition is a technical system should have founders who are visibly, demonstrably technical. I'm not seeing that here.

This is a company with a real schlep, a defensible niche, and a plausible path to acquisition -- but the organic problem-founder connection is weak, the timeline is long relative to visible progress, and the evidence of user pull is a contingent letter rather than actual usage. I'd want to see the technology working in a clinical setting, even informally, before writing a check.
