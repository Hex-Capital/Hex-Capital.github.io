# Proximitty -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The most interesting thing about Proximitty is also its most dangerous vulnerability: the entire differentiation rests on computer-vision browser automation that bypasses API integration — and Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google are all shipping exactly this capability as a platform feature. When I backed Juniper, Cisco had dismissed TCP/IP routing. The incumbents weren't building what we were building. Here, the foundation model providers are actively building what Proximitty is building, and they're doing it as a general-purpose capability that any developer can access. Proximitty's "proprietary computer vision" for browser automation is running directly into the exhaust plume of the most well-capitalized AI labs on earth. That's not an incumbent dismissing your approach — it's the ground you're standing on shifting beneath you.

Let me start where I always start: if Proximitty succeeds completely, what changes? Banks and fintechs spend less money on loan servicing agents. Collections rates improve by some percentage points. The $3-5 billion loan servicing and debt collection software market gets a better tool. That's a real business — potentially a very good one — but nobody's life fundamentally changes. The lending system still operates the same way. Borrowers still take loans, still default, still get collected on. The consequence of full success is a more efficient back office. I've passed on dozens of companies like this. A team comes in with strong domain credentials, a legitimate market, working technology, and I ask: so what? If you win everything you're playing for, you've built a $500 million enterprise software company in a competitive market. That's not what I invest my own money for. The ambition is precisely calibrated to the market size analysts publish in their reports, not to the scale of any underlying problem that demands a solution.

The founders are competent and well-matched to the problem — but that's part of what concerns me. Ho comes from McKinsey credit risk advisory and scaled Taptap Send from $75M to $200M ARR, which is a genuine operational credential. Zhang brings five years of Bloomberg infrastructure engineering and built ACI.dev to 4.7k GitHub stars, demonstrating real open-source AI agent expertise. This is a team that knows exactly what it's doing. But they're industry insiders building the next-generation tool for their own industry. McKinsey credit risk advisor builds AI for credit risk management. Bloomberg engineer builds enterprise financial infrastructure. There's nothing wrong with this — but the pattern I look for is the biochemistry professor who doesn't know what the food industry considers impossible, the New Zealander with no aerospace pedigree building rockets. Proximitty's founders have reduced the probability of building the wrong thing, but they may have also constrained the ambition to what the industry considers achievable. When your entire career has been inside the lending ecosystem, you optimize the lending ecosystem rather than replace it.

The strongest bull case: the no-API, no-code deployment model could be the Square insight for loan servicing. Square didn't invent payments — it made accepting payments trivially easy for anyone with a smartphone. Proximitty's browser automation approach could make AI-powered servicing accessible to every credit union and community bank without an integration project. If the compliance layer is genuinely robust across FDCPA, TCPA, CFPB, and state regulations, that's a non-trivial moat — regulatory complexity is one of the few barriers that doesn't get commoditized by better models. And the data flywheel is real in theory: per-borrower collections performance data could compound into a genuine advantage over time. If I squint, I can see a path where this team's domain expertise and technical execution build enough switching costs and data advantage to survive the platform risk from foundation model providers. But "survive the platform risk" is not the kind of success narrative that gets me excited. I want to fund companies that create the platform risk.

The timing is reasonable — LLM capabilities crossed production thresholds for financial conversations, browser automation became viable, and rising delinquency rates create buyer urgency. But Proximitty isn't ahead of the curve. They're arriving simultaneously with the technology wave, alongside well-funded competitors like Prodigal ($14.2M), Skit.ai ($47.6M, $22.4M revenue in 2024), and the looming presence of the foundation model labs themselves. When I invested in OpenAI in 2019, nobody else was writing checks. When I invested in cleantech in 2004, other VCs were laughing. Here, the category is already populated and the technology enablers are already consensus. That's not where my best returns come from.

This is a well-constructed company pursuing a real market opportunity with appropriate technology at reasonable timing. And that sentence is exactly why I pass. They've done everything right to build a successful business, and in doing so, they've ensured that the consequences of their success — while commercially meaningful — won't change how any industry fundamentally operates. Some problems demand a solution regardless of the odds. Loan servicing efficiency is not one of them.
