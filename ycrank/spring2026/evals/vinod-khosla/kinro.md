# Kinro -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Kinro is that they've made the safe choice. Harper raised $46.8 million to replace insurance brokers entirely -- that's the ambitious bet, the one where success means the distribution system is obsolete. Kinro chose to enable brokers and carriers. Sell technology to the incumbents. Don't threaten the channel partners. It's the approach that maximizes adoption probability and minimizes consequence of success. If Kinro succeeds completely, insurance gets sold 27% more efficiently and quotes arrive in 23 seconds instead of minutes. The brokers are happier. The carriers save on commissions. Nobody's life changes. That's a real business, but it's not why I write checks.

The genuine timing angle here deserves honest examination. AI platforms -- ChatGPT, Gemini -- are becoming surfaces where consumers make purchasing decisions, and this happened in the last 18 months. WTW's stock dropping 12% on ChatGPT insurance app announcements tells you the market recognizes this as a structural shift. Kinro is building the plumbing layer for regulated commerce on these platforms before most people understand the channel exists. That's the kind of "skate to where the puck will be" positioning I've rewarded before. And landing WTW -- one of the three largest insurance brokers on Earth -- as a pre-seed client is not nothing. But the puck metaphor only works if you're building something the world needs when it arrives. Kinro is building a better pipe, not a new system. When AI platforms mediate insurance purchases at scale, will the enabling technology layer capture transformational value? Or will it be commoditized between OpenAI's platform power above and carrier procurement leverage below? I've seen this squeeze kill picks-and-shovels companies in every technology wave.

The founding team is strong on paper -- Pierre-Alexandre from DeepMind working on Gemini for financial services, Parth building ML infrastructure at Zoox, Corentin as first employee at an insurance startup. That's exactly my concern. This is an expert team executing the expert playbook. A DeepMind researcher building AI agents, an insurance insider bringing industry relationships, a systems engineer connecting the pipes. Everyone is working within their prior domain. Nobody is attempting something the consensus considers crazy. Compare this to Peter Beck building rockets in New Zealand or Pat Brown engineering meat in a biochemistry lab. Kinro's founders know what the insurance industry considers possible and they're building within those boundaries. That's how you get a good business. It's not how you get a transformational one.

The bull case deserves a full hearing. If AI assistant platforms become the primary interface for consumer purchasing -- not just insurance but healthcare, financial services, legal -- then whoever builds the compliance-and-commerce layer for regulated verticals on those platforms is building critical infrastructure. Kinro could be the Stripe of AI-mediated regulated commerce. The 92% customer satisfaction score and 27% conversion rate suggest the technology genuinely works better than human agents for straightforward insurance sales. The carrier integration moat deepens with each client. And the regulatory complexity -- state-by-state licensing, UDAAP compliance, carrier-specific rules -- creates barriers that horizontal AI platforms won't cross. If that's the trajectory, Kinro at pre-seed is undervalued. But this requires Kinro to generalize beyond insurance enablement into something much larger than their current positioning suggests, and it requires AI platforms to remain open infrastructure rather than verticalizing into regulated commerce themselves. OpenAI building its own insurance app infrastructure would crush this thesis.

At the core, this is a vertical AI SaaS company making insurance distribution more efficient in a market with multiple well-funded competitors converging on similar technology. Harper has $46.8 million. COVU has $50 million. FurtherAI has $30 million from a16z. The underlying AI agent technology is increasingly commoditized. Kinro's differentiation -- compliance guardrails, carrier integrations, SOC 2 certification -- is real but incremental. It's the kind of moat that takes time to build and can be replicated with capital. I don't see the 100x outcome that justifies absorbing the risk of platform dependency, competitive convergence, and regulatory fragmentation simultaneously.

I pass on this one. The team is capable, the market is real, the timing on AI platform distribution is genuinely early. But Kinro has narrowed its ambition to fit comfortably within the existing insurance system rather than attempting to replace it. They're building a better interface for a process that will still exist in its current form if they succeed. Some of the smartest people I meet are working on problems that are too small for their talent. I'd tell this team: you understand AI agents, you understand regulated industries, you have WTW as a client. Stop enabling the incumbents and go build the thing that makes them irrelevant.
