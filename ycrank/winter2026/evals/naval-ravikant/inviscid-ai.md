# Inviscid AI -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is what the founders have published versus what the company needs them to know. Ziming Qiu is a real ML researcher -- fifth-year PhD at NYU, papers in IEEE and arXiv on camera pose estimation and medical image analysis. That's legitimate computational expertise. But physics-informed neural networks for computational fluid dynamics sit at the intersection of two deep disciplines: machine learning AND fluid dynamics. The published record covers one side. Where's the thermodynamics? Where's the turbulence modeling intuition? The company is named after a fluid dynamics concept -- inviscid flow -- but naming yourself after a domain is not the same as having spent years living inside it. Kabir Jain's public footprint amounts to "likes physics and AI." When I backed Extropic, the founders had spent their careers at the frontier of thermodynamic computing physics. When I invested in Stack Overflow, Atwood and Spolsky had been writing about developer tools for a decade. Here, I see capable ML researchers reaching into a domain where their documented knowledge doesn't compound. Anyone who's completed a graduate ML program could identify PINNs as a promising technique for CFD acceleration -- that's a literature review insight, not specific knowledge.

The leverage architecture concerns me almost as much. Real-time building simulation sounds like software leverage -- write the neural network once, deploy everywhere. But each building is physically different. Each data center has unique rack configurations, airflow patterns, sensor placements. The "Schedule Demo" GTM and the integration complexity of connecting IoT data streams to physics models suggest this scales more like an enterprise services business than a platform. Each deployment requires custom calibration, domain-specific tuning, customer-specific sensor integration. Compare this to Notion, where one product serves millions of users with zero marginal deployment cost, or to Alchemy, where one API layer serves every blockchain developer. Inviscid AI is building what appears to be a high-touch vertical SaaS with physics underneath -- which is fine as a business, but it's not the kind of leverage architecture that generates asymmetric returns.

The competitive field tells a clear story that this is consensus, not contrarian. Trane Technologies acquired BrainBox AI for $103M+. PassiveLogic raised $125M+ with backing from NVIDIA Ventures and Johnson Controls. 75F took $45M with Carrier and Breakthrough Energy Ventures on the cap table. Cadence, an $80B public company, already has a Reality Digital Twin Platform doing CFD for data centers. Smart money has already decided that AI-driven building optimization is a winning category. That's the opposite of what I look for. My best returns came from bets that looked wrong at the time -- Twitter when microblogging was dismissed as trivial, Uber when ride-sharing was illegal, Bitcoin when it was nerd money. Here, the consensus is fully formed and priced in. The specific PINN angle is somewhat differentiated, but NVIDIA's Modulus framework exists precisely to commoditize physics-ML inference, and PINNs are an active academic research area with published methodologies from multiple groups.

The strongest bull case: data center cooling is becoming the binding constraint on AI compute expansion, and whoever cracks real-time CFD-grade thermal optimization owns a critical chokepoint in the most important infrastructure buildout since the internet. The sales channel mismatch between traditional CFD vendors (selling per-seat licenses to engineers) and real-time operational optimization (selling per-building SaaS to facilities managers) creates a structural gap that a nimble startup could exploit before incumbents retool. Trane's $103M acquisition proves acquirers will pay handsomely. If the founders developed genuine fluid dynamics expertise through work that simply isn't in their public record, and if they focused relentlessly on data centers instead of spreading across HVAC, coastal infrastructure, AND storm surge forecasting, this could be a compelling acquisition target within three to five years. But the "if" is doing too much work. Three case studies in three different verticals from a two-person team signals passion for the underlying technique rather than obsession with a specific customer's pain -- which is precisely the founder-problem mismatch I filter against.

The multi-vertical diffusion is the tell. When I see a team of two showcasing HVAC vents, coastal infrastructure, and storm surge forecasting as their case studies, I see founders who are excited about physics-informed neural networks as a hammer looking for nails. That's the "passionate about founding" red flag inverted -- they're passionate about the technology rather than about making buildings more efficient or keeping data centers cool. The best deep-tech founders I've backed constrain themselves obsessively to one problem until they own it. Spreading across three verticals at pre-seed with two people is a choice that reveals priorities.
