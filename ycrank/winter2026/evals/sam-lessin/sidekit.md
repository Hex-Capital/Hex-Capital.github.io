# SideKit -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

SideKit is a developer tools SaaS company whose headline differentiator is an AI feature, priced on a subscription model, competing against Firebase (free, backed by Google), PostHog ($180M+ raised, open-source), LaunchDarkly ($330M raised), and Statsig (now owned by OpenAI after a $1.1B acquisition). If I were designing a company in a lab to test whether founders read my writing before pitching me, this would be it. It sits at the intersection of three categories I've spent the last two years publicly arguing are structurally broken: SaaS as a business model, AI as the core value proposition, and application-layer software with no physical-world anchor. The software IS the business, and the AI IS the pitch -- which means every structural trend I track is working against this company, not for it.

The sane-person-insane-idea calibration doesn't fire here. Ashish Selvaraj has Databricks and Microsoft intern experience and shipped a sudoku app to 50K installs -- that's a competent young engineer, not someone who built critical infrastructure at scale. The website claims engineers from "Jane Street, Citadel" but Ethan Pronev's credentials couldn't be independently verified, which is a due diligence flag I take seriously. More fundamentally, the idea itself isn't insane -- it's "bundle three existing developer tools into one lightweight SDK." Every developer tools company in history has pitched consolidation. PostHog is already executing this playbook with 138% year-over-year revenue growth and a hundred-million-plus in funding. When I backed Coinbase, Brian Armstrong was proposing something most of Silicon Valley thought was genuinely deranged. When I backed Helium, the idea of crowdsourced IoT wireless infrastructure sounded operationally impossible. "One SDK for mobile analytics and version management" provokes no skepticism from anyone -- it provokes a shrug and a question about how you beat Firebase, which is free.

The structural moat question is where this falls apart completely. My entire framework over the past two years has been built around one observation: AI commoditizes software faster than it creates defensible software categories. SideKit's three capabilities -- analytics, feature flags, version management -- are individually well-understood, collectively available from multiple providers, and technically replicable by a competent engineer in weeks. The "self-healing AI" feature that ships fixes as pull requests sounds impressive in a demo but is exactly the kind of obvious LLM application that every competitor with API access to Claude or GPT can replicate by next quarter. When I look at Craftwork, the moat is the painting crews -- software makes dispatch more efficient, but no LLM can hold a roller. SideKit has no equivalent. There's no physical operation, no protocol lock-in, no regulatory position, no proprietary data flywheel proven at scale. Seven GitHub stars and twelve commits is not an installed base that creates switching costs. This is a pure software business in a market where the floor is free (Firebase) and the ceiling is owned by companies with orders-of-magnitude more resources.

The strongest bull case would require believing three things simultaneously: that the wave of AI-assisted coding tools (Cursor, Copilot) is about to create millions of new indie developers who need dead-simple production infrastructure; that privacy regulation will structurally disadvantage Firebase's data-collection model, creating a genuine opening; and that SideKit's bundling of analytics plus version gating plus AI-driven feedback processing creates enough switching cost and data flywheel dynamics to defend against PostHog, which already bundles similar capabilities open-source. If all three are true, you could squint and see a world where SideKit becomes the default production toolkit for the long tail of mobile developers the way Stripe became default payments for internet businesses. The version-gating-for-free wedge is a reasonable GTM mechanic. But the Stripe analogy breaks down precisely where it matters: Stripe created a new primitive for internet payments with genuine regulatory and compliance moats. SideKit is bundling commoditized features on top of platforms it doesn't control, priced at $29-$99 a month -- the exact subscription SaaS model I've been arguing is dying. The narrative can expand, but it expands into territory already occupied by better-funded, faster-growing competitors with no structural reason to cede it.

I'll be direct: this is a pass. Not because the founders aren't smart -- they clearly are -- but because the structural logic runs the wrong direction on every dimension I care about. The business gets weaker, not stronger, as AI gets cheaper. The moat is software that other software can replicate. The differentiation is an AI feature that every competitor will match. The pricing is subscription SaaS in a market where the dominant player charges zero. I'd rather fund a painting company with a tech layer than a tech company competing against Google's free tier.
