# Asimov -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The thing that strikes me first about Asimov isn't the founders or the product — it's the competitive landscape. When I look at who else is in this space right now, I see micro1 at $100 million in annual recurring revenue shipping Ray-Ban glasses for the same use case, General Intuition sitting on a $134 million seed round, and Scale AI looming at $16.4 billion. The robotics data market was literally described as reaching "the tipping point" in late 2025. This is not an unpopular idea. This is not difficult to assess. This is a market where consensus has already formed and capital is flooding in. And my entire investing life has taught me that by the time everyone agrees something is important, the best entry window has usually closed. The companies I've done best with — SpaceX, Uber, Crusoe — were companies that made people look at me sideways when I wrote the check. Nobody is looking sideways at robotics training data in 2026.

I wish I knew more about these founders' stories. Lyem Ningthou came from Mississippi, attended a specialized math and science school, and built a GPS tracking system for school buses as an eighth grader — that early builder instinct is real, and making it from Mississippi to Berkeley tells me there's probably a story underneath that I'd want to hear. Anshul Verma's time at Scale AI gives him direct exposure to how data marketplaces actually work, and his BAIR research adds technical credibility. These are clearly smart, capable people. But I can't find the biographical thread that connects them to this specific problem in a way that feels inevitable. Why robot training data? What happened in their lives that makes this the problem only they can solve? When I backed Contraline, the founder had spent years in academic research that others ignored — his entire biography pointed toward that company. Here, I see talented Berkeley undergrads building in the hottest market of the moment, and that pattern — chasing a hot category rather than solving a personal problem — is one of the things I'm most cautious about.

The piece that genuinely resonates with me is the contributor model. Five thousand people across three continents wearing headband cameras while they clean hotel rooms, work in restaurants, operate in factories — and earning money for it. That's economic access creation in a way that reminds me of what I loved about Uber and Postmates. You're taking people who are already doing valuable physical work and creating an additional income stream from activity they're performing anyway. A hotel housekeeper in Southeast Asia demonstrating how humans actually navigate physical spaces while earning supplemental income — there's something beautiful about making that invisible labor visible and valuable. The privacy protections they've built in (no audio, faces auto-blurred, PII removed) show they're thinking about this with care.

Here's what would have to be true for this to be a great investment despite my concerns: Asimov would need to execute so fast on contributor network density and data quality that even micro1's $35 million war chest can't catch up, because the real moat isn't in the concept — everyone can ship cameras — but in the accumulated dataset of diverse, annotated human motion from real environments across geographies. If they build that dataset flywheel before the better-funded competitors, and if the frontier robotics labs (Physical Intelligence, Skild AI, Figure, 1X) need orders of magnitude more data than any single provider can supply, then being purpose-built for robotics rather than bolted on from a horizontal platform could matter enormously. Infrastructure at inflection points is something I've backed before — Crusoe started as a weird idea about gas flares and became a $3 billion AI infrastructure company. But Crusoe had the benefit of being genuinely weird and dismissed. Asimov is entering a space where everyone already believes.

The co-founder dynamic is a question mark rather than a red flag. Two Berkeley classmates building together is a common pattern, and I don't see the warning signs I saw with HQ Trivia's 50/50 split. But I also don't see evidence of a stress-tested relationship or complementary temperaments. The third team member is unnamed in the dossier. At three people trying to manage hardware logistics across three continents with well-funded competitors closing in, I'd want to understand a lot more about how this team handles pressure.

I'm passing on this one, though not with any sense of certainty. The economic access mechanism is genuinely compelling, and I could be wrong about the timing — maybe the market is big enough that consensus formation doesn't eliminate returns. But when I close my eyes and try to daydream about where this goes, I don't feel that jolt. What I feel instead is the anxiety of a footrace against better-capitalized competitors in a space that's already been discovered. The best investments I've made were in companies that were building something nobody else wanted to build yet. That's not what I see here.
