# Overdrive Health -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The most interesting thing about Overdrive Health is also the thing that makes me lean away: they acquired a traditional ambulance billing agency, plugged in AI agents, and doubled revenue per employee. That is an operationally clever move. It solves the cold-start problem, gives them immediate cash flow, and provides proprietary claims data from day one. Most pre-seed founders would kill for that kind of distribution advantage. But when I look at what they've done, I see a services business with better unit economics, not a technology company transforming healthcare. They bought a billing agency and made it more efficient. If that's the ceiling of ambition -- and the dossier suggests it is -- then the consequence of success is a well-run billing company with strong margins. That's not why I write checks.

Let me be direct about the consequence magnitude question. Medical billing in the U.S. is a $16.8 billion market. If Overdrive Health wins the entire ambulance billing vertical, their own stated SAM is $2 billion. If they succeed spectacularly, ambulance companies spend less on billing and collect a few more percentage points of revenue. That matters to those companies' P&Ls, but nobody's healthcare experience changes. No disease gets treated that wasn't treated before. No fundamental cost structure in medicine shifts. When I invested in Guardant Health, the consequence was replacing invasive surgical biopsies with a blood test -- if it works, cancer detection happens years earlier for millions of people. When I look at Overdrive Health, the consequence of success is that ambulance billing is cheaper. I've told founders before: you're too capable to spend your careers on something this operationally bounded.

The founders are clearly talented. Both came from EliseAI, where they built AI agents that helped scale from $35M to over $100M in ARR. Michael Schroeder also worked at Up&Up, a vertically integrated property manager we backed at Khosla Ventures, so I know the caliber of people that company attracted. Daniel Inge has Harvard CS degrees and Jane Street on his resume. These are strong athletes. But that's exactly my concern -- they're running the EliseAI playbook in an adjacent vertical. They know what worked before and they're replicating it. That's experienced insiders executing a proven strategy, which is the opposite of the outsider courage I look for. When three separate YC companies in recent batches -- Overdrive, Amby Health, CareSwift -- are all chasing ambulance billing with AI, nobody in that group is doing something the industry considers impossible. They're doing what everyone in healthcare AI considers obvious.

The strongest bull case here deserves honest engagement. If the acquire-and-automate model can be replicated -- buy billing agencies across medical specialties, deploy AI, dramatically improve margins -- this could become a next-generation R1 RCM at scale. R1 RCM does $1.8 billion in revenue. The timing is real: LLMs and voice AI reached production quality in 2024-2025, creating a genuine window where AI-native operators can undercut labor-intensive incumbents. And there's a legitimate business model tension for incumbents -- deploying AI that reduces headcount would cannibalize their own labor-based revenue in the short term. That's a structural window that rewards the first mover willing to operate at AI-native cost structures. If I squint, I can see a path to a meaningful business. But a meaningful business is not what I invest for. I invest for companies that make the existing way of doing things obsolete at an industry level. A cheaper billing service still operates within the existing billing paradigm.

The technology here is real but undifferentiated. AI-powered claims processing, insurance verification, denial management -- AKASA has raised $205 million doing this, Candid Health $99.5 million, CodaMetrix $95 million. The tools are broadly available. Overdrive's edge is operational -- they own the workflow end-to-end rather than selling software to billing teams. That's a distribution advantage, not a technology moat. And distribution advantages in services businesses are notoriously difficult to compound into winner-take-all outcomes. Every competitor can acquire a billing agency. The "nearly doubled revenue per FTE" claim is operationally impressive but tells me nothing about whether their AI is differentiated versus what any well-funded competitor could build with the same underlying models.

I'll pass. The founders are capable, the timing is right, and the GTM is clever. But the ambition is calibrated to the size of the ambulance billing market, not to the scale of what's broken in American healthcare. They've minimized risk at the cost of constraining what success looks like. If these founders pivoted to something where the consequence of getting it right was genuinely transformational -- say, using their billing data and AI infrastructure to fundamentally restructure how healthcare gets paid for, not just how claims get processed -- I'd want to talk. But as currently constructed, this is an optimization of an existing back-office function, and I don't fund optimizations.
