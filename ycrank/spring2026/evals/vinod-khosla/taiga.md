# Taiga -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

$265 billion lost annually to billing errors sounds like a massive problem — until you ask what the world looks like when it's solved. If Taiga fully succeeds and becomes the dominant AI billing agency for every independent practice in America, what changes? Doctors collect more revenue. Administrative overhead shrinks. Practice margins improve. That's a real business. But nobody's life fundamentally changes. Patients don't get cheaper care. Healthcare doesn't become more accessible. Taiga is optimizing the revenue extraction apparatus, not transforming the cost structure of a basic human need. This is precisely the class of company I describe when I explain what I pass on: a large TAM, an underserved vertical, a 40% efficiency gain — and consequence magnitude that caps out at "a successful company in a competitive market." The $265 billion figure measures waste in the billing system, and recapturing waste is arithmetic, not revolution.

Candid Health has raised $99.5M and grew revenue 250% in 2024. AKASA has $205M. Even LunaBill, one YC batch ahead of Taiga, reports $764K contracted ARR. Taiga enters with zero public traction into a space where multiple well-capitalized competitors have already proven that AI can handle medical billing. This tells me two things: the technology works (which is good), and everyone knows it works (which is bad for Taiga). When I backed Rocket Lab, nobody else was building electric-turbopump engines for small-sat launch — Peter Beck was exploring territory competitors hadn't mapped. Taiga is entering a mapped market with foundation-model capabilities the dossier itself admits are "technically reproducible" and "rely on foundation models accessible to any well-funded competitor." The denial-prediction engine and coding validation are LLM applications, not proprietary breakthroughs. Seven EHR integrations create some switching costs, but integrations are engineering effort, not a technology moat. Taiga's non-linear score reflects what the dossier makes explicit: this is a wrapper, not a wedge.

Nanda Guntupalli (Penn CS) and Adam Wax (Lehigh CS + Math, hackathon wins, AWS Bedrock/Textract project experience) bring genuine founder-market proximity — both grew up watching their physician parents struggle with billing. That's authentic. But proximity to a problem is not the same as the learning velocity I weight most heavily. I have no evidence of pivots, adaptation, intellectual surprises, or the kind of rapid trajectory change I can observe even over a three-month YC batch. Neither Taiga founder has a prior startup, a prior exit, or a public track record of building anything beyond hackathon projects. No Twitter, no substantive GitHub repos, no press. YC acceptance provides a moderate signal, but I need to see the rate-of-change curve, not the starting position. When I backed Mach Industries, the founder was also young — 19, an MIT dropout — but the ambition level was building weapons systems to compete with Lockheed Martin. Taiga's ambition is to be a better billing agency for small practices. That's competent, not audacious.

The bull case an advocate would make: Taiga's full-service agency model is the right wedge because small practices don't want SaaS tools — they want the problem to disappear. Candid Health and AKASA target larger organizations; incumbents like Waystar can't profitably serve 200,000+ independent practices at their cost structure. Taiga could own the long tail. The data flywheel from processing claims across specialties and payers could compound into a durable advantage. And the DoorDash exception applies — execution-heavy, no technical novelty, but enormous consequence. I've looked at this argument seriously, and it fails on consequence magnitude. DoorDash's consequence was transforming food access for hundreds of millions of consumers. Taiga's consequence is better billing for practice owners. Tony Xu demonstrated extraordinary learning velocity building DoorDash; Taiga's founders have no comparable evidence yet. The exception requires planetary-scale consequence AND a high-velocity learning machine founder — Taiga shows neither.

Taiga's path to radical affordability is actually inverted from what I look for. My highest-conviction healthcare bets — Curai Health, Guardant Health — make expensive care cheaper or accessible to more people. Taiga makes revenue collection more efficient for providers. The value flows toward the practice's bottom line, not toward patients. If anything, a perfectly efficient billing system means every billable service gets fully collected — that's good for doctors, neutral for the healthcare cost crisis. When I proposed "free healthcare," I meant AI delivering primary care at marginal cost, not AI making sure every invoice gets paid. Taiga's pricing model (likely 4-10% of collections, standard for billing agencies) further confirms it captures value from the existing payment flow rather than collapsing costs.

One condition would flip me to Invest: if Taiga demonstrated that its denial-prediction engine produced a genuinely non-linear improvement — say, a 10x reduction in denial rates versus human billers, driven by a proprietary model trained on data competitors can't access — and if Nanda and Adam showed evidence of rapid learning and adaptation since entering YC that suggests they're building something the well-funded competition hasn't conceived of. Without that, Taiga is a competent pre-seed company in a crowded, validated market. The dimensions most decisive for this tier are Consequence Magnitude — billing optimization doesn't change how millions live — and Non-Linear Technical Approach — applying available LLMs to a known workflow is incremental, and Candid Health's $99.5M head start confirms the market agrees this is an execution race, not a technology frontier.
