# RunAnywhere -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The structural dynamic buried in this dossier is more interesting than the product itself. Google, Meta, and Microsoft each maintain the open-source runtimes that RunAnywhere abstracts over — CoreML, TensorFlow Lite, ONNX Runtime, ExecuTorch. But all three monetize through cloud inference revenue. Building an enterprise control plane that intelligently routes traffic *away* from the cloud and onto devices would cannibalize their own businesses. This is the exact kind of business model conflict that creates structural openings for startups — the incumbents aren't just slow, they're conflicted. Stripe benefited from a similar dynamic: banks could have built better payment APIs but had no incentive to make integration easy when friction kept developers locked into existing rails. The question is whether RunAnywhere's founders understand this dynamic deeply enough to exploit it, or whether they're just riding the timing of on-device AI becoming viable.

On the origin of the idea: Sanchit Monga shipped SDKs on millions of devices at Intuit. Shubham Malhotra built distributed systems at AWS. The product they've built — an on-device SDK paired with a cloud control plane — maps almost suspiciously well to the intersection of their two resumes. That's actually a positive signal. The best ideas often look like the obvious thing the founders were destined to build. But there's a gap between "I have the skills to build this" and "I've been living with this pain and can't stand it anymore." The FoundersBrief says they "realized in July 2025 that their skill sets were deeply complementary," which suggests the partnership preceded a specific problem, not the other way around. I'd want to hear Sanchit describe a specific moment at Intuit where he hit the wall of device fragmentation — the concrete experience that made this problem feel personal rather than strategic. The professional background provides credibility, but I'm not seeing the obsessive founder-problem connection that separates the best organic ideas from well-reasoned market entries.

The schlep, though, is real. Abstracting across CoreML, ONNX, GGUF, and MLX on five different platform targets — iOS, Android, React Native, Flutter, and Web — while handling thermal throttling, memory constraints, and OTA model updates without App Store releases is genuinely ugly work. This is the kind of infrastructure that makes experienced mobile developers wince and reach for cloud APIs instead. 1,758 commits across the SDK repos tells me these founders have been in the trenches doing the tedious, painstaking work of making things actually function on real devices. That's not the kind of commit count you get from a weekend prototype. Combined with the fact that Sanchit applied to YC five times before being accepted — five times — the determination signal is strong. You don't keep coming back after four rejections unless something deeper is driving you.

The user pull evidence is where I start getting nervous. Ten thousand GitHub stars on a developer tool is a meaningful signal, but the dossier notes a discrepancy between earlier reports of 3.9K stars and the current 10.2K. If those stars grew organically through genuine developer adoption, that's excellent — it would mean developers are actively seeking solutions to on-device AI deployment. But if the count was inflated, it undermines the primary traction metric. Beyond stars, the evidence is thin: "thousands of developers exploring our SDKs" is a vague claim, 128 Product Hunt upvotes is modest, and the dashboard screenshot showing "1,234+ devices" might be a demo mockup. More importantly, there's no revenue and the control plane isn't even priced yet. At pre-seed I don't penalize absence of revenue, but I want to see at least one concrete signal that someone with budget authority cares — a signed LOI, a pilot with a named company, a team at a Fortune 500 that's blocked on this exact problem and asking for the enterprise features.

The bull case is compelling if you believe the timing thesis. On-device LLMs only became practical in 2024 with quantized small models fitting in mobile memory budgets. The market is crossing a hardware threshold with AI-capable NPUs in consumer devices. If you squint, this looks like where cloud infrastructure was in 2008 — everyone knows they'll need it, nobody has built the operational layer yet. The founders have the technical background to build the right product, the schlep is large enough to deter casual competitors, and the incumbent business model conflict provides structural protection. If RunAnywhere can convert even a small fraction of their open-source users into paying control plane customers, the open-core model works. The founders being technical, having shipped SDKs at scale before, and having the determination to apply to YC five times — these are real signals. But I keep returning to the fact that this is squarely in the hottest category in technology right now, with EdgeRunner AI ($17.5M), Nexa AI ($16.5M), and Qualcomm acquiring Edge Impulse. When every serious player is circling the same territory, execution speed becomes everything, and a two-person team is fighting uphill against well-funded competitors who are already in market.

These are competent technical founders building real infrastructure in a market that's clearly forming. But I'm not seeing the organic founder-problem obsession that distinguishes the companies I'm most excited about, and the hot-category dynamics work against the structural advantages. The company passes my schlep test and my technical-founders test, but falls short on organic discovery and demonstrated user pull.
