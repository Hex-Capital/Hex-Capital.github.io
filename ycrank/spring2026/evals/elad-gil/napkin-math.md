# Napkin Math -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Paper Star is running three consumer products -- Napkin Math, "Pooping With," and "Stampede: Bay Scouts" -- with three people (paperstar.cc). That fact alone tells me more than anything else in this dossier. When a pre-seed team splits focus across three distinct apps, each requiring its own acquisition funnel and retention loop, it signals they haven't identified which problem is worth their full commitment. I've backed hundreds of companies at the earliest stages, and the pattern that predicts failure most reliably isn't bad markets or weak technology -- it's founders who hedge instead of committing. Napkin Math may be the product they ultimately focus on, but right now the organizational signal says "searching," not "building."

Cal AI generated $40M in trailing revenue before MyFitnessPal acquired it in December 2025 (TechCrunch, March 2026). That number proves consumer demand for AI-powered food tracking exists. But it proves demand for *calorie counting* specifically -- the exact behavior Napkin Math explicitly rejects. Napkin Math's YC page says "Stop counting calories," positioning around social food journaling and AI-generated insights instead of macro tracking. The bull case here is that Napkin Math targets the much larger population of health-conscious people who abandoned MyFitnessPal's tedious logging -- the majority who churn within two weeks. If Strava proved that social accountability sustains exercise habits without clinical precision, maybe Napkin Math can do the same for eating. Cal AI's acquisition removes the leading independent competitor, opening a gap. And the team is technically strong: Claire Nord built OS security at Apple, Manuel Castro led hardware product management at Verkada, and Jynnie Tang worked at Retool -- my own portfolio company -- with a design background perfectly suited to social consumer products. That's a real argument. But it has a structural flaw I can't get past.

Napkin Math's market timing fails my first test. The vision-LLM capability shift that enables AI food recognition is fully consensus -- Cal AI, PlateLens, Foodvisor, and every computer vision startup recognized this threshold by 2024. Napkin Math isn't exploiting a nonobvious window; it's entering an obvious market with a contrarian positioning bet. When I backed Coinbase, crypto infrastructure was dismissed by mainstream investors. When I wrote the seed check for Perplexity, AI search was considered a failed category. In both cases, I was reading a structural shift that consensus underestimated. With Napkin Math, I see a market where the structural shift has already been recognized, captured, and monetized to $40M in annual revenue by a bootstrapped competitor. The "anti-calorie" angle isn't a structural insight about market timing -- it's a product design hypothesis about user preferences. Those are very different things, and the distinction matters for how I allocate capital.

Napkin Math sits at the pure application layer with zero infrastructure characteristics. There's no API other businesses integrate, no data layer that compounds, no workflow embedding that creates switching costs. Compare this to Stripe, which became the payments API an entire generation of internet businesses built on, or to Gusto, where payroll complexity and regulatory compliance create deep workflow integration that makes ripping it out operationally painful. Napkin Math's food journal creates some individual data accumulation over time, but a user's photo history is a weak lock-in compared to, say, a company's entire payroll infrastructure. The "Strava for food" social graph could theoretically create network effects, but social consumer apps face a cold-start problem that requires a distribution miracle Napkin Math has shown no evidence of solving -- the product is TestFlight-only with no public user counts, no App Store listing, and no press coverage.

Napkin Math needs at least three things to go right simultaneously: a distribution miracle to solve the social cold-start problem (the food-sharing feature has near-zero value for solo users), a retention miracle to prove that non-quantified food journaling sustains engagement without calorie targets, and a monetization miracle to demonstrate that users will pay for AI insights without the numerical feedback loop that drives willingness-to-pay in competing products. That's three miracles with compounding small odds. My framework says each startup gets one miracle -- if you need more, the math kills you regardless of execution quality. On founders, Manuel Castro, Claire Nord, and Jynnie Tang are technically capable MIT graduates with experience at strong companies, but no one on the team has prior founding experience, domain expertise in nutrition or consumer health, or evidence of operating through a scaling phase. The dossier doesn't identify a designated CEO among the three co-founders, which maps to my concern about equal co-founder structures without a clear decision-maker. I've seen some mediocre founders succeed in strong markets with strong product-market fit, but Napkin Math's market structure doesn't provide that tailwind.

The condition that would flip me: if Napkin Math showed 30-day retention above 40% on a cohort of 1,000+ users with the anti-calorie journal format, that would be a genuine signal that they've discovered a retention mechanism the calorie-counting incumbents can't easily replicate. Retention data would collapse the three-miracle problem into one (monetization), because proven retention implies the distribution and engagement mechanics work. Without that evidence, Napkin Math is a consumer app design bet in a consensus market with multi-product dilution, no infrastructure positioning, and no structural moat -- the profile that my Wish investment taught me to avoid. The two dimensions that lock this verdict are Nonobvious Market Timing (the AI food-tracking window is fully consensus, and Napkin Math's contrarian positioning is a product hypothesis, not a structural insight) and Infrastructure-Layer Positioning (pure consumer application with no platform characteristics and no compounding switching costs).
