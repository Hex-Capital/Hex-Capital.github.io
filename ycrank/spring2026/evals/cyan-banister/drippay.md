# Drip -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Stripe paid ~$1B for Metronome in January 2026 (Lago blog, 2026) — that is the single fact that should dominate any serious conversation about Drip. Drip is a pre-seed company proposing to displace the exact combination Stripe just bought its way into owning. When I look at Drip's pitch — high-throughput metering plus prepaid-balance settlement for AI-era event volumes — I'm not seeing a contrarian bet in a hard space. I'm seeing a company building directly into the blast radius of a freshly-acquired incumbent whose distribution runs through every developer checkout flow on the internet. That's not a space I back pre-seed; that's a space I'd want a founder who has personally bled inside of, and I don't yet see that founder in the Drip dossier.

Mike Levin is a 2026 UCLA CS grad who worked on GitHub's metered billing platform (search snippet, 2026), and I want to be honest — that's genuine, non-trivial domain context. Most pre-seed billing founders have not actually touched a metered billing engine at scale. But when I ask "tell me your story, not the business," the Drip dossier gives me a resume, not a forge. No survival story, no prior exit, no biographical reason Mike Levin of all people had to build Drip. His co-founder Lucas He is listed as CTO on the YC profile but his background could not be verified in public sources — that's not a disqualifier at pre-seed, but combined with an unverified CTO and a 22-year-old founder, the execution risk on a high-throughput settlement engine sits higher than my usual bar for a space this contested.

Drip's framing — "Stripe Billing maxes out at 1,000 events/sec while modern AI companies need 100,000+" (Lago blog, 2026) — is a real observation, but it's a consensus real observation. Orb, Metronome, Lago, Flexprice, Zenskar, and Schematic are all pointed at the same ceiling. The Drip pitch that would make a room of sophisticated VCs shift uncomfortably doesn't exist here; if anything, Stripe's $1B Metronome check turned usage-based billing into the most consensus infrastructure category of 2026. When I ran the 300-investor-pass test on Uber, the rejection itself was the signal. Drip will not be rejected by 300 investors. It will be competed for by them, which is precisely the opposite signal.

Drip's prepaid-balance settlement angle is the most interesting mechanical idea in the dossier — batching thousands of micro-charges into single settlements to escape per-charge processor fees (drippay.dev via search snippet). That's the kind of structural move I'd normally lean toward, because it rewrites a fee structure rather than optimizes around it. But the invisible system test asks whether an opaque structure extracting value from people who don't understand it is being made transparent. Drip is a B2B2B plumbing layer — it sells to API companies, who sell to developers, who sell to users. The end user never sees Drip. That's different from Affirm, which put the loan terms in front of the human being signing them, or Ownwell, which put property tax appeals in reach of homeowners who didn't know they had a right to fight. Drip may lower the cost of a fee stack but it doesn't change who has power in a system that exploits information asymmetry.

Lago is an open-source, YC-backed metering primitive (Lago GitHub) — that alone tells me the metering layer is commoditizing, which pushes Drip's real defensibility onto the prepaid-balance float. Float creates real switching costs, and if Drip accumulates enough prepaid customer balance, that's a sticky moat. But holding customer float in the US likely triggers state-by-state money-transmitter obligations (not confirmed, but standard for stored-value), and that's a legal overhead that a 2-person team with team size listed as 0 on the YC profile is unlikely to absorb before Stripe+Metronome ships the integrated competitor. Flock Safety built a camera network that compounds with each installation; Carta built switching costs by becoming the system of record. Drip hasn't yet built the thing that would make the first mover win structurally rather than temporally.

The bull case I owe Drip: Stripe integrating Metronome will be slow and messy, the prepaid settlement model is a genuinely different primitive than post-paid card processing, and Mike Levin's GitHub billing experience is closer to operator-grade than most YC billing founders get. If Drip signed two AI infrastructure companies processing real micro-transaction volume in the next six months and the co-founder relationship proved to be long-dated and high-trust, I'd reconsider. There's a RTFKT-style precedent in my portfolio where a narrow timing window and a clear acquirer landscape — Stripe itself, Adyen, possibly a cloud hyperscaler — could substitute for a durable moat. But RTFKT had a cultural inflection that was genuinely unownable by incumbents; Drip is building a product category that the incumbent already bought.

My pass example on this one almost writes itself — credentialed early-career founders, consensus AI-adjacent infrastructure, no biographical inevitability, an acquirer who just paid $1B for the nearest neighbor. The one condition that would flip me: a verifiable story about why Mike Levin and Lucas He couldn't stop building this — a founder meeting where I hear lived experience inside the fee stack, not a clean observation about Stripe's event ceiling. Until then, Drip is a bet I'd expect several other funds to lead, and I am not the right first check.

**Verdict-decisive dimensions:** Contrarian Conviction locked low by the $1B Metronome acquisition datapoint turning this into consensus infrastructure, and Biographical Forge locked in the low-middle by a real-but-thin GitHub billing credential for Mike Levin against a wholly unverified Lucas He.
