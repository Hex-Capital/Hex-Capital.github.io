# InsForge -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Supabase has $500M+ in funding, 80K+ GitHub stars, a $5B valuation, and has already announced MCP support for AI agent interaction. InsForge is building a "semantic layer" — a context-optimized interface — over the exact same commodity primitives Supabase already hosts: Postgres, authentication, cloud storage, edge functions. InsForge's entire bet is that this interface abstraction constitutes a durable advantage against an incumbent with 500x more capital and 10x more community. I have seen this pattern before. It is not one I fund. When the moat is a software wrapper over standard components, and the well-funded incumbent can build the same wrapper in a quarter while leveraging its existing user base, you are in an execution race, not a technology race. I prefer technology risk to market risk. InsForge has chosen market risk.

InsForge's self-reported MCPMark benchmark claims 1.6x faster task completion and 1.7x higher accuracy versus Supabase. Let me be direct: 1.6x is not a discontinuity. It is an incremental improvement. When I backed Rocket Lab, the Rutherford engine represented a fundamentally different approach to propulsion — electric turbopumps and 3D-printed combustion chambers creating a step-function cost reduction in dedicated small-sat launch. InsForge has taken Postgres and wrapped it in a context layer for LLMs. No novel science. No proprietary data advantage. No physics breakthrough. The benchmark itself is self-reported with no third-party validation, and the methodology (MCPMark) is InsForge's own creation. Even if the numbers hold, 1.6x faster backend setup is the kind of efficiency gain I describe when I talk about companies I pass on — useful for the developers who adopt it, invisible to the world.

Hang Huang applied to Y Combinator six times before acceptance. I respect that. Failure does not matter — what matters is whether you learned something each time the world told you no. The "building in public" strategy that doubled InsForge's database count from 2,300 to 4,000+ between November 2025 and January 2026 shows marketing adaptability. Tony Chang's background at Databricks working on networking infrastructure gives him directly relevant systems engineering experience, and ten hackathon wins suggest rapid prototyping capability. But here is what concerns me: six applications, and InsForge is still fundamentally the same product — BaaS optimized for AI agents. I look for founders whose rate of learning manifests as surprising pivots, not founders who persisted with the same thesis until the market shifted enough for YC to say yes. Persistence and learning are not the same thing.

The consequence magnitude question is where InsForge loses me entirely. If InsForge fully succeeds — dominates the agent-optimized BaaS market — what happens? AI coding agents set up backends faster. Software developers ship 1.6x quicker on backend tasks. That is a productivity gain for a profession that is already among the best-compensated in the world. Nobody's life fundamentally changes. Compare this to what happens if Commonwealth Fusion Systems succeeds (limitless clean energy), or if Impossible Foods scales (protein without animal agriculture), or if OpenAI achieves AGI (everything changes). InsForge's success means a better developer tool exists. I want to either lose my money or make 50 times my money, and the consequence magnitude of "faster backend provisioning" does not generate the asymmetric return profile I require.

The bull case deserves honest examination. AI coding agents are accelerating — 51% of professional developers use AI tools daily, per MarketsandMarkets. InsForge has 7,700 GitHub stars, hit #1 on Product Hunt, and trended #1 on GitHub during launch week with 1.5M+ X impressions. The team of six is shipping aggressively with 3,546 commits and 32 releases. An advocate would argue that InsForge is positioning for the picks-and-shovels play in the AI agent revolution — that as agents write most code, the infrastructure they consume becomes critical. I have considered the DoorDash exception: an execution-heavy play without deep technical novelty where consequence magnitude justified the investment. But DoorDash transformed food access for hundreds of millions of consumers. InsForge optimizes workflow for developers. The consequence magnitudes are categorically different. No Known Exception applies here.

One condition would flip me to Invest: if InsForge demonstrated that its semantic layer enables AI agents to build entire production applications autonomously — not 1.6x faster backend setup, but a 10x reduction in the total cost of creating and deploying software, collapsing the $200K/year cost of a backend engineer to near-zero. That would change the consequence magnitude from "better developer tool" to "radical affordability of software creation." InsForge's current framing and benchmarks do not support this larger claim.

InsForge is a competent team building a well-timed product in a real market. That description applies to hundreds of YC companies I pass on every batch. The two dimensions that lock this verdict are consequence magnitude (bounded to developer productivity, not world-changing) and non-linear technical approach (a software abstraction over commodity primitives producing 1.6x improvement, not a 10x discontinuity). InsForge is fighting an execution race against Supabase with $2M against $500M. I do not fund execution races without a technology wedge.
