# Bits -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The most striking fact in this dossier is not about Bits -- it's about SimpleClaw. A wrapper startup hit $30K MRR in two weeks, then its founder listed it for sale within a month. Multiple OpenClaw wrapper founders are simultaneously listing their companies on marketplaces. The industry itself has coined the phrase "90-day wrapper window." When the founders of your direct competitors are racing to exit the category before the window closes, that is not a market -- that is a liquidation event dressed up as a startup opportunity. I have never invested in a company where the competitive dynamic was a countdown timer set by the participants themselves.

Let me start where I always start: if Bits succeeds completely, what changes? OpenClaw users get a more secure, more convenient way to host their AI agent. That's it. Nobody's life transforms. No trillion-dollar system gets displaced. The managed OpenClaw hosting ecosystem -- all 143 startups in it -- generates roughly $320K per month in aggregate revenue. Even if Bits captured every dollar of that, it would be a rounding error. The consequence of success here is a modest hosting business in a market that is already commoditizing before it has fully formed. This is the archetype of what I warn founders about: you've narrowed the scope, picked the safe market, optimized for probability of success, and arrived at a destination where success itself doesn't matter. DigitalOcean, Hostinger, and Amazon Lightsail already offer one-click OpenClaw deployment as a feature. You are building a company around something that incumbents treat as a checkbox.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. OpenClaw's adoption is genuinely explosive -- 250,000 GitHub stars in 60 days is a growth rate that demands attention. The ClawHavoc supply-chain attack compromised 9,000+ installations and poisoned 20% of the skills marketplace. Security is a real, urgent pain point, and Bits is positioned at exactly the right moment to capture fear-driven demand. If AI agents become the standard interface for knowledge work -- and I believe they will -- then the "Heroku of AI agents" could be a meaningful company. Robbie and Bailey have directly relevant experience from Console, and Bailey's systems engineering depth (Rust, Go, C projects; container implementations) is exactly what you'd want for building secure infrastructure. If OpenClaw consolidates as the dominant agent framework and enterprise adoption requires managed, compliant hosting, Bits could ride that wave. But here's where the thesis breaks: Heroku succeeded because web deployment was genuinely hard and novel infrastructure. OpenClaw hosting is already trivially deployable. The security differentiation is a configuration layer, not a technology moat. Emergent has $100M and 104 employees. NanoClaw is free, open-source, and MIT-licensed. The security hardening Bits offers sits atop a 430,000-line codebase with known architectural weaknesses including plaintext credential storage. You cannot sell security when your foundation has structural security flaws you don't control.

The platform dependency risk is the kind of thing that should make any investor pause. OpenClaw has been forcibly rebranded twice in weeks due to trademark complaints. Its creator is leaving for OpenAI and handing the project to a foundation. The governance is unstable, the licensing could change, and the architecture could pivot in ways that invalidate Klaus's entire product. Building your company on top of an open-source project whose creator is walking away and whose governance structure is being improvised in real-time is not a bet I want to make -- I learned enough about platform risk from watching what happened when upstream technology decisions destroyed portfolio companies. This is not analogous to building on Linux, which has decades of stable governance. This is building on a project that is two months old and already fracturing.

The founders are smart -- Stanford Math/CS, Jane Street, systems engineering chops -- but they are executing the industry's playbook, not defying it. Building an OpenClaw wrapper is what every technically competent team in San Francisco attempted in February 2026. There is no outsider perspective here, no contrarian thesis, no willingness to attempt what experts consider impossible. This is two capable engineers choosing the most legible, most consensus opportunity in the AI agent space. I would tell Robbie and Bailey the same thing I tell every team I pass on for this reason: you are too talented to spend your careers on a problem this small. Go find the hard problem underneath the OpenClaw ecosystem -- the agent security architecture itself, the fundamental question of how autonomous software should be sandboxed, the trust infrastructure that AI agents will need at scale. Build that. Don't wrap someone else's answer in a hosting layer.
