# Molagri -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Molagri is two University of Toronto coronavirus biochemists, Zaky Hassan and Min Jin, pointing six years of wet-lab partnership at a real schlep zone — pollinator-safe biopesticides through EPA and PMRA — without a single dossier sentence telling me what they did before the YC check landed. The pairing that defines Molagri in my portfolio is high on schlep, low on animal: a credentialed lab team aimed at a problem nobody else in the SaaS-heavy S26 batch would touch, with no cereal-box equivalent on the table.

**Coinbase** (2012) is the load-bearing analogy for Molagri's strongest dimension. Brian Armstrong picked bitcoin compliance because every other YC applicant was unconsciously routing around it, and the grind itself became the moat once the regulators finally stamped him. Molagri sits in the same shape — Canadian PMRA and EPA biopesticide registrations run multiple years per the agriculture.canada.ca status reports, and incumbent Bayer/Syngenta/Corteva won't cannibalize their broad-spectrum SKUs to chase a pollinator-sparing alternative. Where Molagri diverges from the Armstrong rung is that Armstrong had already built the Android wallet prototype when I met him; Molagri's dossier names no pre-submission meeting with EPA, no field trial, no named pest target, no grower LOI. The schlep is real but Molagri has not yet picked up the shovel.

The pass example from my own essay file — the two-MBA AI-wrapper team with a glossy deck and no shipped artifact — is the failure mechanism I have to test Molagri against on animal resourcefulness, and the pattern is uncomfortably close in shape if not in costume. That team failed my filter because the dossier described credentials instead of pre-funding stunts. Molagri's dossier describes Hassan as a "PhD Candidate in Biochemistry" with Nature Communications and Nature Microbiology co-authorships, and that is the entire pre-funding artifact on offer. No Drew Houston demo video, no Armstrong-style overnight prototype, no 1,000 cereal boxes at $40 each. Hassan and Jin may well be animals in the lab, but animals leave tracks and this dossier has none outside PubMed.

On idea organicity, Molagri fails the Houston-USB-stick test in a specific way worth naming: coronavirus structural biology and aminopeptidase-N receptor work, which is what Hassan and Jin actually publish on per ResearchGate, is not the workflow that produces "I kept losing pollinators on my own farm and built a tool for myself." The distance from Nature Microbiology virology to small-molecule entomology is large, and over the next 18 months that distance compounds — every pest-biology question Molagri can't intuit becomes a hire, every grower conversation becomes a translation problem, and the team has no named agronomist or ag-industry advisor in the dossier to close it.

Hacker genealogy is where Molagri scrambles my usual filter rather than failing it. Patrick Collison's Lisp prize before **Stripe** (2010) and Aaron Swartz's RSS spec before **Reddit** (2005) are the canonical markers, and neither has a wet-lab analogue. Molagri's equivalent is two Nature-tier publications — genuine technical depth, but in a tradition that ships papers, not artifacts. The absent GitHub trail isn't disqualifying for a biochemistry founder the way it would be for a SaaS founder; the more relevant absence is any named patent filing or pest-target identification, which would be the wet-lab equivalent of a shipped repo.

On cofounder configuration Molagri does well by counterfactual: if Hassan were solo, I'd be at a 3 here and probably writing a pass note already. Six years as PhD lab mates at the same University of Toronto bench, co-authoring in Nature Communications and Nature Microbiology, is the strongest chemistry signal in the dossier — closer to the Collison-brothers rung than to a hackathon-pairing red flag. The unnamed team members three and four are noise; the Hassan-Jin pair is the company.

My 36-month forecast on Molagri: alive but pre-revenue, under $250K in non-dilutive grants plus maybe one small CRO-style discovery contract, no EPA or PMRA registration yet, raising an extension round into a tighter ag-biotech market — the AgBiome arc without AgBiome's $230M to cushion the landing. The mechanism is the high/low signature itself: the schlep is real enough to consume the runway, the animal evidence is thin enough that Molagri probably won't out-hustle the consumption curve.

The opposing case that stings: **Airbnb** (2009) is a Known Exception precisely because I rejected the idea silently and the founders overrode me with a stunt I hadn't seen yet. Molagri's dossier may simply not have surfaced the equivalent — a private field trial, a grower handshake, a synthesis route Hassan ran on his own dime. The bull mechanism would be Hassan-as-Armstrong: a wet-lab founder whose appetite for the regulatory grind is the actual asset and whose Nature publications are the equivalent of Patrick Collison's Young Scientist prize. The dossier evidence that wins the argument is the silence on pre-funding artifacts paired with the silence on ag-industry exposure — two absences pointing the same direction. The verifiable condition that flips Molagri to invest: a named pest target, an in-vivo efficacy result, or a signed letter from a named grower or distributor before Demo Day. Until one of those lands, Molagri is a paper, not yet a company.
