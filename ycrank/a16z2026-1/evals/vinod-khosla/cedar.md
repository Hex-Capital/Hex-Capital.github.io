# Cedar -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

$2.5M to $3M in contracted revenue, in stealth, with four people. That's a number that would make most seed investors salivate. Greg Gunn and Beier Cai are clearly exceptional operators — they scaled Hootsuite from 5 to 1,500 employees and $0 to $200M+ ARR, co-founded Commit.dev, and assembled an advisory board that reads like a recruiting hall of fame: Laszlo Bock (Google's former SVP of People Operations), Tiffany Fenster (former Head of Recruiting at Stripe), Brendan Browne (former VP of Global Talent Acquisition at LinkedIn). This is a team that knows how to sell to enterprises and build products that HR leaders buy. I respect the execution. But execution alone has never been my entry point.

My first question is always: if Cedar fully succeeds — if they build the definitive private professional relationship graph and every Fortune 500 company routes hiring through their system — does the world materially change? The honest answer: no. Companies hire faster and cheaper. HR departments reallocate budget from LinkedIn Recruiter seats to Cedar contracts. That's a redistribution of existing enterprise spend, which I've explicitly identified as a structural limitation — companies competing for share of existing budgets have lower consequence magnitude by construction. Stripe reportedly spent $10M/year on referral programs. Making that $2M/year is valuable to Stripe's CFO, but nobody's life fundamentally changes. Compare this to Impossible Foods making protein without animals, or Commonwealth Fusion Systems delivering limitless clean energy. Cedar's ceiling, even in the most optimistic scenario, is a $5-10B recruiting infrastructure company. That's a great outcome for most funds. It's not my kind of bet.

The technical approach confirms my concern. The dossier is explicit: "The core technology (LLM-driven conversational extraction) is not proprietary, but the dataset is." Cedar conducts 15-minute AI agent calls with employees to map relationships. That's a clever product insight — proactive graph extraction versus passive referral submission — and it differentiates Cedar from ERIN, Teamable, and Boon, which all wait for employees to self-identify candidates. But the underlying mechanism is an LLM conducting structured interviews. Perfect ($23M seed) is already building agentic AI sourcing. Any well-funded competitor could replicate the call format within months. The accumulated graph data creates a time-based advantage, which is real, but it's a data moat, not a technology discontinuity. I look for 10x step-function improvements — the Rutherford engine's electric turbopumps, Impossible Foods' heme synthesis. A 15-minute chatbot call extracting referral names is a 2-3x improvement in referral program efficiency, not a discontinuous leap.

The bull case I have to contend with: maybe Cedar's proprietary relationship graph becomes something larger than a recruiting tool. If they map the private trust networks of millions of professionals across thousands of companies, that's a foundational data asset — the private graph that LinkedIn's public profiles never capture. LinkedIn's business-model conflict (referral extraction could cannibalize Recruiter per-seat revenue and antagonize enterprise customers by surfacing their employees' networks) creates a structural window. And the founders aren't pure domain experts repeating a playbook — Gunn is a 3x founder, and the transition from Commit.dev (engineer matching) to Cedar (agent-driven referral graph) suggests they learned something non-obvious about how professional relationships convert into hiring outcomes. The $2.5M-$3M revenue in stealth is real market signal, not a slide deck TAM calculation. I should consider whether this is a DoorDash exception — an execution play where consequence magnitude and founder learning rate override my technical novelty requirement. But DoorDash transformed food access for hundreds of millions of people daily. Cedar transforms how HR departments source candidates. The consequence gap between those two end-states is enormous.

Gunn and Cai deserve credit as operators. A decade together across three companies, founding engineer writing the first line of Hootsuite code, scaling to $200M+ ARR — that's the kind of founder history that demonstrates resilience and adaptation. But my framework asks whether they're reasoning from first principles or running a pattern they've mastered. Everything about Cedar suggests experienced operators applying hard-won domain knowledge to an adjacent problem they lived at Hootsuite. That's high-quality execution, not the naive irreverence I funded at Mach Industries (a 19-year-old building weapons systems) or the civilizational ambition I backed at OpenAI. "Killing LinkedIn" is bold copy, but the actual product is a well-designed enterprise SaaS tool for referral hiring optimization — competent, not audacious.

Cedar will likely be a successful company. Gunn and Cai will close enterprise contracts, expand the relationship graph, and build a real business. That's precisely why this is a pass for me. I either want to lose my money or make 50 times my money. Cedar is designed to generate solid returns in a well-understood market — 5-10x, maybe 20x if the graph effects compound aggressively. The consequence of success is a profitable recruiting platform, not a world that works differently. The two dimensions that lock my tier: consequence magnitude (enterprise workflow optimization, not civilizational change) and technical approach (LLM wrapper with a data moat, not a step-function technology breakthrough).
