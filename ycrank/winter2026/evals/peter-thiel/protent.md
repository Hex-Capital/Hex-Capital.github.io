# Protent -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The competitive landscape tells you everything you need to know about whether a secret exists. Verkada has raised $700 million and generates a billion dollars in annualized bookings. Lumana AI raised $64 million last year building "vision-language models" that turn "any camera into a proactive sensor" -- language that is functionally indistinguishable from Protent's own positioning. Spot AI has $93 million. Actuate has $24 million. Coram has $30 million. Five well-capitalized companies are already building AI-powered video intelligence, and every single one of them would describe its mission as transforming passive surveillance into proactive detection. When I see five funded players converging on the same thesis, I do not see a market opportunity -- I see a Girardian nightmare of mimetic imitation, where companies that look increasingly alike compete themselves into oblivion. The question I always ask is: what is the secret? "Surveillance cameras should analyze footage in real time instead of storing it for later" is not a secret. It is the explicit business thesis of every company in this space. It is consensus dressed in contrarian language.

The Palantir comparison will occur to anyone reading this dossier -- two founders, government intelligence applications, early law enforcement deployments, defense-sector technical background. But the comparison is structurally misleading. Palantir's founding insight was genuinely lateral: that the anti-fraud algorithms we built at PayPal to detect financial crime could be adapted for counterterrorism intelligence analysis. Nobody in the intelligence community was thinking about payment fraud patterns. The connection was non-obvious, and the resulting data-fusion platform created a category that did not exist. Protent's thesis -- apply computer vision and NLP to surveillance feeds -- is the most obvious possible application of those technologies to that domain. There is no lateral insight, no hidden connection that competitors have missed. The law enforcement vertical is a go-to-market choice, not a secret. Any of the five competitors listed above could decide tomorrow to pursue police departments, and several of them have the capital and infrastructure to execute faster.

The bull case deserves articulation. If the founders possess genuinely differentiated escalation-pattern models -- if Srihan Balaji's reinforcement-learning work at Lockheed Martin produced insights into threat-signal detection that cannot be replicated from open-source computer vision models -- then the law enforcement beachhead could create a data flywheel. Every deployment generates labeled data on what escalation actually looks like in the field, and that data trains models that become harder to replicate. Government procurement cycles, once navigated, create switching costs. CJIS compliance is a real barrier. Three metro-area deployments at pre-seed is a meaningful signal of execution ability. If Protent can achieve deep integration with dispatch systems, bodycam infrastructure, and officer workflows in its initial cities, it could build a government-specific moat that the commercially-focused competitors would struggle to replicate through their existing sales channels. The path would be: own three cities completely, expand to thirty, become the standard that procurement officers specify by name. That is a real playbook, and Palantir proved it works.

But the bull case requires believing something I do not see evidence for: that the underlying technology is discontinuous rather than incremental. The dossier itself acknowledges that "the underlying technologies -- computer vision, NLP, sentiment analysis -- are increasingly commoditized through open-source models and cloud AI services." Escalation-pattern detection and sentiment analysis in video are features, not platforms. Lumana could add escalation detection to its vision-language model stack. Verkada, with a billion in bookings and full-stack hardware relationships across thirty thousand customers, could build a law enforcement product line in a quarter. The technology here is competent engineering applied to a specific vertical, not a 10x improvement that makes existing approaches structurally obsolete. When I backed SpaceX, reusable rockets were considered physically impossible by the aerospace establishment. Nobody thinks real-time video analysis is impossible -- they are all building it.

The founders are young, likely 22 or 23, which is not inherently disqualifying -- Zuckerberg was 19. But what distinguished Zuckerberg was not youth but the specificity and stubbornness of his vision. He could articulate exactly why real identity and university-based density would create irreversible network effects, and he refused a billion-dollar acquisition offer to prove it. I see no evidence of that kind of definite vision here. The positioning -- "turns passive surveillance into proactive intelligence" -- is a marketing tagline, not a theory of the world. What would Protent's founders say if I asked them what important truth very few people agree with them on? If the answer is "real-time AI surveillance can prevent crime," that is the thesis of an entire industry, not a contrarian insight. The Lockheed Martin experience is relevant but not exceptional -- it is the kind of domain background that makes you a qualified engineer in this space, not the kind that gives you an insight no one else has.

The surveillance ethics dimension adds a layer of risk that cuts against the investment. I am not reflexively opposed to surveillance technology -- the companies I have built and backed operate deep inside the national security apparatus. But Palantir survived political opposition because it was embedded in agencies with existential mandates and billion-dollar budgets before public scrutiny intensified. Protent is selling to municipal police departments, which are the most politically exposed buyers in the surveillance chain. Multiple cities have already enacted bans or restrictions on AI surveillance. A two-person pre-seed company does not have the political capital or government relationships to navigate the regulatory minefield that a company like Palantir spent a decade building. This is not a fatal flaw, but it compounds the other structural weaknesses.
