# Rex -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Merlin Kafka and Lewis Blackwood spent four years building Sequence — an a16z-backed B2B finance-ops platform — and now they are building Rex, "an AI Operations Partner for Enterprise Order to Cash" sold into Fortune 100 procurement with WorkOS SSO/SCIM stood up on day one. That sentence is the entire signature: specific knowledge at the top of my ladder, permissionless leverage at the bottom. Rex is a domain-expert team building a labor-replacement enterprise SaaS, which is the cleanest possible inversion of the architecture I fund. Real founders, wrong shape.

I run the leverage check first, the way I ran it on Twitter in 2007 and on Replit in 2023. Twitter was media, marginal cost of replication zero, anyone can post — permissionless to the protocol layer. Rex is the structural opposite: the dossier explicitly names "direct enterprise sales to pre-IPO tech and Fortune 100 finance teams," and the WorkOS case study on rex.inc exists because F100 procurement gates on SOC 2 Type II (still "pending" per Rex's own security page), SSO, SCIM, and Directory Sync. That is the textbook bottom-quartile anchor — App-Store-class permissioned distribution, except the gatekeeper is enterprise IT and a Workday auth review rather than Apple. Rex's growth is not bounded by the marginal cost of code; it is bounded by procurement cycles measured in quarters. Code and media are permissionless leverage. Rex is labor leverage delivered through capital leverage — exactly the two boxes from nav.al/product-media that "require permission."

Single-sided SaaS where each F100 customer is economically independent of the next is the low rung on the network ladder, and Rex sits squarely on it: a customer adding Rex's agents to their NetSuite or SAP instance does not make any other Rex customer's agents more valuable. The dossier gestures at "cross-customer exception patterns" compounding into a workflow data moat, but two customer classes and >$500M of self-reported receivables under management is not a network — it is training data. Uber's marketplace got denser with every driver; Rex's product gets marginally smarter with every long-tail exception. Those are different geometries, and the 10,000x upside math at angel stage lives in the first one, not the second.

Sequence is the anchor for the specific-knowledge dimension and it is the one place Rex genuinely earns its tier. Merlin Kafka was CPO at Sequence for four years, Lewis Blackwood ran engineering there for four years, and they shipped a B2B finance-ops product to Series A together — the dossier's "saw firsthand that at-scale, O2C still runs on manual labour" is not a memo-founder line, it is the lived artifact. Ivan Zhao at Notion was rebuilding personal productivity tools since adolescence; the Rex founders were rebuilding billing infrastructure for four years at a16z's expense. That is non-trainable knowledge in exactly the sense that matters. The only deduction is that the knowledge points them at the wrong leverage architecture for me, not at the wrong problem.

Order-to-cash at "$60B outsourced" is a real market — broad enough for one or two pivots into adjacent finance-ops surfaces (AP automation, close, FP&A, the Qurrent surface area) without refinancing. For the score to flip a tier here, Rex would need a documented pivot vector into something that isn't gated by F100 procurement — a self-serve mid-market product, a developer API for embedded AR, a creator-economy invoicing primitive. None of that appears in the dossier; the entire GTM is enterprise, and the pricing — "outcome and SLA-based, pay for outcomes only" — locks Rex deeper into enterprise contract math, not shallower. Big market, narrow pivot path inside it.

The Sequence adjacency is the integrity-adjacent flag I cannot ignore. Sequence raised a $20M Series A in December 2025 to build "AI agents to automate revenue operations — billing and invoice automation" (Axios), and Rex's founders left Sequence to build AI agents for order-to-cash — which is billing and invoice automation. Shared a16z ecosystem, adjacent product surface, no public statement addressing the non-compete posture. I am not calling this dishonesty; I am noting that the dossier leaves the cleanest version of the question unanswered, and the Buffett trio asks me to want monthly contact with these humans for a decade. The Epinions partner litigation taught me to read this kind of unresolved adjacency carefully rather than charitably.

Thirty-six-month forecast: Rex reaches $8–18M ARR with 12–25 enterprise logos by mid-2029 and is acquired by NetSuite, Workday, or HighRadius at a strategic premium — a clean 3–6x outcome for the seed, not a fund-returner. The mechanism is the signature: deep finance-ops specific knowledge plus outcome-priced labor replacement plus F100 distribution is exactly the profile incumbents buy rather than get disrupted by. HighRadius at $3.1B has $484M of raised capital and an explicit channel-conflict incentive to acquire the agentic layer rather than let it cannibalize seat-based ACV. That is a good company. It is not my company.

The strongest bull case routes through the Perplexity exception — single-sided product, top-quartile specific knowledge, pre-consensus AI timing, three of five gates clear emphatically and criterion #2 gets waived. The argument would be: Rex's founders are top-quartile on specific knowledge, agentic finance ops is pre-consensus in the same way AI search was in 2022, and the #1 of 2,500 Vercel AI Accelerator slot plus Guillermo Rauch's selection signals category timing. The case stings because the founder gate is genuinely real. It loses on the first criterion: Perplexity distributed through a permissionless consumer product that anyone could hit at perplexity.ai, while Rex distributes through F100 procurement with SOC 2 Type II pending. Perplexity cleared three gates at top quartile; Rex clears one. The exception does not extend that far. The verifiable condition that would flip my verdict: Rex ships a self-serve, developer-API, or open-source primitive that books $1M+ ARR outside the F100 enterprise channel within 18 months — at which point the leverage architecture has actually changed and I will re-underwrite.
