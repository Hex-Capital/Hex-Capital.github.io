# Drafted -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Nicholas Donahue grew up in a homebuilding family, then spent ~5 years running Atmos designing $200M of custom homes before the Fed broke the business in March 2025 — that biography is the single dossier fact that decides how I read Drafted. The signature pairing is a founder whose contact with custom residential design is unmistakably autobiographical, wrapped inside a consumer-AI category whose cap table — Patrick Collison, Ben Silbermann, Jack Altman, Samsung, Alex Blania, Ryan Tedder, Moses Moody — already reads consensus. Drafted is a repeat-founder pivot where the founder is "of" the problem, but the round and the category are not where I usually live.

Palmer Luckey shipping VR from his parents' garage before turning that same hardware muscle into Anduril is my high rung on lived experience, and Donahue's path mirrors the shape if not the consequence: ship a comparable product to scale in the adjacent domain (Atmos — 50 homes built, $200M designed), watch the operational layer fail to replace humans, then come back with a software-first replay of the same problem. Where Drafted diverges from the Luckey template is that Atmos didn't get acquired for $850M like Oculus — it shut down — so Donahue carries lived experience *and* a scar tissue map of exactly which part of the workflow the old approach couldn't automate. That's why the line in the TechCrunch piece — "an extremely operational business, kind of like a glamorized architecture firm" — reads to me as a founder who finally understands the shape of his own problem.

HQ Trivia is the cautionary low rung on the magically-weird axis, but Drafted's problem isn't co-founder feud — it's the opposite: too many sophisticated checks already on the cap table. Patrick Collison, Ben Silbermann, Samsung, Jack Altman, Alex Blania, Kevin Mahaffey — when six elite operators are already in at a $35M post on a Spring 2026 YC company, the round is not underpriced and the deal is not non-consensus. Drafted is not Crusoe burning gas flares in North Dakota; Drafted is a TechCrunch feature in December 2025 with Forbes 30 Under 30 founder and Ryan Tedder on the SAFE. That is a tell. The category — AI floor plans — is also crowded: Maket has been shipping since 2019, Snaptrude raised a $14M Series A from Foundamental + Accel in November 2023. I'm never playing the game I seem to be playing, but the game on offer here looks exactly like the game everyone is playing.

Sole-founder structure with Donahue named CEO and four early Drafted employees imported from Atmos means the governance question I usually drag founders through — *who is the tiebreaker* — answers itself on the YC page. The Atmos-alumni cohort is also a quiet positive: this team has already survived a shutdown together, which is a stronger trust signal at pre-seed than any cap-table logo. Eighteen months from now the governance dimension compounds positively or it doesn't show up at all; in Drafted's case I'm pricing it as a non-issue.

Drafted's frontier-hardware score would flip a tier only if Donahue announced he was using the 85,000-plan dataset and CAD export pipeline to bid permitted-plan packages directly to regional homebuilders or to capture the structural-engineer review loop — that would move Drafted out of consumer-AI-app territory and into the regulated, unsexy lane where Vigil Labs lives. As the dossier stands, "Free AI House Plans with PDF & CAD Downloads" on drafted.ai is a software wrapper, not a moat that compounds with regulation. The 0.2¢-per-generation vs 13¢ cost advantage is real engineering but it is not the kind of moat I underwrite at the SpaceX-2008 end of the spectrum.

Flock Safety is my single anchor on physical verifiability: I saw the founder's Wi-Fi name at a Four Seasons before I saw a deck. Drafted clears the Flock test in a different register — any normal human can go to drafted.ai today, define a footprint and room program, and walk away with a PDF and a CAD file inside a minute. 120k users generated 325k+ floor plans last month; 9,399 followers on @DraftedAI since November 2025. The artifact is tactile, downloadable, and shareable. This is the dimension where Drafted earns the back half of its score.

My 36-month forecast: Drafted reaches $4–12M ARR by monetizing permit-ready CAD exports and a builder-leads marketplace, then gets acquired by Autodesk or a homebuilder-ERP incumbent for $80–250M rather than becoming the defining residential-design company. The mechanism is the signature: lived-experience founder with a tactile product but a consensus cap table and a software-only moat — that profile produces credible mid-cap outcomes, not Anduril.

The bear case that actually stings is that Atmos already taught Donahue the punchline — when rates rise, custom-home clients evaporate — and Drafted's free-CAD-download GTM has no insulation from that exact macro. The same Fed move that killed Atmos in March 2025 kills Drafted's conversion to paid plans in 2027. Maket has been compounding a comparable product since 2019 and a general model lab fine-tuning on a permitted-plan corpus eats the 0.2¢ cost edge in a quarter. What dismantles the bear is the dossier's single hardest fact: 120k monthly users and 1,000 DAU on a product that is <12 months old, by a founder who already knows where the previous attempt broke. The verifiable condition that would flip my verdict to a pass is Donahue announcing the next round at >$150M post led by a tier-1 generalist fund before Drafted has shown paid conversion — that signature is the consensus trap, and at that price the magically-weird filter forecloses.
