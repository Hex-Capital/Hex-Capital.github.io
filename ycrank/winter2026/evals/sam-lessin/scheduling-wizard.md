# Scheduling Wizard -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The most interesting thing about Scheduling Wizard isn't the technology -- it's the business model inversion. Every competitor in physician scheduling sells software: QGenda, Lightning Bolt, Amion, YouShift. They hand hospitals a tool and say "figure it out." Scheduling Wizard takes in constraints and hands back finished schedules. That's not a SaaS company. That's an operations company with a software layer. And I'll be honest -- that distinction activates something in my framework. When I backed Craftwork, the thesis was that painting crews plus scheduling software creates a moat that pure-software competitors can't touch. A managed scheduling service for hospitals has structural similarities: the operational complexity is the moat, not the code. Incumbents would have to cannibalize their per-seat software revenue and build an entirely different organizational structure to compete. That business model mismatch is real protection.

But here's where my primary filter stalls: this is a sane idea pursued by sane people. Oberly's background -- Cambridge MS in applied math, Department of Defense operations research, Minor League Baseball scheduling -- maps almost too perfectly to the problem. The "sane" calibration is strong. The issue is the "insane" half. Outsourcing physician scheduling to a specialized service provider doesn't make VCs scratch their heads in confusion or dismiss the idea as impossible. It makes them ask "can this scale?" -- which is a fundamentally different question than "will this work at all?" When Brian Armstrong pitched Coinbase, people thought cryptocurrency exchanges were delusional. Nobody thinks outsourced scheduling is delusional; they think it's a services business. And the co-founder formation -- three JHU classmates from the class of 2024 who met through the university's accelerator -- is exactly the pattern that gives me pause. Not because these aren't talented people, but because the founding relationship was forged in an entrepreneurship context rather than years of shared operational experience.

The "cherry on top" test does pass cleanly, and I want to acknowledge that. Would this business be good if AI didn't exist? Yes -- departments outsource scheduling because the process is miserable, not because AI makes it possible. AI makes the service more efficient and potentially more scalable, but the core value proposition stands without it. That's the right structural position. The risk, though, cuts the other direction too: as AI tools improve, self-serve scheduling platforms like YouShift could become good enough that the managed service layer feels unnecessary. The Scheduling Programming Language is the crux -- if it genuinely encodes domain complexity that can't be replicated by a general-purpose LLM with a scheduling prompt, the moat deepens with every department onboarded. If it's essentially a constraint-satisfaction framework that a well-resourced team could rebuild in six months, the moat is thin.

The strongest bull case: this is Teamshares for hospital scheduling. An operationally unglamorous, department-by-department grind that most tech investors find boring -- exactly the kind of business that flies under the radar while building irreplaceable operational infrastructure. Fourteen hospitals including Mass General, Johns Hopkins, UT Southwestern, HCA, and UCSF at pre-seed is genuinely exceptional traction. Those aren't small community clinics; those are marquee academic medical centers. If the Scheduling Programming Language evolves into something approaching a standard for expressing healthcare scheduling constraints -- a domain-specific protocol that hospitals, EHR systems, and workforce tools integrate with -- the ceiling lifts dramatically. QGenda's $2.5B exit to Hearst proves the market values this category. The expansion into patient triaging and provider management systems suggests the founders see scheduling as a wedge into broader hospital operations infrastructure. For this to be a great investment, you'd need to believe that (a) the managed service model scales through automation rather than headcount, (b) the DSL becomes genuinely irreplaceable rather than replicable, and (c) the founders can navigate enterprise healthcare sales as 24-year-olds competing against 694-employee incumbents. That's three bets, not one, and all three need to land.

Where I ultimately net out: this is a competent team in a validated market with an interesting business model differentiation, but it lacks the non-consensus voltage I need to write a check from my own money. The idea doesn't provoke enough cognitive dissonance. There's no protocol-layer play -- this is an application business that could become infrastructure but isn't positioned that way today. The managed service model gives it an operational moat that most software-only competitors lack, but it also caps the narrative at "really good services business" rather than "infinity story." I don't see the compounding dynamics that made Solana or Helium or even Venmo feel like they were building a new layer of reality. I see a well-executed vertical services company with smart founders and strong early customers. That's good. It's just not my bet.
