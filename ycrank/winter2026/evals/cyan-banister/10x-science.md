# 10x Science -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I notice first about 10x Science: this is a Stanford lab spinout from a Nobel laureate's group, building AI software for biopharma protein characterization, in 2026. That's about as consensus as it gets. AI applied to biotech is the category every fund in the Valley is chasing right now, and "postdocs from famous lab start company" is one of the most well-worn founder archetypes in Silicon Valley history. When I look at a deal, I'm asking myself — is this unpopular? Is it difficult to assess? Does it make people uncomfortable? 10x Science makes nobody uncomfortable. It's the kind of company that looks good to every investor at Demo Day, and that's exactly the pattern that makes me cautious.

I want to hear the founder's story — not the business, the story. And what I see here is deeply credentialed academic researchers: David Roberts with 37 publications and a Damon Runyon Fellowship under Carolyn Bertozzi, Andrew Reiter from the Broad Institute and the same Stanford lab, Vishnu Tejus with his XPRIZE work and time as founding engineer at Nooks. These are genuinely impressive resumes. But I'm not hearing the survival story. I'm not hearing about the obstacle that forged an unbreakable connection between these founders and this specific problem. The founder-problem connection here is professional and intellectual — they worked on mass spectrometry in a lab, so they're building mass spectrometry software. That makes perfect sense. But "makes perfect sense" is not the signal I invest on. Vishnu's path is the most interesting to me — De Anza College, self-taught through online courses, working his way up as an engineer — that's closer to the kind of scrappy trajectory I recognize from my own experience. But his biography connects to engineering, not to the domain problem of protein characterization.

The bull case is real, though, and I want to be honest about it. The domain expertise is genuinely rare — the intersection of top-down mass spectrometry knowledge, glycoproteomics experience, and production-grade AI engineering is an extremely thin talent pool. The incumbents have been consolidated into large instrument conglomerates (Genedata swallowed by Danaher in 2024, Protein Metrics by Dotmatics in 2021), and big companies that acquire analytical software tend to slow-walk innovation because they're protecting hardware revenue. That creates a real window for a nimble, AI-native competitor. The regulatory complexity of biopharma software — FDA 21 CFR Part 11, GxP validation — is the kind of moat I've invested through before with companies like Contraline, where the regulatory pathway itself becomes a competitive advantage once you navigate it. If these founders fully commit and build a regulatory-compliant platform that breaks vendor lock-in, they could genuinely own this niche. The claimed $150K+ monthly time savings per team, if real, suggests willingness to pay is not the problem.

But I keep coming back to what's missing for me. This company doesn't make invisible systems visible for people who lack access — it makes analytical workflows faster for biopharma companies that already have access to everything. Uber created income for drivers. Affirm gave transparent credit to people who were getting squeezed by hidden fees. Carta made equity legible to employees who couldn't read their own cap tables. 10x Science makes mass spectrometry data interpretation faster for teams that were already interpreting it. That's valuable, but it's efficiency, not access. And the two domain founders still hold academic positions at Stanford — Roberts as a postdoc, Reiter as a PhD student — which raises real questions about whether they've made the full leap. The transition from academic research to startup operations is one of the hardest pivots a founder can make, and I've seen too many lab spinouts where the founders kept one foot in academia and never developed the relentless, all-consuming drive that building a company from nothing requires.

I respect the technical depth and the team composition is thoughtful — pairing deep domain scientists with a CTO who's already built and shipped product at a YC company is smart architecture. But this doesn't trigger my primary filters. It's not weird enough, the founders aren't outsiders enough, and the impact isn't direct enough. For an investor looking for well-credentialed teams in large markets with clear technical moats, this probably scores well. That's just not how I invest.
