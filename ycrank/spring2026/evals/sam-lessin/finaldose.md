# FinalDose -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

FinalDose is Oxford PhDs with eight years of CRISPR screening experience pointed at the most capital-intensive asset class in venture — a preclinical oncology platform that needs $100M+ to see a Phase I patient — which makes Jeff Liu's missionary temperament the highest thing on my ladder and the financing shape the lowest, and that pairing is the entire story. The signature is "deep-domain academic conviction trapped inside a biotech cap-table profile Slow cannot underwrite," and FinalDose's claim of unlocking >80% of currently undruggable cancers via a single reprogrammable DNA-sensing kill-switch is exactly the kind of swing where the science could be real and the venture math still won't work for me.

On founder conviction, the load-bearing analogy is my own **Drop.io** arc — three-plus years grinding a primitive nobody believed in before a buyer materialized — and FinalDose's Jeff Liu clears that filter on paper more cleanly than most YC founders I see: Oxford DPhil in Oncology, five postdoc years at the MRC Weatherall running CRISPR screens for druggable cancer targets, and a prior co-founded sepsis diagnostics company (Vivid Dx) that raised $15M. Li-Yao Huang's Cas9 discovery-to-validation framework and Steven Lin's Wellcome Trust computational biology pedigree round out a team where each pillar maps directly to the platform's technical claim. Where FinalDose diverges from the Drop.io rung is the public artifact — I cannot find opinionated long-form writing from Liu, Huang, or Lin on *why* DNA-sensing structural-change kill-switches are the right modality versus base editing or ADC payloads; conviction is credentialed but not published, which is a real haircut on Criterion 3.

Capital structure is where the **Ro** Known Exception either rescues FinalDose or it doesn't, and I think it doesn't. Ro's failure mode against my capital-efficiency criterion was redeemed because the 50-state prescription-licensing schleps were themselves the moat — burn bought legibility VCs couldn't replicate, and there was telemedicine revenue from week one to anchor each tack. FinalDose has the inverse profile: the burn buys preclinical data, not regulatory positioning, and there is no revenue line of any kind to cushion the regatta tacks between today and a Series A. The competitive set — Beam Therapeutics (~$1B+ raised pre-IPO), CRISPR Therapeutics (Casgevy with Vertex), Intellia, Scribe ($100M+ with Sanofi), KSQ ($200M+) — has already shown what this category's funding ladder looks like, and it is five-to-seven specialist biotech rounds before any reasonable exit, none of which Slow can lead. The Ro override does not extend here because Ro distributed approved molecules; FinalDose is discovering them.

On Criterion 1, the regatta question is whether biotech-at-YC is itself non-consensus enough to compensate for an undisclosed price. Mechanism analysis: FinalDose's pre-Series-A tacks need to produce in-vivo specificity data and a delivery modality (LNP? AAV? — the dossier discloses neither) before any non-biotech-specialist fund can underwrite the next round, which means over the next 18 months the cap table compounds away from generalist seed money toward Flagship / ARCH / RA Capital territory where my check is non-additive. The category is genuinely off-consensus for generalist YC investors — most of this batch is AI agents priced at $30M post — but "non-consensus because nobody else can write it" is not the same as "non-consensus because I have edge," and that distinction is the entire Value-Seed essay.

Counterfactual on founder-as-brand: for Criterion 4 to flip a tier on FinalDose, Jeff Liu would need to be running a substack-quality oncology-mechanisms newsletter with a working oncologist audience, or the team would need to have published the kill-switch mechanism in *Nature* with the kind of citation velocity that becomes its own pharma-BD funnel. Neither is in the dossier — the @LaunchFD2026 handle and an unretrievable LinkedIn follower count are the entire owned-distribution surface — and biotech doesn't really reward founder-brand the way creator businesses do, so the counterfactual is structurally hard to achieve in this category. This is closer to a generic academic-spinout community footprint than to **John Fish / Bookshelved**.

The single-anchor parallel on category sleeve is **Ro**, and FinalDose fails the comparison cleanly. Ro fit my prescription-rail healthcare sleeve because the asset was the consumer relationship and the operational moat around delivery; FinalDose sits in therapeutic discovery, which is not a Slow sleeve at any price — it is not pre-consensus crypto with on-chain economic activity, not DTC consumer with margin and brand, not Wizard-of-Oz services-to-software, not employee-equity rollup. The dossier's tags — "Gene Therapy, Therapeutics, Genetic Engineering" — describe an asset class my fund has revealed no convicted position on across hundreds of portfolio companies, and category absence at this dimension is informative, not coincidental.

Thirty-six month forecast: FinalDose raises a $40-80M Series A from a biotech-specialist syndicate (Flagship, ARCH, or RA Capital most likely) on the strength of Liu's Vivid Dx track record and a first in-vivo proof-of-concept dataset, remains fully preclinical with zero revenue and no IND filed, and any seed position I'd take today is non-marked, non-followable, and non-liquid through the entire window. The mechanism producing that forecast is exactly the high/low signature: missionary academic founders are precisely the profile biotech specialists fund, and the capital intensity that makes FinalDose un-underwritable for Slow makes it eminently underwritable for the people whose LPs signed up for ten-year preclinical timelines.

The strongest bull case against passing is that FinalDose is **Ro** if you squint — regulatory complexity creates a venture moment only deep-pocket capital can clear, the science is genuinely novel (DNA-sensing structural-change effectors are not what Beam or Intellia are building), and Liu has already shown he can raise $15M into a prior healthcare company, which means the legibility translation to a biotech Series A is more achievable than for a typical academic spinout. The dossier evidence that wins the argument anyway: Ro had revenue and an operational distribution moat from day one, and FinalDose's YC Launch page discloses neither a delivery modality nor any in-vivo specificity benchmark — the entire pitch is mechanism-of-action with no asset-level milestone — which means the schleps haven't started, let alone been cleared. The verifiable condition that would flip my verdict is FinalDose disclosing a working in-vivo tumor-clearance dataset with a named delivery vehicle and a sub-1% off-target rate in a preprint within 18 months; absent that, the regatta tack count exceeds what generalist-seed money can ride.
