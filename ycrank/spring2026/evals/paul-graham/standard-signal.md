# Standard Signal -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Michael Royzen has been building things since he was twelve years old. iOS apps as a kid, SmartLens at sixteen, UT Austin Turing Scholar, ML roles at Lyft, Cloudflare, and Microsoft, then Phind — an AI search engine for developers that processed 150 million searches and grew 5-10% week-over-week in DAUs. Standard Signal is his second time through YC. That's a real builder. The career trajectory has the shape I associate with the "animal" quality — someone who passes through professional and crosses into obsessive. If Royzen had walked into a YC interview with a co-founder and told me about a developer tool he'd been using himself that didn't exist yet, I'd be leaning forward in my chair. Instead, Standard Signal is an AI hedge fund, and Royzen is running it alone.

Solo founders are one of the things I've warned about for twenty years. "Starting a startup is too hard for one person. Even if you could do all the work yourself, you need colleagues to brainstorm with, to talk you out of stupid decisions, and to cheer you up when things go wrong." Standard Signal is not just solo-founded — it's solo-founded in a domain Royzen has never worked in. His entire career is NLP, search, and developer tools. The dossier documents zero quantitative finance experience, zero hedge fund background, and no announced senior hire with that expertise. Standard Signal's website lists open roles for an ML Research Engineer and a Quantitative Researcher — positions that would address the gap — but they're unfilled. A hedge fund requires SEC registration, LP reporting, compliance with the Investment Advisers Act, risk management frameworks, and the institutional relationships to raise capital from family offices and endowments. These are not engineering problems. This is the kind of domain where I'd want to see someone who lived in the future of quantitative finance and built what was missing — not someone who built something excellent in an adjacent domain and decided to apply it to a new one.

Standard Signal claims a Sharpe ratio of 2.0+ on its website, but provides no time period, no auditor, and no context. A Sharpe of 2.0 would place Standard Signal in the company of Renaissance Technologies' Medallion Fund, widely considered the greatest trading operation in history. Renaissance was founded by Jim Simons, a former NSA codebreaker and world-class mathematician, backed by decades of proprietary research. Standard Signal was announced on Twitter in April 2026 by a solo founder whose GitHub shows 10 public repos, all forks. I'm not saying the claim is false — I'm saying it's the kind of claim that demands extraordinary evidence, and the dossier contains none. Institutional LPs typically require 12-24 months of third-party audited track records before writing checks. Standard Signal's $100,000 minimum investment and presumed 2-and-20 fee structure are standard, but the path from "website with performance claims" to "fund with institutional AUM" is one of the longest schleps in finance.

The bull case for Standard Signal is real and I want to take it seriously. Royzen demonstrated at Phind that he can train frontier LLMs — the website claims he built "the best-performing coding LLMs of 2023-2024" — and the transition from coding LLMs to financial reasoning LLMs is a genuine technical insight. If LLMs can perform multi-step financial analysis that traditional statistical models cannot, and if Standard Signal's proprietary models generate persistent alpha, then the track record becomes self-proving and the domain expertise gap closes. Phind's 5-10% weekly DAU growth shows Royzen can ship and iterate fast. Patrick Collison was a teenager when I first backed him at Auctomatic, and he had no payments experience before Stripe. The pattern of backing technically brilliant young builders who enter unfamiliar domains is one I've followed before. And the hedge fund industry entered 2026 with its highest inflows in almost two decades — the timing could be right.

But here's where Standard Signal breaks from the Stripe parallel in every way that matters. Stripe had two co-founders. Stripe was built by developers for developers — the Collisons were solving their own problem, which is the purest form of organic discovery. And Stripe tackled a schlep that repelled other engineers — PCI compliance, bank integrations, regulatory complexity. Standard Signal is the opposite on all three counts. It's solo-founded. Royzen is not building for himself — he's building for institutional LPs, a customer segment he doesn't belong to and hasn't documented studying deeply. And "AI hedge fund" is not a schlep that repels people — it's a glamorous idea that attracts them. Open-source AI hedge fund frameworks exist on GitHub. Numerai has $550 million in AUM at a $500 million valuation with 25.45% net returns in 2024. Earthian AI is positioning as an "AI-native hedge fund." Rebellion Research has been doing AI-only trading since 2007. Renaissance Technologies has $130 billion in AUM and could adopt LLM-based approaches with its existing infrastructure. When everyone immediately agrees an idea is good, the returns have already been competed away. Standard Signal sits squarely on the wrong side of the "seems like a bad idea / is a good idea" Venn diagram — it seems like a good idea to everyone, which means it almost certainly isn't one for an angel investor looking for asymmetric returns.

The condition that would flip me: a co-founder with deep quantitative finance credentials — someone who spent years at a Two Sigma or Citadel — plus six months of audited live returns that confirm the Sharpe claim. That combination would transform Standard Signal from a technically talented solo founder chasing a consensus idea into a complementary team with domain credibility and proven performance. Without it, Standard Signal is a Pass. The two dimensions that lock this are Organic Problem Discovery — Royzen's problem origin is domain-hopping, not lived frustration — and Idea That Repels Before It Attracts, where Standard Signal fails the Black Swan test as cleanly as any company I've seen. The builder quality is real, but it's pointed in the wrong direction.
