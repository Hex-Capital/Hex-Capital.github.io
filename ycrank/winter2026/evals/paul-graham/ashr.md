# Ashr -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Ashr is that it's in a consensus category. "AI agents need better testing" is an observation that every engineer building agents arrived at simultaneously in 2024-2025. At least four funded startups -- Janus, Coval, Maxim AI, LangWatch -- are working on overlapping versions of this problem, and the large observability platforms like Datadog and LangSmith are one product sprint away from adding simulation features. When I see five companies independently converging on the same idea within eighteen months, that tells me the idea wasn't hiding. It was obvious. And obvious ideas that lack deep structural schlep attract exactly this kind of crowding. Stripe worked because everyone knew payments were broken but nobody wanted to deal with banks, compliance, and fraud -- the schlep was institutional, not technical. The schlep in agent testing is purely engineering complexity: building better synthetic data and evaluation pipelines. Any strong team can tackle engineering complexity. That's why there are already five of them doing it.

The organic question is closer to a draw. Shreyas spent two years building AI agents in finance and DevOps. Rohan built an AI companionship app for seniors and exited it. They didn't read about this problem in a market report -- they encountered it while building. That's a genuine signal. But encountering a problem is different from being uniquely positioned to solve it. The pain of testing AI agents is felt by every team that ships one. When the pain is universal and visible, the organic advantage disappears because thousands of other builders felt the same thing and some of them are also starting companies to fix it. The strongest organic discoveries involve problems that are specific to the founder's particular vantage point -- problems that require you to have been in a specific place at a specific time to notice. "Agent testing is hard" is something you notice from any vantage point where you're building agents.

The bull case is real, and I want to be honest about it. If agent proliferation continues at its current pace -- and if the testing problem gets genuinely harder as agents become multi-step, multi-tool, multimodal -- the winner in this space could build something very large. Ashr's focus on generating full user journeys rather than single prompt-response evaluations could matter. Their multimodal coverage (voice, image, file generation) is broader than most competitors. And Rohan's prior exit demonstrates he can ship and sell a product, which is a non-trivial signal at this stage. If they execute faster than the field and lock in enterprise customers before the market consolidates, the current crowding becomes irrelevant -- in every crowded category, one company eventually wins. The question is whether I see specific evidence that these founders will be that company, and I don't yet.

What concerns me beyond the competitive dynamics is the absence of revenue signal. The product is listed at $0 with no visible paid tiers. The customer logos -- UC Berkeley, Stanford, a few early-stage companies -- look like free academic and network-driven adoption rather than genuine economic dependency. When I invested in companies that worked, even at the earliest stages, there was usually someone who would have been economically hurt if the product disappeared. A Stanford research group using a free testing tool can switch to Langfuse tomorrow. I don't see evidence of the kind of user pull where customers are pulling the product toward them because they genuinely need it, not just because it's free and convenient.

The founders are technically capable. Rohan was CTO of his previous company and built the product. They have a working platform with SDK integration, a dashboard, multimodal support -- this isn't vaporware. At pre-seed with two Berkeley engineers, having a functioning product with real users is baseline competence, and they've met that bar. But I'm not seeing the signal that elevates this above the field. No evidence of creative, unscalable user acquisition. No story of an obstacle they navigated in an unexpected way. No sign that they're doing things that don't scale to understand their users at a visceral level. They may be doing all of this -- but the evidence available doesn't show it.

I'd want to see Ashr with a paying customer who chose them over Coval or Janus and can articulate why. That would change my calculus significantly. Right now, this looks like a competent team building in a crowded consensus category without the structural schlep or visible user dependency that would give me conviction with my own money.
