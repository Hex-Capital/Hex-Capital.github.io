# Maywood -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The structural moment here is clear but not early. When I invested in Harvey in 2022, the thesis that LLMs could generate professional-grade documents in regulated industries was genuinely contrarian -- most investors dismissed legal AI as a hallucination liability. By March 2026, "AI for professional services" is a well-mapped category with dozens of funded companies across legal, accounting, and consulting. The specific M&A execution niche -- generating CIMs, pitch decks, and teasers -- is more non-obvious than "AI for finance" broadly, because most AI investors are chasing agents, reasoning systems, and consumer applications rather than automating middle-market bank document workflows. That's the "too boring" archetype in my framework: CIM generation is the kind of work that's invisible to anyone outside investment banking but consumes enormous analyst hours. The structural trigger is real -- LLM fidelity crossed the threshold for coherent financial document generation in 2024-2025, and the M&A volume rebound to $5 trillion creates capacity pressure that makes banks willing to adopt. But "real structural trigger" is different from "non-obvious structural trigger," and by now the timing thesis is closer to consensus than contrarian.

What stands out most is the execution velocity against one of the hardest buyer segments in enterprise software. Investment banks are notorious for 6-12 month procurement cycles with extensive security reviews. Maywood already has SOC 2 Type II certification, is working with "several banks" at pre-seed, and has customer testimonials citing doubled deal capacity with the same headcount. Getting SOC 2 Type II alone typically takes 6+ months of sustained effort -- achieving that plus live bank deployments at this stage signals a team that understands the operational reality of selling into regulated finance. This is the kind of execution evidence I weight heavily. Drake Goodman's Blackstone experience isn't just a resume line -- it means he's lived through the pain of manually assembling CIMs and pitch decks, and he understands the procurement politics of a bank's technology committee. Esteban Vizcaino's Balyasny background adds buy-side AI deployment experience. The team composition is unusually well-fitted for this specific problem.

The product-to-distribution trajectory is where I have mixed conviction. The "unified workspace" framing -- where a data update in one place propagates across all deal documents -- suggests the founders are thinking about becoming the execution layer, not just a document generator. If they integrate deeply enough into a bank's digital infrastructure (files, emails, meetings, data vendors, internal databases), that integration creates genuine switching costs and a distribution channel for additional capabilities: due diligence automation, deal analytics, compliance tracking, counterparty management. The firm-specific style learning compounds over time -- the more deals processed, the better the platform emulates that firm's standards, which is a real data moat. But the fundamental constraint is buyer concentration. Gusto targets every employer in America. Stripe targets every developer building on the internet. Maywood targets maybe a few hundred meaningful M&A advisory firms globally. Even if per-account revenue is high, the distribution channel has a ceiling that limits how many products you can push through it. This isn't a fatal flaw -- Veeva built a $40 billion business selling to a concentrated pharma buyer set -- but it changes the upside calculus relative to companies with broader buyer universes.

The competitive dynamics deserve scrutiny. Datasite is on an acquisition spree -- SourceScrub, BlueFlame AI, Grata, Ansarada -- with $2 billion in total funding and $158 million in revenue. The fact that they're acquiring AI capabilities rather than building them organically is actually a positive structural signal for Maywood: it suggests the incumbent can't easily replicate generative content creation within their existing data room architecture. But it also means Datasite could simply acquire Maywood or a competitor once the category is validated. The M&A execution software market may consolidate before Maywood reaches the scale to be an independent platform. This is a realistic outcome, and a decent acquisition exit rather than an independent company path is something I factor into return expectations.

The single-miracle test is where I'm most concerned. The way I'd frame Maywood's miracle: "Can we build AI that generates investment banking-grade documents accurately enough that bankers trust it as a starting point?" That's a legitimate single miracle, and the foundation model labs are helping deliver it -- similar to how Harvey's miracle was partially externalized to OpenAI. But there's a compounding factor: they simultaneously need to close enterprise bank sales fast enough to build a defensible installed base before Datasite or another well-capitalized competitor enters the content generation layer. That's approaching two miracles. The early traction with "several banks" suggests they may have already cracked the first part of the sales challenge, which de-risks the compounding somewhat, but the sustained velocity of bank sales at scale remains unproven.

The bull case is genuinely strong: this is a team with authentic domain expertise attacking a boring, high-value workflow in a regulated industry where compliance requirements (SOC 2, single-tenant deployment, self-hosted options) create structural barriers to casual entry. If the product works well enough that banks standardize on it, the firm-specific data moat compounds and switching costs make it sticky. A few hundred banks at enterprise contract values with expansion into adjacent deal workflow products could build a meaningful business. The Veeva analogy -- concentrated buyer set, deep domain specificity, regulatory moat, expanding product suite -- is the right mental model. But Veeva's founding team had decades of pharma software experience and entered a market with virtually no competition. Maywood faces Datasite's $2 billion war chest and the commoditization pressure of rapidly improving foundation models. I'd want to see more evidence that the AI output quality truly meets investment banking standards at scale, and that the pipeline of bank customers extends beyond the initial "several" before committing capital.
