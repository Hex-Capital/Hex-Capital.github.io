# Bits -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what jumps out at me before anything else: 143 OpenClaw-related startups generating a combined $320K a month. SimpleClaw hit $30K MRR in two weeks and its founder immediately listed it for sale for $225K. Multiple wrapper companies listed for sale simultaneously. Industry observers are calling it a "90-day wrapper window." This isn't a market -- it's a gold rush stampede where the prospectors themselves are already heading for the exits. When the founders building in a space are flipping their companies within weeks of launch, that tells me everything I need to know about whether anyone believes there's a durable business here. I've spent my career looking for companies that are not popular and difficult to assess. Bits is the inverse: it's in the most popular category of the moment, and it's trivially easy to assess -- which is the problem.

When I evaluate a company, I always start with the founder's story. Not the business, the person. How did they get here? What forged them? With Bits, I see two technically competent people -- Robbie from Stanford and Jane Street, Bailey from Cal Poly math research and Amazon -- who met at Console building AI agents, watched OpenClaw go viral, and decided to wrap it in a managed hosting layer. That's a career move, not a calling. I don't see the biographical thread that connects either founder to the security problem they claim to solve. Compare that to when I was at IronPort -- I spent years in the trenches of email security before Cisco bought us, and the reason that company worked was because we had genuine, deep, proprietary technology for reputation-based filtering that took years to build. Klaus's security features -- pre-configured malware protection, smart permission controls, sensible defaults -- these are configuration choices, not technology. Any DevOps engineer can set up a hardened OpenClaw instance in an afternoon. Emergent, sitting on $100M with 104 employees, can add these same features as a product update.

The bull case I'd want to believe is that ClawHavoc was a genuine wake-up call -- 9,000+ compromised installations, 800+ malicious skills in the marketplace, an OpenClaw maintainer publicly warning the project is "too dangerous for casual users" -- and that someone needs to be the trusted, managed layer for this ecosystem. Security crises do create real demand. And the founders do have relevant infrastructure experience from Console. If they could build genuine security IP, something proprietary and deep rather than just thoughtful configuration, and if OpenClaw's trajectory holds, the underlying market heading toward $183 billion by 2033 is enormous. But for that bull case to work, I'd need to see evidence that Bits is building something structurally defensible rather than racing alongside dozens of identical wrappers. I don't see that evidence. And the platform dependency risk is severe -- OpenClaw's creator is joining OpenAI and transferring the project to a foundation, which means the entire ground under this company could shift without warning.

The unauthorized Solana memecoin trading under their brand name concerns me too -- not because it's their fault, but because the suggestion that a developer may have acknowledged it shows either poor judgment or a cavalier attitude toward brand and regulatory risk at a stage where you can't afford either. Meanwhile, this company creates no economic access for underserved populations, makes no invisible system visible, and serves a technically sophisticated user base that already has alternatives. There's nothing about this that would make me drop everything to work for these founders. There's no jolt, no felt sense of something others are missing. Everyone sees this opportunity -- that's precisely why there are 143 companies in the space already. I'm passing.
