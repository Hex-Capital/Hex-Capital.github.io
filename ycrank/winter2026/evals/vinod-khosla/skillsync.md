# Skillsync -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Let me start with the question I always start with: if Skillsync succeeds -- wildly, completely, beyond all expectations -- what changes? Companies hire specialized engineers a bit faster. Recruiters save some hours per week browsing GitHub profiles. The consequence of that success is a modestly profitable SaaS business in one of the most overcrowded categories in venture capital. Nobody's life is fundamentally different. No industry is restructured. LinkedIn Recruiter still works fine for 95% of hiring. This is a $499-a-month tool making an existing process incrementally more efficient. I've seen a thousand of these. The founders are too capable to spend their careers building a better recruiting filter.

The competitive landscape tells me everything I need to know about the disruption potential. SeekOut has raised $186 million. hireEZ has $76 million and $42.6 million in revenue. Prog.AI is a direct competitor already indexing 60 million developer profiles with code analysis -- launched in 2023, covered by TechCrunch, founded by someone with a prior exit to Dun & Bradstreet. When well-funded incumbents and direct competitors are already serving your market and not dismissing your approach, that's a signal the innovation is incremental enough to be non-threatening. Contrast this with Juniper, where Cisco's CTO told me they would never build a TCP/IP router. Nobody is laughing at the idea of code-based developer sourcing -- they're already doing it. That absence of expert dismissal is, in my framework, evidence that the problem space isn't radical enough.

The strongest bull case goes something like this: LLMs crossed a capability threshold in 2023-2025 that makes semantic code analysis qualitatively different from prior metadata scraping, and Skillsync's "Cursor-like" natural language search could be a genuine UX breakthrough in recruiting. The founders have authentic domain credibility -- growing Hyperswitch from zero to 20,000+ GitHub stars means they lived inside the open-source talent ecosystem and understand how developers actually signal expertise through code. If they build a sufficiently rich developer knowledge graph, it could become a network-effect moat. And the timing indicators are real: AI/ML job postings grew 163% in 2025, and 65% of hiring managers report increasing difficulty finding specialized talent. But even granting all of this, the structural ceiling remains: the product only surfaces engineers with public GitHub activity. Many of the world's best engineers work on proprietary code and have minimal open-source footprints. You can't become the definitive way to hire engineers when you can only see a fraction of them. That's a feature, not a platform.

The pivot from internal codebase talent mapping (June 2025 Product Hunt: "Discover hidden talent in your codebase") to external recruiting sourcing within six months shows the team iterates -- that's a positive signal for learning rate. And the founder pairing is sensible: Aaditya brings the business and HR lens from XLRI, Nishant brings deep technical credibility as a top-percentile Rust developer. But learning rate without contrarian ambition is just efficient execution of a small idea. Nothing in this dossier suggests the founders are attempting something the consensus considers impossible. They're building a better version of tools that already exist. The core technical capability -- LLM-based analysis of public GitHub code -- is explicitly described as "technically replicable" and "increasingly accessible." That's the opposite of a structural technology moat.

I keep coming back to the magnitude problem. The dossier cites a $198 billion IT recruitment market, but Skillsync's actual addressable slice is a fraction of a fraction: specialized engineering recruiting for roles where open-source contributions are relevant and discoverable. Even if they dominate that niche, the consequence is a decent business -- not a transformation of how the world operates. I tell founders this all the time: don't reduce your probability of failure by narrowing your scope to the point where success doesn't matter. Skillsync has found a defensible-sounding niche, and in doing so, they've capped the upside that would make the bet worthwhile. Pass.
