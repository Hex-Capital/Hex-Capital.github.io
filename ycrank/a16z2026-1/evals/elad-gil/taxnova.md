# Taxnova -- Elad Gil Evaluation

R&D tax credit automation is not a market that consensus has overlooked. SPRX has raised $46M, Boast.ai has $30M and eight-figure revenue with 1,000+ customers, Neo.Tax closed a $10M Series A back in 2022. Nearly $90M in aggregate competitor funding tells me the structural opportunity here was recognized years ago. When I apply my first filter -- what structural shift is making this possible NOW that wasn't possible two years ago, and does consensus underestimate the window? -- Taxnova faces a problem: the window was spotted, and multiple well-capitalized teams walked through it. The company's own cited catalysts -- LLM capability for generating technical narratives from engineering artifacts, UK HMRC inquiry rates spiking from 0.5% to 20%, US Section 174 changes -- are real demand drivers, but they're visible to every player in this market. The one genuinely less-obvious angle is the UK regulatory urgency combined with a global-first architecture. Every funded competitor (SPRX, Neo.Tax, Boast) is US-only or US-plus-Canada. A 40x increase in HMRC audit rates creates acute demand for audit-defensible documentation in a market where no well-funded startup has built compliant coverage. That's a narrow but real nonobvious wedge -- the UK-specific timing, not the broader R&D tax automation category, is where the alpha lives.

Where Taxnova gets more interesting through my framework is the advisor-agnostic infrastructure model. This is the pattern I look for after market timing: is the company building a layer that other businesses depend on? Taxnova's strategy of becoming the data and documentation infrastructure that advisory firms (EY, BDO, Grant Thornton) run on, rather than replacing those firms, mirrors a dynamic I've seen work repeatedly. The parallel to Gusto is instructive -- Gusto didn't try to eliminate accountants, it built the payroll platform that accountants and SMBs both used, creating two-sided switching costs. Taxnova's 15+ read-only integrations across Jira, Linear, GitHub, GitLab, Slack, Xero, QuickBooks, and NetSuite create genuine integration depth. The completed pilot with a Top 5 R&D advisory firm suggests the channel strategy isn't theoretical. If Taxnova becomes the default data-extraction-and-narrative-generation layer that advisory firms standardize on, the switching costs compound with each claim cycle. That's infrastructure behavior, not application behavior, even if the end output is a document.

The single-miracle test is where Taxnova scores strongest. The technology miracle -- can LLMs generate audit-defensible technical narratives from engineering artifacts? -- is the only breakthrough required. Tax advisory firms are proven buyers of efficiency tools. Regulatory approval isn't needed because Taxnova works alongside existing advisors who already hold regulatory relationships. Distribution has a clear channel through the advisory partnership. $100K ARR with named customers like incident.io and Paddle, plus an enterprise pilot at $200K+ ACV potential, constitutes early evidence that the miracle is happening. The incident.io testimonial -- "made R&D tax claims effortless and audit-ready" -- is exactly the signal I want: the output meets the audit-defensibility bar, which is the single threshold that matters. Compare this to a company that needs AI accuracy AND regulatory approval AND channel development simultaneously -- Taxnova needs the AI accuracy, and early customers suggest it has it.

George Nichkov (Oliver Wyman, Gett product lead) and Maria Malykh (Criteo program manager coordinating 9 R&D teams, prior co-founder at awaitly) bring competent B2B product backgrounds without the hypergrowth scaling experience I weight heavily. Neither operated at a company during its 10-to-1,000 sprint. What partially compensates: the broader five-person team includes domain expertise from EY, BDO, Grant Thornton, and the IRS, and Revolut's Head of Tax serves as an advisor. In a compliance-heavy category, domain expertise on the team can matter more than scaling pedigree at the earliest stage. The clear CEO/CTO structure avoids the equal-cofounder red flag. Still, when I compare this founder profile to Parker Conrad rebuilding after Zenefits or the Brex founders who'd been operating since age 14, there's a gap in demonstrated scaling capacity.

The strongest bull case for Taxnova requires believing three things: first, that the multi-jurisdiction moat is genuinely durable -- that building HMRC-compliant coverage, then US federal and 30+ state schemes, then EU jurisdictions compounds into a barrier no single-market competitor can easily replicate. Second, that the advisor-channel strategy creates a distribution flywheel where each advisory firm partnership brings dozens of end clients. Third, that $100K ARR at pre-seed with $200K+ ACV enterprise potential signals real pricing power in a category where companies are recovering millions in tax credits -- making the software a small fraction of value delivered. If those three things hold, Taxnova could become the multi-jurisdiction infrastructure standard for R&D tax automation, a Gusto-like outcome in a different compliance vertical. The $3.28B projected software market by 2034 provides enough ceiling for venture returns, though it's not the kind of massive TAM that generates 100x outcomes at scale.

My primary concern remains the capital asymmetry. Taxnova enters the US market -- the $32B annual R&D credit opportunity -- with $1M against SPRX's $46M. Even with a differentiated multi-jurisdiction approach and advisor-channel strategy, competing for US enterprise customers against a competitor with 46x your capital and a multi-year head start requires exceptional execution. The advisor-agnostic model mitigates this somewhat -- Taxnova doesn't need to out-spend SPRX on direct sales if advisory firms become the distribution channel -- but the US "coming soon" status on Taxnova's own website tells me this competitive test hasn't begun. Evidence that would flip me toward stronger conviction: proof that advisory firm channel converts at scale (not just one pilot), retention data showing annual claim renewals at 90%+ rates, and a credible US launch timeline with signed LOIs from US-based advisory firms.

Taxnova lands in Neutral territory driven by two dimensions pulling in opposite directions. Infrastructure-Layer Positioning and Single-Miracle Viability both score well -- the advisor-agnostic model is genuine infrastructure thinking, and the one-miracle structure with early customer validation is favorable. But Nonobvious Market Timing, my most heavily weighted criterion at 30 points, works against Taxnova. This is a recognized market with well-funded competitors, and the nonobvious wedge (UK-first, multi-jurisdiction) is narrow rather than structural. At $100K ARR and a $1M raise, Taxnova has earned attention but not yet conviction.
