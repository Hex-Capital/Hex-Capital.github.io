# DroneTector -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Here's something unusual: I've already written a check to one of these founders. Thomas Doherty's previous company, Quantum Fabrix, raised a £2M seed that I led. QFX appointed a new CEO in October 2025, and now Doherty is CTO of DroneTector. I need to think carefully about whether my prior bet on Doherty biases me here. What I can say is that someone who has been through the deep-tech startup crucible once -- even if the first venture didn't work out the way they planned -- comes back knowing things that first-time founders don't. I funded Emmett Shear and Justin Kan after Kiko died. The pattern of backing founders through pivots and second acts is one I trust.

The origin of this idea passes my organic test cleanly. Matthew Moore didn't read about the drone threat in TechCrunch and decide to build a company. He spent years in the Millimetre Wave group at St Andrews literally constructing 94 GHz radar hardware and publishing peer-reviewed research on drone micro-Doppler signatures. The idea grew directly from his hands-on laboratory work -- he was one of a small number of people on earth who had actually built and tested the specific sensor that solves this problem. That's the opposite of manufactured. When I look at how founders find their problems, this is the deep-tech equivalent of Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive: Moore knew the existing radar systems couldn't see small drones because he'd spent his PhD proving it experimentally.

The schlep here is immense, and it's the right kind of immense. Custom millimetre-wave radar hardware. Defense procurement cycles that stretch 12-36 months. Export controls (ITAR, EAR). Manufacturing supply chains for specialized components. Certification processes. Every one of those words represents months of painful work that software founders unconsciously filter out. This is why Dedrone went software-only and got acquired by Axon -- they chose the easier path, which means they literally cannot detect autonomous drones flying without RF emissions. The hard path is the right path here, and most people can't even see it. Echodyne has $195M and operates at lower frequencies. Robin Radar pivoted from birds. DroneShield is a public company with AU$221M in cash. But none of them have built what Moore built because the specific physics expertise required to engineer production-grade W-band micro-Doppler radar is genuinely scarce. The schlep protects them.

The technical depth of this team is exceptional. Three PhDs -- physics, atomic/laser physics, computer science. Moore built the actual hardware. Doherty brings a decade of optical and laser systems plus deep-tech startup experience. Frances de Mas brings automated reasoning, which maps directly onto the signal classification problem. This is a team that can build the core product themselves, which matters enormously when the core product is custom radar hardware. You cannot outsource this. You cannot hire your way around it with a bigger engineering team unless those engineers have very specific expertise. The founding team IS the product at this stage, in a way that's even more true than for software startups.

Where I hesitate is on evidence of pull. NATO DIANA support and DASA backing are real institutional signals -- you don't get into NATO DIANA without someone in defense deciding this technology matters. But institutional support is not the same as a customer pulling the product toward them. I want to see a military unit or an airport authority that has tested this radar and is asking for more. The macro demand is undeniable -- Ukraine faces 1,600 drone attacks per month, Red Sea incidents doubled global shipping costs -- but macro demand doesn't automatically translate into demand for THIS specific product from THIS specific company. At pre-seed with a hardware product, thin traction evidence is expected, not damning. But it means I'm betting almost entirely on founder quality and technical differentiation, with minimal market validation.

The bull case is genuinely compelling. If the drone threat continues escalating -- and every indication is that it will -- governments will spend billions on detection systems, and the companies that built purpose-designed sensors rather than adapted existing technology will have a fundamental advantage. Moore's micro-Doppler approach solves a physics problem that software can't solve: distinguishing a $50 nano drone from a bird at range. The team has the scarce expertise, the institutional relationships, and the technical foundation. The Boom Supersonic analogy applies: when the ambition is high enough and the technical foundation is real, hardware bets can produce outsized returns precisely because the schlep scares everyone else away. The question is whether a three-person team can survive long enough to navigate defense procurement and scale hardware manufacturing before well-funded competitors converge on their frequency band.

I'm investing, but with clear eyes about the risks. Defense sales cycles could starve them. Doherty's transition from QFX needs scrutiny -- I want to understand what happened there and confirm his full commitment. The path from research prototype to field-deployable production unit is long and capital-intensive. But the organic origin is genuine, the schlep is protective, and the founding team has the rare combination of deep technical capability and startup experience. When I see three people who can actually build the thing they're trying to sell, working on a problem they discovered through years of focused research, in a market where the tailwinds are geopolitical rather than fashionable -- that's a bet I'm willing to make with my own money.
