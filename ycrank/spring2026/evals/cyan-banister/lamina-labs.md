# Lamina Labs -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

What draws me in here is the biographical thread. Sudip Rokaya grew up in Humla District — and if you haven't been to Nepal, let me tell you, Humla is not Kathmandu. It's one of the most remote regions in the country, accessible only by foot or small aircraft for much of the year. Getting from there to MIT through the international olympiad pipeline is a survival story with genuine teeth. Rohan's path from Butwal through Caltech via astrophysics research carries similar weight, maybe less dramatic but still — two kids from Nepal who fought through one of the world's most competitive academic gauntlets to reach the institutions they're at now. When I see that kind of journey, I want to know: is the problem they're solving the one that this specific journey prepared them for? STEM education accessibility — the idea that the tools to explain physics and mathematics visually have been locked behind either Python expertise or professional animation budgets — that resonates as something these founders would have felt personally. They lived the gap between having the aptitude to learn and not having the visual tools to learn from. I just wish I could hear them tell it to me directly. The dossier gives me the facts but not the fire.

Here's where I get stuck, though. "AI for education" is not a non-consensus bet in 2026. It's one of the most crowded, most discussed categories in the entire startup ecosystem right now. When I backed SpaceX, people thought Elon was insane. When I invested in Crusoe Energy, the idea of mining Bitcoin on gas flares made people physically uncomfortable. Lamina Labs is building something technically differentiated — deterministic rendering versus probabilistic generation is a genuinely distinct architectural choice — but the category itself doesn't make anyone uncomfortable. Nobody at a dinner party is going to say "you invested in WHAT?" This matters to me because the best returns in my portfolio have come from ideas that were actively unpopular, not just underserved. EdTech animation tools for STEM is underserved, yes. But underserved and unpopular are different things. The consensus capital is already circling this space — LASEV and TeachMaster papers are published, Runway is targeting educational content with Gen 4.5, and any motivated engineer could build a Manim-plus-LLM wrapper over a weekend hackathon.

The economic access angle is the strongest signal in my framework. This is genuinely about making something that was previously locked behind technical skill available to anyone with a text prompt. I think about how Affirm made transparent credit available to people shut out by opaque credit card systems, or how Thumbtack connected skilled tradespeople with customers who couldn't find them. If Pictor works as claimed — mathematically precise STEM animations from a single text prompt in under eight seconds — that could transform how community college instructors in rural America teach calculus, or how self-taught students in Sub-Saharan Africa learn physics. That's the kind of real-world impact I care about. Two founders from Nepal building tools that democratize STEM education globally has a beautiful symmetry to it. But I need to be honest: the access story is potential, not proven. There's no traction, no waitlist numbers, no user testimonials. The product is pre-launch. The access narrative is compelling in theory but untested in practice.

If I'm being fair about the bull case: if these founders have genuinely built a deterministic rendering engine that hits the 8-second speed claim at production quality across the full breadth of STEM subjects, that is a real technical moat. The pipeline architecture for converting arbitrary natural-language prompts into symbolically accurate animations is fundamentally different from what Runway or Synthesia has built — you can't just bolt this onto a diffusion model. And the founders have exactly the right technical background to pull it off — olympiad-level physics and mathematics combined with ML research at MIT's Graybiel Lab. The best version of this story is that they're building the infrastructure layer for all educational animation, the way Crusoe built the infrastructure layer for waste-energy-to-compute. In that scenario, the 8-second deterministic pipeline becomes the moat, the bottom-up adoption by students and educators creates distribution, and the founders' personal connection to educational access in the developing world keeps them focused when consensus competitors get distracted. That's a version of this company I'd want to back.

But I keep coming back to what's missing. The co-founder dynamic is unexamined in this dossier — I learned from HQ Trivia that even perfect timing and product can't overcome founder conflict, and I have no evidence of how Rohan and Sudip handle disagreement under pressure. They share a background through Nepal's olympiad ecosystem, which suggests alignment, but shared origin isn't the same as stress-tested partnership. The space itself doesn't have the regulatory or stigma complexity that typically creates durable moats in my portfolio — there's no FDA pathway, no defense procurement maze, no financial regulation to navigate. And the commoditization risk is real: academic research is already converging on this exact approach, and Manim Community is a sleeping giant with 3Blue1Brown's distribution behind it. For me, this lands as a competent founding team with a real insight in a space that's too consensus and too exposed to competition for the kind of bet I make. I'd tell these founders: I see you, I respect the journey, and I want to track this — but I need to see users loving this product before I write a check.
