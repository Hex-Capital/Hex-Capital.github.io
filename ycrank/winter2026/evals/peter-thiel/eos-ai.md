# Eos AI -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The competitive landscape tells you everything you need to know before you examine a single line of this company's code. Commure has raised over $820 million and is valued at $6 billion. Redox generates $181 million in annual revenue. Health Gorilla has $80 million in funding. Epic controls 38% of the US hospital EHR market and is building its own interoperability features. Google, Microsoft, and Amazon each offer healthcare data harmonization services. And now a two-person team fresh from Caltech proposes to build "the autonomous operating system for healthcare" -- in the middle of all of this. The healthcare data interoperability space is not a market with a secret hiding in it. It is a market where every participant shares the same thesis, the same customer base, and the same regulatory tailwind. When CMS interoperability mandates are driving demand, you do not have a contrarian insight -- you have a policy-driven consensus that every well-funded player is already exploiting. This is mimetic convergence at industrial scale.

What is the secret here? The stated problem -- that healthcare data is fragmented across EHRs, PACS, labs, and billing systems -- is perhaps the most widely discussed problem in health IT. It has been the subject of billions in government spending, decades of standards work (HL7, FHIR), and hundreds of startups. Every hospital CIO, every CMS administrator, and every healthcare VC already agrees this is important. A truth that everyone agrees with is not a secret. It is a convention. Eos's more specific claim -- that a federated data layer with AI-native harmonization is the right architecture -- is more interesting but still not genuinely contrarian. Federated approaches are standard computer science, and "AI-native" is the adjective that every 2025-era startup attaches to whatever they are building. The imaging harmonization engine VERA, which standardizes across scanner manufacturers and modalities, is the most technically specific element, and it represents genuine domain knowledge. But a technical capability is not a secret about the world. It is an engineering achievement, and engineering achievements without structural monopoly dynamics get competed away.

The bull case requires you to believe something specific: that the convergence of clinical AI deployment and data readiness creates a brief window where a purpose-built harmonization layer becomes critical infrastructure, and that Eos's particular combination of federated indexing, imaging harmonization, and clinical text normalization is sufficiently differentiated from both the incumbents and the well-funded startups to capture that window. If clinical AI models proliferate rapidly across radiology, pathology, and clinical decision support -- and if those models require preprocessing that existing interoperability tools cannot provide -- then the "ML-ready data" layer becomes a genuine bottleneck, and whoever owns that bottleneck owns significant leverage. The Stanford Medicine pilot via ONCOVATE suggests Eos has a relationship with a top-tier health system, and the founder's published research on physics-aware neural operators for medical imaging demonstrates that the technical depth is real, not performative. If you squint, you can see a version of this where VERA becomes the standard imaging preprocessing pipeline for every hospital deploying AI -- a narrow, defensible wedge. But this requires Eos to outexecute Commure's $820 million war chest, navigate 12-18 month enterprise sales cycles with two people, and avoid being absorbed by Epic's expanding platform -- all simultaneously.

The founder, Arya Khokhar, has the right technical pedigree: Caltech CS and mathematics, Stanford research, published ML work in medical imaging. The ONCOVATE cancer triage project piloted at Stanford Medicine shows genuine engagement with clinical problems, not theoretical interest. But I see no evidence of the kind of definite conviction that distinguishes a monopoly-building founder from a talented engineer solving an interesting problem. The company's tagline -- "the autonomous operating system for healthcare" -- is the opposite of definite. It is maximally vague and maximally ambitious simultaneously, which in my experience signals a company that has not yet decided what it actually is. Compare this to Palantir's founding thesis, which was brutally specific: the fraud algorithms we built at PayPal can be adapted for intelligence analysis. That is a definite plan derived from a specific insight. "The autonomous operating system for healthcare" is a category description, not a vision. A two-person team with no disclosed revenue, no named customers, and zero open job postings is not yet building toward anything I can evaluate as a concrete future.

The durability question is where this investment thesis collapses entirely. Even if Eos builds a superior harmonization layer, Epic has the structural power to render it irrelevant. Epic does not need to build a better product -- it needs only to offer a "good enough" native feature to its installed base of hospitals that are already locked into its ecosystem. The history of healthcare IT is littered with technically superior startups that were marginalized by incumbent platform power. Oracle Health has the same option. The cloud providers have even more resources. You must study the endgame before everything else, and the endgame here is a market where the structural power belongs to the platforms that already own the customer relationship. A startup that builds middleware between systems controlled by incumbents is, at best, building a feature that will eventually be absorbed -- and at worst, competing directly against entities with three to four orders of magnitude more capital.

I pass. The problem is real, the founder is technically capable, and the imaging harmonization angle has genuine specificity. But there is no secret here -- only a consensus problem that is already attracting enormous capital from multiple directions. Competition in healthcare data integration is intensifying, not decreasing, and nothing in this company's positioning suggests it can escape that competition to build a monopoly. The power law demands that I invest only in companies where the outcome, if positive, is extreme and structurally defensible. A two-person startup entering a market with a $6 billion incumbent, multiple well-funded competitors, and dominant platform players controlling distribution does not meet that threshold.
