# Perfectly -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Perfectly is the competitive landscape. Juicebox has $36 million and $10M ARR. Paraform has $25 million and 40x revenue growth. A company literally called "Perfect" raised $23 million for AI recruiting. Dover raised $23 million. Moonhub got acquired by Salesforce. That's over $100 million in funding pointed at variations of the same problem. When I see a category this hot, I get nervous. The best startup ideas are the ones nobody else wants to touch -- not because they're contrarian, but because the work involved makes people unconsciously look away. AI recruiting is the opposite. Everyone sees the problem. Everyone is building the solution. The schlep here isn't hidden. It's so visible that it attracted nine figures of capital before Perfectly wrote their first line of code.

Now, the organic discovery is real, and I want to be clear about that. Victor Luo sat through 800+ agency-sourced interviews at TikTok. That's not someone who read a TechCrunch article about the recruiting market and decided it looked big. He lived inside this problem, day after day, watching information get lost at every handoff between intake and sourcer, sourcer and candidate, candidate and hiring manager. And his specific diagnosis -- that the problem is fundamentally one of preference capture and matching, not just search -- is the kind of insight you'd expect from someone who spent years building recommendation systems. He didn't just see that recruiting is broken. He saw *why* it's broken in a way that maps directly onto his technical expertise. That's genuine founder-market fit.

The technical team is strong. Three ML engineers from TikTok, all of whom worked on recommendation systems -- arguably the most sophisticated matching technology in the world. When your core product challenge is "match candidates to jobs based on rich preference signals," and your team literally built TikTok's For You page, that's not a coincidence. They can build this product themselves, which matters. The question isn't whether they can build something technically impressive. The question is whether technical superiority in matching algorithms is what wins in recruiting, or whether recruiting is fundamentally a relationship and trust business where the algorithm is necessary but not sufficient.

The bull case deserves a full hearing. If Perfectly's intake process genuinely captures what hiring managers want at a fidelity that human recruiters can't match -- the unstated preferences about team dynamics, growth trajectory, interview style -- then each placement generates training data that makes the next placement better. The reusable candidate AI screen is clever: candidates do the screen once and it applies across multiple jobs, which creates a candidate-side network effect that's genuinely hard to replicate. Success-based pricing removes all risk for the customer, which is a strong GTM wedge. And the fact that one customer dropped both Paraform and Juicebox within two weeks suggests the end-to-end approach -- from intake through delivery -- is categorically different from tools that only handle sourcing or only provide a marketplace. If the data flywheel compounds fast enough, they could build a moat that Juicebox can't replicate by adding features and Paraform can't replicate without firing their recruiters.

But I keep coming back to the market structure. When I funded Stripe, the reason nobody had built it wasn't that the opportunity was invisible -- every programmer knew payments were painful. It was that nobody wanted to deal with banks, fraud systems, and financial regulation. The schlep was the moat. In AI recruiting, the schlep is... what exactly? Building LLM-based matching? Every funded competitor is doing that. Automating outreach? That's table stakes. The hard part of recruiting has always been on the human side: convincing passive candidates to engage, managing the emotional dynamics of hiring decisions, building trust with both sides of the market. Those are schleps that don't get easier with better ML models. And the regulatory overhang -- EU AI Act classifying hiring AI as high-risk, NYC's bias audit requirements -- is the kind of thing that could slow everyone down, but hurts a three-person startup more than it hurts Juicebox with $36 million in the bank.

Victor being a second-time founder is a positive signal, though I don't have enough information about his first company to evaluate what he learned from it. The team's shared TikTok background means they know each other professionally, which reduces co-founder risk. But I don't see evidence of the kind of creative resourcefulness that distinguishes great founders from merely competent ones. No stories of adapting when plans failed, no signs of doing things that don't scale to acquire early users. They launched through YC's network and a Fondo blog post. That's fine, but it's also exactly what a well-credentialed team would do by default.

This is a competent team with genuine domain pain in a category that's too crowded for my taste. The organic discovery earns real points. The technical depth earns real points. But when I count five well-funded competitors in the same space, I'm not looking at a problem hiding in plain sight -- I'm looking at a consensus bet. The companies I've made the most money on were the ones where the competition was structurally deterred, not the ones where the founding team was marginally better than a dozen others chasing the same opportunity.
