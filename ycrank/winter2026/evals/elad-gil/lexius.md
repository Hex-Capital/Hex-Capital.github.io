# Lexius -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Lexius is the competitive landscape, and it's not encouraging for my framework. Veesion has raised €53M and deployed in 5,000+ stores across 25 countries. Everseen has ~$90M and serves 10 of the top 20 global retailers. Spot AI raised $93M. Auror has NZ$82M and a network of 45,000 stores connected to 3,000 law enforcement agencies. ArcadianAI uses the identical "AI Security Guards" tagline. When I see a pre-seed company entering a market where competitors have collectively raised $300M+, my non-obvious market filter triggers hard -- in the wrong direction. This isn't PagerDuty, where the market seemed too boring for anyone to fund. Retail AI security is consensus. Every LP deck in venture right now has a slide about $112B in retail shrinkage. That number is in the news constantly. When a market's pain point is on the front page of the Wall Street Journal, you can be certain every growth-stage fund has already made their bet.

The structural "why now" is real but it's not recent enough to create asymmetric opportunity. Computer vision models crossed the commercial viability threshold for real-time behavioral detection in 2023-2024, IP cameras with RTSP/ONVIF protocols are ubiquitous, and post-COVID theft surges created budget urgency. All true -- and all visible to Veesion when they raised their €38M Series B in May 2025, to Everseen when they raised $71M in 2023, and to every other player in the space. When I invested in Harvey, legal AI looked impossible to most investors because GPT-3 couldn't handle legal-grade accuracy. Nobody was funding it. That's a structural moment. When I look at retail AI surveillance in 2026, the structural moment happened two to three years ago and the well-capitalized first movers are already scaling.

Where Lexius genuinely impresses me is execution velocity. Landing 7-Eleven, Erewhon, and Prada as named customers at pre-seed stage is notable -- these are not easy enterprise accounts to close, especially for a three-person team that relocated from the Netherlands. The case studies show real quantified outcomes: FineFare cut theft by 63% in 30 days, an unnamed retailer saw 4.7x ROI in six weeks. David Elskamp's track record -- founding a media agency at 14 with Adidas and Heineken as clients, most entrepreneurial student at Twente -- signals someone who closes deals. The sub-10-minute remote install is a genuine distribution innovation that could enable faster deployment velocity than competitors requiring on-site work. If those enterprise logos represent real multi-location deployments rather than single-store pilots, the execution story is strong.

The bull case I'd want to believe: Lexius's software-only, zero-hardware approach is a distribution wedge analogous to Stripe's developer-first API. Where Veesion and Everseen require integration complexity, Lexius deploys in minutes. Over time, the camera integration becomes a distribution channel for broader retail intelligence -- foot traffic analytics, planogram compliance, inventory tracking, employee safety. The known offender database across chains creates a network effect where each store addition increases the value for every participant. If Lexius captures the "easy deploy" segment of the market while competitors fight over complex enterprise deals, they could build the largest installed base and win through network density rather than sales force size. That's a path I can sketch -- but the founders haven't articulated it, and the current product is purely single-purpose loss prevention. The platform trajectory is plausible but unspoken.

Here's my concern with that bull case: Auror already built the cross-chain crime intelligence network with 45,000 stores and law enforcement connections. Veesion already has the behavioral AI in 5,000+ stores. The "easy install" differentiation is real but potentially thin -- if Veesion or Spot AI simplifies their onboarding (which they will, because that's what competition does), Lexius loses its one structural advantage. The core technology -- behavior detection via video -- is reproducible. Multiple teams have already reproduced it. This company requires at least two things to go right: winning enterprise accounts against vastly better-funded competitors AND building a cross-chain network effect before Auror extends its existing network into real-time detection. That starts to feel like a multi-miracle plan.

I respect the execution here more than the market positioning. The team ships fast, closes hard accounts, and has a privacy-first approach that aligns well with the regulatory trajectory (EU AI Act, BIPA). But my framework demands a non-obvious market at a structural inflection point, and retail AI surveillance in 2026 is neither non-obvious nor at its inflection -- it's mid-cycle with established leaders. Strong operators in a consensus market produce good companies. They rarely produce the asymmetric outcomes I'm underwriting.
