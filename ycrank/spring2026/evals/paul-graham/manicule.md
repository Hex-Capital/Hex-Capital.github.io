# Manicule -- Paul Graham Primary Source Research: Structured Quote Record Evaluation

The thing that jumps out immediately about Manicule is a structural question, not a quality question: this is a services business. They have five people serving five clients at $15K MRR. That's $3K per person per month — in San Francisco. I wrote that "a startup is a company designed to grow fast," and the defining characteristic of growth is that it compounds. Services businesses don't compound. They grow linearly with headcount. Every new client requires more human attention, more review cycles, more time. The founders describe their AI agents handling "mechanical tasks" while humans handle "positioning, architecture, and refinement," but at the current ratio — five people, five clients — the AI isn't yet doing enough of the work to change the fundamental economics. This isn't a startup problem; it's a consultancy that got into YC.

The founders do have genuine domain expertise, which matters. Naman spent 3+ years doing exactly this work — technical content for devtools companies — and Shreyans was a founding engineer at Supermemory, which became Manicule's first showcase client. They noticed the problem firsthand, which aligns with how good startup ideas actually emerge: "The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas. It's to look for problems, preferably problems you have yourself." They've worked together before at Duelance, so the co-founder relationship has been tested. These are competent people who understand their domain. But domain expertise in technical writing is different from the kind of obsessive founder energy that makes you say, as I did about the Airbnbs, "it was impossible not to like them." Building a profitable docs agency is solid work. It's not the same as selling $40 cereal boxes to keep your startup alive.

The bull case — and I want to take it seriously — is that Manicule is doing what the best companies do early: things that don't scale. "Startups take off because the founders make them take off." Maybe every client engagement is training data for a future platform. Maybe the human-in-the-loop services model is a wedge into building documentation generation software that actually works, because they'll have seen hundreds of real documentation architectures and know what the AI gets wrong. The Supermemory case study going viral on Twitter, driving a "+30% answer success rate" and closing enterprise deals — that's a real signal of impact. If the ratio of AI-to-human work shifts dramatically over the next year, the unit economics could transform. Documentation is genuinely a schlep that companies avoid — "your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps" — and the timing is real: thousands of AI startups launching developer tools, all needing docs, none hiring technical writers.

But here's what troubles me. There's no public indication of a planned product pivot. The dossier says so explicitly. And Mintlify — already at $10M ARR with $21M raised from a16z — acquired Trieve for RAG infrastructure and is moving toward AI-powered documentation generation. If the endgame is "software that writes docs," Mintlify has a massive head start and far more resources. If the endgame is "agency that writes docs," then this is a fine business but not one where "effectively all the returns are concentrated in a few big winners." I keep coming back to the question I ask every founder: are you default alive or default dead? They're profitable, which is good — but $15K MRR split across five people isn't really alive in any venture-meaningful sense. It's surviving. And the commoditization risk is immediate: any technical writer with Claude or GPT can do a version of what Manicule does. The "AI-native" label doesn't constitute a moat when the AI tools are available to everyone.

The founders are earnest, they have domain knowledge, and they're solving a real problem. I don't doubt any of that. But when I think about whether to invest my own money, I have to ask: what's the path to returns that justify the risk? "You have to ignore the elephant in front of you, the likelihood they'll succeed, and focus instead on whether they'll succeed really big." Manicule might succeed — they might build a healthy, profitable documentation agency that serves hundreds of developer tool companies. But "really big" requires either a product transition they haven't signaled or a services scaling model that defies the structural constraints every services business faces. I don't see the black swan here.
