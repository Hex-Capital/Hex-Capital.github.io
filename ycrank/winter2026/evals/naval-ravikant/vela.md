# Vela -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I look for is what the founder knows that can't be taught. The Korisepati brothers are clearly intelligent and high-energy -- Gobhanu founded a microfinance operation across 77 countries at fourteen, Saatvik completed a dual degree at CMU in two years with highest honors and shipped code on AWS's ML Supercompute team. That's impressive raw horsepower. But when I ask the specific knowledge question -- what do these founders understand about scheduling coordination that a smart generalist with six months of research couldn't replicate -- the dossier is quiet. Gobhanu's stints at BCG AI and Perplexity give him general AI product intuition. Saatvik's AWS engineering gives him infrastructure chops. Neither has spent years embedded in the recruiting coordination workflow, watching how scheduling breaks at scale, developing the kind of non-transferable pattern recognition that comes from living inside a problem. They observed a pain point and applied capable technology to it. That's a different animal than founder-problem authenticity.

The leverage test reveals a similar gap. Vela replaces a human scheduling coordinator with an AI agent. That's labor substitution -- valuable, but not the kind of leverage creation I look for. When Uber launched, it didn't just automate taxi dispatch; it turned every car owner into a micro-entrepreneur and every smartphone into a dispatch system. That's new leverage. Vela makes recruiters 15 minutes faster per interview. That's efficiency, not a new category of capability. The product sits at the application layer, solving a specific workflow problem for a specific vertical. My portfolio consistently gravitates toward infrastructure -- platforms that other products get built on, tools that create leverage for entire categories of workers. A scheduling agent for recruiting teams is a useful product, but it's not a picks-and-shovels play.

The contrarian timing test is where this falls apart most clearly. "AI agent for scheduling" is perhaps the single most consensus startup thesis in early 2026. The "why now" story -- LLMs crossed a capability threshold that makes conversational scheduling possible -- is true, but it's true for everyone simultaneously. Clara Labs failed in 2019 because the NLP wasn't ready. Now that it's ready, every team with API access to a frontier model can build a scheduling agent. The market has Motion at $550M valuation, Clockwise with $76M raised, Reclaim acquired by Dropbox, and Paradox acquired by Workday for serious money. Smart people are not dismissing AI scheduling -- they're funding it aggressively. You don't get paid for being right when everyone else is also right. The biggest risk isn't even from other startups; it's from Google shipping a "schedule this for me" button inside Gemini + Google Calendar, or Microsoft doing the same with Copilot + Outlook. When the platform owners can commoditize your entire product with a feature update, you're not building on solid ground.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement. The traction signals -- thousands of interviews scheduled weekly, logos like Netflix, LinkedIn, and AWS -- are meaningful at pre-seed and suggest the product actually works in enterprise environments. The sibling co-founder relationship, stress-tested since they were teenagers building SWIFTmfi together, eliminates co-founder risk in a way few startups can claim. The multi-channel approach (email, SMS, WhatsApp, Slack, Teams, phone) creates integration surface area that becomes switching cost over time. And Paradox's absorption into Workday genuinely opens a market gap for non-Workday recruiting teams. If Vela can accumulate enough organizational scheduling data to make its priority inference and edge-case handling measurably better than a generic LLM agent, the data flywheel could become a real moat. The path to a meaningful outcome exists -- I just don't see what prevents a dozen well-funded teams from running the same playbook.

What would have to be true for this to be a great investment: the scheduling negotiation problem would need to be far harder than it appears -- hard enough that accumulated data from thousands of enterprise deployments creates a quality gap that no new entrant can close. The multi-channel integration would need to function as a genuine network effect rather than just a feature list. And Google, Microsoft, and Apple would all need to decide that autonomous outbound scheduling negotiation is too niche for their platforms. That's three independent assumptions, each requiring something specific to go right. I don't invest in companies that need multiple things to break their way simultaneously. I invest in companies where the founder's specific knowledge and the leverage architecture make the outcome feel almost inevitable if you look at it from the right angle. I don't see that inevitability here.
