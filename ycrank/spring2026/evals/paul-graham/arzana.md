# Arzana -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that catches my eye about Arzana is how deeply unsexy the work is. When I think about the typical Stanford CS graduate's career trajectory, it involves building developer tools, or a social app, or maybe an AI wrapper with a clean API. It does not involve integrating with Epicor and JobBoss, parsing handwritten factory notes, and driving out to plants in Iowa to watch how quoting actually works. This is schlep blindness territory — the kind of problem your unconscious won't let you see because every part of it sounds terrible. Eleven ERP integrations maintained by four people. Manufacturing RFQs arriving as PDFs, emails, and literal handwritten notes. Implementation cycles of one to three months. These are the details that make most founders flinch and move on to something cleaner. Which is exactly why the competitive landscape is so thin despite the market being enormous.

What I find most interesting about William Alexander is not his resume but his autodidact pattern. He taught himself Mandarin in Hong Kong, learned discus from YouTube videos well enough to rank at the state level, and is now building a manufacturing automation company having taken one coding class. That last fact is simultaneously the most concerning and the most revealing thing in the dossier. It's concerning because I've always believed founders need to build the product themselves — iteration speed depends on it. But the pattern underneath is telling. This is someone who decides to learn something, figures out the fastest path to competence, and gets to a level that surprises people who've been doing it for years. The discus story is a small thing, but it's the kind of small thing that reveals the right underlying structure. You don't teach yourself a throwing sport from video and reach state competition without the relentlessly resourceful quality I look for.

The Iowa angle is what separates this from a market-research-driven pitch. Alexander grew up in Okoboji. Mason Vander Pol, the founding field engineer, is also from Okoboji. The company maintains a physical presence there alongside San Francisco. This isn't two Stanford kids who read a Grand View Research report about the $17 billion manufacturing operations management market. This is someone who grew up watching how manufacturing offices actually work — the emails, the manual quoting, the five-day turnaround times — and came back with the tools to fix it. The consulting work for Italian manufacturers through Brilla adds another layer. The pivot from meal delivery to manufacturing AI isn't the cleanest origin story I've ever seen, but the underlying thread is that these founders kept circling back to manufacturing because that's the world they came from. ClassDojo worked in part because the founders were willing to slog through school-district procurement that repelled everyone else. Arzana has a similar dynamic — these founders are willing to sit in factory offices in Iowa because they're from factory towns in Iowa.

The traction at this stage is genuinely surprising. Twenty-plus plants. Schneider National and Zeiss as customers — one Fortune 500, one global industrial brand. A customer testimonial about cutting time-to-quote from five days to one. Published pricing at $2,500 to $7,000 per month. For a pre-seed company with four people, landing enterprise logos like these suggests either extraordinary sales ability or a product that solves something so painful that customers pull it toward them. Either signal is good.

The non-technical CEO is the gap I keep coming back to. Alexander took one coding class. The entire technical capability rests on Joe Politi and Mason Vander Pol, neither of whom has a visible public track record. At a four-person company, if one of those engineers leaves, the whole thing is in serious trouble. This isn't disqualifying — plenty of great companies have had non-technical CEOs — but at the earliest stage, I want at least one founder who can open a terminal and ship a fix at midnight. The wrestling background and the making-$10K-a-year sacrifice tell me the determination is real. But determination without technical depth in the founding team means you're one bad hire or one departure away from a crisis. Endeavor AI sitting out there with $7M from Craft Ventures and presumably a deeper engineering bench makes this more acute.

If I'm wrong about this concern — if Alexander's one-coding-class claim understates his actual technical involvement, or if Politi turns out to be the kind of engineer who punches above every expectation — then this could be a very good company. The manufacturing front office is a massive schlep that creates natural moats. Each ERP integration is switching cost. Each customer's document formats become training data. The combination of Iowa-rooted distribution and San Francisco AI talent is genuinely hard to replicate. And the "AI for manufacturing" timing window is real — LLMs crossed the threshold for parsing unstructured manufacturing documents in the last two years, and labor shortages in factory admin roles are creating pull. If the technical team holds together and keeps shipping, the compounding dynamics are there.

I'd invest, but with less conviction than I'd like. The schlep factor is exactly the kind of competitive moat I look for. The Iowa roots make the problem discovery more organic than typical. The traction is impressive for the stage. But the technical founding risk keeps me from full enthusiasm. This reminds me a bit of the early Airbnb dynamic — an idea I wouldn't have picked, founders whose background didn't scream "this is the team" on paper, but with traction that suggested users were pulling the product out of their hands. The difference is that Brian Chesky could build. Here, I'm betting on Alexander's learning velocity and the hope that the engineering team is stronger than what the public record shows.
