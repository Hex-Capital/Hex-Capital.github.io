# Sazabi -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the thing that jumps off the page: Sazabi is entering a category where LangSmith just raised $125M at a billion-and-a-quarter valuation, Arize pulled in $70M, ClickHouse paid real money to acquire Langfuse, and Datadog — a $50B+ public company doing $2.1B in annual revenue — is building every single feature on Sazabi's roadmap into their existing platform. This is the opposite of a mispriced opportunity. Every investor at every demo day this cycle is nodding along to "AI observability." When every smart VC in the room is excited about your category, the upside is already in the price. There's nothing left for me. I back things where the market is wrong — where I'm buying at four cents because everyone else thinks it's zero. Sazabi is operating in a space where the market has already poured hundreds of millions into near-identical bets. The risk isn't in the price. It's priced for success in a category that may not support another winner.

The AI kill test here is almost too clean. Datadog's entire reason for existence is observability. When someone pitches me "better observability with AI," that's the definition of "this is perfect for Datadog." Conversational debugging? Feature. Autonomous alerts? Feature. Agent telemetry? Datadog's product team is probably shipping that next quarter. Software companies that had moats are learning they aren't moats anymore, and Sazabi is building a pure software company in the exact category where the largest incumbent has maximum incentive and capability to absorb the innovation. There are no physical assets, no protocol-level network effects, no regulatory moat. The SOC 2 and ISO certs are table stakes that Datadog has had for years. The "Perfect Memory" learning from past incidents could theoretically create switching costs, but that's a hope, not a moat.

Now — the bull case deserves a real paragraph because there's one genuinely interesting idea buried here. The "logs are all you need" thesis — that metrics and traces can be reconstructed from logs via AI, giving you three pillars for the price of one — is a provocative architectural bet. If true, it would fundamentally restructure observability pricing. Datadog makes money charging separately for logs, metrics, traces, and APM. A company that collapses all of that into log ingestion alone would force Datadog to cannibalize its own revenue model to compete. That's actually the kind of business model disruption that creates real opportunity — not a feature advantage, but a pricing structure that the incumbent can't copy without blowing up their P&L. And Callaway's background is legitimately strong for this: he built observability at Brex, founded and sold Opkit (AI phone calling, acquired by 11x), then ran engineering on one of the largest agentic systems in production. He's seen the problem from the infrastructure side, the AI product side, and the agent-at-scale side. That's real founder-market fit, not résumé decoration. If the logs-only thesis works at production scale and if AI coding agents truly generate fundamentally different failure modes that require purpose-built tooling, this could be the company that redefines how observability is priced and delivered. That's a real "what if they're right" scenario.

But I don't think they're right — or more precisely, I don't think being right matters enough. Even if logs-only works technically, the idea isn't insane. It's sensible. A rational person building a reasonable product in a hot market. The sanity-insanity ratio is inverted from what I want: the idea is too sane, the market too validated. Nobody hears "AI observability platform" and says "that's fucking crazy, tell me more." They say "oh, like Datadog but for AI? Yeah, makes sense." That's the death knell for me. I want the deals where smart people think I've lost my mind. This is a deal where everyone nods approvingly, which means the consensus has already priced it. The 10-person team with zero public revenue or customers, heavy compliance spend, and a waitlist-only product also gives me pause on capital discipline — that's a lot of burn for a pre-product company, and it suggests they're building for enterprise sales cycles before proving the core thesis works.

The founder's good. The market's real. The product thesis is intellectually interesting. But none of that makes it my kind of bet. This is a crowded application-layer AI startup in a consensus category where the largest incumbent has every incentive to eat them alive. Pass.
