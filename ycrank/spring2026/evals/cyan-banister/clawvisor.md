# Clawvisor -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Eric Levine sold Berbix to Socure for ~$70M in June 2023 and is now building Clawvisor solo as the authorization layer for AI agents — and that one sentence is the whole tension for me. Clawvisor's signature in my portfolio is a credentialed serial-founder play (his highest dimension) running directly into a consensus, well-funded category (his lowest). When I ask "tell me your story, not the business," the Clawvisor answer reads like a thoughtful adjacency walk — Google to Airbnb Trust & Safety to Berbix identity verification to agent credential vaulting — not a biographical crucible. It's a methodical thread, and I respect threads, but threads are not forges.

On Founder's Biographical Forge, the high rung in my portfolio is Substrate's James Proud — I wrote that he was "forged in fire and came out unbroken" — and the low rung is the pass-example founder who says "I was at Google and saw the opportunity." Eric Levine sits closer to my Affirm-Max-Levchin mid-rung than to either pole: a real builder with a $70M exit and direct domain adjacency between Berbix's spoofed-ID forensics and Clawvisor's prompt-injection detection, but no disclosed survival story, no homelessness-equivalent, no evidence the problem found him before he reasoned his way to it from a YC Visiting Group Partner seat. His GitHub at 70 followers and Berbix-to-Socure pattern tells me he is excellent at building shippable security infrastructure to an acquihire-shaped outcome — which is a real skill, and not the same as biographical inevitability.

Contrarian Conviction is where Clawvisor sits weakest, and this is the dimension that decides the verdict for me. My high rung is Anduril, which Silicon Valley actively shunned; my low rung is the consensus AI wrapper from my pass example. Clawvisor lives near the low rung: Composio just raised $25M Series A from Lightspeed in July 2025, Arcade closed $12M led by Laude in March 2025, Anon raised $6.5M from USV and Abstract Ventures, and Auth0 plus WorkOS plus HashiCorp are all shipping agent-specific auth modules from within mature distribution. Eric Levine is not getting told "no" by 300 investors the way the Uber driver's company did when I wrote that check — he is getting onto Garry Tan's roster as a known-quantity ex-VGP. No room of sophisticated VCs is shifting uncomfortably when Clawvisor is described. The space is loud, legible, and consensus.

On Invisible System Made Visible, my high rung is Uber dismantling the medallion cartel and Affirm exposing credit card APR opacity; my low rung is HQ Trivia, which had no system to disrupt. Clawvisor sits in a genuinely interesting middle: there is a real argument that agent runtimes receiving raw OAuth tokens — 45.6% API keys, 17.8% mTLS per the Gravitee survey — represents a hidden blast-radius problem most users don't see. But this is a developer hygiene problem inside enterprises, not an extractive system grinding against vulnerable people who lack information. Affirm gave a teenager visibility into APR; Clawvisor gives a platform engineer a policy gateway. Adjacent shape, very different moral physics.

On Defensibility-at-Inflection, my high rung is Crusoe and Substrate — physical and regulatory moats compounding at a real inflection — and my low rung is IRL with its fabricated metrics and zero structural barrier. Clawvisor sits closer to the low rung than I want: 14+ adapters versus Composio's 500+ and Nango's 700+, an open-source Go repo at 86 stars and 11 forks, no proprietary risk-scoring corpus disclosed, and an explicit standardization risk if MCP absorbs authorization into the protocol the way Arcade's "Core MCP Capability" suggests is coming. The moat thesis would have to be policy/audit data accumulated over years — and the dossier flags this is not present today. On Cascading Impact, my Postmates/Serve Robotics rung expands gig access to underserved geographies; Clawvisor's B2B security framing has no comparable second-order access mechanism for ordinary people. It's mid-to-low — useful, not transformative.

My 36-month forecast for Clawvisor: acquihired by Okta/Auth0, WorkOS, or HashiCorp for $40-100M as a team-and-tech tuck-in, with no breakout independent outcome. The mechanism is identical to the Berbix-to-Socure path Eric Levine has already walked once — a credentialed solo founder builds shippable security infrastructure inside a category with obvious acquirer logic, raises modestly, ships adapters, and gets absorbed when the incumbent identity vendor decides agent auth is a feature rather than a category. The acquirer landscape isn't even hidden — Auth0 launched Token Vault for AI Agents and WorkOS launched FGA, both targeting the same buyer Clawvisor is courting.

The bull case stings, and I want to honor it: Eric Levine is exactly the founder I'd want building this if it were a category I believed in — Airbnb Trust & Safety engineering plus Berbix's spoofed-ID forensics is a more direct credential-domain résumé than any competitor founder I see, and the agentic-AI cybersecurity TAM projection of $322B by 2033 is real-shaped if MCP fragments instead of standardizes. Garry Tan knowing him from his VGP seat is unusual signal density. If I'm wrong about consensus, this could be the AWS IAM of agents. But my pass example was literally written about this profile — credentialed founder, hot category, no biographical inevitability, no stigmatized space, no 300-no's — and I trust that pattern more than the credentials. The condition that flips my verdict: Clawvisor signs two named Fortune-500 enterprise customers on multi-year contracts before Auth0 ships its v1 Token Vault GA, demonstrating that purpose-based per-call enforcement actually beats incumbent distribution. Absent that, this is a respectful pass.
