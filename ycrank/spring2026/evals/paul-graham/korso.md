# Korso -- Paul Graham Primary Source Research: Structured Quote Record Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Korso is that five other funded startups are already building AI quoting tools for manufacturers. Paperless Parts has $30M and a customer base. Uptool has Khosla, Eclipse, Bessemer, and Kleiner Perkins behind a $6M seed. Gendra, Mercura AI, and Endeavor AI are all circling the same problem. I wrote once that "it's exceptionally rare for startups to be killed by competitors," and I believe that — but I believe it about startups that have found product-market fit, not about pre-seed companies with no visible traction entering a field where the idea is clearly consensus. The best startup ideas look initially like bad ideas. "AI for manufacturing quoting" does not look like a bad idea. It looks like an idea that everyone simultaneously realized was obvious the moment GPT-4 could parse a technical drawing, which is exactly what happened.

Now — I also wrote about schlep blindness. "Your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps." And manufacturing quoting is genuinely schlep-heavy: messy RFQ emails, WhatsApp messages, technical drawings, pricing histories trapped in spreadsheets. It's a lot more intimidating to build this than a recipe site. Three young founders choosing to wade into the pain of manufacturing operations rather than building another developer tool or social app — that's not nothing. It tells me something about their orientation. But schlep willingness is the entry ticket, not the differentiator. Half a dozen other teams also noticed the schlep.

The founders are where this decision lives, as it always does at pre-seed. Daichi Hiraoka and Martin Pan share a genuine bond — Penn Electric Racing, both from Irvine, California. That co-founder relationship matters enormously. "Startups do to the relationship between the founders what a dog does to a sock." These two probably won't get pulled apart. Martin's year at GM as an Automations Engineer gives him some manufacturing context, and Daichi's ML research (reducing dose prediction discrepancies by 97% in radiation therapy) demonstrates real technical capability. Alex Liu is the wildcard — a dropout who says he reads more research papers now than in school. I respect dropping out when you're compelled to build. But "I read papers" is not the same as "I built something people use." What I'm looking for is evidence of relentless resourcefulness — the Airbnb cereal boxes moment, the Collison installation where Patrick would grab your laptop and set you up on the spot. Some signal that these founders make things happen through sheer force of will. I don't find it in the public record. No customers mentioned. No prototype usage data. No waitlist. No scrappy early revenue. Nothing that says "we couldn't not do this."

The strongest bull case: these are technical founders in their early twenties with complementary engineering backgrounds (electrical, mechanical/robotics, software) attacking an enormous underdigitized market at exactly the right moment. LLMs genuinely crossed a capability threshold for parsing unstructured manufacturing documents in 2024-2025. Only 35% of procurement teams use AI for RFQs — the adoption window is early. If Korso nails the product for even a narrow vertical within manufacturing, the data flywheel (accumulated pricing history, customer relationships, quoting patterns) could compound into real switching costs. Young founders with no baggage sometimes see the product opportunity more clearly than experienced operators weighed down by "how things have always been done." Zuckerberg succeeded "despite being a complete n00b at startups" because he had domain expertise in his users. If these founders develop that depth of understanding of their manufacturing customers — not just the technical problem but the humans and their workflows — they could win a segment despite being outfunded.

But I keep coming back to a gap. "Good people can fix bad ideas, but good ideas can't save bad people." At this stage I'm picking founders, not ideas. And what I need to see in founders is evidence they pass the animal test — "someone who does what they do so well that they pass right through professional and cross over into obsessive." The public record shows three capable engineering students with relevant academic backgrounds and a reasonable idea. It doesn't show the kind of determination that makes you think they'd sell cereal boxes to keep the company alive. It doesn't show domain expertise deep enough to be a wedge against better-funded competitors. Martin's one year at GM is exposure, not expertise. I don't see the signal that distinguishes Korso from the dozens of similar pre-seed teams I'd meet in any given batch applying with "AI for [underdigitized industry]." Enterprise sales cycles will be brutal for a three-person team of recent graduates without industry relationships, and nothing in the dossier suggests they've figured out a distribution hack to get around that.

I'm not writing this company off — YC acceptance means Andrew Miklas saw something in the interview that isn't visible in the public record, and some of my best investments came from founders who didn't look impressive on paper. But I'm investing my own money, and what I need is a signal of extraordinary founder quality to compensate for a consensus idea in a crowded field. I don't have that signal. This is a pass, but one I'd revisit the moment I saw evidence of early customer love or a scrappy traction story that changed my read on the founders.
