# One Robot -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that jumps out to me here isn't the founders or the technology — it's that every sophisticated robotics investor in the Valley would immediately understand why this company should exist. That's actually a problem for me. When NVIDIA has already validated the "world foundation model" category with Cosmos, when every VLA team knows evaluation is slower than training, and when a YC LinkedIn post gets 674 likes, we're not in the territory of unpopular ideas that are difficult to assess. We're in consensus. The most promising companies I've backed — SpaceX when space was a punchline, Crusoe when gas-flare computing sounded deranged — were the ones that made smart people uncomfortable. One Robot makes smart people nod. That's a very different signal.

Now, let me be honest about what I can't see here, which is the founders' personal stories. Hemanth's credentials are extraordinary on paper — a NASA Early Career Achievement Medal, McLaren, JPL, Google, Georgia Tech with perfect grades, and he bootstrapped Crescer to profitability, which shows he can build a real business and not just publish. Elton spent five formative years at Tesla during the Model 3 ramp and on the Dojo team, which is as close to the fire as you get in production robotics. But what I'm missing is the biographical thread — the story underneath the resume. When I back someone, I want to hear how they arrived at this specific problem through some combination of obsession and personal necessity that can't be replicated by the next credentialed team. What I see instead is two highly capable engineers who identified a bottleneck at their previous company and built the logical solution. That's competence. That's good pattern matching. But I can't find the survival story, the hardship that forged an unbreakable bond to this specific mission. Maybe it's there and the dossier doesn't capture it — I'd want to hear it from them directly before making any final judgment.

The strongest bull case here, and I want to genuinely engage with it, is that this could be an infrastructure play at a major inflection point — and I've made those bets before. Crusoe was infrastructure. SpaceX was infrastructure. When the paradigm shifts from hand-crafted physics engines to learned world models, whoever builds the task-specific layer for contact-rich manipulation could own a critical piece of the robotics stack. The founders' combined experience — Industrial Next gave them the exact domain knowledge, Tesla gave Elton production-scale systems thinking, JPL and Google gave Hemanth research depth — might make them the single best-qualified two-person team in the world for this specific niche. And the data flywheel argument is real: each customer's robot data improves the world models, which improves accuracy, which attracts more customers. If VLA-based manipulation scales the way it could, the team that owns the simulation layer for textiles and deformables and sub-millimeter assembly will be very valuable.

But several things hold me back. First, this is a tooling play for other robotics companies — it doesn't create economic access or make invisible systems transparent for underserved populations. It makes well-funded robotics teams more efficient. I've always been drawn to companies where the positive-sum economics are obvious: Uber created income for drivers, Affirm opened credit access, Thumbtack connected tradespeople with customers. One Robot optimizes an engineering workflow. That's important, but it doesn't stir the same conviction in me. Second, the competitive dynamics concern me deeply. NVIDIA's Cosmos already has three million downloads and is open-source. World Labs just raised $1.23 billion. A two-person pre-seed team racing against that kind of capital concentration needs some structural advantage beyond domain expertise — regulatory moats, network effects, data lock-in — and I don't see those yet. Third, the initial market is genuinely small: the number of teams actively training VLAs for industrial manipulation today might be measured in dozens, and growth depends on the broader VLA ecosystem maturing on someone else's timeline.

What I do respect is the co-founder relationship. Six years of working together at Industrial Next, complementary skill sets — ML research depth paired with production systems engineering — and they chose each other for this venture after extended collaboration. That's the kind of stress-tested partnership I look for after the HQ Trivia lesson. This is one of the stronger co-founder signals I've seen at pre-seed. It doesn't override the other concerns, but it means this team won't implode when the first crisis hits.

Ultimately, One Robot feels like it was built for a different investor's portfolio — someone who weights technical credentialing and market timing more heavily than biographical grit, weirdness, and economic access. The founders are impressive. The timing might be right. But it's not my kind of bet. When I write a check, I need to feel that jolt — the sense that I'm seeing something the world hasn't priced in yet. Here, the world has already priced it in. NVIDIA validated the category. Every robotics VC can see the opportunity. That's exactly the moment I tend to step back.
