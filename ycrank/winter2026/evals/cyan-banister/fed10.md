# Fed10 -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what jumps out at me first: three college-age founders who were passing real legislation before they could legally vote. That's not something you hear every day. Armand Iorgulescu was apparently the youngest gubernatorial appointee in his state, drafting recommendations that became law while still a teenager. Winston Wei was doing the same in government affairs. And they did it together -- they were lobbying as a team before Fed10 existed as a concept. There's something genuinely precocious about that, and the founder-market fit on the skills axis is undeniable. But when I sit with it, I keep coming back to my central question: tell me the story. Not the resume -- the story. Why does this problem burn for you? Being a talented young lobbyist who noticed the consulting industry was inefficient is a professional observation, not a biographical imperative. I backed SpaceX because Elon was trying to make humanity multiplanetary with a conviction that bordered on delusional. I backed Uber because I'd spent years in my own life raging about the taxi medallion racket. What I'm looking for is the moment where the founder's life and the company's mission become inseparable, and I can't find that thread here. These are impressive young achievers who came through elite institutions -- Harvard, Berkeley, Williams -- and spotted an optimization opportunity in a domain they knew. That's smart. But it's not the kind of origin story that tells me they'll endure the long journey when things get brutal.

The positioning itself concerns me. "AI agents that replace your policy consultants" is not a weird idea in 2026 -- it's possibly the single most consensus pitch in Silicon Valley right now. Every B2B startup is pitching "AI agents that replace your [expensive professional]." Lawyers, accountants, recruiters, analysts -- the template is identical. The GovTech vertical adds a slight wrinkle, because government affairs isn't glamorous and most founders avoid it, so there's a thin contrarian edge in the category choice. But the core mechanic -- LLMs reading documents and generating analysis that humans used to do -- is as mainstream as it gets. When I think about the investments that have defined my career, they all shared that quality of being unpopular and difficult to assess. Mining Bitcoin on gas flares in North Dakota? People literally laughed. A male contraceptive gel? VCs wouldn't touch it. An AR game that makes you walk around outside staring at your phone? Fed10 doesn't have that quality. Any competent team at a top accelerator could articulate this exact pitch, and several already are -- USLege, Plural Policy, and the incumbents themselves are all racing to add AI analysis layers on top of the same public legislative data.

Now, the bull case deserves honest engagement because there IS a version of this that excites me. If Fed10 prices this at a level where a small nonprofit advocacy group in rural Nevada -- the kind of organization that could never afford a $500/hour lobbyist -- can suddenly track every bill that threatens their community, that's the Affirm pattern. Affirm made credit transparent for people who were being gouged by hidden fees. Carta made equity visible when cap tables were opaque spreadsheets controlled by lawyers. There's a world where Fed10 does the same thing for legislative intelligence -- takes something that was gatekept by expensive Beltway consultants and puts it in the hands of any organization that cares about policy. The "making invisible systems visible" pattern is genuinely present here. But the current positioning is B2B enterprise -- trade associations, corporate government affairs teams, law firms. Those buyers already have budgets for FiscalNote and Quorum. If the founders told me they were building this because they watched communities in Las Vegas get blindsided by legislation nobody was tracking for them, and they wanted to make sure no small organization ever got caught off guard again, I'd feel that jolt. Instead, this reads as a cost-reduction play for existing budget holders.

The co-founder dynamic is actually the brightest signal for me. After HQ Trivia taught me that co-founder conflict can destroy even a perfectly timed product, I probe team relationships hard. These three have a pre-existing working relationship forged in the very domain they're building for. They lobbied together, passed legislation together, and then decided to start a company together. That's meaningfully different from co-founders who met at a hackathon or were paired by a matching service. Two of them share Las Vegas roots. The relationship predates the business, which is exactly what I look for -- it suggests they've already navigated disagreements in high-stakes environments. I don't have evidence of how they handle real conflict, but the foundation is solid.

The competitive landscape gives me pause. FiscalNote is stumbling -- stock down 97%, CEO transition -- but they launched PolicyNote as their AI response in early 2025. Quorum has $61 million in revenue and 50% of the Fortune 100 as customers. These incumbents have distribution Fed10 will spend years building. The underlying legislative data is entirely public, so the moat has to come from analysis quality and domain-specific accuracy. The founders' lobbying experience gives them an edge in knowing what good policy analysis looks like, but that advantage compresses as LLMs improve and incumbents fine-tune their own models. I don't see the kind of structural defensibility -- network effects, regulatory barriers, proprietary data -- that would let me sleep well on this bet. The founders are capable, the market is real, and the timing with incumbent distress is reasonable. But this doesn't activate the part of my brain that lights up when I encounter something genuinely new. It's a well-executed application of current technology to an underserved vertical, and that's a fine business. It's just not the kind of bet where I feel compelled to write my own check.
