# Alchemy -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the career path. Baek goes biomedical engineering to psychological counseling to marketing analytics at Tropicana to EP mapping at Boston Scientific to... AI microscopy analysis. That's not a path — that's a random walk. When I invested in Perplexity, Aravind had spent years at Google Brain and OpenAI doing nothing but the thing the company does. When I backed Stack Overflow, Spolsky and Atwood had been living inside developer knowledge problems for a decade. Specific knowledge is the thing you'd be doing even if no one paid you. A founder whose resume reads like they're sampling career buffet items doesn't signal someone who was pulled irresistibly toward bioimage analysis and couldn't stop building it. McBride has strong technical credentials — patent at 19, UIUC CS — but nothing tying him specifically to microscopy or computational biology. If both founders disappeared tomorrow, a competent ML team with a biology advisor rebuilds this in six months. That's the test, and this company fails it.

The leverage architecture is where the story gets more interesting, and it's the reason I don't dismiss this outright. An AI agent that assembles analysis pipelines from natural language input is pure code-plus-AI leverage — zero marginal cost per analysis, scales to thousands of labs without adding headcount. Two people building this is the right structure. And there's a genuine principal-agent problem in the market that favors a startup: Leica, Zeiss, and Nikon are agents of their hardware revenue. They bundle analysis software with microscope sales because that's how they justify margins. They structurally cannot unbundle and offer a $50/month SaaS tool that competes with their own $200K instrument bundles. CellProfiler and QuPath, meanwhile, are grant-funded projects with no commercial entity incentivized to invest in agentic UX. The structural gap is real. But a structural gap is not a moat — it's an open door that anyone with legs can walk through.

That's the core problem: this is perilously close to the "AI wrapper" pattern I consistently pass on. The underlying models — Cellpose, StarDist, foundation vision-language models — are open-source or API-accessible. The value Alchemy adds is pipeline assembly and researcher UX. That's real value, but it's a UX layer, not a knowledge layer. When OpenAI or Google ships better vision models next quarter, Alchemy benefits — but so does every competitor. And the moment a well-funded biotech tools company (or an ambitious PhD student with Claude) decides to build the same natural-language-to-pipeline interface, Alchemy's advantage compresses to execution speed. Innovation without a durable moat routes all the surplus to the customer.

The strongest bull case I can construct: CellProfiler has been cited in over 30,000 papers despite requiring real programming expertise. That's extraordinary latent demand. "CellProfiler but you speak English" is genuinely democratizing — it gives hundreds of thousands of wet-lab researchers access to quantitative image analysis they currently can't perform. If Alchemy builds a library of validated, domain-specific processing nodes and those nodes get embedded in published methods sections and lab SOPs, switching costs compound. Every pipeline that appears in a paper becomes a citation-backed lock-in mechanism. The data flywheel — labeled image-model pairings from thousands of diverse labs — could eventually create model selection accuracy that no new entrant can match. And with two technical founders using AI-first development, they could reach meaningful scale before anyone reacts. If the specific knowledge I'm not seeing in the resume actually exists in conversation — if Baek's time in research labs gave him insight into microscopy workflows that would take a competitor years to internalize — then this is a genuine unlock. But that's an "if" I can't verify from what's in front of me.

The access democratization angle is the strongest thesis-fit signal here. Removing the programming gatekeeper between researchers and their own microscopy data is structurally similar to what AngelList did for startup investing or what Udemy did for education — taking a capability restricted to a technical elite and making it universally accessible. I weight this. But democratization alone, without the founder's specific knowledge acting as a compounding engine, produces a company that creates value without capturing it. The value flows to the researchers, then eventually to whichever foundation model provider ships the feature natively. Giving society what it wants is necessary — but you need specific knowledge to be the one who captures the returns.

Two-person team, AI-native product, genuine market gap, real democratization pattern — but no evidence the founders carry the kind of domain-specific obsession that makes a company irreplaceable rather than merely useful. I pass, but I'd take the meeting. If Baek teaches me something about research microscopy workflows that I didn't know — something that reveals years of accumulated intuition rather than market research — I'd reconsider.
