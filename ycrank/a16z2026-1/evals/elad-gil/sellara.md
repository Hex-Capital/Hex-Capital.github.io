# Sellara -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The Harvey comparison is the first place my mind goes with Sellara, and it's precisely the comparison that reveals the problem. When I backed Harvey in early 2022, legal AI had been a graveyard for a decade -- every prior attempt had failed, consensus was that language models couldn't handle the precision requirements of legal work, and almost no one else was writing checks in the category. The structural shift was real but invisible to most investors. Sellara targets AI automation for institutional trading operations -- booking, allocations, reconciliations, compliance -- and the "why now" is that LLM capability thresholds crossed a line making semi-structured financial document processing viable. That shift is real. But it's also one of the most consensus investment categories in 2026. The dossier itself cites a 600%+ projected increase in finance teams using agentic AI. Beam AI already has ~$4.5M in revenue targeting adjacent BFSI workflows. Blueflame raised a $5M Series A for investment-focused AI. Accenture is running a FinTech Innovation Lab specifically for this space. When I look at Sellara's market timing, I don't see a window that most investors have missed -- I see a window that everyone is simultaneously trying to climb through.

Sellara's strongest signal is the founder-market fit on the distribution side, and I want to be precise about why it matters and where it falls short. Charles-André Jolly covered 180 institutional clients at Citi across Macro Credit Derivatives, IG Credit, and Short Term Credit Sales. Spencer Secord spent six years on Cross Asset, Fixed Income, and Convertible Sales desks and specifically built workflow automations integrated into core risk systems. These aren't founders who read a McKinsey report about financial automation -- they sat on the desks where the pain lives, and they have the Rolodex to get into the first ten accounts. That's a meaningful distribution edge in a market where vendor onboarding cycles commonly exceed six to twelve months. But distribution advantage and scaling ability are different things. Neither Jolly nor Secord has operated inside a company during its hypergrowth phase -- they operated inside a 200,000-person bank, which teaches you very different lessons about organizational design. Ahmad Roumie, the CTO, brings strong ML credentials (ETH Zurich MSc in Robotics, YC W23 CTO at BabylonAI), but his domain pivot from nutrition tracking and robotics to institutional trading operations is significant. The 60%+ outperformance claim over leading LLMs needs to reflect genuine financial domain specialization in the model architecture -- and the person responsible for that architecture hasn't worked in financial services before.

The infrastructure-layer question is where Sellara loses me relative to my framework. The product description -- "custom AI agents deployed within financial institutions," "copy-and-paste, drag-and-drop execution with file inputs," deployment in a three-step configure-validate-deploy cycle -- reads as an application-layer workflow tool, not infrastructure. Compare this to Stripe, which didn't build a payments application but became the payments API that thousands of companies integrated into their core architecture. Or compare to Harvey, which is building a platform that embeds in law firm workflows to the point where removing it would require restructuring how associates do research. Sellara integrates with Outlook and Bloomberg -- it sits on top of existing infrastructure rather than becoming infrastructure itself. "Custom AI agents" deployed per institution is, structurally, closer to a consulting engagement than a platform play. Each deployment requires configuration specific to that institution's systems, which means revenue scales linearly with implementation effort rather than compounding through network effects or API adoption.

The single-miracle test produces a mixed result. The technology miracle -- LLMs becoming reliable enough for semi-structured financial workflows -- is happening exogenously, which is favorable. Sellara doesn't need to invent the underlying AI capability; they need to apply it with sufficient financial domain specificity. But selling AI agents into regulated financial institutions is an independent challenge: SR 11-7 model risk management frameworks, information security reviews, and vendor onboarding processes create sales cycles that can starve a capital-constrained startup. Jolly's 180 institutional relationships partially de-risk the distribution miracle, but partially isn't fully. And then there's Beam AI -- already at $4.5M revenue with 57 employees, backed by Accenture's FinTech Innovation Lab -- occupying adjacent territory. Beating a more-resourced competitor in the same geography targeting overlapping buyers is a third independent challenge. I count two miracles minimum: technology validation in financial domain AND enterprise distribution at scale in regulated markets. That's manageable but not the clean single-miracle profile I prefer.

The bull case I'd construct for Sellara goes like this: the specific sub-niche of institutional trading desk operations -- the actual booking, reconciliation, and compliance workflows on a derivatives desk -- is meaningfully different from horizontal BFSI automation. Beam AI does data entry and customer support across financial services; Sellara automates the specific workflows that Jolly and Secord touched daily for years. That domain specificity, combined with SOC II and ISO 27001 certifications at pre-seed (unusual and signal-rich for enterprise readiness), creates a wedge that horizontal competitors can't easily replicate. If Sellara's claimed 60%+ performance advantage reflects genuine fine-tuning on proprietary financial workflow data rather than prompt engineering on commodity models, the data moat compounds with each institutional deployment. And regulated financial services create switching costs -- once an AI agent is configured into a bank's risk systems, the compliance re-validation cost of switching is substantial. For this bull case to hold, Sellara needs to prove the performance claims are real and close enough institutional deployments to build a proprietary training data flywheel before Beam AI or an incumbent moves down-market into their niche.

Sellara lands in neutral territory for me because of a fundamental mismatch on my highest-weighted criterion. The market structure timing is real but consensus -- this is the opposite of the nonobvious window I look for. The founder-market fit on distribution (Jolly's 180 institutional clients, Secord's desk-level workflow knowledge) is the strongest signal in the dossier, but it doesn't compensate for obvious market timing and application-layer positioning. If I saw evidence of a genuine infrastructure play -- an API layer that other fintech companies build on, a platform architecture with network effects -- I'd lean differently. As it stands, Sellara looks like a competent team applying AI to a real pain point in an increasingly crowded category, with the distribution relationships to get early traction but without the structural positioning to build a compounding moat.
