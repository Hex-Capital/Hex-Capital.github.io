# Ressl AI -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Ressl AI is that four well-funded competitors are building essentially the same thing. Netic has $43M and a $450M valuation. Avoca has $48M. WorkHero has $5M. ServiceTitan has $772M in revenue and its own AI division. When every venture-backed startup and the public incumbent are converging on the same opportunity at the same time, nobody is skating to where the puck will be -- everyone is skating to where the puck already is. That is a consensus bet masquerading as innovation. I have never made my best returns in spaces where the competitive landscape looks like this at the pre-seed stage.

The consequence of success here does not justify the effort for my portfolio. If Ressl AI wins completely -- every collision shop, every HVAC company, every plumber uses their AI agents for back-office work -- what changes? Admin headcount drops. Trades businesses save money on "glue employees." That is a real efficiency gain for those operators, and I do not dismiss its commercial value. But the world operates the same way afterward. Cars still get repaired. Pipes still get fixed. The FSM market is $5.5 billion heading to $9 billion -- a meaningful market, not a transformational one. Compare this to what I look for: Impossible Foods aimed to make the $1.7 trillion meat industry structurally obsolete. Commonwealth Fusion targets the entire fossil fuel system. Square displaced the foundational infrastructure of payments. Ressl is making existing businesses run with two fewer admin people. They have reduced their risk to the point where success, by my framework, becomes inconsequential.

The strongest bull case requires you to believe three things simultaneously: (1) the PE roll-up distribution channel gives Ressl a unique wedge that scales faster than competitors' direct sales, allowing one relationship to deploy across dozens of portfolio businesses; (2) the $440K revenue with four people demonstrates unusual capital efficiency and founder hustle; and (3) trades represent a beachhead for a broader AI back-office platform that eventually expands to every underdigitized SMB vertical. If all three are true, you could see a path to a meaningful company. The PE channel is genuinely clever -- Arushi's prior experience building CRMs for VC/PE firms at Fundinc gives her credibility with that buyer persona. But that distribution advantage is tactical, not structural. It does not change the underlying problem: the technology layer is commoditized, and anyone with an LLM and API integrations can build agents that read from CCCOne and write to QuickBooks.

The pivot history raises questions I would need answered before going further. Ressl started as "Devin for Salesforce" -- AI for Salesforce administration. Salesforce-related content appears as recently as mid-2025. The $440K revenue figure may include Salesforce product revenue, which means the trades-specific traction could be significantly lower than it appears. Now, I have backed founders who pivoted -- pivots can demonstrate learning rate, which I value enormously. The question is whether this pivot reveals founders who spotted a pattern and moved decisively toward it, or founders who are searching for product-market fit in whatever vertical seems receptive. The dossier does not give me enough to distinguish these two stories. What I can observe is that neither founder has worked in trades or home services, which is fine -- I prefer outsiders -- but outsiders pursuing an incremental efficiency play is not the same as outsiders pursuing something the industry considers impossible. Nobody in the trades industry thinks AI back-office automation is impossible. They just have not gotten around to buying it yet. That is a sales problem, not an invention problem.

The technology here does not displace incumbents -- it layers on top of them. That is a fundamentally different value proposition from what I typically fund. When I backed Juniper, the thesis was that TCP/IP routers would make Cisco's legacy architecture structurally obsolete. When I backed Square, the thesis was that mobile payments would make traditional POS infrastructure uneconomic. Ressl's thesis is that AI agents will make it easier to use the software you already own. That is a feature, not a disruption. And features get absorbed by platforms. ServiceTitan's Titan Intelligence division will build agent capabilities within its own ecosystem. The moment the incumbent decides to add your feature, your differentiation evaporates -- unless you have proprietary technology or data that creates a structural moat. At this stage, I see no evidence of either.

I pass. The founders are hustling -- $440K revenue with four people, even partially attributed to a prior product, shows energy. But the problem is too small for my framework, the technology is reproducible, and the competitive landscape is already crowded with better-capitalized players. These are smart founders who should be working on something where the consequence of success is worth the years of their lives it will consume.
