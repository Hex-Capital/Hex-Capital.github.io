# sitefire -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that jumped out to me about sitefire isn't the founders or the product — it's the competitive landscape. Profound just closed a $96 million Series C at unicorn valuation with 700+ enterprise customers. Peec AI has $29 million and is adding 300 customers a month. Relixir, another YC company, is building essentially the same thing with forward-deployed engineers. Semrush and Ahrefs are bolting on GEO features. This is a consensus market by every definition I know. The most promising companies are not popular — they are difficult to assess. GEO optimization is neither of those things right now. Every VC in the ecosystem understands the thesis that search is migrating to AI answer engines, the TAM projections are clean, and there's a well-funded category leader already proving demand. When I see that kind of validation, I know the price is wrong and the weirdness has been extracted from the opportunity. This is the opposite of where I find my best returns.

When I try to hear the founders' stories — and that's always where I start — I find impressive credentials but not the biographical thread I need. Jochen has a PhD from TUM, studied at Stanford, was valedictorian of Germany's top finance program. Vincent started a company at 18 and taught himself to code at 15 during a year abroad in Vancouver. These are capable, accomplished people. But their prior company together was pulse Energy, an energy-tech venture. The pivot to AI marketing tooling is a domain shift without a personal anchor. I'm always listening for the specific hardship or lived experience that makes a founder the only person who could solve this particular problem. I don't hear that here. I hear two smart technologists who identified a growing market and built a product for it. That's respectable, but it's not the signal that makes me write a check from my own money. When I backed the Contraline founder, his years of dedicated research in male contraception told me something about his commitment that no pitch deck could. When I invested in Uber, I'd spent years discussing the medallion system with my husband — the problem was personal before the investment was financial. I don't feel that biographical inevitability with sitefire.

The strongest bull case I can construct: GEO is a genuine paradigm shift, the founders' optimization and machine learning backgrounds are unusually well-suited to cracking how AI citation works, they've already landed BMW and DWS as clients, and the analytics-plus-content-generation combination creates a tighter loop than pure monitoring tools offer. The incumbents — Semrush, Ahrefs — face a real innovator's dilemma because GEO threatens their core search-centric business. If sitefire can build a proprietary dataset across 180+ markets that compounds with every customer, there's a defensibility story worth paying attention to. And the co-founders have worked together before at pulse Energy, which means they've already stress-tested their relationship through at least one venture. That matters to me after what I learned from HQ Trivia. But even granting all of this, I keep coming back to the fundamental problem: sitefire is entering a market where Profound already has a $155 million head start and hundreds of enterprise customers. The content generation layer relies on generally available LLM APIs. There's no technology risk here — and counterintuitively, that's a negative in my framework, because technology risk is what creates the moat that keeps competitors away.

I also notice what this company doesn't do for the world. My best investments — Uber, Postmates, Affirm, Thumbtack — created economic access for people who didn't have it before. They made invisible systems visible and turned opaque power structures transparent. Sitefire helps Series A+ marketing teams optimize their brand visibility in AI search results. It's a useful product for well-resourced companies, but it doesn't expand the pie for anyone underserved. It doesn't make something previously invisible suddenly transparent in a way that changes lives. That dimension matters to me more than most investors realize — it's not a nice-to-have in my framework, it's how I filter for companies that build durable, positive-sum value rather than zero-sum competitive advantage.

This is a pass. The founders are technically strong and have a prior working relationship, which I respect. But a consensus market, no biographical inevitability connecting them to this problem, no economic access story, and well-capitalized competitors who are already at scale — none of my conviction signals are firing. I don't feel the jolt. The market has already told us this category works, which means the opportunity for an outsized first-check return has already been competed away.
