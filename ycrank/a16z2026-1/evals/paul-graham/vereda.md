# Vereda -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Vereda is that neither João nor Pedro has a public GitHub repository. For a company whose entire pitch is "AI procurement agent," this is the kind of detail that stops me cold. Pedro Galindo is listed as CPTO and taught a Generative AI class at USC — but teaching undergraduates to build chatbots and shipping a production system that handles multi-vendor agricultural procurement in Portuguese are separated by a chasm that most people underestimate. When the Collison brothers showed up with Stripe, they had been writing code since childhood. Patrick had shipped Auctomatic as a teenager. I knew they could build. With Vereda, I see two USC-educated finance professionals — one from BTG Pactual, one from Patria Investments — and a team of six to eight. Someone is building this product, but the founders' own hands aren't visibly on the keyboard, and that's a signal I can't ignore.

João's farming family background, though, is the part of this story that holds my attention. He grew up in the problem. He watched his family navigate the intermediary-heavy distribution layer that takes roughly 50% of the channel in Brazilian agricultural inputs — R$167 billion in distribution revenue in 2024. That lived experience, combined with his years structuring over $1 billion in agribusiness transactions at BTG Pactual, gives him a view that pure outsiders don't have. He's seen both sides: the farming family that overpays, and the financial infrastructure that enables the overpayment. This isn't quite "live in the future, then build what's missing" — it's more like "live in the present inefficiency, then apply finance to fix it." The organic discovery is real but it filtered through a banker's lens, not a builder's lens. Pedro's path through Patria Investments, the most active PE investor in LatAm agriculture, reinforces this: the Vereda founding story reads like two sharp finance minds who identified a structural market inefficiency, not two frustrated farmers who couldn't stand the status quo anymore.

The WhatsApp-native approach is where Vereda shows genuine product thinking. In rural Brazil, 99% of smartphones have WhatsApp. Building a separate app would be the classic mistake — forcing users to adopt your workflow instead of meeting them in theirs. Vereda's "Vera" agent captures purchasing intent through the channel farmers already use to talk to everyone else in their lives. This eliminates download friction entirely and creates a distribution advantage that web-based competitors like Agrofy, which raised $66 million, cannot replicate in the countryside. The structural insight here reminds me of what made Stripe's approach work: the Collisons embedded payments into the developer workflow at the point where developers already were (their code editor), just as Vereda embeds procurement into the communication channel where farmers already are. The schlep of dealing with CPF tax verification, CAR and CCIR document processing, and multi-vendor order consolidation through a chat interface is genuinely unglamorous work. Most founders' unconscious wouldn't even let them see this idea.

Vereda's claimed traction — $155 million GMV and $8 million in "potential revenue" across 800,000+ acres in 45 days — would be extraordinary if verified, but the word "potential" does significant work in that sentence. Potential revenue is not revenue. It could mean pipeline, projected commissions on quotes that haven't closed, or the theoretical value of demand that's been captured but not transacted. The 300,000+ hectares on the website versus 800,000+ acres (approximately 324,000 hectares) in the company data are roughly consistent, which is reassuring, but the gap between potential and actual is exactly where marketplace startups hide their weakness. The seasonality risk compounds this concern: Brazilian agriculture runs on crop cycles, and a 45-day sprint might perfectly coincide with a purchasing window that closes. Whether Vereda can sustain engagement through the off-season — when there's no purchasing intent to capture — is entirely unproven.

The strongest bull case for Vereda is the channel conflict that traps incumbents. Orbia is owned by Bayer (68.8%), Bravium (17.2%), and Yara (14%) — all manufacturers or distributors. They structurally cannot disintermediate their own distribution networks without cannibalizing existing revenue. This is the kind of strategic paralysis that creates space for startups, and it's durable. If Vereda can get even a handful of manufacturers to sell directly through its aggregated demand channel, the data flywheel — proprietary farmer demand signals, credit behavior, purchasing patterns — becomes a moat that no incumbent can replicate without dismantling their own business model. The embedded fintech optionality, building credit products on top of transaction data, is where the superlinear returns could emerge. For this to work, though, Vereda needs signed manufacturer agreements, and the dossier provides no evidence that any exist. The entire model depends on suppliers agreeing to bypass their distributors, and that's a sales challenge that requires exactly the kind of relentless resourcefulness I want to see more evidence of.

What holds me back from investing is the gap between the sophistication of the market analysis and the absence of builder evidence. João and Pedro clearly understand agricultural finance at an institutional level — BTG Pactual and Patria Investments are serious shops. But understanding a market and building a product that conquers it are different skills. The founding team profile pattern — business degrees from the same university, careers in finance, no visible code — is closer to the consultants-who-identified-an-opportunity archetype than the hackers-who-couldn't-stand-the-problem archetype. João's farming roots and the WhatsApp-native insight prevent this from being a clean pass. There's real domain knowledge and a genuine schlep here. But I need to see evidence that these founders can build, not just analyze — and that the $155 million GMV figure reflects transactions that actually closed, not a pipeline projection dressed up for fundraising.
