# Aemon -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Aemon is that they've positioned themselves as the commercial instantiation of something Google DeepMind already built and validated internally. AlphaEvolve demonstrated in May 2025 that LLM-powered evolutionary coding agents can make genuine discoveries on hard optimization problems. Aemon's founders -- twin brothers, both research prodigies with ICLR and EMNLP publications before age 20 -- saw this and said: "We can do this better and sell it to everyone." They claim to have beaten AlphaEvolve on circle packing with under ten dollars of compute. That's a real technical achievement. But this is the Xerox PARC problem inverted: instead of a research lab inventing something and failing to commercialize it, a startup is commercializing something a research lab invented and explicitly chose to keep internal. The question is whether that's Juniper building TCP/IP routers when Cisco refused to -- or whether it's a thin productization layer over a capability that will commoditize within eighteen months. When I look at the competitive dynamics -- OpenEvolve already open-sourced on GitHub, Sakana AI's ShinkaEvolve accepted at ICLR 2026, Lila Sciences and Periodic Labs with $550M and $300M respectively -- I see a rapidly closing window, not an opening one.

My primary concern with Aemon is that the consequence of success is calibrated too small. If Aemon fully succeeds -- becomes the dominant AI R&D optimization tool -- what happens to the world? Engineering teams solve combinatorial optimization problems faster. R&D cycles compress. That's genuinely useful. But it doesn't make any incumbent system structurally obsolete. It doesn't replace the way an industry operates. It makes an existing process -- research engineering -- more efficient by some meaningful but bounded percentage. When I backed Pat Brown at Impossible Foods, the consequence of success was eliminating animal agriculture's claim on half the planet's arable land. When I backed OpenAI, the consequence was artificial general intelligence. Aemon's consequence of success is a good B2B software business that helps engineering teams iterate faster. The $3.1 trillion R&D market they cite is real but misleading -- they're not replacing R&D, they're accelerating one narrow slice of it. That's exactly the kind of outcome where I'd tell the founders: you're too talented for the ambition you've set.

The founders themselves are interesting. Publishing at top-tier AI venues before turning 20 is genuinely rare. International medals in math and robotics, dropping out of UIUC and Waterloo to build this -- these are people with real intellectual horsepower and real conviction. But I'm looking for contrarian courage, not competitive execution. Beating DeepMind on a benchmark is impressive engineering, but it's not contrarian thinking. They're running faster in a direction DeepMind already validated. The most innovative companies I've backed were doing things the consensus actively mocked -- Impossible Foods was "plant-based hamburgers" when the entire food industry thought it was absurd, Rocket Lab was a New Zealander building rockets when aerospace veterans said it couldn't be done from that geography. Aemon is doing something that DeepMind proved works and the AI research community considers valuable. Where's the contrarian bet? Where's the thing that makes experts say "that's crazy"?

The strongest bull case: what if evolutionary AI code generation is the primitive that unlocks a fundamentally new paradigm for all engineering R&D? Not just optimization problems with clean metrics, but the general ability of AI systems to autonomously explore solution spaces, recombine approaches, and discover solutions that human researchers wouldn't conceive. If that's what Aemon becomes -- the platform through which every chip designer, materials scientist, and drug researcher runs their hardest problems -- then the consequence magnitude is enormous and I'm wrong about the ambition calibration. The founders' youth and research depth would make them exactly the right people to ride this curve. And the structural gap created by DeepMind's unwillingness to commercialize is real -- there's a genuine market opening. The legal retrieval case study (Recall@10 jumping from 0.540 to 0.912) hints at generalizability beyond pure math benchmarks. If they can demonstrate this kind of improvement across five or six wildly different domains in the next year, the story changes from "optimization tool" to "autonomous research infrastructure." But that's a bet on what they might become, not what they've shown.

The team construction concerns me for the enterprise play they're running. Both founders are young AI researchers -- same age, same publication venues, twin brothers. For a company whose website says "Book a Discovery Call" and whose distribution requires selling into R&D organizations at companies that move slowly, there is no visible go-to-market expertise on the team. Gene pool engineering means identifying your five biggest risks and recruiting against them. One of their top risks is clearly enterprise sales execution, and I see no evidence they've addressed it. The homogeneity of the founding team -- both researchers, both brothers, both dropouts from CS programs -- means they likely share the same blind spots.

Aemon has real technical talent building a real product in a category with genuine tailwinds. The LLM capability curve is exponential and favors everything they're doing. But the ambition is calibrated to build a good software company, not to transform a system. I'd want to see these founders aim at something where the consequence of success is so large that the difficulty of the attempt is justified -- not a better optimization tool, but the wholesale replacement of how R&D is conducted across entire industries, with a path to making human-only research engineering as obsolete as manual switchboard operation. Right now, they're building a faster horse.
