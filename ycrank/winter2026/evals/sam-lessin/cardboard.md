# Cardboard -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the thing about Cardboard that jumped out immediately: the company's own tags on YC are "Artificial Intelligence, SaaS, Consumer, Video, Media." That's not a description of a company -- it's a description of a category that every investor and their mother is chasing right now. When I run my first filter -- is this a sane person with an insane idea? -- Cardboard inverts the pairing. The founders are credible. Ishan spent five years building performance-critical browser applications at HackerRank, which maps directly to constructing a WebGPU/WebCodecs-based NLE. Saksham built AI agent systems at Iterate AI and creates content himself at 250k views a month, so he personally feels the pain. They've known each other fifteen years -- and I weight co-founder relationships heavily, because speed-dating partnerships crack under real adversity. The "sane" component checks out. But "agentic AI video editor" is not an insane idea. It's the most consensus idea in creative tooling right now. Descript raised $101M. OpusClip raised $50M from SoftBank. Kapwing is doing $10M in revenue. Adobe is bolting AI into Premiere. CapCut ships to a billion phones. Multiple HN commenters on Cardboard's own launch thread flagged "lots of players in the market doing this." When the idea is obvious, the returns are already arbitraged away. Sane person, sane idea -- the configuration that produces mediocre outcomes because you're buying execution risk in a commodity opportunity.

Now let me apply the kill test that dominates my analysis since 2024: does AI commoditize this business or strengthen it? The answer is unambiguous. Cardboard's core value proposition is that multimodal LLMs can reason over video footage and produce coherent edits. That capability is built on commercially available foundation models. Every improvement in those models helps Cardboard's competitors identically. When GPT-5 or Claude 5 or Gemini Ultra ships with better video understanding, Descript gets the same upgrade for the same API cost. The moat here is emphatically software -- a custom browser renderer and an agentic orchestration layer -- and I've been saying publicly for two years that software moats are dissolving. The founders acknowledge they're "building two hard things at once," which I respect as honest self-assessment, but engineering difficulty is a temporary barrier, not a structural moat. Adobe can throw hundreds of engineers at rebuilding their rendering stack for the browser if the market demands it. The "cherry on top" test asks: would this business be compelling if AI didn't exist? Without AI, Cardboard is a browser-based video editor competing with CapCut and Canva's video tools. The AI isn't the cherry on top -- it IS the sundae. That's the exact profile I avoid.

The strongest bull case would require me to believe that Cardboard is actually building a new primitive -- that their browser-native NLE becomes the infrastructure layer that other applications integrate with, creating an ecosystem and compounding network effects. If the custom WebCodecs/WebGL2 renderer became a standard that third-party plugins, effects marketplaces, and workflow automations built on top of, you'd have something closer to what Airtable achieved: a tool that looks like SaaS but functions as a protocol. The XML export to Premiere, Resolve, and Final Cut Pro hints at interoperability ambitions. And if user editing sessions generate proprietary training data that compounds into a meaningfully better AI agent -- a real data flywheel, not a theoretical one -- the dual-system complexity could become a genuine barrier. The 1,000+ early users and eight named customers including PostHog suggest real demand exists. If this team shipped thirteen releases since November 2025 with just two people, the velocity is impressive. But none of these primitive-layer dynamics are actually present today. Cardboard is an application on other people's infrastructure -- OpenAI's models, Google's WebGPU spec, browser vendors' WebCodecs implementations. Every layer beneath them is controlled by someone else.

The Birchbox lesson haunts me here. Narrative magnitude without structural moat produces the worst venture outcome -- a company successful enough to raise real money but too fragile to compound into a durable business. Cardboard's narrative can expand from "AI video editor" to "AI content production platform," but that narrative is shared by every competitor in the space. The infinity story test requires a narrative that ONLY this company can credibly tell, one that grows more believable with each milestone. "We edit videos faster with AI" is a narrative that gets less differentiated as AI improves, not more. The subscription pricing at $60 and $150 per month is the business model I've specifically flagged as structurally vulnerable -- monthly software access fees in a world where the underlying AI capabilities are deflating in cost every quarter. The pricing will face relentless downward pressure as competitors offer equivalent capabilities at lower price points.

I'll give the founders credit where it's due: the co-founder relationship is real, the technical skills map cleanly to the problem, and they're shipping at a pace that suggests operational intensity -- the "tizz" half of what I look for. Saksham creating content at scale means he's not theorizing about a problem he read about in a report. But I fund businesses that get stronger as software gets cheaper, and Cardboard gets weaker. I fund new primitives that create compounding ecosystems, and Cardboard is an application consuming other people's primitives. I fund ideas that make most investors uncomfortable, and "AI video editing" makes every investor salivate. This is a pass for me -- not because the founders aren't capable, but because the structural logic of the business sits on the wrong side of every filter I run.
