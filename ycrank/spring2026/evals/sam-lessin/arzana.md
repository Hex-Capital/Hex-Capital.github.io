# Arzana -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The competitive landscape here tells me everything I need to know before I even get to the founders. Conexiom has raised $170 million and claims 16 of the top 20 distributors. Paperless Parts has $51 million and deep quoting expertise. Rossum raised a $100 million Series A from General Catalyst. Endeavor AI just took $7 million from Craft Ventures targeting nearly the same market. And Arzana -- a four-person pre-seed team -- is trying to cover more surface area than any of them. The strategic logic says "we'll be the bundled solution, not the point solution," but the operational reality is that four people maintaining integrations across ten-plus ERPs while building AI agents across quoting, order entry, procurement, AP/AR, CRM, and customer service is not a plan. It's a wish.

My first question with any company is whether I'm looking at a sane person with an insane idea. Here I've got two Stanford kids from the Midwest -- Alexander is 21 from Iowa, Kools is 24 from Wisconsin, neither has a technical background, Kools literally took one coding class -- and they're building AI automation for manufacturing offices. The Midwestern roots are genuinely interesting. Growing up in Okoboji, Iowa and Neenah, Wisconsin gives you relationships and cultural fluency with manufacturers that no amount of cold outbound from San Francisco can replicate. But "sane person" in my framework means deep operator credibility -- Anatoly Yakovenko spent years at Qualcomm building consensus algorithms before he touched Solana. These founders are smart, clearly hustlers (Alexander ran a pizza business as a teenager, Kools was a D1 wrestler), but they haven't built anything at infrastructure scale, and the engineering execution rests entirely on one person (Joe Politi) whose background I can't even evaluate from public sources. That's a fragile foundation for a company promising to replace 25 out of 30 office workers.

The idea itself is the bigger problem for me. "AI automation for manufacturing" is not insane -- it's on every VC's hot list right now. Every pitch deck in 2026 includes a slide about how 98% of manufacturers are exploring AI. When I describe the best investments as "sane person, insane idea," I mean ideas that provoke genuine skepticism from most investors -- not ideas that every investor is already chasing. This is a consensus opportunity in a hot category with well-funded competition, which is the exact configuration where I expect returns to get arbitraged to zero. The "why now" section in any honest analysis of this company would say "because LLMs can now parse unstructured documents" -- but that same enabling technology is available to Conexiom, to SAP, to Oracle, and to every other competitor. The technology unlock doesn't create a moat; it creates a land rush.

Here's where my structural framework gets loudest: would this business be good if AI didn't exist? No. Without LLMs, there is no product. The entire value proposition is that AI agents can automate manufacturing front-office workflows. Strip out the AI layer and you have nothing -- no physical operations, no protocol, no installed base, no primitive. This is the opposite of what I look for. Craftwork is a painting company that uses software to dispatch crews and optimize scheduling -- take away the software and you still have a painting company. Arzana is software that happens to serve manufacturers. Take away the AI and you have an email inbox. The subscription pricing at $2,500-$7,000/month is literally the SaaS business model I've been publicly declaring obsolete. "Software is a business tool, not a business model." When I see an AI SaaS company selling subscriptions into a market where SAP, Oracle, and Epicor all have native platform incentives to build the same functionality, I see a feature that will eventually get absorbed.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. If those customer logos are real -- Schneider National, Zeiss, Koike -- that's remarkable pre-seed traction. A testimonial claiming quoting time dropped from five days to one is the kind of concrete value that manufacturers will pay for and tell their peers about. The Midwestern distribution angle is legitimately differentiated -- these founders can walk into a Wisconsin machine shop in a way that no San Francisco AI startup can. And the bundled approach might actually resonate with mid-market manufacturers who don't want to manage five point solutions. If Arzana can get deep enough into these accounts that switching costs become real, and if the accumulated data on pricing patterns and order structures creates genuine accuracy advantages, there's a path. But that path requires surviving a competitive war with companies that have 50x their resources, and doing it with a four-person team where the founders can't write code. The Birchbox lesson haunts me here: compelling early traction in a category where the moat turns out to be replicable is the most expensive kind of startup failure.

The company is building an application layer on top of other companies' infrastructure -- ERPs and CRMs that could absorb this functionality natively. There's no new primitive being created, no protocol that other businesses build on, no compounding network effect. The "Office Execution System" is a marketing term, not a new category of infrastructure. Every successful investment in my portfolio that generated outlier returns was building a new primitive -- Venmo for peer-to-peer payments, Solana for high-throughput blockchain consensus, Aleo for zero-knowledge proofs. Arzana is building features on top of SAP.
