# Fort -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Whoop bought Push Band in 2021 -- the closest product to what Fort is building -- and killed it. That acquisition is the most analytically revealing fact in this entire dossier, and it cuts both ways. The bear read: Whoop, with $454 million in capital and 260 million in revenue, decided that a dedicated strength-tracking wearable wasn't worth maintaining as a standalone product. They absorbed the IP, shipped a simplified muscular load estimate, and moved on. The bull read: Whoop's subscription model at $30/month is structurally misaligned with building a niche strength product -- they need broad-audience positioning, and going deep on the weight room would confuse their marketing. Both readings might be true simultaneously, but the implication for Fort is the same: if this market is real, Whoop already owns the IP and engineering talent to address it whenever they choose. Fort is building in the blast radius of an incumbent that literally holds the prior art.

The team passes the "sane" test with flying colors. Three Tesla engineers who shipped hardware at genuine scale -- Miranda on 4680 battery cells for Cybertruck, Paul on sensing systems and autonomy architecture for Semi and Robotaxi, Zac with a resume that reads like a tour of American hardware: SpaceX, Rivian, Astranis, Tesla. These are people who understand power-constrained miniaturized electronics, sensor fusion, and manufacturing complexity. If you told me this team was building the sensor layer for an autonomous vehicle startup or an industrial robotics platform, I'd be very interested. The "sane" credentials are impeccable for a hardware challenge. But the idea -- a better wearable for strength training -- isn't insane. It's the most obvious product gap in fitness tech. Everyone who lifts weights already complains that their Whoop or Apple Watch can't track reps properly. Strength training grew 122% year-over-year on Whoop's platform. Peter Attia has mainstreamed resistance exercise as a longevity intervention. This is consensus demand looking for a product. "Sane person, sane idea" is the worst category in my framework because it means the opportunity is already priced into the market and every well-resourced competitor sees the same gap.

My "cherry on top" test produces a clear negative signal here. Would Fort be a good business if AI didn't exist? Without the ML models doing automatic exercise detection, rep counting, and velocity estimation, Fort is just another accelerometer strapped to your wrist. The entire differentiation over Whoop, Garmin, and Apple Watch IS the AI-powered exercise classification. The dossier itself acknowledges this vulnerability: "The core technology (IMU-based exercise detection from wrist motion) is not patented in a way that prevents replication. Multiple academic papers and prior products have demonstrated velocity-based training measurement from wearable sensors." This is precisely the category I avoid -- where AI constitutes the value proposition rather than enhancing an already-defensible business. The hardware layer provides some friction, sure, but consumer electronics at a $289 price point isn't Metropolis installing checkout-free parking infrastructure across thousands of lots. Anyone with a Shenzhen manufacturing partner and a decent ML team can build a wrist-worn IMU device. The moat is thin.

The strongest bull case would require believing three things simultaneously: first, that incumbent incentive misalignment is permanent -- that Whoop, Garmin, and Apple will never properly address strength tracking because their business models reward broad-audience optimization. Second, that Fort's per-rep kinematic data creates a compounding dataset that becomes genuinely irreplaceable once users accumulate years of training history. Third, that the strength training market is large enough and passionate enough to support a standalone hardware company at venture scale. I can construct a scenario where all three hold -- the longevity movement creates a durable category of consumers who treat strength data the way runners treat Strava, Fort becomes the default for that tribe, and switching costs from accumulated progressive overload data lock them in. But this scenario describes a product company with a ceiling, not a platform with infinite optionality. "Best wearable for lifters" doesn't expand into a new primitive. It doesn't create an ecosystem that other businesses build on. It's not Venmo inventing a payment layer or Solana creating blockchain infrastructure -- it's a well-executed consumer electronics product serving a niche. At best, this is a hundred-million-dollar outcome, which is fine for the founders but doesn't produce venture returns at the valuations the market is currently pricing hardware-plus-subscription companies.

The Birchbox parallel haunts me here. Katia and Hayley had impeccable credentials and identified genuine consumer demand for curated product discovery. The problem was structural: subscription boxes were trivially replicable, and data on customer preferences never compounded into genuine lock-in. Fort's position looks similar. The team is clearly capable. The demand is real. But the defensibility depends on ML models trained on movement data -- and Whoop literally owns the prior art from the Push acquisition, Garmin has $2.36 billion in fitness revenue to fund R&D, and Apple has billions of Apple Watch users generating motion data. The barriers the dossier identifies -- "the time and data needed to train models on 50+ exercises" and "the effort to design a multi-placement form factor" -- are speed bumps, not moats. I'm passing because the structural logic doesn't survive my framework: consensus opportunity, AI-dependent differentiation, application-layer positioning, and incumbent competitors holding the relevant IP. These are talented builders who deserve a harder problem.
