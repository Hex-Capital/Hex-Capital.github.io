# The Token Company -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is that this company is building a cost-optimization layer on a commodity whose price is deflating at roughly 10x per year. That's not a business -- that's a race against thermodynamics. When inference costs are halving every few months, a middleware that saves you tokens is selling umbrellas in a drought that's ending. The only way this works is if the claim that compression *improves* output quality holds up across diverse workloads, not just one gaming customer's blind arena. That would make this a signal extraction layer rather than a cost play. But that claim requires extraordinary evidence, and a 268K-vote study on a single customer processing strategy game prompts doesn't constitute it.

The specific knowledge question is where this falls apart for me. Otso Veisterä is clearly a talented young engineer -- programming since age 7, national physics champion, comfortable with PyTorch and CUDA and Rust. That's a strong technical toolbox. But toolbox is not the same as domain knowledge. He conceived this product at a hackathon. Compare that to the kind of obsessive, years-long immersion that produces genuinely non-teachable insight. Prompt compression is a well-studied academic field -- NAACL 2025 survey papers, Microsoft Research publishing LLMLingua with 4,400 GitHub stars, Apple publishing hierarchical compression research. When a problem space has this much academic coverage, a hackathon insight isn't specific knowledge -- it's pattern recognition applied to publicly available ideas. The second team member has no public GitHub profile and comes from a VC analytics internship. I need a technologist on the founding team -- I have one here, but only one, and his knowledge of this particular domain appears to be months deep, not years deep.

The leverage architecture is the wrong kind. My best investments -- Notion, Replit, AngelList -- create *new forms* of leverage that didn't exist before. They let a new type of person do a new type of work. The Token Company doesn't create leverage; it marginally optimizes existing leverage. It makes your LLM calls slightly cheaper or slightly faster. That's an incremental efficiency gain on someone else's platform, not a new capability. At $0.05 per million compressed tokens, even their largest named customer (Pax Historia at 193B tokens/month) generates roughly $9,600/month. The unit economics require staggering volume to build a meaningful business, and staggering volume in a space where LLM providers have every incentive to internalize this function once it serves their margins.

The contrarian test is the most damning. Two companies in the same YC W26 batch are building essentially the same product -- Compresr has a four-person team with EPFL PhD-level ML research backgrounds and claims 100x compression versus The Token Company's 66%. When your direct competitor is in the same accelerator batch, neither of you has found something that can't be taught. This is an execution race in a known problem space, and execution races don't produce the asymmetric outcomes I invest for. Microsoft Research gave away the core technology as open source. Apple published research. There is no secret here.

The strongest bull case: what if LLM prompt compression becomes as standard as image compression -- a preprocessing step in every inference pipeline? If compression genuinely extracts signal from noise in prompts, improving accuracy rather than just reducing cost, then the TAM isn't "LLM cost savings" but "LLM accuracy infrastructure." That would be a platform layer I'd want to own. The 268K-vote arena and the +5% purchase lift are genuinely interesting data points. And the structural observation that LLM providers are economically misaligned with offering compression (they monetize on token volume) creates a real window. But windows close. Google, Anthropic, and OpenAI all have the research capability to build this in-house the moment it serves their strategic interests -- and making their models work better on shorter prompts clearly serves those interests. The window exists, but it's a timing arbitrage, not a durable structural advantage. I need compounding technology that gets harder to replicate over time, and I don't see it here -- each compression doesn't make the next compression better in any defensible way.

I respect the founder's raw technical ability. An 18-year-old comfortable with CUDA and Rust, backed by angels from Hugging Face and Supercell, getting into YC -- that's a signal of intelligence and energy. But intelligence and energy applied to a consensus problem in a deflating market without deep domain-specific knowledge is not the bet I'm looking for. I want founders who know something about their problem that would take a competitor years to learn. Here, a well-funded team of ML researchers could replicate the core technology in months -- and one already has, in the same YC batch.
