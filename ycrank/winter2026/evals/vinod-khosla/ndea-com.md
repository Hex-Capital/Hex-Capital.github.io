# Ndea -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

François Chollet built the ruler that measures how far we are from AGI, looked at where every frontier lab's approach was heading, and concluded they were all wrong. Then he left Google to build what he believes is right. That is the most interesting structural signal in this company. Chollet didn't just identify a gap in AI capability — he literally constructed the ARC-AGI benchmark that exposes the gap, watched the entire industry throw hundreds of billions at scaling transformers, saw that approach plateau at 55% on his own test, and decided the path to AGI runs through program synthesis, not through more GPUs and more data. When the person who built the measuring stick tells you the dominant approach will never reach the mark, and then bets his career on an alternative, you pay attention. This is not a research scientist writing papers about what's wrong. This is someone who defined the problem walking away from a Senior Staff position at Google to go solve it.

The consequence of success here is unbounded. This isn't a company pursuing a better chatbot or a marginally improved enterprise workflow. If program synthesis achieves what Chollet believes it can — genuine invention, novel reasoning, scientific discovery — every industry on the planet restructures simultaneously. Drug discovery, materials science, energy, robotics. There is no TAM estimate that captures this because AGI is not a market segment; it is a phase transition. The "AGI market" projections in the dossier ($1 billion to $27 billion by 2030) are absurdly small if the technology works, and irrelevant if it doesn't. This is the kind of bet where the consequence of success justifies the attempt regardless of what any financial model says. I wrote my largest check for OpenAI in 2019 on exactly this logic — the consequence magnitude overwhelmed every other consideration.

What strikes me most is the structural parallel to Juniper Networks. In 1996, Cisco's CTO told me they would never build a TCP/IP router for public telecom networks. The entire networking industry was committed to one paradigm, had invested billions in it, and dismissed the alternative. Pradeep Sindhu came from Xerox PARC — inside the research world but outside the networking establishment — and said the architecture was fundamentally wrong. Chollet occupies the same position: deeply inside AI research but fundamentally outside the LLM scaling consensus. He's telling OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, and xAI that their approach will never produce AGI. The capital asymmetry is staggering — $43 million against hundreds of billions — but that's exactly what Juniper faced against Cisco. If the paradigm is right, the capital follows. If the paradigm is wrong, no amount of compute fixes it. And Chollet's claim that "the state of program synthesis in 2025 looks a lot like deep learning circa 2012" is either one of the most prescient observations in AI history or spectacularly wrong. There is no middle ground. Either the money expires, or this becomes one of the most consequential companies ever built.

My framework typically penalizes industry experts — experience creates biases that prevent invention. Chollet is undeniably an expert. But the distinction that matters is whether the expert is defending the consensus or attacking it. Chollet is attacking the consensus of his own field with more intellectual authority than almost anyone alive. He created Keras, which powers deep learning at YouTube, Waymo, Spotify, ByteDance — and he's saying deep learning alone is insufficient. That takes a specific kind of courage that goes beyond mere contrarianism. Knoop's pairing is strategically sound: a Zapier co-founder who scaled to millions of users, a YC alumnus who understands commercialization. The researcher-operator combination directly addresses the two largest risks — "can we build the technology?" and "can we turn it into something that generates economic impact?" This is deliberate gene pool engineering, not accidental team formation.

The honest bear case is the KiOR lesson I carry in my bones. KiOR had brilliant technology. The biofuel chemistry worked. But it cost $5-10 per gallon to produce when the market price was under $3. Technology achievement without economic viability is a trap. Ndea has no product, no revenue, no pricing model, no announced path to commercialization. It's a pure research lab burning $43 million on a thesis that program synthesis will eventually produce AGI. If "eventually" means five years, this could be transformational. If it means twenty years, the capital runs out long before the breakthrough. The ARC-AGI benchmark improvement from 33% to 55.5% is encouraging, but one competition cycle does not establish an exponential curve. I need to see whether the rate of improvement is accelerating — that's the signal I tracked with OpenAI, where the pace of model capability improvement and talent inflow were the investment thesis. The talent signal here is positive (15 people, specialized hires, top researchers), but the capability trajectory remains thin. And the commoditization risk is real: if Chollet demonstrates that program synthesis works, Google DeepMind can redirect a fraction of its hundred-billion-dollar AI budget and overwhelm the approach. Ndea's defense is the tacit knowledge and specialized team advantage, but I've seen that defense erode under massive capital pressure.

Despite these risks, the investment logic holds. The consequence magnitude is maximum. The founder is making the most credible contrarian bet in AI — not a random outsider guessing, but the person who defined the measurement of AGI saying the dominant approach fails his own test. The technology paradigm is genuinely orthogonal to everything the major labs are doing, which means $43 million is competing against zero dollars allocated to this specific approach, not against $100 billion. The timing feels early — possibly too early — but early is where transformational returns live. I would want to see the next twelve months of ARC-AGI improvement data to confirm an accelerating trajectory, and I would push hard on a commercialization roadmap that translates research breakthroughs into economic impact before the capital runs out. But the skeleton of this bet is exactly the kind I was built to make.
