# Maquoketa Research -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The competitive landscape here is a Girardian textbook case. AeroVironment has $990 million in Army contracts. Anduril -- in which Founders Fund just made the largest single investment in our history -- builds the Altius-600M and Bolt. Helsing has a €12 billion valuation and 6,000 units ordered by Ukraine. Stark Defence won a €268 million German contract. Neros was selected for the Army's PBAS program. And now a five-person pre-seed team from Berkeley wants to enter this same arena with no contracts, no disclosed prototype, no press, and a website that returns a 403 error. I ask my signature question -- what is the secret here? -- and the answer I get back is silence. "Militaries need cheap autonomous drones" is not a secret. It is the loudest consensus in defense technology since the War on Terror validated ISR platforms. Every defense ministry in NATO, every venture fund with a defense thesis, every think tank publishing on the future of warfare has reached the identical conclusion. When five well-capitalized companies and every major prime are pursuing the same insight, you are not looking at a secret. You are looking at a market where competition will destroy returns.

There is one signal worth isolating: the CTO's specialization in phased array systems and RF design from Berkeley Wireless Research Center. This is genuinely atypical for a pre-seed drone company. Most startups in this category are either assembling commercial off-the-shelf components into FPV platforms or writing software layers on top of existing hardware. In-house phased array design operates at a different level of technical depth -- it touches the physics of guidance, communications, and electronic warfare resistance. EW is the existential threat to every drone system deployed in Ukraine; Russian jamming has neutralized vast numbers of commercial drones. If Hernandez has developed RF capabilities that make a loitering munition structurally resistant to jamming in contested electromagnetic environments, that would be a genuine technological discontinuity -- the kind of thing that makes existing approaches not just inferior but obsolete. But I am inferring this entire thesis from a single line on a YC page. I have no evidence the founders have articulated this as their secret, no evidence of a working prototype demonstrating EW resistance, and no evidence that the phased array work has produced anything beyond academic research. A potential secret is not a secret until someone has the conviction to bet their career on it being true, and I see no evidence of that conviction here.

The bull case would require believing three things simultaneously: first, that in-house RF/phased array capability creates a genuine 10x superiority in EW-contested environments that software-overlay competitors cannot replicate; second, that the defense primes are structurally incapable of building high-volume, low-cost munitions because their profit models depend on expensive, low-volume platforms; and third, that this specific team can navigate DoD procurement, obtain ITAR compliance and security clearances, and win contracts against competitors with hundred-million-dollar war chests -- all on pre-seed runway. Individually, each claim has some merit. The primes genuinely face a business-model cannibalization problem; Germany awarded €9 billion in strike drone contracts to startups over incumbents, which validates the structural opening. But the conjunction of all three, required simultaneously, makes this a low-probability path to monopoly. And even if the technology proves superior, defense procurement does not reliably reward technical merit -- Stark Defence won €268 million despite failing all four test strikes, which tells you that relationships, politics, and industrial policy matter as much as capability.

I must also confront the portfolio logic. Anduril is already my bet in autonomous defense hardware. Trae Stephens, a Founders Fund partner, helped build it. Palmer Luckey is a protege. We just wrote a billion-dollar check. If I believe the autonomous munitions thesis -- and I do -- I already have a $30.5 billion company executing on it with the Altius and Bolt programs. Investing in Maquoketa would be investing in a competitor to my own portfolio company at a thousand-to-one resource disadvantage. My revealed pattern in defense is not to sprinkle capital across the sector but to incubate or co-found a single company and build it into the dominant platform. Palantir. Anduril. General Matter. Valinor. Each of these involved deep relational trust and operational involvement from inception. A cold YC company with no visible connection to my network does not fit that pattern.

The founders cover the right technical disciplines -- software, RF/electronics, and mechanical engineering -- but nothing in the dossier suggests the kind of definite, civilization-level vision I require. The CEO's prior experience is at a trading technology firm. There are no public statements, no interviews, no articulated thesis about why the world must look different. Compare this to Palmer Luckey, who was building VR headsets in his parents' garage as a teenager and could articulate exactly why autonomous defense hardware would transform warfare, or to the General Matter team drawn from SpaceX, Tesla, and national labs with a specific plan for HALEU enrichment. I am looking for founders who have already refused an easier path in service of a vision that most people consider impossible. I see technically competent Berkeley graduates entering a crowded market. That is not the same thing.
