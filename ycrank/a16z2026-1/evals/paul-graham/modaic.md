# Modaic -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Modaic's competitive position is the first thing I'd want to understand if Farouk Adeleke and Tyrin-Ian Todd walked into a YC interview. Braintrust raised $80M at an $800M valuation in February 2026 and already counts Dropbox among its customers. Modaic claims Dropbox as a "design partner" — unverified — while operating as a two-person team with 23 stars on their core Python SDK. When a pre-seed company names as a partner the same enterprise that's already paying a competitor with 50x their resources, I get skeptical. Not because two people can't beat a well-funded incumbent — Stripe started as two people against an entire payment processing industry — but because Stripe was attacking a schlep that repelled everyone, while Modaic is entering a space where capital is already flowing freely. "DevOps for Declarative Self-Improving AI Agents" sounds good to everyone in 2026. That's the problem.

The founders have real technical credentials. Tyrin-Ian Todd trained transformer models at the MIT Media Lab and contributed to HuggingFace's datasets library — these are genuine builder artifacts, not resume padding. Farouk Adeleke dropped out of BU's CS program, shipped Spydr to #12 on Product Hunt, worked at Wayfair and Harvard. Both have GitHub repos that show actual code, not pitch decks. This is the "hackers building for hackers" pattern I gravitate toward — the pattern behind Stripe, Replit, Retool. But the comparison to Replit is instructive in what's missing. When I found Amjad Masad on Hacker News in 2017, he'd been obsessing over making programming accessible for years. His commitment to that specific problem was palpable. Farouk and Tyrin built Spydr (LLM context management) and Dyle (AI customer success agent) respectively, then pivoted to Modaic — three different products across what appears to be roughly twelve months. I don't have stories of creative workarounds or doing-things-that-don't-scale; I have a sequence of launches. Rapid iteration can signal learning velocity or it can signal a lack of the right kind of stubbornness — persistence toward a goal rather than attachment to whichever idea feels newest.

The problem discovery doesn't pass my "USB drive" test. Drew Houston built Dropbox because he kept forgetting his USB drive — he lived in the future and built what was missing from his own life. Farouk's LinkedIn describes meeting Tyrin and developing Modaic after discussing DSPy. The origin story is a framework enthusiasm, not a personal frustration. Neither founder has worked in content moderation, classification systems, or the judgment-heavy enterprise workflows Modaic targets. They're building for a customer segment they don't inhabit. The DSPy coupling runs deeper than a technology choice — Farouk's LinkedIn headline is literally "dspy infra" — and it means Modaic's foundation depends on an open-source Stanford project maintaining community momentum against alternatives. Stripe built on immovable payment rails; Modaic built on an academic framework that's existed for roughly two years.

The strongest bull case for Modaic: the serial pivoting is actually convergent learning. Farouk tried the application layer with Spydr, Tyrin tried the application layer with Dyle, and both independently discovered that the missing piece was the optimization infrastructure beneath their products. That's how many great infrastructure companies emerge — you build on top of something broken until you realize you should fix the foundation instead. If the Accenture, Dropbox, and Vercel partnerships are real design partnerships with named contacts and defined deliverables, that would be extraordinary enterprise validation for a pre-seed team and would significantly change my assessment. The architectural insight — coupling confidence-based routing with human feedback and automatic instruction improvement — is genuinely more ambitious than Braintrust's observe-then-fix model. The data flywheel Modaic describes, where each customer's human feedback accumulates into a proprietary optimization dataset, could produce real switching costs if the cycle works. But "if" is doing a lot of work in that sentence. I'd need to see the partnerships verified and at least one customer whose workflow measurably improved through the feedback loop before I'd write a check.

Modaic sits in a zone I find uncomfortable: technically competent young founders building something that sounds good to everyone in a space where $150M+ in competitor funding has already validated the category. The idea doesn't repel — it attracts, immediately and from all directions. The two dimensions that most determined my score are Organic Problem Discovery (framework-driven rather than pain-driven, building for a customer segment they haven't inhabited) and Idea That Repels Before It Attracts (consensus AI tooling in a crowded, well-funded market). Founder Learning Rate is the single dimension pulling upward — the convergence from application to infrastructure suggests real iteration, and the MIT Media Lab and HuggingFace contributions demonstrate technical depth that's harder to fake than a pitch deck. But depth in one dimension doesn't overcome the fundamental question: are these founders solving a problem they discovered in their own work, or one they identified by looking at what's trending in the DSPy ecosystem?
