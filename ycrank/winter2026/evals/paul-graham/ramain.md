# RamAIn -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the competitor table. Simular has $27 million and a partnership with Microsoft. Browser Use has $17 million and a leading benchmark score. Browserbase has $40 million. And sitting directly above all of them, Anthropic and OpenAI are building computer-use agents as first-party features of their own platforms. When the foundation model providers are your direct competitors, you need an extraordinarily strong reason to believe you'll win. RamAIn's reason is "pre-training on specific interfaces makes us 10x faster." That might be true. But an architectural optimization on top of someone else's foundation model is a thin layer to build a company on, especially when that someone else could add the same optimization in a quarter.

The question I always ask first is how the founders found this problem. And here I don't see an answer. There's no story of Shourya or Vansh personally struggling to move data between legacy systems in a procurement office, or watching a claims adjuster toggle between five browser tabs. Shourya's previous company was "an AI studio for enterprises" -- which is about as generic as startup descriptions get. The path from "AI studio" to "AI computer-use agents" looks like it followed the news cycle: Anthropic shipped Computer Use in October 2024, the category got hot, and two smart students saw an optimization opportunity. That's the failure mode I've written about repeatedly -- ideas conceived by watching what's trending rather than stumbling into a problem you can't stop thinking about. The laundry list of target verticals -- procurement, insurance, healthcare, finance, retail, pharmacy, logistics -- is another tell. When you've organically discovered a problem, you know exactly which vertical you're starting in. When you've manufactured an idea, you list every possible market because you haven't committed to any of them.

The strongest thing here is Vansh Ramani's technical credentials. An ICLR 2025 publication, research at CMU's ML department, and a nearest-neighbor algorithm that got integrated into Meta's FAISS library -- for an undergraduate, that's genuinely impressive. This is someone who can build. And Shourya bootstrapped a previous company to six-figure revenue, which at least shows he can sell something. The combination of a real ML researcher and someone with entrepreneurial reps is not nothing. If I squint, it reminds me a little of early Reddit -- young hackers building something before the market fully understands it. But Reddit's founders were building something they themselves wanted to use every day. I don't see that here.

The bull case would require two things to be true simultaneously. First, that pre-training CUAs on specific enterprise interfaces creates a durable advantage -- not just a speed improvement, but a reliability improvement so dramatic that enterprise buyers choose RamAIn over Anthropic's native offering or Simular's well-funded alternative. Second, that two college students with no enterprise sales experience can navigate procurement cycles in regulated industries like healthcare and insurance. Both of those are possible. But both need to be true at once, and that starts to look like the "multiple miracles" problem. The technical miracle is hard enough; combining it with an enterprise go-to-market miracle from a two-person team with no visible domain relationships pushes this into territory where I'd want to see at least one enterprise pilot or LOI before getting excited.

There's zero evidence of user pull. No users, no revenue, no waitlist, no case studies, no organic sharing. The product is listed as free at $0. At pre-seed I don't demand much traction, but I do want some sign that someone besides the founders cares. Even a single enterprise customer running a pilot would change this picture meaningfully. Without any signal of demand, I'm left evaluating the idea and the team in isolation -- and the idea, while technically sound, sits in the most crowded category in AI right now. When I said recently that the most impressive YC companies I'd seen were not working on AI, this is the kind of company I was thinking about: smart people, real technical skills, but building in a space where you're competing against the richest companies in history for essentially the same customer.
