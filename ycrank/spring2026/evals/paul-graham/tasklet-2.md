# Tasklet -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Andrew Lee built Firebase through YC S11, got it acquired by Google, watched it become infrastructure for roughly 15% of all developers, and is now back at YC twelve years later with Tasklet. I pay attention when a founder comes back. Patrick Collison built Auctomatic, then Stripe. Sam Altman did Loopt, then OpenAI. The pattern is: the first company teaches them how to build; the second company is what they actually build. Tasklet has $5M ARR with over 1,200% growth since January 2026, a $20M Series A at $175M from USV, Lightspeed, Jeff Dean, and — notably — Patrick and John Collison. The Collisons are among the most selective angel investors alive. When they write a check into a company building agent infrastructure, that's a signal I weight heavily, because they understand what it takes to build a platform layer better than almost anyone.

Andrew Lee and Jonny Dimond have been building together for over a decade — Firebase, then Google post-acquisition (where Dimond led Cloud Firestore), then Shortwave, now Tasklet. That co-founder relationship has survived a startup, an acquisition, a large-company stint, a second startup, and a pivot. Most co-founder pairs haven't been tested under a single one of those stresses. Tasklet's founding team has been through all of them and chose to keep working together. When I evaluate resourcefulness, I look for evidence that founders can navigate ambiguity and constraint. Building Firebase from a YC demo day to the real-time database layer for millions of apps is not something you do by following a playbook. The pivot from Shortwave is itself a resourcefulness signal: by January 2026, Tasklet was adding more monthly revenue than Shortwave had accumulated in its first four years. Most founders would cling to the thing they spent years building. Lee and Dimond saw a bigger opening and ran toward it. That's the right kind of stubborn — persistent about the goal, flexible about the path.

Tasklet's origin story is genuine organic discovery, which matters to me more than most investors realize. The team didn't sit down and brainstorm "what's hot in AI." They were building Shortwave's AI email features, developing context engineering and agent infrastructure for that product, and saw demand for a general-purpose agent runtime emerge from their own work. The technology and context engineering from Shortwave got redeployed into Tasklet. This is "live in the future, then build what's missing" — they were living inside the problem of making AI agents execute real tasks reliably, and Tasklet is what was missing. Drew Houston built Dropbox because he kept forgetting his USB drive. Lee built Tasklet because he was already building the pieces of it inside another product and realized the pieces were more valuable than the product.

Here is where my framework creates genuine tension with Tasklet. "AI agents for knowledge work" is possibly the most consensus idea in technology in 2026. Zapier is at $310M revenue. Lindy has raised $50M. Relevance AI raised $37M. Google, Microsoft, OpenAI, and Anthropic are all building agent capabilities. The TAM estimates — $7.63B growing to $183B — read like they came from a pitch deck designed to make every investor nod. Nobody hears "AI agent that automates your workflows" and says "that's crazy, it'll never work." They say "obviously." And in my framework, when everyone agrees an idea is good, the returns have already been competed away. Tasklet sits squarely in the danger zone I described in "Black Swan Farming." The CEO himself acknowledged "it is super expensive to run" with Ubuntu VMs and browser automation at $35-$250/month price points — and foundation model providers like Google (Gemini + Workspace) can offer similar agent capabilities bundled with productivity suites used by hundreds of millions. The commoditization risk is real: the dossier itself states the core technology is "reproducible."

The bull case an advocate would make is formidable: $5M ARR at over 1,200% growth is not a hypothesis, it's a scoreboard. Tasklet's growth rate — if sustained even at half that pace — puts it on a trajectory toward $50M+ ARR within a year. The team has Firebase-grade infrastructure pedigree, the co-founders have a decade-plus working relationship, and the Collison brothers' investment validates both the technical approach and the founders. USV GP Nick Grossman called Tasklet "fully addictive once you get going," which sounds like the kind of intense early user love I look for — "a small number of people want a large amount." Firebase proved Lee can build a platform layer that becomes infrastructure for an entire ecosystem. Tasklet's combination of Pipedream integrations, MCP server support, code sandboxes, and cloud browser automation creates a genuine integration moat that deepens with usage. The Stripe parallel is instructive: Stripe succeeded in a competitive payments market not because payments were contrarian but because the Collisons executed at a level that made the competition irrelevant. Execution quality at the infrastructure layer can overcome consensus markets.

That parallel holds — but only partially. When Stripe launched, online payments were a schlep that repelled developers. Bank integrations, PCI compliance, regulatory complexity — nobody wanted to touch it. That disgust reaction was Stripe's moat. Tasklet's domain doesn't repel anyone. It attracts everyone. The schlep of making agents reliable across enterprise systems is real, but it's a schlep that hundreds of well-funded teams are happily tackling. Still, I keep coming back to the founders. Andrew Lee has already demonstrated he can build a platform that millions of developers depend on. Jonny Dimond has built cloud infrastructure at Google scale. Their learning rate — visible in that growth curve and in the speed of the Shortwave pivot — suggests they're iterating faster than competitors who lack their infrastructure instincts. Tasklet's team of eight, pulling from Firebase, Google, Amazon, and OpenAI, is building with the earnest intensity of people who care about the runtime layer itself, not about riding a trend. The honest admission that "it is super expensive to run" — rather than hand-waving about margins — is the kind of direct communication I associate with founders who think clearly.

My verdict: invest, despite the consensus market. Tasklet's founders are the kind of infrastructure builders I've bet on successfully before — the Firebase pedigree, the decade-long partnership, the organic pivot, the extraordinary growth rate all override my discomfort with the market's consensus enthusiasm. The near-term evidence that would flip me to Pass: if growth decelerates sharply over the next two quarters as foundation model providers ship native agent capabilities into Workspace and M365, revealing that Tasklet's current traction is early-adopter enthusiasm rather than durable platform lock-in. The two dimensions that lock this tier are Founder Resourcefulness (Firebase exit plus Shortwave pivot demonstrate the running-back quality across a full career) and Organic Problem Discovery (the Shortwave-to-Tasklet arc is authentically "build, then build what's missing"). The consensus-market penalty on Idea That Repels is real and significant — it's what keeps Tasklet from scoring higher — but I've learned to bet on founders over markets, and these founders have already proven they can build infrastructure that lasts.
