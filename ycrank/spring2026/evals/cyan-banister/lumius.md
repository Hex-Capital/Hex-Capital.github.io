# Lumius -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Junjie Yao has 11,000+ citations, an NSF CAREER Award, an OPTICA Fellowship, and runs the Photoacoustic Imaging Lab at Duke where the entire Lumius technology was born. That's a staggering scientific pedigree for a pre-seed company. And yet when I sit with this dossier, the first thing I notice is what's missing — not from Lumius's technology stack, which is genuinely impressive, but from the founding story. I ask every founder: tell me your story, not the business. Lumius's story is: four Duke researchers invented a novel 3D imaging technique in an academic lab and are now commercializing it. That's a legitimate origin, but it's not a biographical forge. Tri Vu went from Duke PhD to Research Assistant Professor to entrepreneur. Luca Menozzi is a PhD candidate and NSF Fellow who built the 3D-DAT system. Chenhang Li came from a different Duke lab working on wearable electronics. Nobody on this team appears to have watched a patient bleed out from a botched central line, or spent nights in a hospital where failing equipment decided who lived. The problem found them through a microscope, not through life. That matters to me — not because academic founders can't build great companies, but because the medical device slog is brutal, and when FDA timelines stretch to 24 months and your fifth hospital procurement officer ghosts you, the founder who lived inside the suffering stays while the founder who saw a market opportunity recalculates.

Lumius's 3D diffractive acoustic tomography — 3D-DAT — is the kind of technology I respect deeply. Luca Menozzi developed it using an off-the-shelf linear-array transducer with single-slit acoustic diffraction, achieving real-time volumetric imaging that existing 3D systems from GE and Philips deliver only through cart-based machines costing $50K–$200K+. The innovator's dilemma here is textbook: those incumbents won't cannibalize their high-margin radiology suite products to sell a portable device at bedside. This reminds me of my Substrate investment — deep tech challenging an entrenched monopoly at a regulatory inflection point, with a team that has genuine IP ownership. When I wrote about Substrate's founder James Proud, I said he was "forged in fire and came out unbroken." Lumius has the technical infrastructure parallel but not the biographical one. Proud had been through startup crucibles before Substrate. Lumius's founders have Duke faculty positions and PhD programs to return to if the commercial path gets ugly. That fallback option changes incentive structures in ways that matter.

Butterfly Network has raised $353.5M and generates $97.6M in trailing twelve-month revenue — all in 2D. Exo has raised $428M with Samsung potentially adding $100M more — also 2D. Clarius is actively developing 3D volume registration using IMU data. The portable ultrasound space is not contrarian — it's hot. Capital is flowing freely into POCUS, and investors are not shifting uncomfortably in their chairs when you pitch them a better ultrasound device. Lumius's 3D angle is genuinely differentiated, and the technical approach (single-transducer volumetric imaging) is novel enough that a well-funded competitor can't trivially replicate it. But "hard engineering in a well-funded space" is different from "operating in a space that makes sophisticated investors visibly uncomfortable." Anduril was politically toxic. Contraline was stigmatized. Lumius is technically ambitious — which I admire — but it's not the kind of bet that 300 investors passed on because the idea provoked discomfort.

The invisible system question is where Lumius lands somewhere in the middle for me. There IS an opaque structure here: hospitals spend millions on 2D ultrasound training because the technology itself is inadequate, and 50% of vascular access attempts fail on the first try — a statistic that translates directly into patient suffering, complications, and costs that patients never see itemized on their bills. The medical device pricing structure that charges 10x for 3D capabilities is a form of institutional gatekeeping. But Lumius isn't framing its mission as tearing down that wall — it's framing it as better technology at lower cost. That's a product pitch, not a structural revolution. Affirm didn't just offer cheaper lending; it exposed the specific mechanism by which credit card companies extract value through opacity. Lumius could make that argument about medical imaging pricing, but the dossier shows no evidence they're thinking in those terms.

The bull case deserves honest construction. Lumius has perhaps the strongest technical moat I've seen at pre-seed: Duke-originated IP in a specific imaging modality, a founding team that includes the lab PI who invented the underlying science, and a regulatory barrier that becomes a moat once crossed. GPU-accelerated signal processing has just crossed the threshold that makes real-time 3D reconstruction feasible on portable hardware — a genuine inflection point. The clinical need is measurable and urgent. If Lumius achieves FDA clearance and delivers affordable 3D imaging at point-of-care, every hospital in the country becomes a customer, and the developing world opens up after that. The cascading impact — bringing volumetric imaging from $100K+ radiology suites to rural clinics — would be the kind of access expansion I care about. The condition that would flip me: if I met Tri Vu and learned that a family member suffered from a preventable complication caused by failed vascular access, or if Luca Menozzi told me about spending clinical rotations watching residents struggle with 2D imaging while patients paid the price — that biographical connection to suffering, not just to science, would fundamentally change my read. Alternatively, if Lumius showed me early evidence that Junjie Yao has left his tenured position and burned the academic boats, signaling that this team has no fallback.

Lumius is a company I'd want to track but not one I'd write a check for today from my own money. The defensibility dimension is real — 3D-DAT, Yao's 11,000+ citations, and the regulatory moat-in-waiting are the strongest pre-seed technical signals I've seen in this batch. But my framework weights the biographical forge at 30% for a reason: it's the signal that predicts whether founders survive the valley of death. Every member of Lumius's team has a comfortable academic position waiting if the commercial path breaks. The space isn't contrarian enough — POCUS is a consensus-attractive category with nearly $800M in competitive funding. And the invisible system being disrupted, while present in the pricing structure of medical imaging, isn't being articulated as a structural mission. Lumius is competent founders building impressive technology in a real market — and for many investors, that's enough. For me, it's missing the forge, the weirdness, and the rage against a hidden system that drives my highest-conviction bets.
