# Taiga -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Candid Health has raised $99.5M with 250% revenue growth in 2024. AKASA has pulled in $205M. LunaBill, a YC F25 company, already reports $764K in contracted ARR for AI-powered insurance follow-up calls. AI-enabled healthcare startups captured 62% of all digital health venture funding in H1 2025 at an 83% premium per round over non-AI companies. So let me ask the obvious question: where is the mispricing? Taiga is entering what might be the single hottest consensus category in venture right now — AI applied to revenue cycle management. Every sophisticated investor at every demo day is nodding along to this pitch. When every other investor in the room is excited, the price already reflects the upside. There's no asymmetry left. Taiga isn't a bet the market has dismissed — it's a bet the market has already made, repeatedly, at scale.

Taiga's core capabilities — ICD-10 and CPT coding from clinical notes, denial prediction, claims scrubbing against payer rules, automated appeals — are the exact kind of thing I warn about constantly. Replace "Adobe" with "Epic" or "Athenahealth" in my framework and you have Taiga's problem. Taiga lists seven EHR integrations on its website: Athenahealth, eClinicalWorks, NextGen, AdvancedMD, Tebra, Elation, and Epic. Every single one of those platforms is looking at Taiga's feature set and thinking "we should add that." The dossier says it plainly: "LLM-based medical coding and claims scrubbing are technically reproducible." No physical assets, no protocol-level lock-in, no network effects that compound beyond a modest data flywheel that any competitor with more volume will build faster. Taiga fails the AI kill test as cleanly as any company I've seen this batch. When I backed Metropolis at seed, the parking infrastructure was the moat — no LLM replicates parking garages. Taiga has no equivalent.

Nanda Guntupalli (Penn CS) and Adam Wax (Lehigh CS + Math, hackathon wins, AWS Bedrock experience) are clearly sharp. They've been friends since third grade — that's a real co-founder relationship, not some incubator matchmaking exercise, and I respect that. Both have physician parents who own independent practices, which means they've watched this problem bleed revenue from their families' dinner tables. That's genuine. But here's where my "sane person, insane idea" framework cuts against Taiga: the idea isn't remotely insane. "Let's use AI to automate medical billing" is the most obvious application of LLMs to healthcare. It's the first thing any CS student with a doctor parent would think of. Sane people pitching a sane idea — that's just ordinary. Neither founder has prior startup experience, prior healthcare industry work, or a prior exit. I backed Raj Dittmar on Solana after watching him through three companies. With Taiga's founders, I'm being asked to take a first-time bet on two recent graduates in a category where Candid Health alone has a $99.5M head start and demonstrable traction.

The strongest bull case for Taiga goes like this: large RCM incumbents like Waystar ($8B valuation) and R1 RCM have cost structures built around enterprise contracts with hospital systems. Serving 200,000+ small independent practices is economically unattractive under their model — the unit economics don't work. Taiga's full-service agency positioning (not SaaS, not a point solution) with AI-driven cost structure could profitably serve this structurally abandoned segment. Percentage-of-collections pricing aligns incentives perfectly and creates natural capital efficiency. And the founders' personal network into the independent practice community through their parents gives them authentic distribution others can't fake. I'll grant one thing: the underservice of small practices is real, and the agency model with AI leverage is a more interesting structural bet than yet another SaaS dashboard. But this argument proves the market is right about the category, not wrong. If the segment is genuinely attractive, Candid Health — with $99.5M and 250% growth — will move downmarket. LunaBill is already in YC building adjacent capabilities. The competitive moat for serving small practices with AI billing is not a category that others have dismissed — it's a lane everyone sees.

Taiga's two-person team and likely percentage-of-collections model suggest reasonable capital discipline — you get paid when you collect, and two founders aren't burning through a war chest. That's the one dimension where Taiga shows a positive signal, though it's table stakes for any pre-seed company. On the primitive creation axis, Taiga creates nothing new. Medical billing is among the oldest categories in American healthcare. Automating it with AI is an incremental improvement, not a new behavior — nobody will say "Taiga me" the way they say "Venmo me." There's no new financial instrument, no new social structure, no new market. It's "better billing," and better billing competes on execution in an existing market with capped upside defined by the existing market's willingness to switch.

One verifiable condition would flip me to Invest: if Taiga demonstrated that its denial-prediction model, trained on proprietary payer-specific data from hundreds of small practices, achieved measurably superior claim acceptance rates (say, 15%+ improvement over baseline) that no competitor with less small-practice volume could replicate — that would be a real data moat emerging from the agency model. Show me the data flywheel actually spinning, not theoretically possible. Until then, Taiga is a sensible company in a consensus-hot category with no mispricing, no new primitive, no structural moat against AI commoditization, and first-time founders competing against $300M+ in combined competitor funding. The two dimensions that locked this verdict are Consensus Mispricing (AI healthcare billing is the most crowded category in venture right now) and Structural Durability (every EHR platform Taiga integrates with can build this in-house). Pass.
