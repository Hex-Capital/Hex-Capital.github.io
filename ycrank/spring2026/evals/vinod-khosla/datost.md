# Datost -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here is the uncomfortable truth about Datost: Salesforce owns Slack and Salesforce owns Tableau. The company is building an AI data analyst inside a platform whose parent already possesses both the distribution channel and the analytics engine to do exactly the same thing. This is not a hypothetical competitive risk — it is a structural inevitability. When I look at platform dependency, I ask whether the startup has a technology advantage so profound that the platform owner would rather buy than build. Text-to-SQL plus a sandboxed execution environment is not that. Any competent engineering team at Salesforce can ship this feature in a quarter. Building your entire company on another company's real estate, where that landlord has every economic incentive to evict you, is not a risk I can price into an option — it is a terminal condition.

The consequence question is where this falls apart for me. If Datost fully succeeds — every Slack workspace has a data analyst bot — what changes? Knowledge workers at companies that already have databases, data teams, and Slack subscriptions get answers 30 minutes faster. That is a productivity gain. It is not a transformation of how people live. Nobody who lacked access to data analysis suddenly gains it. The expensive does not become free — the moderately inconvenient becomes slightly more convenient. When I backed Impossible Foods, the consequence was feeding billions without animal agriculture. When I backed Commonwealth Fusion Systems, the consequence was limitless clean energy. "Faster ad-hoc queries for enterprise teams" does not belong in the same sentence. The tagline says "Democratize data," but democratization means making something available to people who currently cannot access it at all — not making it marginally easier for people who already have it.

The technical approach is the clearest disqualifier. The dossier itself states it plainly: "The core capability (LLM + SQL generation + execution) is reproducible. Any Slack bot developer could build a basic version." That is not my inference — that is the observable market reality. The "first AI data analyst that has its own computer" is a feature, not a technology discontinuity. Compare this to Rocket Lab's electric turbopumps and 3D-printed combustion chambers, which constituted a genuinely novel engineering approach to cost reduction. Datost is an integration layer on top of commodity LLMs. There is no proprietary science, no novel algorithm, no data advantage that compounds over time. The claimed defensibility — "data context accumulation" — is an inference, not evidence, and it assumes competitors cannot also index an organization's schema, which they trivially can.

The strongest bull case I can construct: if you believe that AI will genuinely eliminate the data analyst role entirely — not augment it, eliminate it — then whoever owns that workflow captures a massive labor market. A $150K/year analyst replaced by a $500/month Slack bot is a 25x cost reduction. That is a real affordability argument. And two 21-year-old outsiders without BI domain experience could, in theory, be the kind of irreverent, fast-learning founders I have funded before. If they iterated rapidly during YC, discovered a wedge into enterprise data workflows that incumbents cannot replicate, and built trust features (permissions, audit trails, SOC 2) faster than expected — there is a version of this story that works. But I see no evidence of that trajectory. Zero GitHub stars. No traction signals of any kind. LinkedIn headline still reads "Software Engineer Intern." The demo repo has zero stars and was last touched two weeks before this evaluation. The gene pool is two engineers with overlapping skills from the same prior employer — that is a team optimized for comfort, not capability. I need to see evidence of learning velocity, and I see none.

I regularly see YC companies building AI assistants for specific enterprise workflows. The pitch follows a template: large TAM, underserved by technology, we automate a chunk of the work. My framework requires that success means the world visibly changes. Datost's success means enterprise Slack users type questions instead of opening Tableau. That is a feature, not a future. When I write a check, I am buying an option on a world that looks fundamentally different. This world looks the same, just slightly more efficient. Pass.
