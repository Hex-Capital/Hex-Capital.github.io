# InstaAgent -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that registers first about InstaAgent is the pairing -- a real schlep wrapped around a credential stack that does not look like hackers. Kyle Wong is Cornell triple-major plus ex-Goldman China TMT; Colin Tseung is Oxford Mathematics, "repeat founder (acqui-hired)" per the snippet; CW Liu is a blank. The work itself -- "hundreds of social accounts engineered to reach each niche" plus the pivot from self-serve to agency because "customers do not just want AI tools" -- is unpleasant in exactly the way I like unpleasant. But the people running it read more like the consulting-into-Goldman track than the dorm-room track that produced Stripe or Reddit. That tension is InstaAgent's whole signature.

The schlep dimension is where InstaAgent's strongest rung lives, and the analogy that loads is Coinbase -- not because creative ops is regulated the way bitcoin custody was, but because the mechanism is the same: a category that everyone else routes around because it is ugly. Brian Armstrong's moat at Coinbase was the compliance grind nobody else wanted to do; InstaAgent's analog is running hundreds of niche social accounts against Meta and TikTok's platform-integrity teams while simultaneously doing services delivery for consumer brands. AdCreative.ai (acquired by Appier for $38.7M) and Pencil (acquired by Brandtech) both refused to do the services half -- they kept the SaaS margin and got acquired at modest multiples. InstaAgent went the other way, "as customers do not just want AI tools; they need help producing quality creatives." That is a Schlep Blindness move. Where InstaAgent diverges from Coinbase is that Armstrong's schlep compounded into a regulatory moat; InstaAgent's schlep compounds into an agency P&L that nobody pays a software multiple for.

Hacker genealogy is the rung where I have to lean down, and the failure mechanism I keep watching for is the polished-AI-wrapper team from my own pass example -- the two-MBA outfit with a $50B TAM slide and no shipped artifact. InstaAgent is not that team -- they have shipped $1M ARR in ten months, which is more than most pre-seed bets ever produce -- but the founder traces have the wrong texture. No GitHub repos for Wong. No GitHub repos for Tseung. No open-source footprint, no Hacker News presence, no competitive programming wins, no Lisp prize the way Patrick Collison had before Stripe. Cornell statistics-economics-philosophy plus Goldman Sachs TMT is exactly the credential pattern that, in my experience, produces good operators and rarely produces the kind of technical taste that converts an agency into a defensible platform. The dossier explicitly says "no public repos found" for both named founders. Hackers leave trails; the absence is the signal.

On organic-idea fit, the mechanism that matters is whether the founders themselves felt the problem before they built the tool. InstaAgent's snippet says "the founding team are repeat founders who burned millions on paid social" -- if that is verified, it is exactly the Dropbox-USB-stick pattern, the founder solving their own past pain. Wong's claimed "1M+ users in 1 month" at a prior product would have meant running real paid-social budgets at scale. The dossier flags this as unverified search-snippet text, which is the right caveat -- but if it holds up in a one-hour conversation, this dimension flips from a default to a genuine organic-idea signal, and the whole evaluation moves.

Animal resourcefulness has to be measured against what InstaAgent actually shipped versus what the founders said. $1M ARR in ten months without a hiring page and only six people is a real artifact -- not a cereal-box stunt, but not a deck either. The right counterfactual is: would I give InstaAgent the same score with $200K ARR? No. The revenue ramp is doing the work here, because the dossier does not describe a single named pre-funding stunt -- no Obama O's, no demo video on Hacker News, no manual operation Wong personally ran at 3am. The animal test passes on output, not on a recognizable in-the-trenches anecdote, and that is a softer version of what I want.

Co-founder configuration for InstaAgent is three people, which is the Airbnb/Twitch count rather than the Stripe/Reddit pair count. The dossier offers no shared employer, no shared university, no prior project together -- compare to Huffman and Ohanian as dorm roommates at UVA, or Kan-Shear-Vogt coming out of the Kiko wreckage together. InstaAgent's trio could be a long-running team or three strangers who paired for YC; the dossier cannot tell me which. CW Liu in particular is a void -- no background, no role, no public footprint. This is the default-neutral case and it stays neutral until an interview surfaces the chemistry.

My 36-month forecast for InstaAgent: plateaus between $5M and $15M ARR as a managed creative-services agency for DTC brands, then gets bought by a holding-company aggregator -- Brandtech is the obvious buyer given they already absorbed Pencil -- at a low revenue multiple, somewhere in the $30-60M range. Not zero, not a Black Swan. The mechanism producing this forecast is the signature pairing: a real schlep run by credentialed-operator founders rather than hackers tends to capture the unit economics of an agency rather than the compounding of a platform, because the technical taste required to automate the services half out of existence is not visibly present in the founder trace.

The strongest case against that forecast is that the agency pivot is exactly "Do Things That Don't Scale" -- Airbnb shooting photos door-to-door, Wufoo hand-writing thank-you notes. The bull mechanism says InstaAgent is in the manual-grind phase that precedes the platform; the $1M ARR in ten months is the evidence that the manual work is teaching them what to automate, and the performance feedback loop on creative is where the data moat compounds. I take this seriously because if Wong and Tseung genuinely burned millions on paid social before InstaAgent, the organic-pain signal could override my hacker-genealogy skepticism the way the cereal boxes overrode my idea skepticism on Airbnb. But the evidence that wins on the page right now is the absence of a GitHub trail for either founder -- you can fake a deck and you can buy a credential, but you cannot retroactively manufacture a decade of hacker output. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: a working demo from Wong or Tseung that shows the persona-creative pipeline running end-to-end without human creative ops in the loop, plus named confirmation of which prior company hit "1M+ users in 1 month." Without those two things, InstaAgent is a competent agency in hacker clothing, and I do not write the check from my own money.
