# Fed10 -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The Harvey comparison is the obvious one to reach for here -- AI agents replacing expensive professionals in a legal-adjacent domain. But the comparison actually reveals Fed10's structural challenge more than its opportunity. When I looked at Harvey, the legal AI market had no credible incumbent response. Law firms weren't building AI products, and the enterprise legal software players were a generation behind on LLM integration. Fed10 faces the opposite dynamic: FiscalNote launched PolicyNote, an explicitly AI-focused platform, in January 2025. Quorum, at $61M in revenue with 1,800+ customers including half the Fortune 100, is already integrating AI features. Plural Policy raised $14M for AI-powered policy collaboration. The structural moment for AI in legislative analysis is real, but unlike defense AI post-Project Maven, there's no vacancy here -- every significant incumbent is already moving into the space Fed10 wants to occupy.

The market structure concerns me more than the market size. The advocacy and public affairs software market at $1.4 billion isn't inherently too small -- you can build a great business in a $1.4 billion market. The issue is that this is a known, well-mapped market with entrenched distribution. Quorum has 1,800+ enterprise customers. FiscalNote covers 12,000+ local governments. These companies have the customer relationships, the sales infrastructure, and the switching cost advantages that come from being embedded in government affairs workflows. When I invest in "boring but critical" infrastructure, the pattern I'm looking for is a market that established investors dismiss -- PagerDuty, Gusto, Checkr. GovTech legislative tracking is boring, but it's not dismissed. It has attracted hundreds of millions in revenue and multiple well-funded competitors. That's the wrong kind of boring for my framework.

The bull case rests on two structural observations worth taking seriously. First, FiscalNote's 97% stock decline and organizational transition suggest a market leader in genuine distress -- and distressed incumbents lose customers. If Fed10 could position as the modern alternative for enterprises fleeing FiscalNote, that's a real acquisition channel. Second, the market expansion thesis has merit: at $500/hr for policy consultants, legislative intelligence is currently rationed to organizations that can afford it. AI dropping the cost floor by 90% could expand the addressable market well beyond the current software TAM, bringing legislative monitoring to the thousands of mid-market companies and trade associations that are affected by regulation but can't afford traditional consulting. That's the Gusto pattern -- payroll existed as a market, but making it accessible to every small business expanded the category dramatically. If Fed10 is actually building the Gusto of policy intelligence -- accessible, affordable, AI-native -- the market could be 5x what current estimates suggest.

The founder-market fit is the strongest signal in this dossier. All three co-founders passed legislation together before starting the company. The CEO was a gubernatorial appointee and active lobbyist while still at Harvard. The COO passed bills before voting age. This isn't a technical team that discovered GovTech during a pivot -- these are practitioners who lived inside the workflow they're automating. When I see builder-practitioners, I pay attention, because domain knowledge compounds in ways that technical capability alone doesn't. The pre-existing working relationship also reduces co-founder risk, which at pre-seed stage is one of the three things that kills companies.

Where I get stuck is on defensibility and the single-miracle test. The underlying legislative data is entirely public. The analysis layer is LLM-based -- accessible to any well-resourced team or incumbent. Fed10's miracle needs to be achieving consultant-grade accuracy in legislative analysis, which is a reasonable single miracle. But unlike Harvey, where the team could focus on workflow and trust because foundation model labs were delivering the accuracy miracle for them, Fed10's accuracy miracle is also being delivered by the same foundation models that FiscalNote, Quorum, and Plural Policy are plugging into. The miracle isn't unique to Fed10 -- it's available to every competitor simultaneously. That transforms the competitive question from "who can solve the hard technical problem?" to "who has the best distribution?" And on distribution, the incumbents start with a massive advantage. I don't see evidence that the founders have thought about distribution beyond their personal lobbying networks, and there's no traction data -- no revenue, no user counts, no LOIs -- to suggest they've validated demand beyond the YC application.

This is a competent team in a real market, but the market dynamics don't match my investment patterns. The "why now" is generic LLM improvement rather than a specific capability threshold unique to this domain. The competitive landscape is active and well-funded. And the technology moat is thin enough that this feels closer to "software-aware" than "software-driven" -- the AI is a tool applied to legislative analysis, but the underlying competitive advantage lives in domain expertise and distribution, not in compounding technical differentiation. I'd want to see evidence that Fed10 has found a wedge the incumbents can't easily replicate before writing a check.
