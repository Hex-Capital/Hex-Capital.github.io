# General Instinct -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

General Instinct is a technically literate AI-infrastructure team — Guanming Wang ex-DeepMind, Bill Jiao on Siemens' first multimodal foundation model — building exactly the kind of cross-silicon runtime layer the chipmakers monetize for themselves. The dimensional signature is high founder credibility paired with a category I have explicitly written off as "just more internet": General Instinct's Jetson Orin NX demo (111ms cold start, 100% inside a 150ms budget) is a real engineering artifact, but the asset class it lives in is the one I deselect by default.

Sublime Security is the right comparison on the non-consensus axis, because Sublime was a pre-consensus enterprise security check while every other security investor was crowding late-stage SOC SaaS. The shared mechanism would be: a category the consensus has wrong, entered at a price the consensus won't pay. General Instinct diverges sharply — "Deploy AI into any edge device" is the 2026 YC consensus pitch, not the contrarian one, and General Instinct's competitor list (Edge Impulse acquired by Qualcomm March 2025, Modular at $1.6B post on $380M raised) reads as a category the largest strategics have already decided is theirs to own. The $500K YC entry isn't a price discount, it's the SAFE every other P26 batchmate is taking, and PitchBook lists $500K against StartupHub.ai's conflicting $5M — even the funding figure is illegible in the wrong direction, ambiguity without optionality.

The Pass Example I keep in my head is the canonical "AI agent for [enterprise vertical], $25K MRR, $30M post, four tier-1s already in" — the failure mechanism is that the round is mispriced for what the pre-Series-A regatta actually looks like once the open-source tooling catches up. Does General Instinct exhibit that pattern? Partially. The round isn't yet consensus-priced and there is no tier-1 pile-on, so the price-discipline failure mode hasn't fully fired. But the underlying asset is exposed to the same commoditization curve: llama.cpp, ONNX Runtime, TensorRT-LLM, plus first-party compilers from NVIDIA, Apple, and Qualcomm covering every device General Instinct names as a target. That's not a venture moment; that's a tooling gap that closes on its own.

On capital structure (D2): General Instinct's mechanism over 18 months is straightforward and bad. The "model + device + latency budget → runtime" delivery requires deep certification work per silicon vendor, none of which compounds into the kind of revenue line that makes a Series A cheap. General Instinct will need three to five more rounds to clear the hardware-partner schleps NVIDIA and Qualcomm front-run with their own SDKs, and the cap table will dilute through every certification cycle. This is not Teamshares-shaped, it's not Mogilko-shaped, it's a standard infrastructure SAFE walking into a standard infrastructure burn.

On founder conviction (D3): the counterfactual is what would have to flip this tier. If Jiao or Wang had three years of public writing on edge inference economics — opinionated essays on why chipmaker SDKs structurally fail at frontier-model-class compression, ideally with benchmarks — General Instinct would score a full tier higher here. The tardigrade-protein patents Jiao filed as a high-school junior are the kind of weird-obsession signal I actually like; it's evidence of a temperament that runs at problems other people walk past. But no GitHub, no Twitter, no public writing on the category, and Wang's 30k Rednote following is a Chinese consumer-tech audience that does not transfer to selling runtime licenses to robotics OEMs in the US.

On founder-as-brand (D4): the single-anchor parallel is John Fish / Bookshelved, where I underwrote a creator with owned distribution into a literary product his audience actually wanted. General Instinct's distribution profile is the inverse — Wang's Rednote 30k is real reach but pointed at the wrong buyer, and B2B robotics procurement does not flow through tech-influencer audiences in any language. The cap table has no distribution asset on it.

My 36-month forecast for General Instinct: acqui-hire by NVIDIA, Qualcomm, or Modular at $8–20M for the engineering team, with the runtime IP folded into the acquirer's first-party stack and the investors taking a 1–2x outcome on paper. The mechanism is direct: General Instinct's Jetson Orin NX demo is genuinely impressive engineering, and that exact engineering is what NVIDIA wants in-house rather than as a third-party dependency on Jetson. The Edge Impulse exit to Qualcomm in March 2025 is the comp — $52.8M raised, modest disclosed terms, the runtime got absorbed by the silicon owner.

The strongest objection: physical AI is not generative AI, and the robotics foundation-model wave (VLA models, multimodal policies) genuinely needs a cross-silicon compression layer that no single chipmaker will build, because every robotics customer runs heterogeneous fleets — Jetson on the chassis, Snapdragon on the handheld, ARM on the sensor. If that's right, General Instinct sits at the switchpoint and becomes the runtime default before any chipmaker builds the cross-vendor version. It stings because Ro is precedent for a Known Exception where capital intensity and certification schleps were the moat rather than the failure mode. But Ro had 50-state prescription rails — a regulatory schlep no incumbent could shortcut. General Instinct's schleps are silicon-vendor relationships the silicon vendors themselves control, which inverts the Ro logic entirely. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: a signed, named multi-device deployment with a top-five robotics OEM (Figure, 1X, Agility, Boston Dynamics, Skydio) at a per-device license that NVIDIA's TensorRT cannot match on the same hardware. Absent that, General Instinct is a strong engineering team in a sleeve I don't buy at a price that has no discount.
