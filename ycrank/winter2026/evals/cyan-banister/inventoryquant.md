# InventoryQuant -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I want to understand about Sander Schulhoff: how does someone who built the first prompt engineering guide on the internet, trained three million people in AI, and won Best Theme Paper at EMNLP as its youngest recipient end up automating insurance contents inventories? That's not a rhetorical question -- it's the question I would literally ask him before anything else. Because his AI credentials are genuinely exceptional for a pre-seed founder. Creating LearnPrompting before ChatGPT launched shows real anticipatory instinct, and organizing a 20,000-participant red-teaming competition with OpenAI sponsorship demonstrates someone who can build community and execute at scale. But when I look for the biographical thread that connects *this person* to *this specific problem*, I can't find it anywhere in the public record. No family in the adjusting business, no personal experience with a devastating claim, no years spent watching the inefficiency from the inside. The domain appears to have been selected analytically rather than lived. And in my experience, analytical selection doesn't produce the same survival-level commitment when the company hits a wall -- which it will, because insurance always puts you through a wall.

The product itself is perfectly sensible, and that's actually what concerns me. When someone tells me "we use AI to automate a manual workflow in insurance," nobody recoils, nobody says it's crazy, nobody passes because it sounds too weird. They nod and say "that makes sense." Compare that to the reaction when I first heard about mining Bitcoin on gas flares in North Dakota, or building a male contraceptive gel, or making a company that lets anyone summon a car with their phone when that was illegal in most cities. Those ideas made people uncomfortable. They were difficult to assess. InventoryQuant is easy to assess -- and that's precisely why Verisk, a public company that already serves 22 of the top 25 U.S. property insurers, has announced XactAI to integrate AI across their entire claims ecosystem including XactContents. When the incumbent can see the same opportunity you can, and they already own the customer relationships, the workflow integration, and the regulatory expertise, you need something deeply proprietary or deeply weird to outrun them. I don't see either here. The core technical stack -- speech-to-text, LLM extraction, retail price lookup -- is built on generally available capabilities.

I want to be honest about the bull case, though, because I've learned not to dismiss smart founders too quickly. If Sander brings the same intensity he brought to LearnPrompting -- where he educated three million people and attracted corporate clients like Microsoft, Deloitte, and OpenAI -- he could move faster than Verisk's bureaucracy allows them to. The one named customer, Goodman-Gable-Gould, with a testimonial from their CFO saying it "already saved us entire days worth of time" after just two months, is a real signal at pre-seed. Insurance is notoriously slow to adopt technology, and a fast, AI-native tool that exports directly into XactContents format could spread through the adjusting community before Verisk's internal AI initiative gets through their own product cycle. The narrow wedge into contents specifically -- not trying to replace Xactimate entirely -- is strategically smart. If Sander accumulates enough structured item-price data from processed claims, that could become a proprietary dataset no one else has. The 100+ language support could open up markets where non-English-speaking policyholders are underserved by existing tools. These are real possibilities. But they require a founder who will persist through the specific grind of enterprise insurance sales, compliance requirements, and relationship-building that has nothing to do with AI research. I need to see evidence that this founder has that persistence *for this domain*, and I don't have it.

On the economic access dimension -- which matters deeply to me -- I see a gap. When I backed Uber, the economic access story was immediate and visceral: drivers earning income, riders getting affordable transportation. With Affirm, consumers got transparent credit instead of hidden fees. Here, the primary beneficiary of automation is the adjuster and the insurance company, not the policyholder who just lost their home in a fire. There's an indirect argument that faster claims processing helps those policyholders, and the multilingual support could genuinely help non-English speakers navigate a process that currently excludes them. But the product as described is a B2B efficiency tool for industry professionals, not a tool that directly empowers the underserved. It doesn't make an invisible system visible to the people trapped inside it -- it makes an inefficient system faster for the people running it.

As a solo founder entering a relationship-driven, compliance-heavy industry without visible domain expertise or an insurance-experienced co-founder or advisor, Sander faces the kind of challenge where I'd want to see someone beside him who knows where the bodies are buried. My HQ Trivia lesson taught me about co-founder conflict, but the flip side is that solo founders in domains they're learning from scratch carry a different risk -- they don't have anyone to tell them what they don't know. Insurance is one of those industries where the unwritten rules matter as much as the written ones.
