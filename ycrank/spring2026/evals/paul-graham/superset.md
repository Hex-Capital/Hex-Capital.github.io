# Superset -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Kiet Ho built Onlook to 25,600 GitHub stars, and now Superset has 9,900 stars roughly two months after its Product Hunt launch on February 27, 2026. That's a genuinely rare ability — shipping things developers want badly enough to star, fork, and file 387 issues on. When I found Replit on Hacker News in 2017, what caught my attention was exactly this pattern: a technical builder making tools for other builders, and the community responding with obvious enthusiasm. Superset fits squarely in my "hackers building for hackers" category — three ex-YC CTOs (Kiet Ho from Onlook W25, Satya Patel from Untether Labs W23, Avi Peltz from Adam W25) building a developer tool they presumably needed themselves. That pattern has produced some of my best investments. But the pattern alone isn't sufficient. The question is whether Superset is Stripe — tackling an unglamorous problem everyone else avoids — or whether it's something closer to the tenth company in a hot space where all the returns have already been competed away.

Superset's competitive landscape answers that question uncomfortably. Conductor raised a $22M Series A from Spark and Matrix. Composio has $29M in total funding from Lightspeed. Cmux hit 9,500 GitHub stars in two weeks. All of them launched in early 2026, all targeting the same insight: developers need to run multiple AI coding agents in parallel with Git worktree isolation. When I see four companies materializing simultaneously around the same concept, I know I'm looking at a consensus idea. The way I think about this is through what I called "Black Swan Farming" — the best startup investments initially look wrong to most people. Superset looks right to everyone. Every VC who reads "IDE for the AI Agents Era" nods along. The TAM slides write themselves. That's precisely the pattern I've learned to distrust. The idea that agent orchestration is valuable is about as contrarian as saying the internet will be important. Superset's agent-agnostic, open-source approach is the smartest strategic position within the space — but being the smartest player in a consensus market still means you're competing against $51M in direct competitor funding for a concept (worktree isolation plus parallel sessions) that is technically straightforward to replicate.

Kiet Ho, Satya Patel, and Avi Peltz have the backgrounds I'd want for this kind of product — Patel did engineering stints at Google, Amazon, and Facebook before becoming a YC CTO, and Peltz co-founded BioGlyph and served as CTO at Adam. But what I can't find in the dossier is the thing I trust most: stories. When I evaluate founders, I'm not reading résumés, I'm asking them what happened when something went sideways. The Airbnb founders sold cereal boxes when they were broke. Patrick Collison built and sold Auctomatic as a teenager before tackling payments. Superset's founders have strong credentials and shipping velocity — version 1.5.8 in eight weeks is genuinely fast — but I don't have a single anecdote about creative problem-solving under constraint. The credentials suggest competence; the shipping speed suggests intensity. But the animal quality I look for reveals itself in specific narratives of resourcefulness, and those are absent here. The open role at $175K–$225K plus 1–2% equity suggests they're building conventionally, not scrappily.

The organic problem discovery question is where Superset sits in an ambiguous zone. Three ex-YC CTOs building developer tools were almost certainly heavy users of Claude Code, Codex, and similar agents during their previous companies. The frustration of running agents sequentially is real — I've experienced it myself. So I'll grant that the problem likely emerged from lived experience rather than market research. But the dossier offers no specific origin moment, no "I was running three Claude Code sessions and realized I'd wasted forty minutes waiting" story. When Drew Houston built Dropbox, the USB drive story was the first thing he told you. Organic discovery produces vivid narratives because the frustration was personal. Superset's founding story reads more like three technically excellent people recognizing an opportunity at the intersection of their skills and market timing. That's not bad — it's just not the signal I weight most heavily.

The platform risk is the bear case that should give any investor pause. Superset derives its entire value from orchestrating third-party agents — Claude Code, Codex, Gemini CLI. If Anthropic ships native parallelism into Claude Code (which they're clearly capable of doing), or if OpenAI builds multi-agent support directly into Codex, Superset's orchestration layer becomes redundant overnight. The logos on Superset's website — Microsoft, OpenAI, Netflix, Google, Amazon — are impressive traction signals, but those same companies build their own internal tooling when a category matures. The bull case would say that Superset's agent-agnostic neutrality makes it the Switzerland of agent orchestration, valuable precisely because no single agent provider can serve this role. That's plausible if the market fragments across many agent providers. But if one or two agents dominate (which is the historical pattern in developer tools), the dominant provider absorbs the orchestration function, and Switzerland gets annexed.

Superset's strongest argument for investment is the Replit parallel: Kiet Ho has proven, twice now, that he can build open-source developer tools that attract massive community adoption. Combined GitHub stars across Onlook and Superset exceed 35,000. Garry Tan personally listed as a Product Hunt maker. The team's iteration speed — eight releases in eight weeks, 817 forks enabling community contribution — suggests a founder learning rate well above baseline. If this were a novel problem space with less competition, I'd be writing a check. But the combination of consensus enthusiasm (everyone thinks multi-agent orchestration is important), well-funded direct competitors (Conductor and Composio with a combined $51M), low technical moat (worktree isolation is a Git primitive, not proprietary technology), and severe platform dependency (upstream agents could absorb this functionality) makes Superset a bet on execution speed in a race where several well-capitalized runners started at the same time. The condition that would flip me to Invest: evidence that Superset has meaningful paid conversion — even $5K MRR from the $15/user/month Pro tier — proving that developers will pay for orchestration rather than using free alternatives or waiting for native agent parallelism. Revenue would signal that the orchestration layer has durable value independent of what the upstream agents do.
