# qomplement -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that jumps out at me about qomplement is that this is one of the most crowded, consensus-validated spaces in the entire current AI wave. Document processing. AI agents. Enterprise automation. Rossum has $111 million and $45 million in revenue. Reducto just raised $108 million from a16z. Nanonets has $42 million and 10,000 customers. When I look at a landscape like that and see a pre-seed company claiming differentiation because they do "filling" instead of "extraction," my instinct is to ask: how long before any of those incumbents adds a "fill this form" button? Probably one engineering sprint. The most promising companies I've backed looked ridiculous to other investors -- SpaceX mining the sky, Crusoe burning waste gas for computation. Nobody was competing because nobody believed the category existed. Here, everybody believes the category exists. They're just fighting over slices of it. That's the opposite of the signal I trust.

I wanted to hear the founders' stories, and what I found was mostly silence. Kerim Taray has a CS degree from Tecnológico de Monterrey, built a bootstrapped nearshoring marketplace called Growsphere, and worked at Contalink -- a Mexican cloud accounting platform. He calls himself a "contrarian" on his personal website. I've learned to be cautious when someone labels themselves that way, because the genuinely unconventional founders I've backed -- the ones building male contraceptive gels or private rockets -- never needed to announce it. They just showed up with an obsession that didn't fit anywhere else. I don't see the biographical thread that connects Kerim to document filling with the kind of intensity that carries a company through inevitable crises. And the CTO, Andres Garza Garcia, is essentially invisible -- no public LinkedIn, no GitHub, no background information at all. My entire evaluation process starts with "tell me your story," and when there's no story to hear, I lose my primary navigation instrument.

The bull case here would require me to believe that "filling" versus "extraction" is a genuinely distinct product category -- that enterprises have such complex, idiosyncratic internal templates that mapping data into them constitutes a defensible workflow layer. If qomplement builds deep template libraries customized to individual enterprise document ecosystems, switching costs could emerge over time. The Contalink adjacency gives Kerim some real exposure to accounting document workflows, and the Monterrey talent network could be an efficiency advantage. If I squint, I can see a version of this where they become the connective tissue between extraction APIs and enterprise back-offices, owned by nobody else because nobody else thought the filling step was worth building a company around. But I'd need to see evidence that this workflow bottleneck is real and painful enough to support a standalone product -- signed LOIs, customer conversations, any signal that enterprises are actively struggling with this specific step rather than just absorbing it into their existing document processing stack. I don't see that evidence.

What I don't see at all is economic access or impact on underserved populations. This is enterprise efficiency tooling for accounts payable departments -- it makes back-office workers faster at their existing jobs. That's fine, but it doesn't create income for people who lack it, doesn't make an opaque system transparent, doesn't unlock opportunity where none existed before. When I backed Uber, the insight wasn't just logistics optimization -- it was that hundreds of thousands of people could earn a living with their cars. When I backed Affirm, it was about making credit terms honest for people who'd been exploited by hidden fees. Document filling for procurement teams doesn't carry that kind of meaning, and meaning is what sustains me through the inevitable moments when a company needs more than capital.

The co-founder dynamic is hard to evaluate with so little information. Both founders likely share a Monterrey connection, which could mean a deep, tested relationship -- or it could mean they're acquaintances who teamed up for YC. I learned the hard way with HQ Trivia that even the most promising product can't survive founder misalignment, and when I can't assess how co-founders handle conflict, I have to discount accordingly. A team of four with an invisible CTO is a question mark, not a red flag, but it leaves me without the conviction I need to write a check from my own money.
