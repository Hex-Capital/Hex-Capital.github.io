# Cosmic Robotics -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Cosmic Robotics is a deep-operator hardware bet with the wrong-shaped cap table for a Slow check — James Emerick's four-generation construction family plus ex-Built Robotics tenure is exactly the missionary founder profile I underwrite, but Terabase Energy already sitting on $130M against Cosmic Robotics's $4M pre-seed tells me the regatta ahead needs seven tacks I can't underwrite at seed. That mismatch — high founder-conviction paired with a capital-structure that resembles a defense-tech Series B rehearsal — is the whole story of this evaluation.

On founder conviction, my anchor is my own **Drop.io** arc: I bootstrapped a file-sharing primitive against free incumbents because I'd lived the problem and wouldn't be talked out of it, and the missionary-temperament criterion I later formalized is essentially "did the founder work on this before the round existed?" Emerick clears that filter in a way most YC applicants don't — Built Robotics field time, Autodesk Research adaptive-robotics work, and literal generational construction inheritance means Cosmic Robotics isn't an opportunity chase, it's a return to family category. Where Cosmic Robotics diverges from the Drop.io analogue is the absence of *artifacts*: no Twitter, no GitHub, no essays on why utility-scale solar labor economics require a mobile-robot rather than Terabase's tent-factory. The conviction is real, but it lives in the resume, not in public writing — and I score conviction I can read higher than conviction I have to infer from a LinkedIn.

Founder-as-brand is where Cosmic Robotics collapses, and the pattern-match to my **John Fish / Bookshelved** check is instructive by inversion — Fish arrived with owned distribution that made the venture-shaped instrument possible before the company existed, whereas Cosmic Robotics arrives with a founder pair whose LinkedIn follower counts weren't even retrievable in the dossier. That's not a failure of Emerick or Jones; B2B hardware doesn't need creator distribution. But it does mean the "founder is the asset" override I'd use to justify an oddly-structured entry doesn't exist here. Cosmic Robotics is a company-shaped asset that will only ever be legible as a standard equity cap table, which forecloses the instrument-design edge I use to get contrarian entries done.

On capital structure — the criterion I evaluate first — Cosmic Robotics is closer to the generic hard-tech factory model than to **Teamshares**'s employee-equity rollup or **Ro**'s regulatory-moat exception. The **Ro** override is worth stress-testing: Ro was capital-intensive because 50-state prescription rails were a schlep only VC dollars could clear, and physical field-deployed robotics on harsh utility sites is superficially the same argument. But Ro's moat was legal-regulatory; Cosmic Robotics's moat is CapEx-vs-CapEx, and CapEx-vs-CapEx against Terabase's $130M raise ($130M / $4M ≈ 30x capital delta) is the losing side of the trade at pre-seed pricing. Over 18 months this compounds against Cosmic Robotics: every Terabase field-factory milestone raises the marginal cost of Cosmic Robotics's Series A because LPs will underwrite the leader, not the challenger, in a capital-intensity fight.

Round pricing at $4M with Giant Ventures leading and Azeem Azhar angel-participating isn't a hot-YC-consensus round in the "five tier-1s bidding" sense that would trigger my automatic deselection — solar-installation robotics is genuinely off the AI-agent consensus lane. To flip Cosmic Robotics into an actual mis-priced non-consensus tier I'd need one of two things: either a tier-1 like Founders Fund or Lux visibly passed at this price (dossier is silent), or the round terms include something instrument-unusual — royalty on installed panels, structured EPC revenue share, DOE-tied non-dilutive stack that materially reduces equity dilution. Cosmic Robotics has the DOE American-Made Solar Prize and JLL Foundation grant, which is real non-dilutive money, but the equity round itself reads as a vanilla SAFE.

Category-sleeve fit is the counterfactual that most cleanly explains my hesitation — Cosmic Robotics isn't in a revealed-preference Slow sleeve. It's not pre-consensus crypto like **Aleo** or **Lightning Labs**, not DTC-consumer like **Allbirds**, not creator like **Bookshelved**, not employee-equity like **Teamshares**. Hard-tech robotics for solar construction plus lunar adjacency is a Lux Capital / Eclipse Ventures shape, not a Slow shape. That doesn't make Cosmic Robotics a bad company; it makes Cosmic Robotics a company I have no natural edge underwriting, which for my check size and pricing discipline means the alpha has to come from somewhere else — and I can't locate it in this dossier.

My 36-month forecast: Cosmic Robotics raises a $25-40M Series A led by a hard-tech fund (Lux, Eclipse, or Playground Global) at ~$100-150M post-money on the strength of Emerick's Built Robotics pedigree and 3-5 additional EPC certified-operator deals beyond SunRobi, reaching $2-6M ARR from robotics-as-a-service licensing, but remains 4-6 rounds from default-alive because Terabase's Terafab V2 will have taken the largest utility-EPC accounts and Cosmic Robotics will be fighting for the mid-market tier. The mechanism producing this: Emerick's founder-market fit clears the Series A bar; Terabase's capital lead caps the Series A price.

The strongest bull argument against this pass invokes the **Ro** exception directly — regulatory and physical-schlep moats justify capital intensity when only VC dollars clear the schlep, and construction-robotics field data across "Earth's harshest worksites" is genuinely an accumulating dataset moat that Terabase's tent-factory approach doesn't build. If Cosmic Robotics's per-panel install rate stays at 30-40 seconds on graded and sloped utility terrain where Terafab V2 physically cannot ship a factory, then Cosmic Robotics owns the sub-segment Terabase can't reach, and the capital-delta becomes irrelevant. That case stings — it's the real steel-man. What defeats it in this dossier is that the terrain-adjusted throughput evidence doesn't exist yet ("tens of thousands of panels" is unqualified by site profile), and the sole certified operator SunRobi/Sunstall is customer-concentration risk that reads as GTM fragility, not sub-segment ownership. **Flip condition:** a signed multi-EPC certified-operator roster (3+ named installers beyond SunRobi) plus published throughput data on sloped/rocky sites where Terafab cannot deploy — that combination reframes Cosmic Robotics as a **Ro**-shaped venture moment and I re-underwrite at Series A.
