# Andco -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Andco's founders dressed as DoorDash drivers carrying donut boxes into personal injury firms until 200 boxes in they had customers — that single fact, more than anything else in the dossier, tells me what kind of company Andco is: a hustler-operator team attacking a real schlep with a thin hacker spine. The highest signal on my ladder is animal resourcefulness; the lowest is hacker genealogy. That pairing isn't Stripe (hackers who happened to find a schlep), and it isn't the polished AI-wrapper MBA team I'd pass on. It's closer to early Airbnb — except Brian Chesky was a designer who lived in the apartment, and Ryan Xue is a McKinsey-and-Google guy who showed up at law firms in a delivery vest. Different texture, same posture.

Airbnb (2009) is the ladder rung Andco's pre-funding stunt actually loads onto. The Obama O's worked as a signal not because cereal scales but because anyone who can convince Americans to pay $40 for $4 of cereal can probably convince strangers to sleep in each other's homes. The donut-and-Dashers stunt at Andco is structurally the same trick: PI firms don't take cold meetings, so Ryan Xue and his cofounders found the one disguise that walks past a receptionist unchallenged. That's not "creative GTM" — that's the animal test from "What We Look for in Founders" (October 2010) executed in costume. Where Andco diverges from the Airbnb rung is that the donuts produced customers, not financing; the $12M oversubscribed pre-seed came from YC and A.Capital rather than from the stunt itself, so the stunt is conviction evidence but not capital evidence. Half a cereal box.

Reddit (2005) is the rung I keep looking for and not finding on Andco's hacker-genealogy axis: Aaron Swartz co-authored the RSS spec before the company existed, which is the kind of public hacker tribal marker I over-index on. Andco's dossier has the inverted profile — Ryan Xue (Cornell, McKinsey, Google policy fellow), Michael Lemm (Harvard Law, Kirkland & Ellis), and David Lee listed as 2x CTO with no GitHub repos found, no Twitter footprint, no open-source trail. The CTO's hacker lineage at Andco is asserted by title and not by artifact, which is exactly the failure mode I described in my Pass Example. The product works — "hundreds of cases and thousands of requests each month" per the YC LinkedIn post — so somebody on Andco's team is shipping. But the dossier reads like a McKinsey-Harvard-Law company with a CTO bolted on, and the absence of a hacker trail is itself a signal in my framework. Animals leave tracks.

Schlep blindness — my own essay from January 2012 — is where Andco scores almost as high as anything in the recent batch. EvenUp ($385M raised, $2B valuation) and Supio chose the clean half of the PI workflow: documents already in hand, demand packages out the other side. Andco took the upstream half nobody wanted: fax machines, postal mail, voice calls to insurance adjusters, HIPAA exposure, state-by-state unauthorized-practice-of-law risk. That is the Coinbase (2012) pattern verbatim — Brian Armstrong's compliance grind was a moat because every other YC applicant in his batch unconsciously routed around it. Andco's fax-and-voice agent layer is the same shape of moat at the same stage. The risk section flagging UPL and HIPAA isn't a bug in the dossier; it's the asset.

Idea organicity is where Andco's score gets pinned down rather than up. The Dropbox (2007) test is whether the founder personally lost the USB stick; the Andco test is whether anyone on the team personally retrieved a police report at 4pm via fax. Michael Lemm practiced at Kirkland & Ellis, which is BigLaw — not a plaintiff PI firm doing intake-to-demand workups. The founder-pain link is adjacent rather than lived, and adjacent is what trips the Pass Example default. Lemm being a real lawyer pulls Andco above the MBA-with-a-deck baseline, but neither he nor Xue forgot a medical record on the way to filing. The idea came from observation, not from the USB stick.

Cofounder configuration is a counterfactual exercise. Ryan Xue and Michael Lemm previously co-founded Layup (W23) and exited to Whatfix — a working relationship documented across two YC batches and one acquisition. That's the Kan-and-Shear pattern from Kiko (2005) into Twitch (2007): prior YC failure or modest exit compounds rather than disqualifies. For this dimension to flip up a tier I'd want David Lee to have built something with the other two before P26; the dossier doesn't show it, so the trio is really a tested pair plus a third. That's why this lands above the default but not at the top rung.

My 36-month forecast for Andco: $6-15M ARR on plaintiff PI workup, acquired by Filevine or EvenUp before reaching Series B, settling at a $150-400M outcome. The mechanism is the dimensional signature — Andco's hustler-with-thin-hacker-spine profile builds revenue fast but produces a company that gets rolled up by an incumbent with deeper technical leverage rather than becoming the category-defining standalone. The donut boxes get them to ARR; the missing GitHub trail caps the ceiling.

The opposing case that stings: Layup → Whatfix is itself a Kiko-shaped exit, and my own Red Flags list calls out "acquihire-coded founders" as a tell of insufficient ambition. Xue and Lemm just did the modest-exit dance once; predicting they'll do it again at slightly larger scale isn't a stretch — it's a base rate. The counter is that Kan and Shear sold Kiko on eBay and came back for Twitch, and I funded them anyway because prior failure compounded hustle. Andco's donut stunt is more animal than anything in the Layup story I've seen, and the schlep zone they picked is too unpleasant for the EvenUps of the world to backfill quickly. The single condition that flips me to Pass: if I learn that the multi-channel agent layer (fax, mail, voice) is mostly humans-in-the-loop offshore rather than agents — that turns Andco from a schlep moat into a BPO with a Filevine integration, and the comp set becomes outsourcing shops, not Coinbase.
