# Adialante -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I look at here isn't the technology -- it's the competitive landscape, because it tells me everything about whether this market window is actually nonobvious. Promaxo already has FDA-cleared in-office prostate MRI with 69 systems deployed and $63-81M in funding. Hyperfine is a public company shipping portable MRI with $20-22M in FY26 revenue guidance. Chipiron just raised $17M for an alternative low-cost approach. Siemens launched Free.Max. When I count four well-funded players already in a market, the "nonobvious" thesis is dead on arrival. Low-cost MRI access is a known, active investment category -- not a structural opening that consensus has missed. The guideline shift toward MRI before prostate biopsy is real, but it's been recognized and capitalized by competitors who are already generating revenue. Compare this to when I wrote a check for Coinbase in 2012 -- nobody else was touching crypto infrastructure. Here, everybody is touching portable MRI. The timing alpha has been captured by earlier entrants.

The miracle count is what really kills this for me. Adialante needs: (1) the FREE technology to produce clinically adequate prostate images at low field strength -- novel physics that has been published but not validated at the clinical quality bar required for cancer screening; (2) FDA clearance for a fundamentally new MRI physics approach, which is not a standard 510(k) pathway when you've eliminated gradient coils entirely; (3) mobile operations competency -- fleet management, staffing, multi-state regulatory compliance, scheduling logistics -- a completely separate operational domain from MRI physics; and (4) enough competitive velocity to build market share against Promaxo, which is installing systems right now and accumulating the clinical evidence that drives physician adoption. That's at least three independent breakthroughs, and I've seen this pattern enough times to know that compounding small probabilities produces very small numbers. I made an exception for Anduril because the structural market opening was generational -- bipartisan consensus on defense modernization is a once-in-a-decade catalyst. The prostate MRI access gap, while meaningful, is an incremental market expansion being addressed by multiple well-funded teams. It doesn't justify overriding the multi-miracle concern.

The strongest bull case would require believing that FREE technology is so fundamentally superior -- 1/10th the cost and size, silent operation, no gradient coils -- that it leapfrogs everything Promaxo and Hyperfine have built. If the physics works at clinical quality and the FDA pathway is cleaner than I'm assuming, the per-scan mobile model does solve a real structural problem: it eliminates the capital expenditure barrier for urology clinics and creates a business model conflict for incumbents like Siemens and GE who can't offer per-scan pricing without cannibalizing their $1-3M hardware sales. The team's credentials are genuinely strong -- Torres invented the technology at one of the world's top MRI research centers, and having an ISMRM Rabi Award winner as principal systems engineer is a real signal about the caliber of the physics work. If I had conviction that FDA clearance was achievable within 18-24 months and the technology produced diagnostic-quality prostate images, the mobile model creates a distribution wedge that's actually quite clever. But "if the physics works AND the FDA clears it AND they can run a mobile fleet" is exactly the multi-miracle bet I filter against.

I also note that this is fundamentally a hardware-plus-services business, not an infrastructure play. Clinics pay a per-scan fee for a mobile MRI truck to show up -- there's no API, no developer ecosystem, no platform that other businesses build into their workflows. The switching costs come from clinical habit and convenience, not from deep technical integration. This is closer to a mobile imaging services company with proprietary hardware than to the infrastructure-layer companies where I concentrate my portfolio. Stripe became the payments API for an entire generation of internet companies. Adialante becomes a vendor you call when you need a scan. The per-scan model has reasonable retention dynamics if the service works, but hardware manufacturing costs plus mobile operations overhead will compress margins below what I'd expect from a software infrastructure business. No pricing data is available, so I'm estimating, but the operational cost structure of maintaining and deploying mobile MRI units with trained operators is materially different from a software company's marginal cost of serving another customer.

The founders have deep domain expertise -- PhDs from UMN's CMRR, which is world-class for MRI research -- but no visible commercial or startup experience. Every founding team member comes from academia. Building an MRI scanner in a lab and building a mobile healthcare services company that navigates FDA clearance, fleet logistics, and physician sales are fundamentally different challenges. I've seen this transition fail enough times that the absence of any operator with commercial healthcare experience on the team is a meaningful gap, not just a nice-to-have.
