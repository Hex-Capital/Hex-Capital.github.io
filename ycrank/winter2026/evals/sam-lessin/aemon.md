# Aemon -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's my core problem with Aemon: the primary marketing claim is "we beat Google DeepMind's AlphaEvolve on circle packing with under ten dollars of compute." That's technically impressive. It's also the most terrifying sentence a seed-stage startup can utter, because what it really says is "our competitor is Google DeepMind." When I invested in Venmo, the insight wasn't "we're faster than Visa" -- it was that peer-to-peer mobile payments were a genuinely new primitive that incumbents didn't recognize as a category. Aemon is operating in a category that DeepMind literally created and named. Google has infinite compute, the best AI researchers on earth, and an enterprise sales machine called Google Cloud. The structural position here is a 3-person team racing the most well-resourced research lab in history on their home turf. That's not a sane-person-insane-idea pairing. That's a sane person with a very sane idea that happens to be suicidal at this scale.

The founders -- twin brothers, ICLR and EMNLP publications before age 20, international math and robotics medals -- are clearly talented. I respect the research depth. Richard Zhou's role as Founder in Residence at Afore Capital shows some startup ecosystem awareness. The twin dynamic actually appeals to me; deep relational trust between co-founders matters enormously, and you can't get deeper than twins who've been publishing together since their teens. But research brilliance and operator credibility are different things, and at 20 with no prior company, no enterprise sales experience, and no demonstrated ability to build an organization, the "sane" credential is narrowly technical rather than broadly operational. The "rizz and tizz" test requires both -- I see potential tizz in the research intensity but no evidence of either trait at the company-building level yet.

Where this falls apart structurally is my AI kill test, except inverted. Normally I ask "does AI commoditize this business?" With Aemon, AI doesn't commoditize the business -- AI IS the business. Strip out the AI and there is nothing. No physical operations, no regulatory position, no installed hardware, no protocol that other businesses build on. This is pure software built on top of other companies' foundation models. The evolutionary code generation approach is already published, already replicated in open source (OpenEvolve on GitHub), and already being pursued by Sakana AI's ShinkaEvolve at ICLR 2026. The technique is a method, not a moat. My Craftwork thesis -- software amplifies physical-world operations that competitors can't replicate -- runs in the exact opposite direction from what Aemon is building. As LLMs get better and cheaper, the barrier to building evolutionary search systems drops for everyone simultaneously. The company's defensibility has to come from orchestration, evaluation frameworks, and accumulated domain knowledge, but none of that is proven and all of it sounds like what every AI startup claims as their future flywheel.

The bull case I have to honestly engage with: DeepMind's organizational DNA genuinely makes it bad at productizing tools for external enterprise customers. AlphaEvolve is internal and may stay internal. The commercial gap is real -- R&D teams at Fortune 500 companies need this capability today and can't call up DeepMind to buy it. If Aemon moves fast enough to lock in enterprise relationships and build domain-specific solution libraries before AlphaEvolve gets commercialized or open-source alternatives mature, there's a wedge. The legal retrieval case study (Recall@10 jumping from 0.540 to 0.912) shows the system works on practical problems, not just math benchmarks. And the competitive landscape, while terrifying in absolute terms, has meaningful segmentation: Cognition targets software engineers, Lila Sciences targets wet labs, Periodic Labs targets physical sciences. Nobody is selling an "AI research engineer for software optimization" as a standalone product today. That's a genuine gap. If these founders have the operational chops to build an enterprise product and sales motion fast enough, the timing window could work. But "if" is doing enormous load-bearing work in that sentence.

The "idea" here doesn't pass my non-consensus test. AI automating R&D is possibly the most consensus thesis in technology right now. Lila Sciences raised $550 million. Periodic Labs raised $300 million. Cognition raised over $900 million. When every sophisticated investor on the planet wants exposure to "AI for research," the narrative premium has been fully priced. I'm looking for businesses that other investors think are crazy -- painting companies, decentralized wireless networks, employee ownership of small businesses. An AI R&D agent operating in the hottest investment category of 2025-2026, competing against billion-dollar companies and the world's premier research lab, with no revenue, no physical moat, and pure software differentiation? That's the exact opposite of where my framework points me.
