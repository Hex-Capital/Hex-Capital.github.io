# Valgo -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

These founders wrote the actual textbook. Not metaphorically -- "Algorithms for Validation," MIT Press, co-authored by Moss and Katz, taught as the Stanford course AA228V. Robert Moss spent seven years on the team that built the FAA-certified ACAS Xa collision avoidance system, which is now a worldwide standard for aircraft safety. Three Stanford PhDs from the same lab, all publishing on the exact problem their company solves. On the "sane" axis of my framework, this might be the most credible founder-domain pairing I've seen at pre-seed. The question is whether the idea is sufficiently insane.

And here's where it gets interesting: proposing that the insurance industry -- one of the most actuarially conservative industries in human civilization -- should price policies based on *simulated* loss data rather than historical claims data is, in fact, a pretty wild proposition. Insurance has run on centuries of "things happened, we counted them, we priced accordingly." Valgo is saying: for autonomous systems, things haven't happened yet, so we'll model what *would* happen and you should trust that enough to write policies. Most insurers hear that and their compliance departments have a collective aneurysm. Most VCs hear "insurtech for autonomous vehicles" and fall asleep. That combination -- technically brilliant founders proposing something that makes both incumbents and investors uncomfortable -- starts to look like the sweet spot I'm scanning for.

What I notice that a generic analyst might miss is the protocol-layer positioning. Valgo isn't writing insurance policies (that's Koop's play as an MGA). They're building the risk quantification *infrastructure* that sits between autonomy companies and insurers -- an API that transforms simulation data into actuarially usable loss estimates. If this becomes the standard, every autonomous truck company, every robotics manufacturer, every drone fleet plugs into Valgo to get insured. That's a primitive, not an application. It reminds me structurally of what Venmo was doing for peer-to-peer payments in 2009 -- creating a new transactional layer that didn't exist before, one that other economic activity eventually routes through. The data flywheel is real too: each new customer's simulation data improves the models, making estimates more accurate, creating switching costs for both sides. That's compounding network effects in an infrastructure position.

Now let me run my AI kill test. Does cheaper software destroy this business? No -- and this is important. The moat isn't a software feature that an LLM can replicate. The moat is domain expertise in safety-critical systems validation (try getting an LLM to replicate seven years of FAA certification work), regulatory relationships with insurers who need to trust your models enough to file rates with state commissioners, and accumulated proprietary simulation-to-loss data across customers. AI actually makes Valgo *better* -- better probabilistic models, faster simulation processing -- without enabling competitors, because competitors lack the domain credibility and the regulatory trust. This is exactly the "cherry on top" pattern I look for: a business that would be valuable if AI didn't exist, where AI non-obviously accelerates the core proposition.

The bear case is the dual-sided market problem, and I take it seriously. You need autonomy companies to share proprietary simulation data (competitive sensitivity is real -- Waymo isn't rushing to hand over failure mode data) AND you need insurers to accept simulation-derived models (state insurance commissioners have never seen a rate filing based on simulated losses). That's a genuine chicken-and-egg problem in two deeply conservative industries simultaneously. Koop Technologies has a four-year head start with $7M and a Lloyd's partnership, and they've chosen vertical integration -- controlling the whole stack from data to policy. If Koop wins the early autonomy companies, Valgo's infrastructure play might get boxed out before it reaches critical mass. Timing risk is also real: if AV deployment stalls due to regulatory setbacks or a high-profile accident, the market window could stay narrow for years. And there's zero revenue signal -- no customers, no LOIs, no traction beyond YC's $500K.

But if I'm honest about what makes this interesting from my seat: the founder credibility is exceptional, the infrastructure positioning is structurally sound, and the narrative can expand from AVs to all autonomous systems to potentially any novel risk category where historical data doesn't exist. The initial market is unglamorous and technically forbidding -- exactly the kind of space where most VCs pass and the few who get in early capture outsized returns. The Teamshares comparison resonates: buying hundreds of small businesses through employee ownership sounded operationally absurd, yet the founders' specific credibility made it tractable. Here, building actuarial infrastructure for a category with zero claims history sounds impossible, yet these founders literally invented the algorithms to do it. That's the pairing I'm looking for. I'd want to dig deeper on the Jon Qian insurance credentials (unverified beyond company description) and on whether they have any LOIs or pilot agreements with either autonomy companies or insurers before writing a check.
