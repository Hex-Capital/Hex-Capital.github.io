# Prism -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Juicebox AI hit an $850M valuation in March 2026. Moonhub got acquired by Salesforce in June 2025. Dover raised $23M. HeyMilo raised $6.25M. Prism walks into this market with zero public traction, zero documented technical differentiation, and a name shared by at least five other companies in recruiting. The dossier itself states what I would have concluded independently: "AI-based resume screening is technically reproducible" and "LLM-based screening can be built on commodity foundation models." Prism is entering a consolidating market with a commodity technology approach. I have seen this pattern hundreds of times. The outcome is a company that fights for share of existing recruiting budgets, generates moderate revenue, and produces 3-5x returns for its investors — which in my framework is indistinguishable from losing money, because it consumes the same attention and opportunity cost as a bet that could return 50x.

Prism's consequence magnitude if fully successful is the dimension that kills this for me. If Prism perfectly executes — replaces every recruiting agency with AI agents, captures the entire $1.3T global recruiting spend — what changes? Companies pay less to hire people. That is a cost optimization for businesses. It does not change how a single human being lives their daily life. Nobody wakes up in a different world because their employer's recruiting costs dropped. Compare this to Commonwealth Fusion Systems, where success means limitless clean energy, or Impossible Foods, where success means feeding billions without animal agriculture. Prism's ceiling is a profitable B2B company. My red flag list includes "targeting redistribution of existing spend rather than creation of new demand," and Prism matches it precisely. The recruiting market exists today, Prism wants a slice of it tomorrow, and the world looks approximately identical either way.

Theo Kitsberg studied philosophy and psychology at Cambridge, did HCI research at MIT Media Lab, and built a study app called Grappl that he later retired. Axel La Pira is a third-year Oxford CS and philosophy student who worked as a CEO Founder Associate at Alan. Both are young, interdisciplinary, and from elite institutions — and I respect that profile. The early support from Eoghan McCabe, CEO of Intercom, suggests someone with real judgment saw something in these founders. The rebrand from HireGlide to Prism within a single YC batch could indicate rapid learning and willingness to iterate. But I have no evidence of the kind of first-principles reasoning or learning velocity that would make me override my concerns about the market. Neither founder has articulated what surprised them, what they learned, or what they would do differently — the signals I use to evaluate rate of change are simply absent. I would need to meet them to assess this properly, and in the absence of evidence, my default is moderate: YC acceptance signals baseline capability, nothing more.

Prism's technical approach is where the gap widens most decisively. When I backed Rocket Lab, Peter Beck had built a genuinely novel engine — electric turbopumps, 3D-printed combustion chambers, a non-linear cost reduction that no incumbent could replicate without rebuilding from scratch. Prism is applying the same commodity LLMs that every competitor in this space uses. The dossier identifies no proprietary architecture, no novel data advantage, no technical insight that creates a step-function improvement. The "agency" framing is a positioning choice, not a technology moat. Any competent team can build LLM-based resume screening in weeks. Lever, Greenhouse, and Workday face no structural barrier to adding this feature. Prism's differentiation would need to come from data flywheel effects or candidate network density — both are plausible but entirely unproven and, critically, require scale that Prism does not have and that multiple better-funded competitors are already building.

The strongest bull case for Prism: traditional recruiting agencies charge 20-25% of first-year salary, and Prism could collapse that cost to near-zero. For a $150K hire, that is $30-37K in savings per placement. If Prism scales this across millions of hires, the affordability impact is real. The outsider founder profile — philosophy students with no recruiting industry experience — is exactly what I claim to prefer. And the DoorDash exception proves I will override the technical novelty requirement when consequence magnitude is planetary-scale. But Prism fails the DoorDash test on consequence: DoorDash transformed food access for hundreds of millions of consumers. Prism optimizes a B2B expense line. The magnitude gap is fatal. Making recruiting 10x cheaper does not unlock a new category of human flourishing the way making food delivery ubiquitous did. The affordability dimension is the one place Prism scores above its default — there is a genuine cost-collapse thesis — but it applies to a business service, not a basic human need.

Prism would flip to Invest for me on one condition: if the founders demonstrated that their AI screening technology produced matching accuracy so superior that it changed hiring outcomes — not just speed or cost, but quality of match — to the point where companies using Prism made measurably better hires. That would elevate the consequence from "cheaper recruiting" to "better talent allocation across the economy," which is a different magnitude entirely. Show me data that Prism-screened candidates have 2-3x better retention rates or performance ratings than traditional screening, and I am interested. Without that, Prism is a cost play in a commodity market, and I pass.
