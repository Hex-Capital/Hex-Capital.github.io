# Hexa -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Hexa is the 2026 YC archetype my Pass Example was written against — "purpose-built AI agents" for manufacturers and distributors, founded by Ishaan Makkar whose LinkedIn reads "Stealth," with no contrarian secret named anywhere in the dossier and a competitor list (Distro, Turian, Go Autonomous, Arphie, Altura) longer than the team. Hexa's highest dimension is founder lineage and only because Makkar is opaque rather than disqualified; its lowest is the contrarian secret, which is the gate I open with. That pairing — a thesis-free pitch behind a thesis-free founder — is the signature.

The contrarian-secret ladder: **Stripe** (2010) sits at the top because Patrick Collison named the specific failure mode of PayPal-era payments — developer experience as institutional blind spot — when the category was declared closed; **Asana** (2010) sits at the mid rung where the secret was thin but Moskovitz's Facebook-shipped pedigree carried the check; the Pass Example sits at the bottom — "AI-powered platform for vertical X" with no named incumbent blind spot. Hexa sits at the bottom rung with the Pass Example. The dossier's framing — "54% of manufacturing companies still rely on spreadsheets" — is consensus market dissatisfaction, not a secret. There is no named architectural reason SAP Joule and Dynamics Copilot cannot ship the same agents inside the systems Hexa is overlaying on top of. The secret a founder would need here is something like "ERP-native agents will fail because cost-plus enterprise software cannot ship agentic write-back at startup velocity" — Makkar does not say this, and the dossier records no public statement that approaches it.

The atoms-heavy / regulated-sector ladder: **Anduril** (2017) sits at the top with Lockheed cost-plus as the named failure mode and hardware capex as the structural barrier pure-software competitors cannot match; **Stripe** (2010) sits at the mid rung where payments regulation gave some regulatory depth without being atoms-heavy; cleantech-1.0-style pure-SaaS-in-commoditizing-markets sits at the bottom. Hexa sits at the bottom — pure software, no regulated customer, no capex, advertising native integrations with eight ERP/CRM systems from a three-person team, which is the inverse of a moat: it is breadth that no one of the three can defend at depth. The Founders Fund manifesto's "we wanted flying cars" critique is operationalized here: Hexa is bits piped between other people's bits.

Monopoly trajectory: **Palantir** (2003) sits at the top with intelligence-grade switching costs; **Lyft** (2010) at the mid rung as a stable duopoly override; the Pass Example at the bottom where the moat hypothesis terminates at "we have a better model." Hexa sits at the bottom — the dossier names five direct competitors plus SAP, Oracle, and Microsoft each shipping native agent layers into the same ERP surface Hexa is overlaying. Pricing power five years out is unwritable when the platform you integrate against is also your substitute. Definite plan: **SpaceX** (2008)'s reusability roadmap sits at the top, **Quora** (2010)'s knowledge-graph thesis at the mid rung, lean-startup iteration at the bottom; Hexa sits at the bottom — no multi-year technical milestone, demo-led GTM via a Calendly link, "we'll iterate" implied by the integration-breadth strategy. Founder lineage: **Affirm** (2014, Levchin) at the top, **Asana** (2010, Moskovitz) at the mid rung, current-batch Stanford CS at the bottom; Hexa sits at the bottom, with one unverified search snippet referencing a "Mann Patira" co-founder and no documented manufacturing, ERP, or supply-chain employment in Makkar's record.

36-month forecast: Hexa is dead or acqui-hired below $5M by 2029, most likely absorbed as a feature into Distro or rolled into SAP's or NetSuite's partner program after ARR stalls under $1M, because the eight-ERP integration surface cannot be maintained at production quality by three engineers while SAP Joule and Dynamics Copilot ship native equivalents free to the same buyer. The mechanism is straightforward — when the platform vendor ships the agent, the third-party overlay's pricing power collapses to zero, and the cross-function suite (sales + procurement + finance + CS) means Hexa loses depth in every workflow to single-stack competitors like Turian in procurement or Distro in distributor order entry.

The strongest bull case borrows from **Asana** (2010): consensus-territory SaaS overridden by founder pedigree and a defensible workflow flywheel. If Makkar turns out to have shipped manufacturing software at scale at a prior company — the snippet hinting at "FR8" suggests freight/logistics adjacency — and if the vertical-document data flywheel (RFQs, POs, spec PDFs) compounds into a parsing moat the ERP-native agents cannot match because their training data is structured-only, then Hexa becomes a credible pick-and-shovel layer above the ERP wave. The bear-case dismantle: Asana's override required Moskovitz with a verified Facebook scale-shipping outcome, not an inferred adjacency from one unconfirmed snippet, and a parsing flywheel against unstructured documents is exactly the capability LLM-orchestration toolchains (LangChain, n8n) commoditize for every new entrant, per the dossier's own competitive note. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: Makkar publishes a named prior shipped-to-scale operator outcome in manufacturing or logistics software AND Hexa discloses two named mid-market manufacturer customers with signed annual contracts over $100K each within twelve months. Absent that, this is the Pass Example with a different vertical sticker.
