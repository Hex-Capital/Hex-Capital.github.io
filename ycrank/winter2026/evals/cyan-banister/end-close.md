# End Close -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what jumps out at me immediately: Sean Bolton spent six years building reconciliation at Modern Treasury — processing a trillion dollars across forty banks — and then left to build... reconciliation. Again. But independently. That's the central tension of this entire evaluation. On one hand, it's extraordinary domain expertise. You'd be hard-pressed to find someone on the planet who knows more about the operational reality of high-volume payment matching and exception handling. On the other hand, when I think about the founders who have generated my best returns, they weren't rebuilding what they'd already built inside someone else's company. They were attacking systems from the outside. Travis didn't work at a taxi dispatch company for six years before starting Uber. He saw the medallion system as a racket and wanted to burn it down. There's a difference between domain expertise and the kind of restless, outsider conviction that carries founders through the dark moments.

And when I ask myself the question I always start with — tell me your story, not the business — I don't hear the answer that lights me up. Sean went to a good Irish university, earned a prestigious fellowship at Notre Dame, and spent six years at a well-regarded YC company. David studied the same degree at the same school, co-founded a YC company, worked at Intercom and Basecamp, then built the fastest-growing product at PostHog. These are genuinely accomplished people. David teaching himself to code as a teenager is a small spark of the self-directed learner energy I recognize. But the biographical thread connecting these founders to THIS problem is professional, not personal. Nobody here overcame something that made reconciliation feel like a mission rather than an opportunity. That matters to me because when Modern Treasury inevitably deepens their own reconciliation features — and they will, because they have institutional knowledge, existing customer relationships, and the same bank integrations Sean helped build — the thing that keeps you going isn't your résumé. It's the fire that your résumé can't explain.

The competitive landscape is what really gives me pause. This is not an unpopular category. Ledge raised $9 million from NEA. Osfin raised nearly $10 million. BlackLine is public with $653 million in revenue. Trintech has 4,200 clients. When I look at a space and see that much capital already deployed against variations of the same problem, I know this isn't the kind of bet where I have an edge. The most promising companies I've backed were the ones other investors thought were insane — SpaceX when private space was absurd, Crusoe when Bitcoin mining on gas flares sounded like a joke, Contraline when male contraception made VCs squirm. "AI-powered reconciliation for payments companies" doesn't make anyone squirm. It makes them nod. And when everyone's nodding, the opportunity for outsized returns has usually been competed away.

Let me steel-man the bull case, because it deserves it. If I'm wrong about this company, here's what would have to be true: Sean saw something at Modern Treasury that nobody else there could see — that reconciliation needs to be a standalone, continuous, AI-native platform rather than a feature inside a payments operations suite. The developer-first API approach would need to be as transformative for reconciliation as Stripe's API was for payments — a category-defining interface that makes legacy vendors irrelevant overnight. And the AI timing would need to be genuinely inflective — exception handling that previously required human judgment becoming automatable for the first time because of LLMs. If all three of those things are true, then Sean's domain expertise becomes a structural advantage rather than a professional credential, and the competitive landscape becomes a validation signal rather than a crowding signal. That's a real scenario. The "making invisible systems visible" pattern I've invested around — Carta for cap tables, Affirm for interest rates, Flock Safety for public safety data — does map here. Reconciliation IS an invisible system, and real-time transparency into where money is getting stuck could create genuine value for the payments ecosystem. But I'd need to feel that jolt of energy about the founder's personal commitment to this vision, and I don't.

The co-founder dynamic has some reassuring signals — both from the west of Ireland, same university, same degree program, deeply embedded in the YC network through different companies. David's been through a startup before with Unflow. Shared cultural roots and overlapping professional networks suggest a foundation, though I'd want to know how they fight before writing a check. The complementary skill set is clean: Sean brings the domain and the customer relationships, David brings the engineering chops and the company-building experience.

This is a well-constructed company by capable people attacking a real problem. For a different investor — someone who weights domain expertise and market timing over biographical grit and non-consensus positioning — this could be a strong bet. But it doesn't activate my framework. I need founders whose life story explains why they won't stop, building something that makes other investors uncomfortable. End Close is too professional, too legible, and too consensus for how I invest.
