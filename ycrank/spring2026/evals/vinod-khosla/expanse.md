# Expanse -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Expanse is a research-grade founder shipping incremental optimization tooling for an existing budget category -- Ismaeel Bashir's EPCC master's dissertation on "Multi-modal HPC Resource and Failure Prediction" is the most credentialed thing in the dossier, and the company's stated mission is to recover the "30% of cloud spend lost to over-allocation." That sentence is the entire pitch and the entire problem. Recovering 30% of an existing line item is the textbook profile of the YC company I pass on.

Consequence magnitude is where Expanse is weakest, and it is the dimension I evaluate first. On my ladder, OpenAI sits at the top -- civilizational consequence justified the apology letter to my LPs. Rocket Lab is the middle rung -- making small-sat launch radically cheaper opened a new market of earth-observation, IoT, and defense customers who were previously priced out. The bottom rung is the AI-for-accountants pitch I described in my pass example: a 40% workflow improvement in one profession. Expanse sits between Rocket Lab and that bottom rung, closer to the bottom. The dossier evidence pinning it there is the framing itself: "32-GPU research cluster operating at 37% utilization." That is a redistribution of existing GPU spend among existing buyers -- national HPC centres and quant funds -- not the creation of a new market of users who couldn't previously access compute. My red flag list explicitly names this: "targeting redistribution of existing spend rather than creation of new demand." Expanse is the canonical example.

Non-linear technical approach is Expanse's highest-placed dimension, which tells you how thin the case is. The high rung is Rocket Lab's Rutherford engine -- electric turbopumps and 3D-printed combustion chambers were a step-function rethink of cost structure. The mid rung is DoorDash, which I funded despite having no novel technical wedge because the consequence magnitude was planetary. The low rung is the LLM-API-wrapper class of YC company. Expanse sits between mid and low. Bashir's claim that his predictor "beat every published baseline" is real if true, but the dossier flags no peer-reviewed publication, and the comparator is academic Slurm predictors (arXiv 2604.02158) that any HPC team can lift. Run:ai being open-sourced by NVIDIA after the $700M close in December 2024 means the productization moat is on a clock.

Founder as learning machine is the dimension I am most willing to give Expanse credit on, but the credit is bounded. Mach Industries' 19-year-old MIT dropout is my high rung -- irreverent, technically credible, attacking Lockheed Martin from first principles. Bashir is in the right tradition: young, outsider geography (Edinburgh, not the Valley), built the actual research IP that became the company. Yafet Melake and Eren Mendi at G-Research provide quant-fund user perspective. But the dossier shows no public footprint for CTO Nikodem Bieniek, no evidence of pivots or surprises since starting, and a brand fragmented across expanse.sh, expanse.org.uk, and getexpanse.io -- not a fatal signal, but not the high-velocity learning trajectory I look for in the three months at YC. Mid-rung placement, closer to mid than high.

Improbable ambition is where Expanse drops cleanly to the bottom of my ladder. Commonwealth Fusion Systems is the high rung -- "limitless energy" provoked incredulity from energy experts. Rocket Lab is the mid rung. The low rung is the competent-but-incremental SaaS company I pass on weekly. Expanse's stated goal is "unlock wasted GPU capacity." That provokes incredulity from no one. No expert reads that sentence and says "that can't be done." Path to radical affordability is similarly low: a 30% reduction in GPU cost is not the 10x collapse I look for in Impossible Foods or Curai Health -- it's a margin improvement, not a price-to-zero collapse that would let a billion new researchers train models who couldn't before.

My 36-month forecast: Expanse reaches $1-3M ARR with five to fifteen HPC and quant-fund customers, then gets acquired sub-$50M by SchedMD, NVIDIA, or a hyperscaler folding it into existing GPU operations tooling -- a respectable founder outcome, an irrelevant venture outcome. The mechanism is the dimensional signature: a credentialed founder applying real ML research to a redistribution problem in a fragmented HPC segment that hyperscalers and SchedMD will eventually absorb. This is a Pass for me, not because Bashir lacks ability, but because the consequence of full success is a moderately profitable infrastructure tool.

The strongest case against my pass invokes my own Cerent and Juniper history -- infrastructure plays where the consequence looked narrow but compounded into multi-billion outcomes because the underlying market exploded. GPU scarcity is real, H100/B200 cycles cost real dollars, and 30% of a $26B 2031 GPUaaS market is $7.8B of recovered spend that has to go somewhere. The bull says Expanse becomes the SchedMD of the AI era, and I funded Juniper at a similar stage of obscurity. The case stings, but it does not win. Juniper attacked an existing $70B telecom routing market with a step-function architecture -- the IP/router itself was non-linear, not a 30% optimization layer over Cisco. Expanse is an optimization layer over Slurm, which is itself open source. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: Expanse demonstrates a 5x or better predictor accuracy improvement that is independently benchmarked against Run:ai's open-source HPC fork, with two named national-lab or top-five quant-fund customers in production paying six figures. Without that, this is a $50M acquisition, not a Juniper.
