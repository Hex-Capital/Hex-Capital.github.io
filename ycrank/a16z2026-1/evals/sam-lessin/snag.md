# snag -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Selin Sonmez and Niko Georgantas have built three companies together — Knock Knock City (acquired by LuggageHero), OneRoof ($4.6M from General Catalyst), and now snag. That pattern is my single favorite signal in venture. I backed Andrew Kortina from Venmo through Fin. I watched Raj Dittmar through three companies before writing the Solana check. When you see two people keep choosing each other across multiple ventures, with a prior exit on the record, you know the relationship is load-bearing. Selin's Forbes 30u30, her growth role at LuggageHero post-acquisition, Niko's IBM design chops — these are grounded, competent operators who've demonstrated they can ship product and navigate pivots. The founder signal here is real.

But here's my problem: the idea isn't crazy. "Marketplace for GenZ sublets" is sensible. It's logical. It addresses a genuine gap between Airbnb's nightly tourist pricing and Zillow's 12-month lease infrastructure. NYC Local Law 18 even created a structural tailwind by pushing demand into the 1-3 month window that snag occupies. All true. All rational. And that's precisely what doesn't work for me — I need a sane person pitching something that sounds insane. Selin is the sane person, no question. But "better Craigslist for student sublets with AI listing conversion" does not make anyone say "what the fuck, that's crazy." It makes people nod and say "yeah, that makes sense." When everybody nods, the upside is already in the price.

The primitive test is where snag falls short of my threshold. Venmo created a verb — "Venmo me" became a behavioral primitive that didn't exist before. Patreon created the behavior of paying creators directly. snag is aggregating sublet posts from Facebook groups into a structured marketplace. That's an improvement on an existing behavior, not the birth of a new one. Students were already finding sublets on social media — snag makes it faster and cleaner. The 30,000 student users and $6.5M in booking requests over 30 days with 40% month-over-month growth tell me the product works. The 4.7-star iOS rating across 184 reviews confirms people like it. But "working product people like" and "new category" are different things. Blueground raised $352M doing furnished apartments. June Homes raised $50M and got acquired. Kopa went through YC. LeaseBreak has 100K+ registered users. This is a contested segment with multiple funded players — none of whom created a new primitive either.

What snag gets right on durability is that housing is physical. You cannot AI away the need for an apartment in Manhattan. The two-sided network density — 3,000 listings, 30,000 users, seven university partnerships including NYU and Pratt — creates genuine local switching costs. An LLM cannot replicate the trust layer of a verified marketplace with engaged students at specific campuses. This is closer to my Metropolis thesis (physical-world operations software amplifies but can't replace) than to the SaaS melting ice cubes I keep warning about. The AI listing-conversion feature is trivially replicable by anyone with a competent ML team, but that's fine — the AI isn't the moat, the liquidity density is. Incumbents have business-model incompatibility: Airbnb is optimized for nightly stays, Zillow for annual leases, and neither will rebuild its entire stack for 1-3 month peer-to-peer sublets.

The bull case I have to honestly confront: if snag achieves liquidity dominance in NYC student sublets and expands campus-by-campus to Boston, LA, SF, and Chicago, the marketplace network effects compound and this becomes the default infrastructure for a genuine structural shift in how young people house themselves. Remote work, gig economy, delayed homeownership — all secular trends pushing toward flexible housing. And the Local Law 18 dynamic is genuinely clever positioning. If Selin and Niko execute the city-by-city playbook the way they've executed in NYC, the $6.5M in monthly booking requests could become $65M within 18 months. At that GMV scale, even a modest take rate produces a real business. The co-founder durability across three ventures is exactly the kind of relationship that survives the grind of city-by-city marketplace building.

But capital discipline concerns me. OneRoof burned through $4.6M from General Catalyst before pivoting to snag five months ago. The cap table carries the weight of that prior raise plus whatever a16z put in through Speedrun. City-by-city marketplace expansion is inherently capital-intensive — you rebuild liquidity from zero in each geography, which means snag's path forward almost certainly requires multiple rounds. That's the factory-farm system I think is dying. The "$6.5M in booking requests" metric is GMV aspiration, not revenue — the dossier found no public revenue signal and no pricing page. I can't evaluate capital discipline when I don't know if the business actually generates cash. The most decisive dimensions against snag are consensus mispricing and primitive creation. This isn't a bet the market has wrong — it's a bet the market hasn't priced at all because it's a niche vertical marketplace, and niche vertical marketplaces don't produce the asymmetric outcomes I'm looking for. Nobody's telling Selin she's crazy. They're telling her "good luck with the city-by-city grind." Those are different conversations, and I invest at the former, not the latter.
