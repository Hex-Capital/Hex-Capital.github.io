# Payna -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I see is the competitive landscape, because it tells me everything about timing. Brico has $21.6M in funding, marquee customers -- Marqeta, BitGo, Better Mortgage -- 600% year-over-year revenue growth, and a Forbes Top 50 Fintech designation. They're building the same product: AI-powered money transmitter licensing automation across US states. When your direct competitor has already been validated by the market, celebrated by the press, and funded by serious capital, the non-consensus window has closed. I invest when smart people think I'm wrong. Here, smart people already think Brico is right -- and Payna is entering the same thesis with fewer resources, fewer customers, and a two-year head start to overcome. Anyone chasing hot markets in this business gets killed.

The specific knowledge question is where this falls apart for me. Aaron Chen is a crypto product designer -- sole designer at an a16z CSX-backed startup, ETHGlobal hackathon winner, Ritual Foundation fellow. That's genuine crypto-native experience, and it's not nothing. But compliance licensing is not a crypto design problem. It's a regulatory operations problem. The founders who should be building a "compliance operating system for financial licensing" are former state examiners, NMLS processors, compliance officers who have spent years navigating the gap between what regulators publish and what examiners actually enforce. That gap -- between the written rule and the unwritten playbook -- is where specific knowledge lives in this domain. Chen's knowledge is transferable to many crypto products; it's not specific to this one. He Song's background is entirely opaque -- no education, no work history, no domain signal whatsoever. When I can't assess specific knowledge for half the founding team, I can't invest my own money.

The pivot timeline sharpens this concern. In November 2025, the team built "Paena," an on-chain earned wage access platform, at an ETHGlobal hackathon. By January 2026, they'd pivoted to B2B compliance infrastructure. That's a domain shift from consumer DeFi to regulatory workflow automation in under three months. This pattern -- searching for a company to build rather than building from obsessive domain knowledge -- is exactly the founder-identity trap I warn about. The best founders have been thinking about their problem for years before they start the company. They don't arrive at compliance automation via a hackathon pivot. The product is at most two to three months old, which means the regulatory data coverage, state examiner playbook logic, and compliance scoring accuracy are all unproven against production-grade requirements.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement. The regulatory tailwind is real: the GENIUS Act expanded stablecoin licensing requirements, state-level VASP registrations are proliferating, and compliance costs are projected to rise 25%. If Payna builds a fully AI-native system -- no human-in-the-loop dependency like Brico's model -- and executes on genuine continuous compliance scoring rather than one-time application processing, the leverage ratio could be meaningfully different. The cryptographically timestamped audit trail creates real switching costs once customers build compliance history on the platform. And the 50-state regulatory mapping, once built accurately, does compound as maintenance burden -- every regulatory change across every jurisdiction makes the dataset harder to replicate from scratch. If Aaron Chen's crypto-native instincts lead to a fundamentally different product architecture than what a compliance consultant would build, the outsider perspective could be an advantage rather than a gap. But "could be" isn't "is." Without evidence of domain depth, early customer validation, or a technical co-founder who has built the core compliance logic, this bull case remains hypothetical.

The leverage architecture is standard B2B SaaS -- code serving multiple customers across jurisdictions, some automation replacing manual workflows. But it's not creating a new form of leverage that didn't exist before. It's making existing compliance work cheaper, not enabling a new category of capability. Compare to Notion, which gave individuals the output of a five-tool stack, or Replit, which turned non-programmers into builders. Payna makes compliance officers slightly faster at filing MTL applications. That's valuable but linear. There are no visible network effects -- one customer's compliance data doesn't make the platform better for other customers. The underlying regulatory data is public. The document generation relies on publicly available legal text. This is an execution race against a better-funded competitor, not a compounding technology bet.

I'd pass. The specific knowledge isn't there, the timing is consensus rather than contrarian, and a well-capitalized incumbent already occupies the position. When I backed Uber, ride-sharing was illegal and no one had proven the model. This market has already been proven -- by Brico -- and Payna is arriving late without the domain expertise to outbuild them.
