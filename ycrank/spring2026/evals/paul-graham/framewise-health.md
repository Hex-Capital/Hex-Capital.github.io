# Framewise Health -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Framewise Health is a regulatory-schlep company without the hacker-genealogy signature that usually carries technical risk in healthcare — Tane Kim dropped out of Brown's BS/MD program after watching 500+ patient interactions, and he and David Cui shipped FHIR connectors into Epic, Oracle/Cerner, athenahealth, ModMed, eClinicalWorks, and NextGen before raising the $500K, but neither founder has a public GitHub trail and the company has not shown me a single patient or hospital who loves the product. That pairing — real schlep, thin hacker tribal markers, and no shipped stunt — is the entire question for Framewise Health.

The closest portfolio analogy on the dimension I weight most is **Coinbase** (2012), where Brian Armstrong ran toward bitcoin compliance grind that no other applicant in the batch wanted to touch. Framewise Health is running toward an adjacent unpleasantness: HIPAA, SOC 2, FHIR plumbing into six different EHR vendors, plus latent FDA software-as-medical-device exposure on AI-generated discharge instructions in 75+ languages. That is genuine schlep territory, and per "Schlep Blindness" (January 2012) it is exactly where mainstream founders unconsciously route around. Where Framewise Health diverges from Coinbase is that Armstrong rescued his rejected application with a hand-built Android wallet I could touch; Framewise Health's dossier names six EHR integrations but zero patients who used the video, zero signed hospital LOIs, and no founder-shipped artifact that I can pull up on my phone and feel.

The Pass Example I wrote — the two-MBA team with the glossy deck and the "we'll hire engineers post-funding" plan — failed because no founder had personally suffered the problem. Framewise Health partly inherits this failure mechanism. Tane Kim observed 500+ patient interactions across a dozen-plus institutions, which is closer to the problem than an MBA scanning a TAM slide, but observation is not the same as Drew Houston repeatedly forgetting his own USB stick at MIT. Framewise Health is building a tool for the patients Tane watched, not for Tane himself, and David Cui's computer-vision research at Brown is even further from the discharge-instruction problem. The organic-idea filter from "How to Get Startup Ideas" (November 2012) wants the founder to be the customer; Framewise Health's founders are the empathetic adjacent witnesses.

Tane Kim walking out of a BS/MD program is the dossier fact I keep returning to on animal resourcefulness. Dropping a guaranteed physician income to build SMS video for hospitals is a real cost paid pre-funding, and that compounds over 18 months the way it did for Houston — once a founder has burned the safe path, the only direction is forward. Against that, Framewise Health shows no cereal-box equivalent: no unscalable stunt, no revenue improvisation, no hand-built demo that crossed Hacker News. Six FHIR integrations done by two people at pre-seed is animal-flavored work, but it is plumbing animals, not delight animals, and I have learned to tell those apart.

If Framewise Health were graded by Stripe's hacker-genealogy bar, Patrick Collison's Young Scientist Lisp prize at fifteen would be the comparison point, and the gap is wide. David Cui has computer-vision research at Brown and a prior engineering internship at Freya (YC S25), which is real but credential-shaped; the dossier explicitly notes "No public repos found" for Tane Kim and that GitHub search returned unrelated "David Cui" profiles. Hackers leave tracks, and Framewise Health's CTO has academic CV research instead of an open-source footprint. This is the dimension where Framewise Health most clearly is not the Reddit/Stripe pattern.

What would have to be true for Framewise Health to flip from Neutral to Invest on the founder dimension is one piece of evidence the dossier does not yet contain: a named hospital running the SMS video with patients in production, or a patient comprehension delta they measured themselves on a Saturday and posted. Either would move Tane Kim from "observed 500+ interactions" to "shipped something a patient loved" — the line between watching and animal.

My 36-month forecast for Framewise Health: acquired by Commure (which already swallowed Memora Health on 2024-12-20) or by an EHR vendor for a $15–40M outcome at sub-$3M ARR, more likely than dead and more likely than Black Swan. The mechanism is that Framewise Health's six FHIR integrations and HIPAA/SOC 2 posture are exactly the asset an incumbent patient-communication platform would bolt on to claim AI-video personalization, and Framewise Health's founder profile reads more like talented infrastructure builders than category-defining animals who will refuse the early offer.

The bull case that should sting: the **Airbnb** (2009) exception teaches me to discount my own idea-quality skepticism when founders pay real costs pre-funding, and Tane Kim leaving a BS/MD program is the same shape of cost as the cereal boxes — it is the move that signals the founder is not optionality-hedging. If Framewise Health's first three hospital pilots come back with patient comprehension lifts that are obviously real, the Commure consolidation becomes their distribution opportunity rather than their substitution risk, because hospitals fleeing the bundle will prefer the independent video specialist. I cannot dismantle this case cleanly; I can only note that the dossier today contains zero pilot evidence, zero patient love metric, and no founder-shipped stunt of the Armstrong-prototype variety. The verifiable condition that flips Framewise Health to Invest within 90 days is one signed hospital paying contract with named counterparty plus a patient comprehension delta measured on real discharge instructions — neither of which currently exists in the public record.
