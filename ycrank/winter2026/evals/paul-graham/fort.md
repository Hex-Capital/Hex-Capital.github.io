# Fort -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The most interesting thing about Fort isn't the product or the team. It's what Whoop did with Push. In 2021, Whoop -- a company with $454 million in funding and $260 million in revenue -- acquired Push, which was the closest thing to what Fort is building. Then they killed the standalone hardware and reduced the entire strength tracking problem to a simplified "muscular load" estimate based on duration. They had the IP, the data, and the engineering talent. They still walked away from the hard version of the problem. That's corporate schlep blindness. Not because they couldn't see the opportunity, but because building a device that accurately classifies 50+ exercises, tracks per-rep velocity, and estimates proximity to failure from a wrist sensor is a grinding technical schlep that doesn't fit neatly into a subscription-recovery-platform business. The institutional incentive structure at Whoop, Garmin, and Oura all point away from this problem. Every serious lifter knows their wearable becomes nearly useless the moment they pick up a barbell. It's been hiding in plain sight for years.

The origin story has the right shape. Miranda met Zac at a gym. They're both strength trainers who experienced the tracking gap firsthand. That's organic -- not "we analyzed the wearable market and found a whitespace," but "we use these products and they don't work for what we actually do." The problem is real and specific: heart rate tells you about cardio load but says almost nothing about whether your last set of squats was close to failure or left three reps in reserve. Every lifter who has worn a Whoop or Apple Watch during a strength session has felt this disconnect. The question is whether "noticing your wearable doesn't work at the gym" constitutes deep enough personal pain to sustain founders through the years of grinding hardware iteration ahead. It's authentic but not visceral in the way Drew Houston's forgotten USB drive was -- that was a daily, physical frustration that made him build the product for himself before anyone else.

The team composition creates an unusual tension. Three mechanical and electrical engineers from Tesla -- Miranda on 4680 battery cells for Cybertruck, Paul on sensing systems and autonomy architecture for Semi and Robotaxi, Zac on powertrain structures across Tesla, SpaceX, Rivian, and Astranis. For a hardware product, this is a dream team. These are people who have shipped complex physical products at the hardest companies in the world. The band itself -- sub-30g, 7-day battery, magnetic equipment attachment, multi-placement form factor -- is the kind of design challenge they were born to solve. But Fort's actual differentiator isn't the band. It's the ML: the exercise classification, the rep detection, the RIR estimation, the per-muscle stimulus categorization. That's software. None of the three founders have visible GitHub repositories. There's no evidence of who is building the algorithms that constitute the product's core value proposition. When I look at the founding teams of companies that succeeded -- Stripe, Dropbox, Reddit -- the people in the room could build the thing that mattered most. Here, the people in the room can build the physical device, but whether they can build the intelligence layer is an open question.

The bull case is genuinely compelling. If Fort nails accuracy, this could be a category-defining product. Strength training is growing explosively -- Whoop's own data shows 122% year-over-year growth in strength workouts. The longevity movement has made resistance training mainstream in a way it wasn't five years ago. And the competitive moat writes itself: every rep generates proprietary training data that improves the models, creating a flywheel that a Garmin bolt-on feature can never replicate. The team has the hardware chops to actually ship a consumer device, which is the graveyard of most hardware startups. And the fact that Whoop tried and retreated means the competitive window is real. If these founders are as resourceful as their Tesla backgrounds suggest, and if they've solved the sensing problem well enough that early users trust the data, the per-rep velocity and RIR tracking would create the kind of switching costs that make users genuinely dependent on the product. That's the Airbnb pattern -- hosts who "actually needed" the income. Lifters who structure their progressive overload around Fort's data would have real economic dependency on the training history.

But I can't get past the absence of evidence on the software side, and the product hasn't shipped. Pre-orders are open but no numbers have been disclosed. No accuracy validation has been published. The device ships Q3 2026, which means the earliest real-world feedback is months away. I don't see the crucible moments that signal relentless resourcefulness -- leaving Tesla is commitment, but raising from YC plus four other investors suggests the fundraising path was relatively smooth. At this stage, Fort is a well-credentialed hardware team with an organic problem and a pre-order page. That's promising but not yet convincing. I'd want to see three things before writing a check: published accuracy benchmarks against a reference device, evidence that someone on the team can build the ML pipeline, and pre-order conversion numbers showing genuine pull rather than press-driven curiosity.
