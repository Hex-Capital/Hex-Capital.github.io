# CatchBack Cards -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The first thing I notice about CatchBack Cards is the Nexus connection, and it concerns me. Joseph Zhang worked at Nexus -- the same blockchain infrastructure company CatchBack is now partnering with to deploy on their Layer 1 mainnet. This has the fingerprint of a protocol team's showcase application rather than a business that independently discovered it needed cryptographic infrastructure. When I backed Solana, Anatoly Yakovenko wasn't building a DApp on someone else's chain -- he was building the chain itself. CatchBack sits on the wrong side of that hierarchy. They're the application on Nexus's protocol, not the protocol. And the most revealing detail: they open-sourced their card selection code. I understand the trust-building logic, but you've literally published the playbook for any competitor to replicate your one technical differentiator.

Joseph Zhang's resume is legitimately impressive -- AWS, McKinsey, Gates Scholar, ISEF finalist. The "sane" part of my framework checks out. But when I ask "is the idea insane?" -- meaning genuinely non-consensus, the kind of thing that makes smart investors uncomfortable -- the answer is no. Mystery packs for trading cards with better randomness verification is a product improvement, not a paradigm challenge. Fanatics already runs "Under Wraps" mystery boxes with official league licensing that CatchBack can't match. Whatnot is an $11.5 billion company in adjacent territory. The entire competitive landscape tells me the market understands this category perfectly well. When I backed Venmo, nobody believed peer-to-peer mobile payments were a real thing. Nobody is confused about whether people want to rip mystery packs of Pokémon cards -- the question is just execution and distribution against entrenched, massively funded incumbents.

The Birchbox lesson haunts me here. The mystery pack experience -- the digital "rip," the customizable odds, the gamification -- is narrative without structural moat. It's replicable by anyone with inventory and a decent engineering team. The zero-fee marketplace is a classic subsidy play that works until you need to sustain a business on actual margins. The buyback guarantees require capital intensity that only works at scale, and at 700 users, the liquidity network effect is nonexistent. Whatnot can add asynchronous mystery packs as a feature next quarter. Fanatics has every sports league's official licensing. CatchBack's position is fragile in exactly the way Birchbox's was: the format is the product, and formats don't compound into defensibility.

Let me steelman the bull case, because there's a version of this that's interesting. If cryptographic randomness verification becomes the expected standard for all online randomized commerce -- not just trading cards but loot boxes, gacha games, online gambling -- then CatchBack could be establishing a trust primitive with broad applicability. The Steph Curry collaboration, if real and material, could provide distribution that bootstraps the marketplace past the cold-start problem. And $13K MRR at 700 users for a company that launched its iOS app in late January is genuine willingness-to-pay signal -- these aren't free users. Trading cards are physical goods with real inventory value, providing a floor. In a world where CatchBack becomes the "verified fair" standard for randomized pack commerce and expands beyond cards into any collectible category, you could see the narrative stretch. But for that to work, the verification layer would need to become infrastructure that other platforms integrate -- and right now it's a consumer app feature, not a protocol others build on.

The crypto/Web3/NFT tags are a liability in 2026 consumer markets. Consumer sentiment toward anything associated with NFTs cratered in 2022-2023 and hasn't meaningfully recovered among mainstream collectors. The blockchain dependency narrows the addressable audience at exactly the moment you need to be broadening it. I also can't assess half the founding team -- Sophia Kim's background is completely undocumented, which in a two-person company means I'm effectively evaluating a solo founder. The IP risk from card publishers (Pokémon Company, major sports leagues) is real and underappreciated; enabling third-party mystery packs of branded products is exactly the kind of thing that attracts cease-and-desist letters. This is a competent founder building a consumer application in a brutally competitive space where the technical differentiation is a feature that can be copied and the crypto positioning alienates more users than it attracts.
