# Scheduling Wizard -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Scheduling Wizard is something most investors would see as a strength: 19 departments across 14 hospitals -- including Mass General, Johns Hopkins, UT Southwestern, HCA, and UCSF -- at pre-seed with three people. That's genuine execution. But for me, execution at this stage is only meaningful when I understand *who* is executing and *why* this problem lives inside them. And when I look for that thread with these founders, I come up short. Samuel Oberly is clearly talented -- Cambridge applied math, Department of Defense war games analysis, Minor League Baseball scheduling. That's a resume that says "I'm excellent at constraint optimization." Zachary Dermody's Amazon and McMaster-Carr operations experience adds operational rigor. Abdelrahman Hamimi brings solid engineering chops. But all three are recent JHU graduates from the class of 2024 who identified a scheduling problem at their university's hospital and built a company around it. That's smart. It's not the same as a chief resident who spent four years drowning in spreadsheets at 2 AM and said "never again." When I backed Contraline, what made me lean in wasn't just that male contraception was a big idea -- it was that the founder had spent years in the trenches of academic research that everyone else dismissed. I'm looking for that kind of inseparability between the founder and the problem, and I don't find it here.

The managed-service model is the most interesting strategic choice this team has made. In a market where QGenda, Lightning Bolt, Amion, and YouShift all sell software tools, Scheduling Wizard says: "We'll just do it for you." That's a genuine insight -- overworked program directors don't want another platform to learn, they want the problem to disappear. It reminds me of how some of the best companies I've seen take a counterintuitive approach to delivery. But here's where it gets complicated. A managed service with three people serving 19 departments is either a testament to remarkable automation or a signal that the founders are personally grinding through schedules and it hasn't been tested at scale. The "Scheduling Programming Language" could be the technical leverage that makes this model scalable, or it could be a sophisticated tool that still requires significant human judgment to operate. I don't have enough visibility to know which, and that distinction determines whether this is a venture-scale business or a very good consulting practice.

QGenda's $2.5B acquisition by Hearst validates that physician scheduling is a real, large market -- but it also means this is consensus territory now. When Hearst writes a $2.5 billion check for the category leader, everyone in healthcare IT knows this market matters. That's the opposite of the signal I look for. The best investments I've made -- SpaceX, Uber, Crusoe Energy -- were in spaces where the prevailing wisdom was that the whole idea was foolish. Nobody is calling physician scheduling foolish. YouShift, one batch ahead in YC, is backed by Bain Capital Ventures and pursuing the same market with a self-serve AI approach. This is a competitive, well-mapped landscape, not an overlooked frontier.

If I force myself to make the bull case, it goes like this: the managed-service model creates a structural moat that no incumbent will copy because it would cannibalize their per-seat software revenue. Each department's constraints get encoded into the Scheduling Programming Language, building switching costs and institutional knowledge that compound over time. Oberly's specific mathematical background is actually rare -- most healthcare IT founders don't have DoD-level operations research training combined with real scheduling experience from MLB. And the traction speaks for itself: landing Mass General and Johns Hopkins as a 3-person pre-seed team in healthcare, where procurement cycles can take a year, shows extraordinary hustle and credibility. If they can automate the managed-service delivery to the point where one person can handle fifty departments instead of six, and if they expand from scheduling into broader physician operations (the UCSF patient triaging signal), then the $2.5B QGenda comp becomes a floor, not a ceiling. That's a legitimate path to a very large outcome.

But I keep returning to what's absent. There's no personal survival story connecting these founders to this pain. There's no "weird factor" -- no moment where I think "that's crazy, but wait..." The company creates real operational value for physicians and hospitals, but it's not making an invisible system visible for underserved people or creating economic access where none existed. It's making already-functioning hospitals function better. That's valuable, but it's not what gets me out of bed. When I think about Uber, I think about drivers like Roger who suddenly had a way to earn a living on their own terms. When I think about Affirm, I think about consumers who could finally understand what credit actually cost them. Scheduling Wizard makes life easier for fellowship program directors at Johns Hopkins. That's meaningful, but it's a different category of impact.

These three founders are clearly capable, and I wouldn't be surprised if they build a successful company. The traction is real and impressive. But the investment doesn't match how I think about deploying my own money. I need a founder story that grabs me, an idea that looks wrong to everyone else, and a vision for impact that extends beyond operational efficiency for well-resourced institutions. This one's a pass for me.
