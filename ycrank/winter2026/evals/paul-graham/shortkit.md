# shortkit -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The most interesting thing about ShortKit is the origin of the technical founder. Michael Seleman spent six years building infrastructure for YouTube Shorts at Google. Six years. That's not someone who read about short-form video trends and decided to build a startup. That's someone who lived inside the machine, saw exactly how hard the problem is, and realized that every company wanting a TikTok-style feed is going to hit the same wall he spent half a decade learning to climb. When I evaluate whether an idea was discovered organically or manufactured from market analysis, this is about as clean an organic signal as you get at the infrastructure layer. He didn't sit down to think of startup ideas. He already knew where the bodies were buried.

The schlep here is real and ugly. Codec optimization, adaptive bitrate transcoding, ML-driven buffer management, device-specific encoding, CDN delivery tuned for the swipe-feed pattern -- this is the kind of work that makes most engineers unconsciously look away. It's not glamorous. It's not a weekend hack. It's years of specialized knowledge compressed into an SDK. The structural parallel to Stripe is visible: thousands of developers knew payment processing was painful, but the Collisons were the ones who actually walked into the banks. Here, thousands of product teams know they want short-form video feeds, but the transcoding and buffering schlep keeps them from building good ones. ShortKit is saying "give us your app, we'll give you TikTok-quality feeds." That's a real proposition born from genuine expertise.

But two things stop me from getting excited. First, the co-founder relationship. Bhammar went to Northeastern, Seleman went to Tufts. Bhammar built student transportation software, Seleman built video infrastructure at Google. No shared employer, no shared school, no visible prior connection. When I see two people from completely different worlds who appear to have found each other specifically to start a company, that's a pattern I've watched fail repeatedly. In one YC batch, nine companies added co-founders they didn't know well, and all nine fell apart within a year. I'm not saying that's what happened here -- maybe they've been friends for years and the public record just doesn't show it. But the absence of any visible pre-existing bond is a genuine concern, not a technicality. Cofounders are for a startup what location is for real estate, and manufactured partnerships crack under pressure.

Second, and more fundamentally: there is zero evidence that anyone wants this yet. No users, no revenue, no named customers, no waitlist, no organic community, no Product Hunt launch. The pricing page says "talk to us about your anticipated delivery volume" -- which reads like they're still waiting for the first serious conversation. At pre-seed I don't demand revenue, but I want some signal of pull. A publisher who integrated the SDK and saw engagement jump. A product team that found them organically. Even a handful of developers poking at the docs unprompted. Make something people want is not an aspiration; it's a test. And right now I can't tell if anyone wants this beyond the abstract level of "yeah, short-form video is important."

The bull case is worth articulating because it's genuinely strong. If the embeddable short-form video market materializes broadly -- if news publishers, fitness apps, e-commerce platforms, and educational products all decide they need swipe-feeds -- then ShortKit is positioned as the specialized infrastructure layer with a six-year head start on understanding the problem. Seleman's YouTube Shorts pedigree gives them instant credibility with engineering buyers. The switching costs after deep SDK integration would be substantial. BytePlus carries geopolitical baggage that limits Western adoption. Mux is generalist and may never prioritize this niche. In this scenario, ShortKit becomes the Stripe of short-form video: obvious in retrospect, inevitable once someone with the right background decided to do it. That's not a crazy outcome. It's plausible enough to make me pay attention.

What keeps this in watch-rather-than-invest territory is the combination of unknowns. The co-founder relationship is opaque. Bhammar brings real startup experience from BusRight -- 700% revenue growth, customers in 23 states, $7M raised -- but his connection to video infrastructure is nonexistent. The technical half of this team is excellent; the question is whether the whole is coherent or assembled. And the market for "embeddable short-form video SDK" might be narrower than it appears. Not every app needs a TikTok feed. The ones that do might build in-house or accept a good-enough solution from Mux. ShortKit needs to prove that the demand is broad and that the specialized product creates enough delta over general-purpose tools to justify a separate vendor relationship. That proof doesn't exist yet.
