# Asimov -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The competitive landscape here tells you everything you need to know. micro1 has raised $35 million and is generating over $100 million in annual revenue while shipping Ray-Ban glasses to contributors for the same crowdsourced robotics data capture. General Intuition raised a $134 million seed round to train spatial reasoning from gaming videos. Sensei, a YC company from two batches ago, is using exoskeleton arms for teleoperation data. Scale AI, sitting atop $16.4 billion in funding, is extending its dominant data labeling platform into robotics. When I count four well-funded companies converging on the same thesis — "robotics has a data bottleneck, let's fill it" — I am not looking at a secret. I am looking at a consensus dressed in different hardware. The mimetic dynamics are unmistakable: every player is imitating every other player into mutual margin destruction, differentiated only by the form factor of the recording device. Headband cameras versus Ray-Ban glasses versus exoskeleton arms is not a strategic difference. It is product design trivia.

What is the secret here? The founders would presumably argue that egocentric video of natural, unscripted human activity in real environments is categorically superior to lab teleoperation data for training physical AI. That organic motion in a restaurant kitchen or hotel corridor captures something that a robot arm pushing objects on a lab bench cannot. This is a reasonable technical hypothesis. But it is not contrarian — micro1 is executing the identical thesis with identical hardware logic. When your supposed secret is shared by a competitor with seventy times your capital and demonstrated revenue at scale, it is no longer a secret. It is a race. And races are won by whoever has the most capital and the best distribution, which in this case is not a three-person pre-seed team.

The founders deserve credit for execution. Building a 5,000-contributor network across three continents while still completing undergraduate degrees at Berkeley is operationally impressive. Anshul Verma's experience at Scale AI — the company that defined data labeling at scale — provides genuine marketplace pattern recognition. His BAIR research adds ML depth. These are not empty credentials. But operational execution without a structural moat is just running faster on a treadmill. The technology — headband cameras, standard computer vision annotation pipelines producing 3D pose and depth maps — does not represent a discontinuity. Nothing here is 10x better than what exists. The annotation pipeline uses known techniques. A well-resourced competitor could replicate the entire stack in months.

The bull case deserves serious engagement. If you believe that the humanoid robot market will grow at 39% CAGR and that every robotics lab will need orders of magnitude more real-world training data, then the data provider that achieves the largest, most diverse contributor network first could become indispensable — a "Scale AI for physical AI." The data flywheel is real in principle: more environments and activities make the dataset more valuable, attracting more buyers, funding more collection. If Asimov captured this position before micro1 or Scale AI fully committed, the switching costs from accumulated dataset depth could create genuine defensibility. This is the Stripe analogy — specialized vertical focus beating the horizontal incumbent. But Stripe had proprietary technology that was an order of magnitude simpler than anything that existed. Asimov's technology is not an order of magnitude better than micro1's. And the buyer pool — a handful of frontier robotics labs — gives customers enormous bargaining power. When five buyers face four suppliers, the buyers win. Network effects in data marketplaces are far weaker than in social networks; a robotics lab does not care how many other labs also buy from Asimov.

The deeper structural problem is that this market may not converge toward a single winner at all. Robotics labs building humanoid robots — Physical Intelligence, Skild AI, 1X, Figure — are constructing their own proprietary data flywheels from deployed robots. As these companies scale, their internal data collection will eclipse any external marketplace. The window for third-party data providers may be transitional: useful while labs scale up, irrelevant once they have their own robots generating data in the field. A transitional data brokerage is not a monopoly. It is a service business with a ticking clock. Add the risk that simulation-first approaches (NVIDIA Cosmos) could reduce demand for real-world capture entirely, and the long-term defensibility picture deteriorates further.

I am passing. There is no secret that the consensus rejects. The competitive landscape is crowded and well-capitalized. The technology is competent but not discontinuous. The customer base is concentrated and has more leverage than the suppliers. The founders show execution ability but no evidence of the kind of definite, singular vision that would make me believe they can win a market that micro1 is attacking with seventy times the capital. This is a 1-to-n improvement in data collection logistics, not a 0-to-1 creation. The company may build a functioning business, but the structure of this market does not produce monopoly returns.
