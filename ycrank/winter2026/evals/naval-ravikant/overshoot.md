# Overshoot -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The founder pairing here is the most interesting thing about this company. Zakaria spent seven years at Uber building surge pricing -- systems where latency isn't a feature, it's the product -- then moved to Meta AI writing GPU kernels for inference engines. Younes was the founding engineer at Cosmonio, where he built a computer vision training and serving platform from scratch before Intel acquired the company. These are cousins who grew up together and then independently accumulated the exact two bodies of knowledge you'd need to build a real-time VLM inference platform: low-latency distributed systems and CV infrastructure engineering. That's not a founding team that read a market report and decided video AI was hot. That's two people whose careers converged on a problem before the problem had a name. Younes watched customers abandon traditional computer vision because it couldn't match the general intelligence of language models -- that's the kind of lived insight that produces conviction, not just a pitch.

The leverage architecture passes my basic test. An API that lets any developer connect a live video feed to a vision language model in three lines of code is genuine leverage -- it collapses weeks of infrastructure work into minutes. One engineering team optimizing the codec, streaming, and inference stack serves thousands of developers who would otherwise each solve the same hard problem independently. This is the picks-and-shovels pattern I've backed repeatedly: Alchemy didn't build DeFi apps, it built the API layer that every DeFi app needed. Overshoot is positioning itself as the inference layer that every real-time vision application needs. The structural shape is right.

But here's where my conviction breaks down: where is the compounding? The models Overshoot serves -- Qwen, InternVL -- are open-source. Any developer, any competitor, any incumbent can serve these same models. The differentiation lives entirely in the streaming and inference optimization layer. That's a real technical advantage today, but latency advantages in infrastructure have a half-life. Fireworks AI has $331 million. Together AI has $554 million. When VLM inference volume grows -- which is exactly what Overshoot is betting on -- these incumbents will optimize for video because the economics will demand it. Overshoot's specialization moat is another way of saying "we got there first in a niche the big players haven't prioritized yet." That's a timing advantage, not a structural one. There are no network effects in an inference API. The 301st developer doesn't make the platform better for the first 300. Each inference call is stateless. Stack Overflow compounds because every question-and-answer pair makes the knowledge graph denser. Overshoot's optimization layer does not have an equivalent compounding mechanism.

The contrarian timing argument is partially convincing. VLMs have just crossed the capability threshold for real-time video understanding, and the traditional CV platforms (Roboflow, Clarifai) are architecturally oriented toward the old paradigm of custom model training. There's a genuine window where the old platforms can't serve VLMs well and the general inference platforms don't prioritize video. But "AI infrastructure for the vision modality" is not a contrarian bet in 2026 -- it's a subcategory bet within the most consensus market in technology. Nobody smart is dismissing the idea that real-time video understanding via VLMs will be valuable. The disagreement is about whether a specialized two-person startup or a well-capitalized general platform will capture that value. That's a competitive question, not a contrarian one.

The bull case is straightforward and worth taking seriously: if video and image inference turns out to be fundamentally, permanently different from text inference -- if the codec, streaming, and latency requirements create a persistently separate optimization surface -- then specialization wins and Overshoot builds a durable infrastructure business in a $30+ billion market. The founders' specific knowledge gives them a genuine head start, and the family relationship between cousins suggests the kind of long-term commitment that survives the inevitable trough. If I squint, I can see a version of this where Overshoot becomes to real-time vision what Twilio became to communications -- the API layer everyone builds on because the underlying complexity is genuinely hard to replicate. That version requires the streaming/codec optimization gap to widen rather than narrow as scale increases. I'm not convinced it will, but I can construct the argument.

I like these founders. The specific knowledge is real, the infrastructure instinct is sound, and the family bond suggests durability. But the absence of a compounding mechanism -- no network effects, no proprietary data, no moat that deepens with usage -- means this is a race against well-funded incumbents where the prize for winning is temporary leadership in a niche that may not remain a niche. I want companies where time is the founder's ally. Here, time may be the enemy -- every quarter that passes gives Fireworks and Together more reason to add video-specific optimizations. The founders have the knowledge to build something valuable. The question is whether they can build something defensible. At pre-seed, I'd watch closely but hold my check.
