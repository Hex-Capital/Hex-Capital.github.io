# Superset -- Paul Graham Primary Source Research: Structured Quote Record Evaluation

The thing that jumps out about Superset isn't the founders or the market size. It's this line buried in the risk section: "Anthropic has already shipped native multi-agent orchestration for Claude Code." That's not a hypothetical competitive threat on a slide deck. That's the primary platform your product orchestrates building your core feature into their own product, right now, while you're in YC. I wrote in "Schlep Blindness" that the best opportunities are the ones your unconscious won't even let you see because they're too intimidating or tedious. Multi-agent orchestration for AI coding tools is the opposite of that. It's the most obvious idea in the hottest space in tech. When an idea is this consensus, it means every platform vendor, every IDE company, and every weekend hacker is building it simultaneously. The best startup ideas look initially like bad ideas. This one looks good to absolutely everyone, which is precisely why it worries me.

Three ex-YC CTOs finding each other and shipping a product to 8,000 GitHub stars in five months — that's real. Kiet Ho built Onlook to 25,000 stars, which demonstrates he knows how to ship developer tools that developers actually want to use. These are people who can build things. I don't question their technical ability. But I notice something: "No shared prior employer or university identified from available data." I've said that a startup does to the relationship between founders what a dog does to a sock — if it can be pulled apart, it will be. Three CTOs from three different YC companies who came together around an idea is a different founder dynamic than co-founders who've been through hell together. Maybe they bonded during their respective YC batches. Maybe they're great friends. But the dossier doesn't show me the relationship, and at pre-seed the relationship is load-bearing. The other question I keep turning over: all three left their prior YC companies. Onlook, Untether Labs, Adam — what happened? Sometimes the best founders are the ones who tried something, learned it wasn't working, and pivoted hard. That's the running back who goes sideways to get downfield. But sometimes serial starting is its own pattern.

The bull case is real and I want to take it seriously. If software development is genuinely shifting from "developer writes code in an editor" to "developer orchestrates a fleet of AI agents," then whoever builds the default orchestration layer is building something as fundamental as the IDE itself. Superset's agent-agnostic positioning is structurally clever — they benefit from every new CLI agent that ships rather than competing with any of them. Cursor can't build an agent-agnostic orchestrator because it would cannibalize their proprietary agent, which is their core differentiator at a $29 billion valuation. That structural incentive misalignment is a genuine insight. And 8,000 stars plus testimonials from builders at Microsoft, Netflix, OpenAI, and Google suggests real developer pull. For this to be a great investment, you'd need to believe that (a) CLI-based agents remain the dominant paradigm rather than IDE-integrated ones, (b) developers will routinely run 3-10 agents in parallel as a daily workflow, and (c) the orchestration layer is complex enough to sustain a standalone product. All three of those could be true. I'm just not convinced they will be.

Here's my core concern, and it's the one I keep coming back to. I wrote that "it's exceptionally rare for startups to be killed by competitors." But that's about other startups. Platform risk is different from competitive risk. When Anthropic ships native multi-agent orchestration into Claude Code itself, that's not a competitor — that's gravity. The dossier says "the barrier is UX polish and ecosystem integration, not deep technical complexity." That's exactly the kind of thing that gets absorbed as a feature. Stripe worked because payments involved years of regulatory complexity, bank relationships, and institutional schlep that no one wanted to touch. What's the analogous barrier here? A nicer diff viewer? Port forwarding? Those are features a platform can ship in a quarter. I don't see the deep structural moat that would let Superset compound its advantage faster than the platforms can absorb the feature. The Elastic License 2.0 is also a tell — it's source-available but not truly open-source, which limits the community flywheel relative to what it could be, and signals the founders are already worried about someone else running their code as a service.

The name collision issue isn't trivial either. Apache Superset has 63,000 GitHub stars. When a developer searches "superset" they'll find a data visualization tool, a startup studio, a stablecoin company, and an Indian hiring platform before they find this. For a developer tool that depends on organic discovery and word-of-mouth, sharing a name with an established open-source project in an adjacent space is genuinely costly. It's the kind of thing that seems like a small detail but compounds negatively over time.

These founders can build. The market is real. But I keep asking myself: would I put my own money into an orchestration layer for third-party AI agents when the agents themselves are evolving weekly and the platform vendors are actively shipping the same features? I wrote that "good people can fix bad ideas, but good ideas can't save bad people." These are good people. But I'm not sure this is a bad idea that's secretly good — I think it might be a good idea that's secretly a feature. I'd want to see evidence that they're going deep with individual developers, understanding workflows that no one else sees, finding the wedge that turns an orchestration layer into something the platforms can't replicate. Right now I see a well-executed launch but not yet the insight that makes this defensible.
