# InsForge -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

InsForge is a software-only, iteration-mode developer tools company with a real but rapidly-compressing interface secret about agent-native BaaS, sold into a category where the $5B incumbent ships the same primitive within a release cycle. Hang Huang and Tony Chang have named Supabase's dashboard-driven configuration as the specific blind spot that prevents agent-autonomous provisioning -- that is more secret than most of the SP26 batch carries, and it is also less secret than it needs to be when Supabase itself already ships an MCP server (sealos.io, 2026). The signature is a thin contrarian thesis stapled to a thick commoditization risk.

**Stripe** (2010) is the load-bearing analogy for InsForge's contrarian-secret dimension, because Patrick Collison named the same kind of failure mode -- legacy processors' merchant-onboarding UX could not be retrofitted to developer-first because the institutional shape of Braintree and PayPal was customer-success-led, not API-led. InsForge names the analogous failure mode for Supabase: human-facing dashboards as the configuration surface, where agents need structured MCP context over schemas, policies, and logs. Where InsForge diverges from the **Stripe** (2010) rung is that Collison's secret was guarded by seven-line integrations, bank partnerships, and PCI complexity that took competitors a decade to replicate; InsForge's secret is guarded by an Apache-2.0 repo, 10.7k GitHub stars, and a self-reported 1.6x benchmark on 127 backend tasks (insforge.dev/alternatives) that Supabase can match by shipping an MCP layer over the Postgres stack it already operates. The secret is real. The half-life of the secret is months, not years.

The atoms-heavy dimension is where InsForge sits flat on the floor of the Founders Fund manifesto. "We wanted flying cars, instead we got 140 characters" -- and what InsForge is shipping is a TypeScript layer over Postgres plus pgvector plus an S3-compatible object store, which is the canonical software-only profile that the 2011 manifesto attacked as indefinite-optimism capital allocation. The Tony Chang Databricks networking-infra pedigree is genuine engineering signal, but the moat InsForge will accumulate is interface convention, not regulatory certification depth or hardware capex. No government customer, no defense angle, no biotech wet-lab cost structure. The InsForge moat lives or dies on developer mindshare against Supabase's $544M raised and Convex's a16z-led Series A -- a fight where the well-capitalized incumbent has already started shipping the differentiator.

Mechanism analysis on monopoly trajectory: InsForge's pricing-power hypothesis terminates at "agent skill libraries create switching costs," and the compounding only works if MCP-specific InsForge semantics become the default that agents target. Over 18 months, the way this either compounds or collapses is binary -- either Anthropic and Cursor surface InsForge as a first-class agent-target in their default skill catalogs (compounding), or Supabase's MCP server becomes the de facto agent-native Postgres BaaS by virtue of its $70M ARR distribution (collapsing). The dossier shows 2,000 hosted databases and 5 enterprise teams self-reported at Product Hunt launch, which is a launch metric, not a switching-cost metric. Supabase's 250% YoY growth (Sacra, 2025) is the compounding curve InsForge needs to bend toward itself before the MCP-interface advantage commoditizes.

Counterfactual on the definite-plan dimension: InsForge would flip from indefinite to definite if the dossier named a 2-5 year technical commitment Hang Huang would not pivot from -- for example, a public commitment to ship a specific agent-runtime primitive (durable execution, transactional MCP sessions, agent-scoped Postgres row-level security) on a named quarter. What the dossier actually shows is v2.1.9, 44 releases, 34 open issues, and four Show HN posts over the past year (HN item IDs 44772898, 45291644, 45449787, 48181342) -- the shape of a project iterating to product-market fit, not the shape of the **SpaceX** (2008) reusability roadmap. Zero to One named this exact mode: it is the indefinite-optimism iteration cycle that Sand Hill defaults to and that capital should be allocated against, not into.

Single-anchor parallel on founder lineage: compared to Max Levchin at **Affirm** (2014), Tony Chang's Databricks networking-and-monitoring tenure plus Meta Ads ML Infra internship is real backend systems engineering, but it is not shipped-to-scale-as-a-founder signal. Hang Huang's Yale MBA, Amazon PM tour, and former professional League of Legends career is the credentialed-outsider biographical texture I sometimes weight toward -- the LoL detail is unusual enough to register on the intellectual-identity axis -- but the pairing is not the PayPal Mafia re-up channel that revealed-preference sources my checks. **Asana** (2010) was the Moskovitz override because he had shipped **Facebook** (2004) to a billion users; InsForge's founders have shipped internal systems at large companies and 4,147 commits to one open-source project, which is competent and not load-bearing.

The 36-month forecast: InsForge is acquired by Supabase, Vercel, or Cloudflare for sub-$100M in a talent-plus-IP deal, or it persists as a sub-$5M-ARR open-source project whose MCP primitives get absorbed back into the Supabase agent stack, with Tony Chang joining the acquirer's platform team. The mechanism producing this is straightforward: the contrarian secret InsForge named is correct but unguarded, the incumbent that the secret indicts has 50x the capital and the same Postgres substrate, and the founder pedigree lacks the technical-credentialed-outsider profile that historically overrides a thin structural moat.

The strongest opposing case is the **Stripe** (2010) parallel itself -- PayPal had won payments in 2010, every VC knew it, and the Collison brothers built a generational company anyway because the developer-experience secret turned out to be deeper than the incumbent could retrofit. Maybe Hang Huang and Tony Chang are running the same play against Supabase, and maybe MCP-native agent infrastructure is a deep enough substrate change that Supabase's human-UI institutional shape cannot pivot cleanly. The case stings because it is exactly the **Stripe** (2010) pattern I funded against the consensus once before. It loses on two facts in the dossier: Collison's secret was guarded by years of bank-integration and PCI complexity that took a decade to replicate, while InsForge's secret is guarded by an Apache-2.0 repository Supabase engineers can read in an afternoon; and Patrick Collison was an IMO-track credentialed-outsider technical founder, while the InsForge founder profile is Amazon-PM-plus-Databricks-SWE, which is competent operator pedigree but not the contrarian-secret-carrier profile that makes a thin moat survive an incumbent's response. The verdict flips if InsForge ships a defense, fintech, or regulated-healthcare customer base in the next 12 months where MCP-native provisioning becomes a compliance moat rather than a developer-convenience moat -- a regulated-customer pivot would put InsForge on the **Palantir** (2003) ladder rather than the consensus-BaaS ladder, and that I would re-underwrite.
