# Clawvisor -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Eric Levine sold Berbix to Socure for ~$70M in June 2023, came back as a YC Visiting Group Partner, and is now coming through YC again as a solo founder building authorization for AI agents. That biography ? repeat builder, prior exit, prior partner ? is a strong founder signal walking into a market that Composio just raised $25M for, Arcade raised $12M for, Anon raised $6.5M for, and Auth0 and WorkOS are both shipping into. Clawvisor's signature in my portfolio is the inverse of Stripe: a founder I would track anywhere, attacking an idea that is currently the consensus answer to "what's the obvious next infra layer for agents?" The whole evaluation turns on whether Levine's running-back quality wins faster than the idea's gravitational pull toward commoditization.

On founder learning rate ? my highest-scoring dimension for Clawvisor ? the high rung is Patrick Collison going from Auctomatic to Stripe, the mid rung is the Octopart team carrying physics-grad-school instincts into electronics search, and the low rung is the founder still pitching the same deck eighteen months later. Clawvisor pins between mid and high: Levine ran Berbix from YC S18 through a $70M Socure exit, then sat on the partner side of the table watching hundreds of YC interviews. That is a founder whose slope I trust. The reason Clawvisor doesn't reach the Collison rung is that Berbix and Clawvisor are adjacent ? identity verification to agent auth is a short leap, not a "I learned an entirely new domain" leap.

On idea-that-repels ? Clawvisor's lowest dimension ? the high rung is Stripe (payments compliance was a schlep nobody wanted) and the low rung is the McKinsey duo identifying a "massive B2B workflow opportunity" through customer interviews. Clawvisor sits one rung above that floor. The Composio Series A press release, the Arcade seed announcement, the Auth0 "Token Vault for AI Agents" product page, and Insight Partners and McKinsey both publishing 2025?2026 thought-leadership on "securing the agentic enterprise" mean every reasonably-informed investor in 2026 nods when they hear "auth for agents." That nod is the tell. The biggest startup ideas tend to repel, and nobody is repelled by Clawvisor's pitch ? they're checking whether the round is open. The 14+ adapters versus Composio's 500+ and Nango's 700+ is a symptom of the same thing: the schlep here is finite and being rapidly worked through by capitalized teams.

On organic problem discovery, the high rung is Drew Houston and the USB drive, the mid rung is the Octopart founders frustrated by parts search, and the low rung is the consultant identifying a TAM. Clawvisor sits at mid: Levine spent five years on Trust & Safety engineering at Airbnb and then built a forensics engine for spoofed identity documents at Berbix ? that is genuine domain adjacency, not market research. But the dossier contains no specific moment-of-frustration story where Levine watched an agent leak credentials or scope-creep through a token. The pattern looks like "I know this neighborhood, what's next door?" rather than "I lived this exact pain."

On founder resourcefulness, the high rung is the Airbnb cereal box story and the low rung is the founder who treats every obstacle as a reason to raise more money. The Berbix-to-Socure outcome puts Clawvisor in the upper-middle band ? Levine has shipped a company to a real exit, which is resourcefulness made manifest. What keeps Clawvisor off the top rung is the solo-founder structure, which is on my explicit avoid list in "The 18 Mistakes That Kill Startups." Solo doesn't kill the deal automatically when the founder is already a known quantity ? but it shaves the score, because there's no co-founder to talk Levine out of stupid decisions while he ships gateway adapters against ten-engineer teams. On earnest builder identity, the high rung is Watsi and the low rung is the buzzword deck; the open-source Go core at github.com/clawvisor/clawvisor and 86 stars suggests a genuine builder, but there is no public artifact of the obsession ? no essays, no Hacker News presence, no working-in-public ? that would push Clawvisor up toward Replit-on-HN territory.

My 36-month forecast for Clawvisor: acquired by Okta/Auth0 or WorkOS in the $40?80M range as a team-and-tech tuck-in, mirroring the Berbix-to-Socure template almost exactly. The mechanism: Levine's pattern is to build a credible identity-adjacent product, sell it to the natural strategic buyer in the identity stack, and move on. Composio and Arcade will continue burning capital chasing the standalone-platform outcome; Clawvisor's solo-founder structure and Levine's prior playbook strongly bias toward the same M&A endpoint, particularly if MCP standardizes the protocol layer and compresses standalone-runtime valuations.

The strongest case against my forecast is the Stripe analogy, where I funded Patrick Collison for $15K because I had tracked him from Auctomatic and trusted the founder more than the consensus read of the payments market. Garry Tan, listed as Clawvisor's group partner, has the same ground-truth on Levine that I had on Collison ? this is exactly the long-term-founder-tracking unstated pattern in my own portfolio. The objection stings. What dismantles it is that Stripe in 2010 was contrarian (developer-API payments seemed like a bad idea to most people), while Clawvisor in 2026 is consensus (Composio just raised $25M from Lightspeed for the same insight). The follow-the-founder pattern is necessary but not sufficient ? it has to be paired with an idea that other smart people are passing on, and Clawvisor fails that second filter. The verifiable condition that would flip my verdict: a named enterprise design partner ? a Stripe, a Notion, an Anthropic ? running production traffic through Clawvisor's gateway with a public case study, demonstrating that purpose-based per-call enforcement is being chosen over Auth0 Token Vault by buyers who have actually compared them.
