# Belong -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Nick Holmstén was in a Swedish Britpop band called Apple Brown Betty before he ever set foot inside Spotify. Ash Pournouri shepherded Avicii from a teenager uploading tracks to one of the most commercially dominant artist brands on the planet. Between them, these two have touched nearly every surface of the global music economy — platform infrastructure, artist management, label operations, conference production, venture investing. When I look at Belong, the founder-market fit density is the most striking feature: Nick spent seven years as Spotify's Global Head of Music watching fans generate billions in engagement data with almost nothing flowing back to them or to artists in a personalized way, while Ash lived the other side of that equation managing an artist whose superfans drove an economic engine across tickets, merchandise, and brand partnerships that fragmented across dozens of intermediaries. That level of dual-sided domain expertise at pre-seed is rare. But here's where I get stuck — when I ask my opening question, "tell me the story, not the business," what I hear is two executives who reached the summit of an industry and looked down to spot a gap. That's market insight from a position of power, not a biographical crucible that made this problem inescapable. Nick's musician roots and Ash's immigrant journey building from Stockholm's margins add texture, but this reads closer to "I was at Google and saw the opportunity" than to founders who would be building this without funding.

The invisible system test is where Belong loses me. My strongest investments attack opaque structures that extract value from people who don't understand them — the taxi medallion cartel beneath Uber, the credit card fine print beneath Affirm, the property tax labyrinth beneath Ownwell. Belong's premise is that fans drive economic activity but receive no recognition. That's real, but it's a missing product, not a hidden tax. Fans know what they pay for concert tickets and merchandise. There's no information asymmetry being weaponized against them, no intermediary deliberately obscuring the value chain to extract rent. Streaming platforms capture fan engagement data, sure — but Belong isn't attacking Spotify's business model. It's building alongside it. Compare this to Carta: before Carta, cap tables were literally managed in spreadsheets controlled by lawyers who charged hourly to tell founders what they owned. That's an extractive, opaque system. "Fans don't get rewarded for being fans" is a product gap, not structural exploitation. The distinction matters in my framework because product gaps attract well-resourced competitors, while structural disruptions create the kind of righteous conviction that carries founders through near-death moments.

Belong doesn't make sophisticated investors shift in their chairs, which is a signal I pay close attention to. Goldman Sachs is publicly projecting $4.2B in superfan revenue by 2030. a16z Speedrun accepted the company. Troy Carter — former Lady Gaga manager, now CEO of Venice Music — wrote an angel check. The category is consensus-attractive: fan engagement, fintech rails, creator economy. None of these terms provoke the discomfort I felt backing Anduril when defense tech was politically toxic, or Contraline when male birth control made VCs physically squirm, or Flock Safety when surveillance cameras triggered civil liberties panic. A fan rewards card is a pleasant idea that generates nodding at dinner parties, not the "that's insane" reaction that has preceded my best outcomes. The novelty of combining fintech with fandom is real — no one has done this exact thing — but novel product combination and contrarian conviction are different animals.

The bull case deserves honest engagement because the Rolodex here is arguably the strongest I've seen at pre-seed in the entertainment space. If Nick Holmstén can pick up the phone and get top-tier artists to offer Belong-exclusive drops, and if Ash Pournouri can leverage relationships with Daniel Ek and the broader Nordic music-tech ecosystem to drive viral adoption loops, the cold-start problem that kills most two-sided marketplaces could be solved through sheer network density rather than marketing spend. The Banking-as-a-Service inflection is genuine — Mastercard BIN programs and sponsor banks like Middlesex Federal Savings have made branded card products possible for startups in a way that wasn't true five years ago. And FanScore, if it becomes the de facto metric for fan engagement, could create the kind of switching costs that made Carta sticky: once your identity is built there, leaving means abandoning accumulated status. The $2.7M in seed funding and SOC 2 compliance achieved by March 2026 suggest execution velocity. What would have to be true is that artist-exclusive experiences become genuinely scarce and desirable enough that fans adopt a new banking product to access them — and that Fanatics ONE (10M existing FanCash members) doesn't expand from sports into music before Belong reaches critical mass.

The defensibility question is entangled with the cold-start problem. A proprietary fan identity graph tied to financial transaction data — something no streaming platform or bank independently possesses — is a legitimate emerging moat. SOC 2, Mastercard BIN, and FDIC-insured sponsor bank partnerships create a compliance barrier that gives Belong a 6-12 month lead over any new entrant. But "emerging" and "potential" are doing heavy lifting here: the alpha launched March 2026, no artist partnerships have been announced publicly, and Belong is competing for brand recognition against multiple companies using the same name across fintech and gaming. The infrastructure is being built, but the network effects that would make it defensible haven't begun to compound.

My decision comes down to two dimensions. The biographical forge is strong on domain expertise but moderate on inevitability — Holmstén and Pournouri are the right people to build this, but the absence of a deeper biographical stake in the problem means I can't distinguish their motivation from any talented executive spotting an adjacent opportunity. The invisible system criterion is where Belong falls furthest from my thesis: there is no opaque, extractive structure being ripped open. Fan spending is visible and voluntary. What Belong builds is new infrastructure, not structural disruption of a hidden system. Those two dimensions together account for 50 of my 100 points, and Belong scores below my conviction threshold on both.
