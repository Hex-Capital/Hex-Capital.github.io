# Panacea -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Panacea is a services rollup wearing an AI wrapper, and the highest-scoring dimension here — Haris Javed-Akhtar's milestone-priced consulting model, which is structurally closer to default-alive than a SaaS SAFE-cap — is fighting the lowest-scoring dimension, which is that two founders with no Twitter accounts, no GitHub, and no opinionated writing on FDA reform have nothing on the cap table except their own labor and an unnamed ex-FDA bench. That pairing tells me what this is: a bench-quality regulatory affairs shop with AI leverage, not a venture moment, and the fact that Weave Bio already cleared $20M from USVP in October 2025 with a Parexel partnership in tow means the consensus money has already picked its horse in this exact lane.

**Capital structure (the high rung).** Teamshares is the load-bearing analogy on this axis — an unsexy execution-machine where the financing instrument did the work the SaaS cap table couldn't, because the underlying asset (small-business equity rollups) was illegible to traditional venture math. Panacea's fixed milestone pricing has a piece of that DNA: payment-on-deliverable for a 510(k) or IND filing is closer to a project-finance instrument than a SaaS contract, and a two-person team billing $26K+ user-fee-adjacent project fees can plausibly reach cash-flow positive on a handful of engagements without a Series A. But where Teamshares structured an entirely non-standard equity vehicle around employee ownership, Panacea is taking standard YC paper into a standard services business — Haris hasn't structured anything I couldn't get elsewhere, and the "AI-native" wrapper doesn't change the underlying asset class. It's venture-shaped enough not to be a drainage round, but it's not the instrument-design opportunity that would make me lean in.

**Founder-as-brand (the low rung).** The Marina Mogilko deal worked because the failure mode of a generic creator deal — no owned audience, depends on paid acquisition — was inverted: she walked in with millions of subscribers and zero CAC for life. Run that test against Panacea: Haris has no public X account, no GitHub, no essays on FDA submission reform, no podcast on medtech regulatory; Yaman has the same null set. The dossier literally records "No public data found" across the founder reach row. For a B2B enterprise sale into risk-averse biotech CROs this isn't fatal — nobody buys 510(k) consulting because they like the CEO's Substack — but it eliminates one of my two cheapest sources of alpha, and combined with the unnamed ex-FDA bench claim on withpanacea.com it means Panacea's entire credibility surface is YC's logo. That's a thin moat against an incumbent like RQM+ ($301M raised, PitchBook) walking into the same room.

**Round dynamics (mechanism analysis).** The pre-Series-A regatta here looks ugly over 18 months. Weave Bio just took $20M from USVP in October 2025 and announced the Parexel partnership in the same news cycle; that's a competitor with a 30-month cash runway and an enterprise distribution channel into the exact biotech buyer Panacea is chasing. By month 12, Panacea's seed runway burns into a market where the consensus AI-regulatory bid is already deployed and selling — meaning Panacea's next tack has to be priced against a comp that already cleared the round Panacea hasn't started. "AI is just more internet" applies cleanly: this is consensus-priced exposure dressed up as a non-consensus services wedge, and the wedge gets narrower every quarter.

**Founder conviction (counterfactual).** For this dimension to flip from low to mid-tier, Haris would need to have published something — a Substack on the structural inefficiency of FDA submission consulting, a Twitter thread on why RQM+'s hourly model is a margin trap, an essay on what he saw at Anterior that made him quit. None of that exists in the dossier. The MBBS from Imperial and the Anterior partnerships role establish founder-market fit, which is necessary but not sufficient — every YC healthcare-AI batch produces three founders with this CV. Without a non-obvious belief Haris will not be talked out of, the prior reverts to "competent operator responding to an opportunity," which is the missionary-vs-mercenary distinction I lose money on when I get it wrong.

**Category sleeve (single-anchor parallel).** Ro is the cleanest analogy in my portfolio — healthcare with a regulatory schlep moat, where the licensing complexity itself was the asset. Ro's exception status in my framework came from the fact that only venture capital could clear 50-state prescription rails; the capital intensity was the moat, not the failure mode. Panacea is structurally adjacent — FDA submission pathways are also schleps — but the analogy breaks because Panacea is selling consulting services about the schlep rather than building infrastructure that resolves it. Ro owns a prescription rail; Panacea rents expertise about a regulatory rail. That's the difference between a venture-moment asset and a high-quality professional services firm, and I price them very differently.

**Forecast.** Over 36 months Panacea reaches $2-6M in services revenue on the strength of Haris's medical credibility and the milestone-pricing wedge against RQM+, then either gets acqui-hired by Parexel, IQVIA, or ProPharma as an AI-augmented regulatory affairs bolt-on at a low-eight-figure outcome, or raises a flat-to-down Series A that struggles against Weave Bio's already-deployed Parexel partnership. The mechanism is straightforward: services revenue compounds linearly with consultant headcount, Weave Bio's SaaS revenue compounds nonlinearly with seat expansion, and the gap widens every quarter until Panacea sells itself into a distribution channel rather than building one.

**The strongest opposing case.** The bull case is the Ro override: FDA regulatory is a genuine schlep, an MD CEO plus an AI platform CTO out of Anterior is a rare two-headed asset, and Weave Bio is SaaS-not-service which means Panacea is actually in a different lane than the funded incumbent — selling the deliverable rather than the tool. If Haris recruits two named ex-FDA reviewers in the next six months, the unverified bench claim resolves into a real moat that RQM+ structurally cannot match because their hourly billing model can't absorb fixed-price downside risk. That's a real argument and it stings. The dossier evidence that wins for me anyway: zero founder writing, zero owned distribution, a standard cap table, and a direct competitor with 30x the capital and a Parexel partnership already announced — none of those resolve by Haris being a good doctor. The one verifiable condition that flips my verdict is two named ex-FDA reviewers joining Panacea with public attribution on the site within 90 days; absent that, I pass.
