# Humwork -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Yash Goenka got into YC twice in under a year — S25 with Qualify.bot, P26 with Humwork — both operating under the same legal entity, Orange AI Inc. That pattern activates two competing signals in my framework simultaneously. On one hand, it's exactly what I saw with Patrick Collison: a young, prolific builder (Yash has shipped at least six ventures, holds a graphene supercapacitor patent, wrote avionics software for a rocket that hit Mach 1.2) whose previous company taught him something that led to something better. Qualify.bot was AI phone agents helping commercial lenders — real-time AI-to-human interaction in a time-sensitive domain. Humwork generalizes that exact mechanic. On the other hand, six ventures in roughly five years means Yash has averaged less than a year per idea. The running back looks for openings, yes. But at some point you have to pick a gap and hit it. The dossier gives me a list of things shipped, not stories of walls hit and hacked through. I don't have Humwork's cereal box story.

Tendem, backed by $72M from Bezos Expeditions and Nebius, already operates an MCP-based expert network with 10,000+ experts — ten times Humwork's 1,000+ — and has existing relationships with Anthropic, the company that created MCP. Humwork's differentiation claim is narrower focus on real-time agent debugging with a 30-second matching SLA versus Tendem's broader task orientation. That's a positioning choice, not a moat. The MCP integration layer is technically replicable; Tendem has already replicated it. Humwork's 87% resolution rate and 130% net retention in software engineering are genuinely good metrics for pre-seed, but they describe quality of a small operation, not defensibility against a competitor with ten times the supply-side density and fifty times the capital. When Stripe took on payments infrastructure, the schlep — PCI compliance, bank integrations, regulatory overhead across countries — created structural barriers that scaled with the problem's complexity. Humwork's schlep is operational (recruiting and vetting experts, maintaining real-time availability) but doesn't compound the same way. A larger expert network is just... a larger expert network.

Humwork's origin story is where my framework gets most skeptical. The dossier describes the problem in market terms — "AI agents frequently stall on edge cases requiring human judgment" — not in personal terms. I don't find Yash or Rohan saying "we were running Qualify.bot and our agents kept getting stuck and we had to manually rescue them every day." The structural similarity between Qualify.bot and Humwork is real, and I weight it: building a product where AI agents interact with humans in real-time and then building the generalized platform for agent-to-human escalation is a plausible organic pipeline. But plausible isn't the same as demonstrated. The best organic discovery stories — Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive, the Octopart founders searching for electronic parts — have a specificity that's unmistakable. Humwork's story reads more like a founder surveying the AI agent landscape and identifying a trend, which is exactly the pattern I've learned to be cautious about. The YC Spring 2026 batch was "dominated by autonomous agent pitches." When everyone is building agents, building agent infrastructure sounds smart. That's the problem — it sounds smart to everyone.

Rohan Datta's five years as a data scientist at Microgrid Labs and his AI voice platform work (1M+ call minutes) give Humwork solid technical depth, and the 16-year friendship between the co-founders — former Berkeley roommates — is the strongest co-founder relationship signal in the dossier. That matters. Co-founder conflict is a top startup killer, and these two have sixteen years of evidence that they can work together. Rohan's Civil Engineering background is unusual for this space, but the ML research portfolio (knowledge distillation, NLP, recommender systems) shows he's built real systems. Together, they can clearly build the product themselves — no non-technical founding team red flag here. But Humwork is fundamentally a marketplace business wearing a developer tools costume. The MCP server is a thin integration layer; the hard problem is supply-side liquidity across domains and time zones, expert quality management, and matching speed. That's an operations challenge more than an engineering challenge, and neither founder's background is in marketplace operations or logistics at scale.

The bull case for Humwork deserves honest construction. Here it is: the human-in-the-loop AI market is projected at $16.4B by 2030, MCP created a new distribution channel overnight, and Humwork is the focused vertical player against Tendem's horizontal sprawl. Yash is a repeat YC founder who has shipped real products with real metrics — Qualify.bot's 4x loan conversion improvement, the voice platform's 1M+ minutes. The Qualify.bot → Humwork transition isn't random pivoting; it's a founder who discovered through building that the real opportunity was one abstraction layer deeper. At 1,000+ experts with 87% resolution and 130% net retention, they've built meaningful supply-side traction before raising significant capital. YC partner Tyler Bosmeny validated this team twice. And the schlep of managing real-time human expertise for AI agents is operationally ugly enough to repel the pure-software founders who dominate the agent space. I take this seriously — especially the structural connection to Qualify.bot and the supply-side traction. But I can't get past the competitive reality. Tendem isn't a slow-moving incumbent; it's a well-funded startup doing the same thing with dramatically more resources and existing platform relationships with Anthropic itself. And the idea doesn't pass my Black Swan test: nobody reacts to "human experts for AI agents" with confusion or hostility. It's an obviously good idea, which means the returns are already being competed away. The condition that would flip me to Invest: evidence that Humwork's resolution rate at 30-second matching speed is structurally unreachable by Tendem's broader model — specific data showing that vertical focus on agent debugging creates a matching quality advantage that horizontal expert networks can't replicate even with 10x the supply.

Humwork scores strongest on founder learning rate — the dual YC admission and Qualify.bot → Humwork structural pivot show adaptive capacity — and weakest on contrarian positioning, where a trending idea with a $72M-funded direct competitor doing essentially the same thing is the opposite of what I look for. The decisive dimensions are Organic Problem Discovery (the Qualify.bot connection is suggestive but the specific frustration narrative is absent) and Idea That Repels Before It Attracts (it doesn't — it attracts immediately, and Tendem proves it). Competent founders, real operational traction, but in a space where the idea is obviously good and someone with fifty times the capital arrived first. That combination puts Humwork in the range where I respect the execution but don't reach for my checkbook.
