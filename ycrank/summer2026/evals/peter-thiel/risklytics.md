# Risklytics -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Samuel Gold and Alexander Risio are two Harvard CS founders selling an "Ember" wildfire risk API into a market where ZestyAI's Z-FIRE is already deployed with carriers writing 40% of California homeowners policies -- Risklytics is the archetype of a regulated-adjacent ML wrapper where the sector is correctly identified but the contrarian secret is missing. The dossier's strongest number is 13,685,446 California homes scored on 131,929 CAL FIRE inspections; the weakest is that CAL FIRE damage data is publicly available and the incumbent trained on 200B+ datapoints across 1,500+ wildfires. Risklytics has picked a real vacuum -- California's $800B?$1.3T wildfire coverage gap and the May 2025 CA DOI approval of forward-looking cat models -- and then walked into it behind three funded competitors.

**Stripe** (2010) is the load-bearing analogy on the contrarian-secret axis, because Patrick Collison named a specific architectural failure mode: PayPal had "won" payments but had abandoned the developer as a customer, and legacy processors could not ship a seven-line integration because their organizations were not built around API primitives. Risklytics tries to occupy the same rhetorical slot -- "retrofit-path-to-insurability as the API output" -- but the divergence is exact and fatal. ZestyAI is not an incumbent that structurally cannot ship retrofit recommendations; it is a well-funded ML company using the same GBT approach on a larger dataset. Risklytics has named a feature-level differentiation and dressed it as a thesis. The Zero to One question -- what does the founder believe that ZestyAI and Moody's-CAPE actively reject -- has no answer in this dossier. Gold and Risio do not appear to hold any secret about wildfire underwriting that Attila Toth at ZestyAI would dispute.

**Cleantech 1.0** is the failure pattern I test Risklytics against on monopoly trajectory, because those companies died not from technical failure but from selling into markets where incumbents controlled the regulated-approval bottleneck and the buyer trusted the incumbent's filed model. Risklytics faces the identical structure: CA DOI release 052-2025 in May 2025 approved exactly three forward-looking wildfire cat models -- Verisk, Karen Clark, and Moody's -- and Risklytics is not among them. That means Ember cannot enter a filed rate model until it clears the same review, which forces Risklytics into non-filed use cases (eligibility screening, underwriting triage) where switching costs are shallow and the buyer is already integrated with ZestyAI or HazardHub inside Guidewire's policy admin platform. The 91% discrimination rate on the January 2025 LA fires is real, but a modeling advantage that cannot enter the rate filing is a feature, not a monopoly.

Definite plan compounds or degrades depending on whether the founder commits to an architectural choice competitors cannot copy within 18 months. Risklytics has a defined product (California wildfire, GBT on CAL FIRE data, retrofit recommendations) but no disclosed multi-year commitment -- no roadmap for CA DOI model certification, no named path to multi-peril or geographic expansion beyond California, no closed-loop retrofit feedback pipeline with a named carrier. Over 18 months, a two-person team without a definite regulatory strategy will iterate toward whichever carrier picks up the phone, which is indefinite optimism in the exact sense Zero to One (2014) attacks.

Counterfactually, Risklytics's founder lineage score flips a tier only if Gold or Risio had shipped catastrophe modeling at Verisk, RMS, or a top-ten P&C carrier -- the Levchin-at-Affirm shape where domain-plus-technical is stacked in one operator. What the dossier shows instead is Samuel Gold leaving Harvard after one year with involvement in Harvard Undergraduate Capital Partners, and Alexander Risio with a Harvard AB/SM in CS focused on "systems, intelligence, biology." No actuarial background, no prior insuretech role, no advisor from the CA DOI or a carrier's chief underwriting office is named. This is credentialed but not credentialed-outsider in the Palmer Luckey sense; it is median YC-CS, and my prior is that median-YC-CS does not clear a regulated-incumbent moat.

**Palantir** (2003) is the single-anchor parallel on the regulated-sector axis, and the comparison is instructive precisely because it fails. Palantir's wedge into intelligence agencies took 15 years of consulting-heavy integration to compound into pricing power because the buyer was structurally unable to build the software internally. Risklytics's buyer -- a California admitted carrier or E&S writer -- is fully able to license ZestyAI's Z-FIRE tomorrow, has probably already done so, and is not blocked by a security-clearance or integration-depth barrier that would give Risklytics multi-year switching-cost protection. Insurance is regulated, but the regulation gates the model, not the vendor relationship. Risklytics inherits the slow sales cycle of a regulated sector without inheriting the moat.

My 36-month forecast on Risklytics is acqui-hire at sub-$15M by a mid-tier insuretech distributor or by ZestyAI itself consolidating its CA position, with the modal outcome being quiet wind-down after the pre-seed runway on failure to sign a top-20 CA carrier. The mechanism is direct: Ember is a better-packaged GBT model in a market where the funded incumbent has 5x the training data and the filed-model incumbents have the CA DOI seal. The retrofit-path framing is a UX layer, not a defensible product category, and two Harvard CS founders without carrier relationships will not out-distribute Attila Toth or Moody's field organization.

The strongest opposing case is that CA DOI's May 2025 opening of forward-looking cat models is a genuine regime change and Risklytics is early in a category still forming -- if Gold and Risio close two mid-tier E&S carriers on retrofit-rescoring within 12 months, the closed-loop data on which retrofits actually convert declines to binds becomes proprietary and unavailable to Z-FIRE, which optimizes for the filed-rate use case ZestyAI already owns. The bull mechanism is real. It fails on the dossier evidence because no carrier partnership is disclosed, no revenue is disclosed, no advisor from the underwriting side is named, and the founders have no prior relationships in the CA insurance market to accelerate the first two carrier signatures. I would flip the verdict on one verifiable condition: Risklytics enters CA DOI review as a fourth forward-looking wildfire cat model, or announces a signed integration with a top-20 California homeowners carrier. Absent either, this is the "AI for X" archetype my Pass example was written for. Pass.
