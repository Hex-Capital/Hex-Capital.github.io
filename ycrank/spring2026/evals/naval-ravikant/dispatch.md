# Dispatch -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

I backed Varda Space Industries. So I know this market from the inside, which means I also know the difference between owning the physics and servicing the physics. Varda creates leverage through microgravity — pharmaceutical compounds that literally cannot exist under Earth's gravity. That's a moat written in the laws of thermodynamics. Dispatch is building the truck that drives the product to the warehouse. Important work. But trucks get commoditized. The leverage lives in what's being manufactured, not in how it gets home. Seven funded competitors are building reentry vehicles right now, and Varda — the most advanced player — already owns its own capsule. Dispatch is a logistics layer in a market where the most important customer may never need them.

Now, the thing that kept me from dismissing this in thirty seconds: two engineers built a full-scale reentry heat shield in an apartment for a claimed 100x less than competitors. That's not a pitch deck claim — there's a Mojave Desert test on video. When someone achieves a 100x cost reduction on something that costs other companies millions, it means they've found something. Case's thermal engineering background from the Lunar Gateway program and Mello's avionics work at Astranis cover the two core disciplines of reentry — thermal protection and guidance. They didn't study this market and hire engineers. They ARE the engineers. Building a heat shield in your apartment because you can't not build it is the kind of behavior I associate with principals, not agents. The co-founder relationship is right — forged at the same company, complementary skills, shared context. These are not strangers from a founder-matching event.

But specific knowledge must be evaluated against the competitive field, not in isolation. Thousands of aerospace engineers at JPL, SpaceX, Lockheed, and a dozen startups possess similar thermal and avionics expertise. Case and Mello's knowledge is relevant — deeply relevant — but I can't call it non-fungible in a field where Varda has $329M, Inversion has $125M, and at least five other teams are attacking the identical problem. The 100x cost claim is the potential differentiator. If it holds at production scale and through actual orbital reentry — not just ground simulation with rocket exhaust — it could constitute genuine specific knowledge productized into a structural cost advantage. But a ground test simulating Mach 20 conditions is not the same as surviving actual atmospheric reentry at Mach 20. Inversion Space had $125M in resources and their first orbital reentry attempt still failed due to a propulsion short circuit. The gap between demonstration and operation in aerospace swallows companies.

The leverage audit is where my framework gets uncomfortable. This is not code. This is not AI. This is capital-intensive hardware requiring orbital launches, recovery operations, refurbishment infrastructure, and eventually space stations. Every additional mission requires significant marginal cost — fuel, launch fees, operations teams, regulatory compliance. The refurbishability angle adds some leverage over time, but this is structurally a business that scales with capital and headcount, not one that scales with code while you sleep. My Varda exception applies when physics creates leverage so unique it overrides the team-density concern — but Dispatch doesn't own the physics of microgravity manufacturing. They own a vehicle. Vehicles are capital equipment, not permissionless leverage.

The strongest bull case: Dispatch becomes the neutral logistics backbone of in-space manufacturing. If ten companies are manufacturing in orbit by 2030, they all need downmass, and a low-cost reentry-as-a-service provider with 100x cheaper heat shields captures an outsized share of every mission. The "picks and shovels" positioning means they don't need to pick the winning ISM application — pharma, semiconductors, fiber optics — they just need the market to exist. The access democratization angle is real: today, only companies that build their own capsule (Varda) or piggyback on NASA contracts (Dragon) can return payloads. Dispatch could give every startup with a microgravity experiment a way home. That's structural access expansion, and it's the pattern behind several of my best investments. If the 100x cost advantage is real and defensible, it compounds — cheaper vehicles mean more missions, more data, faster refurbishment cycles, and a cost curve no competitor can match without rebuilding from scratch.

But the capital gap is a wall. Getting from a $500K YC check to a first orbital flight requires $50-100M+ based on every comparable company's trajectory. That means multiple large fundraises in a market where Varda is already the category-defining winner and Inversion has locked up the DoD lane. Dispatch will be raising against companies with operational flight heritage. And Reditus Space — same YC batch, same stage, same model — creates a direct comparison that dilutes any category ownership claim. Two indistinguishable YC reentry startups is a signal that this opportunity has been pattern-matched by the ecosystem, which means it's approaching consensus. I get paid for being right first. When seven teams are chasing the same idea, being right isn't the hard part — surviving long enough to be the one that's left is.

Competent founders, real problem, genuine technical chops, strong principal signals. But the leverage architecture doesn't fit my framework, the capital requirements are enormous, the competitive field is crowded, and the specific knowledge — while solid — isn't the non-fungible kind that makes a company irreplaceable. I'd want to see an orbital reentry before writing a check.
