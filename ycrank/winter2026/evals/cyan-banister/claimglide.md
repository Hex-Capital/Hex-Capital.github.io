# ClaimGlide -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that strikes me about ClaimGlide isn't the founder or the product -- it's that everyone already agrees this is a problem. Prior authorization is one of the most talked-about pain points in American healthcare. The AMA publishes annual surveys about it. CMS just finalized a major rule mandating standardized APIs for it. Multiple companies have raised hundreds of millions to address various pieces of it. When I look for investments, I'm looking for the thing that makes other investors say "that's crazy" or "that market doesn't exist." Nobody says that about prior auth automation. This is a well-mapped battlefield where Cohere Health has raised $200 million, McKesson's CoverMyMeds is an entrenched incumbent, and Foundation Health just closed a $20 million Series A. The specific niche -- provider-side submission for private practices -- is somewhat less contested, but it's a positioning choice within a consensus category, not a fundamentally different insight. I don't feel that jolt.

When I ask myself the question I always start with -- tell me this founder's story, not the business -- I find a capable young person with excellent credentials and no visible biographical connection to this problem. Nami Lindquist comes from Bellevue, attended Penn's M&T program (one of the most selective dual-degree tracks in the country), interned at SpaceX, co-founded a senior care AI startup that won prizes and ran real pilots. That's an impressive trajectory. But it's impressive in the way that makes me think someone studied a market and picked a problem, rather than being forged by it. Where's the medical office manager in their family who spent nights on hold with Aetna? Where's the parent whose practice nearly went under because of denied authorizations? The pivot from senior care AI to prior auth automation doesn't tell me a story about obsession -- it tells me about pattern matching. And at this stage, before there are customers or revenue or any proof that the product works, the founder's story is nearly all I have to go on.

The strongest case for ClaimGlide lives in the dimension I care about most after the founder -- economic access. Private practices are the backbone of community healthcare in America, especially in underserved areas where hospital systems don't operate. When a small practice loses revenue to denied prior authorizations, that's a doctor who might close their doors, a community that loses access to care, staff who lose their jobs. If ClaimGlide actually works -- if it genuinely increases approval rates and speeds up authorizations for practices that can't afford dedicated billing departments -- that's meaningful impact. It makes a system that's opaque and punishing for small operators more navigable. That resonates with me the way Affirm resonated: bringing transparency and access where bureaucratic complexity creates barriers for the people who can least afford them.

But I keep coming back to what would have to be true for this to be a great investment, and the list is long. You'd need Nami to develop deep domain expertise in a field they've never worked in. You'd need a solo founder to build and maintain live integrations with four different EMR systems while simultaneously selling to fragmented private practices. You'd need the incumbents -- athenahealth, eClinicalWorks, NextGen -- to not build this themselves, even though the CMS-0057-F rule gives them every incentive to do so. You'd need the well-funded payer-side companies not to expand into provider tools. And you'd need all of this to happen without the kind of deeply personal founder commitment that sustains someone through years of healthcare sales cycles and regulatory friction. When I backed Contraline, the founder had spent years in academic research on the specific biology he was commercializing -- his biography and his company were inseparable. That's what gives me confidence someone will persist through the valley. I don't see that biographical anchor here.

This is a smart person attacking a real problem with decent timing, and I genuinely respect the ambition. But for my money -- and it is my money -- I need to see either a founder whose story makes me believe they'll fight through anything to solve this particular problem, or an idea so non-obvious that I'm one of the few people who sees it. ClaimGlide gives me neither. The problem is consensus, the approach is technically replicable, and the founder-problem connection is a gap I can't bridge with credentials alone. I'd want to sit down with Nami and hear the story behind the story -- maybe there's something the dossier doesn't capture. But based on what's visible, this is a pass.
