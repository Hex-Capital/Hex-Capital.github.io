# DroneTector -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The first thing I notice about DroneTector is the competitive landscape. Five funded competitors, one of them public with AU$221 million in cash, another backed by Bill Gates with $195 million, a third already acquired by Axon -- and this 3-person team is entering the ring with a PhD thesis and YC's standard check. Now, I've backed plenty of companies where incumbents were better funded. When I invested in Juniper, Cisco's market cap was in the tens of billions. But the difference was structural: Cisco's CTO told me they would never build a TCP/IP router, which meant Juniper had a window measured in years. Here, Echodyne, Robin Radar, and MatrixSpace are all actively building counter-drone radar and could extend into millimeter-wave frequencies. The window isn't structural -- it's a head start in a specific frequency band that the underlying physics community already understands. That's a race, not a moat.

Let me address the consequence question directly. If DroneTector succeeds completely -- becomes the dominant nano-drone detection radar -- what changes? The anti-drone detection segment reaches maybe $3.4 billion by 2030. That's a real business, and drone threats are genuinely escalating. Ukraine's 1,600 attacks per month, the Gatwick shutdown, Red Sea shipping disruption -- these are serious problems. But "better radar for detecting small drones" doesn't replace a system. It fills a gap within the existing defense and security apparatus. The world doesn't operate differently if DroneTector wins; airports and military bases have slightly better sensors. Compare that to Commonwealth Fusion Systems, where success means fossil fuels become obsolete, or Impossible Foods, where success means we reclaim half the planet's arable land. DroneTector's ceiling is a successful defense technology company -- valuable, yes, but not the kind of consequence magnitude that makes me ignore a 90% failure probability.

The timing, I'll concede, is nearly perfect. The drone threat escalated faster than anyone predicted. Three years ago, counter-drone was a niche concern; today it's a strategic priority for every NATO government. The spending curve is accelerating at 26-29% CAGR, and the specific problem DroneTector addresses -- nano-drones too small for conventional radar -- is only getting worse as $50 commercial drones become battlefield weapons. If I were scoring purely on "skating to where the puck will be," DroneTector is well-positioned. They're building ahead of the nano-drone wave, not after it.

What gives me pause beyond market size is the founder profile. Matthew Moore is one of a small number of people globally who has built and tested 94 GHz radar for drone micro-Doppler detection. That's impressive expertise -- and it's exactly the kind of deep-insider knowledge that, in my experience, constrains rather than enables invention. He built what his PhD prepared him to build. There's no evidence of the "irreverence, foolish confidence, and naivety" that characterized Peter Beck or Pat Brown. The entire founding team came from the same academic cluster at St Andrews, all three hold PhDs, and they're executing the research roadmap. That's a strong technical foundation, but it's an expert team doing expert things -- not outsiders reimagining a field. I'd want to see evidence of rapid evolution beyond the original thesis, pivoted assumptions, or willingness to attempt something their academic peers would consider reckless.

The bull case deserves honest engagement: millimeter-wave micro-Doppler radar at 94 GHz is a genuinely differentiated technical approach. None of the well-funded competitors currently operate at this frequency for drone detection. If nano-drones become the dominant threat -- and the trajectory from Ukraine strongly suggests they will -- then purpose-built detection at this resolution could become the gold standard. NATO DIANA and DASA backing provide real institutional access that software-only competitors cannot replicate. And Doherty's prior venture raising from Paul Graham personally suggests the team can attract serious backing. If the technology works at production scale and defense procurement moves faster than historical norms (which wartime urgency could drive), DroneTector could become a category winner in a market growing at nearly 30% annually. The path exists.

But I keep returning to my core test: has this team reduced failure probability to the point where the consequences of success are manageable rather than transformational? A specialized radar company serving a multi-billion-dollar defense niche is a good outcome, not a great one. The team needs go-to-market capability for defense sales that three PhDs don't naturally bring. The hardware scaling challenge from research prototype to field-deployable production units is significant and expensive. And the competitive convergence risk is real -- when Robin Radar already has 200+ units deployed in Ukraine, time-to-market matters more than frequency-band superiority. I respect the technical depth, I acknowledge the timing, but the magnitude of what changes if this works doesn't clear my threshold for writing a check from my own money.
