# Dispatch -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The thing that stops me with Dispatch is the competitive landscape, and not in the way you might expect. I've written first checks into spaces where everyone said the founder was insane -- SpaceX was my very first angel investment, and nobody wanted to touch private rocketry. But SpaceX was entering a void. Dispatch is entering a battlefield. Varda has $329 million and has actually brought manufactured goods back from orbit multiple times. Inversion has a $71 million Space Force contract. Space Forge has NATO money. There are reportedly 15-plus companies building reentry capabilities right now. When I backed SpaceX, the "weird factor" was that nobody else was doing it. When two guys build a heat shield in their apartment and test it behind a rocket engine in the Mojave Desert, that's genuinely scrappy and resourceful -- I love that image, truly. But "scrappy" is a different signal when you're competing against companies with hundreds of millions in capital and actual orbital return missions under their belt. The question isn't whether the founders are brave enough to try. The question is whether 100x cheaper heat shields (unverified beyond ground tests) can overcome a multi-year, multi-hundred-million-dollar head start.

Here's where my primary filter comes up short: I don't know these founders' stories. The dossier gives me Purdue and Northeastern, Astranis and Zoox and Apple. Those are real credentials -- Astranis builds satellites, so the domain knowledge is legitimate. But when I sit across from someone, the first thing I ask is "tell me your story, not the business." I want to hear about the thing that broke them open and made this problem theirs. I want to understand why Payton Case wakes up every morning thinking about reentry vehicles specifically, not just because there's a market gap, but because something in their life made this inevitable. The dossier doesn't give me that thread. It's possible it exists and just hasn't been captured publicly -- at pre-seed, plenty of extraordinary founders have no press footprint. But I can't evaluate what I can't see, and biographical conviction is where I start.

The bull case is real, and I want to be honest about it. If Dispatch's cost innovation holds at orbital scale -- if they've genuinely figured out how to build reentry-grade thermal protection at a fraction of what Varda or Inversion spends -- that's a structural advantage that could make them the default infrastructure provider for an entire industry. The positioning is smart: they're not trying to be Varda (vertically integrated manufacturing), they're trying to be the return logistics layer. Picks and shovels for the space manufacturing rush. The autonomous space station vision, if they ever get there, would create genuine platform lock-in. And the regulatory complexity of FAA reentry licensing and ITAR could become a moat once they navigate it. I've invested in hard, regulated spaces before -- IronPort was my career before venture, and I backed Contraline and Anduril specifically because regulatory barriers deter competitors. Two aerospace engineers from a real satellite company, building something that requires surviving Mach 20 atmospheric reentry -- that's a hard space in the most literal sense possible, and I respect that.

But here's what I keep coming back to: this company doesn't create economic access. It doesn't make an invisible system visible for people who need it. It serves a handful of well-capitalized space manufacturing companies -- a customer base that barely exists yet. When I backed Uber, I could see how it would create income for drivers. When I backed Affirm, I could see transparent credit reaching people who'd been exploited by hidden fees. When I backed Flock Safety, I could see communities getting safety data they'd never had. With Dispatch, I see infrastructure for an industry that might be enormous someday, but the direct human impact story isn't there for me. That matters. It's not everything, but it's a real part of how I decide where my own money goes.

The co-founder dynamic has one genuinely positive signal: both Payton and Andrew worked at Astranis together before starting Dispatch. They chose each other with eyes open, having already been colleagues in a directly relevant environment. That's meaningfully better than co-founders who met at a hackathon or through a matching service. I don't see red flags here -- but I also don't have the information I'd need to feel confident about how they handle disagreement, who makes the final call, or how they'd navigate the kind of crisis that inevitably comes. After HQ Trivia, I never skip that assessment.

This is a company I'd want to meet the founders in person before making any decision. The credentials are solid, the scrappiness is admirable, and the technical ambition is the kind of thing that either becomes transformative or becomes a cautionary tale. But on paper, without the biographical thread and without the economic access story, it doesn't clear my bar. The space is crowded, the capital requirements are enormous, and the upstream market they depend on is still embryonic. I'm passing -- but if someone told me Payton Case's personal story and it turned out there was a survival narrative I couldn't see in this dossier, I'd want to hear it.
