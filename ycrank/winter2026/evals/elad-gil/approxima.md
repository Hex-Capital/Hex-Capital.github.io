# Approxima -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most important fact about Approxima is what happened to Sweep AI. Sweep entered YC S23 with essentially the same thesis -- automated bug-fix PRs via LLMs -- raised $2.5M, and then pivoted away from autonomous bug-fixing entirely, moving to JetBrains IDE tooling. When a direct predecessor in your own YC network tries your exact positioning and abandons it, that's not a market gap opening up. That's a data point about the market's structural viability. Approxima needs to have a clear answer for why Sweep's retreat signals opportunity rather than warning, and I see no evidence they've articulated one.

The structural moment question here is straightforward: yes, LLM code understanding has crossed meaningful capability thresholds. Claude Opus scores ~80% on SWE-bench Verified, which means autonomous bug detection and fixing is technically feasible in a way it wasn't 18 months ago. But this is the most consensus observation in all of venture capital right now. Autonomous coding isn't a non-obvious market -- it's the single most capitalized category in AI, with Cognition at $10.2 billion, Factory at $300 million, Augment Code with $252 million raised, and foundation model providers like Anthropic and OpenAI shipping Claude Code and Codex directly as their own products. When I write about non-obvious markets, I mean markets that trigger dismissive reactions from other investors -- PagerDuty's "boring alerting" or Gusto's "just payroll." Nobody dismisses autonomous coding. Everyone is pouring money into it. That's exactly the pattern where pre-seed entrants get structurally disadvantaged.

The competitive dynamics here map directly to what I've written about "market-ending moves." When the foundation model providers themselves are shipping autonomous coding agents, the application layer built on top of their APIs faces a structural compression problem. The value creation accrues to the model layer, not the wrapper layer. Approxima's "self-healing software" angle is conceptually interesting -- positioning closer to continuous infrastructure monitoring than to code generation, which at least differentiates from Devin's "replace the developer" pitch. If they could embed deeply into CI/CD pipelines as an always-on maintenance layer, that's closer to the infrastructure pattern I look for. But this is entirely theoretical at this stage. The website returned a connection error. The GitHub organization contains only repositories from SideKit, their prior product. There's no visible Approxima product to evaluate.

The bull case would require several things to be true simultaneously: first, that "self-healing software" is a genuinely distinct category from "AI coding assistant" in the buyer's mind -- more like buying monitoring infrastructure than buying a developer tool. Second, that Sweep AI's retreat was due to Sweep-specific execution failures rather than structural market problems. Third, that Approxima can build enough integration depth and accuracy to create switching costs before well-funded competitors (or the model providers themselves) subsume the category. Fourth, that two recent graduates can out-execute teams backed by hundreds of millions in funding. That's at least three sequential breakthroughs that all need to land -- which fails my single-miracle test. Compare this to Harvey, where the single miracle (model fidelity reaching legal-grade accuracy) was being delivered by the foundation model labs, freeing Harvey to focus on workflow and distribution. Here, the miracle isn't being delivered externally -- Approxima needs to simultaneously build differentiated product, find a defensible wedge, AND out-distribute massively funded competitors.

The founders are UWaterloo CS '25 graduates -- Selvaraj with Databricks and Microsoft internship experience, Pronev with competitive programming background. These are competent technical profiles, but for a market this competitive, I'd want to see either deep operational experience with the specific problem (someone who ran SRE at a large company and deeply understands the maintenance burden) or evidence of extraordinary shipping velocity. The mid-batch pivot from SideKit is a concern: the prior product had minimal traction (7 GitHub stars), and a complete change in product category, customer segment, and technical stack during the batch suggests they're still searching for direction rather than executing against a clear insight. No revenue, no visible product, no distribution strategy beyond Calendly demo booking.

I pass on this. The market is consensus, the competitive landscape is brutally well-capitalized, the foundation model providers are moving directly into the application layer, and the company has pivoted mid-batch with no visible product or defensibility signals. A smart toaster analogy applies here: if the underlying LLM capability to fix bugs is available to everyone via API, and the wrapper application has no proprietary data, no distribution moat, and no regulatory barrier, then the value accrues to the model providers, not the application layer. I need to see that a company is software-driven in a way that compounds -- and right now, Approxima hasn't demonstrated what compounds.
