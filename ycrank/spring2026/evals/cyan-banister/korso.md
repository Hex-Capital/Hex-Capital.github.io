# Korso -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Martin Pan spent time at General Motors before co-founding Korso. That's the strongest biographical signal in the dossier, and I need to be honest — it reads as domain exposure, not as a forge. When I sit down with founders, my first question is always about their story, not the business. With Korso, I get three technically capable recent graduates from UPenn, UC Irvine, and Michigan who connected through Penn Electric Racing and university overlap, then identified manufacturing operations as a market opportunity. Daichi Hiraoka built ML models for radiotherapy and redesigned charger systems on a $3,300 budget — that shows resourcefulness. But nobody on this team appears to have grown up watching a family machine shop lose contracts because quotes took three weeks, or sat in a purchasing office watching tribal knowledge walk out the door when a 30-year veteran retired. The founders "spent time traveling across Asia, talking directly to clients" after YC acceptance — that's research hustle, which I respect, but it's the opposite of the problem finding the founder. Korso's founding story, as far as the dossier reveals, is "we saw a market opportunity in manufacturing AI." That's career optimization, not biographical inevitability.

Korso's thesis — "the great inefficiency of modern manufacturing is not on the shop floor, it's in the office" — contains a genuine insight that almost activates my framework. Manufacturing operations are one of the most opaque commercial environments on earth: pricing locked in one person's head, supplier relationships as personal fiefdoms, quote generation a black box controlled by whoever's been there longest. When Korso talks about converting "tribal knowledge into data," they're describing an invisible system being made legible. That's real. But Korso frames itself as "the intelligence layer" — an automation play, not a transparency play. Compare this to how Affirm attacked credit card opacity: Affirm didn't just make lending faster, it made the terms visible to borrowers who'd been structurally exploited by hidden interest. Korso is making the opaque manufacturing office faster and more autonomous. It's not exposing the hidden structure to the people harmed by it — the small manufacturers overpaying suppliers, the buyers trapped in information asymmetry. The invisible system is there, but Korso is optimizing within it rather than overturning it.

$34.18 billion in 2025, growing to $155 billion by 2030 — that's the AI-in-manufacturing market Korso is entering. And here's where my contrarian antenna goes flat. Paperless Parts has raised $45-51 million for AI-supported quoting. Leverage AI has $14.9 million for purchase order automation. Turian in Berlin has 17 employees building agentic AI for procurement. Epicor has launched Prism as a native AI agent for RFQ workflows inside its ERP. When 80% of CPOs name AI a top priority, you are not operating in a space that makes sophisticated investors uncomfortable — you're operating in the space they're all racing to fund. Nobody shifts in their chair when you say "AI agents for manufacturing operations." My biggest wins have come from spaces where I was the first check because everyone else said no — SpaceX when rockets were detonating, Flock Safety when surveillance cameras provoked civil liberties objections, Contraline when male birth control made VCs squirm. Korso is entering a well-funded, consensus-attractive category. That doesn't mean they can't win, but it means they need to win on execution against better-capitalized competitors, and execution races are not where I have edge as an angel.

The bull case for Korso is real, and I want to be fair to it. ERP incumbents face a genuine business model conflict: their revenue depends on seat-based licensing for the manual workflows Korso automates, so adding AI features cannibalizes their own economics. Korso's ERP-agnostic positioning could be powerful for the many manufacturers running legacy or multi-ERP environments where Epicor Prism can't reach. The technical architecture — long-running workflow orchestration with checkpointing across multi-week manufacturing processes, verification layers, dry-run validation, audit trails — shows engineering sophistication appropriate to the problem's real complexity. And the Carta parallel is instructive: once Korso integrates with a manufacturer's ERP and accumulates pricing history, supplier patterns, and customer preferences, switching costs compound per account. If Korso becomes the system of record for a manufacturer's operational intelligence, displacement becomes structurally painful. An advocate would argue this is infrastructure at an inflection point — frontier LLMs just crossed the capability threshold for parsing unstructured manufacturing documents, and the first mover who nails ERP integration depth wins a durable position. The advocate is not wrong about the opportunity. But Carta had Henry Ward, whose biographical conviction about equity transparency as a democratizing force was inseparable from the company. Korso has a team that identified a market gap and is executing competently against it. Competent execution in a consensus market with $14.9M+ funded competitors is not the bet I write first checks for.

Korso could flip me to Invest with one verifiable condition: evidence that one of these founders has a biographical connection to manufacturing that makes this problem personal rather than analytical — a family in the industry, a formative experience with the opacity they're disrupting, or a prior attempt at solving this that predates the company. Alternatively, a signed pilot with a named manufacturer generating measurable ROI would demonstrate that their ERP integration claims hold up in the messy real world, which would partially compensate for the biographical gap by proving the product has escaped the building. Right now, with zero visible traction, zero public customers, and a competitive landscape that includes Leverage AI ($14.9M), Paperless Parts ($45-51M), and Epicor Prism, Korso needs something that distinguishes it from a dozen similar pre-seed pitches I could hear this month. The absent biographical forge and the consensus market positioning are the two dimensions that lock this verdict: without the founder story, I don't have conviction that this team will survive the inevitable valley when a better-funded competitor or an ERP incumbent closes the gap — and without contrarian positioning, I don't have the asymmetric upside that justifies the pre-seed risk with my own money.
