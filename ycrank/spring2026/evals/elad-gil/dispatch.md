# Dispatch -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first question I ask with any company is what structural change makes this market possible now when it wasn't before. With Dispatch, the answer is clear: Varda proved commercial reentry works -- five successful missions in 2024-2026, $329M raised, an operational flight cadence. Declining SpaceX rideshare costs ($5,500/kg on Transporter missions) have further compressed the economics of getting payloads to orbit. The inflection point for in-space manufacturing logistics is real. But here's the problem: when I can point to a well-funded competitor with five demonstrated missions and a third of a billion in capital, the market is no longer non-obvious. Seven funded startups are pursuing reentry vehicles. Aggregate capital deployed exceeds half a billion dollars. This is a consensus bet in a nascent market -- exactly the opposite of where the asymmetric returns live. When I invested in PagerDuty, nobody thought operations alerting was interesting. When I backed Harvey, legal AI seemed like a science project. Dispatch is entering a space where every aerospace-focused VC has already written checks.

The market itself gives me pause even if the competitive dynamics were favorable. In-space manufacturing was roughly $1-1.4 billion in 2024. Even at aggressive growth rates, you're looking at $5-10 billion by the early 2030s. Compare that to legal services at $900 billion when I evaluated Harvey, or payments processing at multiple trillions when I looked at Stripe. The TAM here doesn't support the number of well-funded competitors already in the field. And Dispatch isn't even vertically integrated like Varda -- they're a logistics provider whose revenue depends on OTHER companies successfully manufacturing in microgravity at commercial scale. That's a dependency, not a distribution channel.

The multi-miracle problem is what ultimately pushes this into pass territory for me. Dispatch needs to: (1) demonstrate that a ground-tested heat shield survives actual Mach 20+ atmospheric reentry -- a leap that Inversion Space's propulsion short circuit illustrates is non-trivial, (2) raise $50-100M+ to reach first flight based on competitor spending patterns, (3) achieve the claimed 100x cost advantage at production scale rather than in an apartment, and (4) time all of this to coincide with sufficient in-space manufacturing demand to fill their vehicles. Four sequential low-probability events. I've written explicitly about why compounding small odds kills companies, and this is a textbook case. Harvey's miracle -- model fidelity reaching legal-grade accuracy -- was being delivered by OpenAI, not by Harvey itself. Dispatch's miracles are all on Dispatch.

The strongest bull case runs through my Anduril exception: capital-intensive, hardware-focused, in a market most software investors avoid, with regulatory and technical barriers creating structural moats. If the 100x cost reduction on heat shield fabrication is real and scales, that's a genuine structural advantage -- refurbishable vehicles at dramatically lower cost per mission could make Dispatch the low-cost logistics provider for an entire category. The "picks and shovels" positioning is the right framing: rather than betting on which material (pharma, semiconductors, biotech) wins in microgravity, bet on the return infrastructure everyone needs. And the founders are well-matched to the problem -- Case's thermal engineering from the Lunar Gateway program and systems architecture from Astranis, combined with Mello's avionics and hardware integration experience from Astranis, Zoox, and Apple, cover the core disciplines a reentry vehicle demands. The ground test in Mojave -- full-scale, built resourcefully -- shows they can execute with minimal capital. If the in-space manufacturing market follows the trajectory of cloud computing (every company that runs software needs operations tools → every company that manufactures in space needs return logistics), this could be infrastructure-layer valuable.

But my Anduril exception required something specific: a structural vacuum with no credible startup competitor. Google's Project Maven cancellation opened a category with zero serious entrants. Here, the category already has a frontrunner with operational proof and a 600x capital advantage. A reentry vehicle built in an apartment, however impressive as a proof of concept, doesn't constitute the kind of distribution moat or switching cost that protects against well-capitalized competitors who are years ahead. The market structure would need to support multiple providers at scale, and at $1-1.4 billion today, that arithmetic doesn't work yet. I need to see either a much larger market or a much clearer structural advantage before deploying capital here.
