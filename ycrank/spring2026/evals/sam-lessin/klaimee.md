# Klaimee -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Armilla AI raised $25M and became a Lloyd's Coverholder. Testudo graduated from Lloyd's Lab with $9.25M in capacity. Corgi closed $108M and runs $40M+ ARR across startup insurance. When I look at Klaimee entering AI agent insurance in Spring 2026, the first thing I see is a category where the capital has already arrived. The whole reason I exist as an investor is finding things the market has dismissed -- "cool shit that others do not believe." But AI insurance isn't dismissed. It's funded. MarketIntelo is publishing $12.8B TAM projections by 2033. Three competitors with real money and real carrier relationships are already writing policies. The risk isn't in the price anymore -- the consensus has caught up, and Klaimee is showing up to a party that started without them. That's the opposite of the Solana trade. When I bought SOL at four cents, nobody else wanted it. Here, everybody wants it.

Ines Boutemadja grew SafetyWing's Nomad Insurance from $5M to $40M over five years -- that's not a line on a pitch deck, that's real operator time inside the insurance value chain managing carriers, claims, and underwriting. Klaimee benefits from a genuine insurance operator running the show, not a tech founder who watched a YouTube video about actuarial science. Julien Catonnet brings the engineering side from Omnipresent plus the consulting rigor from EY-Parthenon, and they both previously worked at Cubyn, so the co-founder relationship has actual shared professional history. These are clearly competent, grounded people. The problem is that "competent, grounded people building a logical product in a category that makes sense" is exactly the profile that bores me. My framework requires a sane person pitching an insane idea. Insuring AI agents because regulators just made deployers liable isn't insane -- it's the most obvious product you could build given AB 316 and the EU AI Act. Every insurance person in a room is thinking it. Klaimee is a sensible answer to a sensible question, and that's not where my returns come from.

Klaimee's certification-plus-insurance bundle is the most interesting structural element -- the idea that a "Klaimee Certified" badge becomes the trust signal AI agent startups need to close enterprise procurement. If that badge becomes mandatory, like SOC 2 for AI agents, you've got a new primitive. But certification and insurance are both ancient categories being applied to a new domain. Venmo created peer-to-peer mobile payments as a verb. Patreon created direct creator monetization as a behavior. Klaimee is applying existing concepts -- compliance certification, liability insurance -- to AI agents. That's a new application, not a new primitive. The behavior it creates ("get your agent certified before deploying to enterprise") is recognizable as a variant of existing compliance workflows, not something that couldn't have been described five years ago.

S&P Global reports traditional carriers are actively excluding AI claims from policies as of February 2026, and that creates a genuine supply gap someone will fill. Here's the bull case an advocate would make: Ines is the rare insurance operator who understands carrier relationships from the inside, entering a market where carriers are retreating. AB 316 eliminated the autonomous AI defense. The EU AI Act imposes fines up to EUR 35M. Every startup shipping agents to enterprise suddenly needs coverage that doesn't exist. Klaimee's self-serve certification bundle is lighter and faster than Armilla's enterprise-focused Lloyd's policies. The timing is perfect, the founder has the domain scars, and the supply vacuum is real. That's a strong case. But it falls apart when I apply my own AI thesis. I've said it publicly: AI benefits incumbents. If AI agents are fundamentally sustaining technology for big companies, then Klaimee's target customer -- startups deploying autonomous agents to enterprise -- may be a shrinking addressable market. The agents that matter will be built and deployed by the enterprises themselves, insured through their existing commercial policies with Chubb or Munich Re. Klaimee is building infrastructure for a customer base my own thesis says might not scale.

Klaimee's structural durability sits in a strange middle ground. Insurance is regulated financial infrastructure -- that maps to my pattern of backing financial rails and plumbing like Robinhood and Gusto. Underwriting data on AI agent failure modes should compound over time. But Klaimee likely depends on carrier partnerships to back actual liability coverage, and those carriers are the same ones currently retreating from AI risk. A two-person team certifying diverse AI agent architectures across multiple frameworks faces an execution challenge that capital alone doesn't solve -- Armilla's $25M buys real actuarial and engineering talent that Klaimee can't match yet. The regulatory tailwind from AB 316 is real, but I explicitly avoid companies whose profitability depends on favorable regulatory outcomes, and Klaimee's demand catalyst IS regulation. If future AI liability law creates government-backed certification standards or mandates specific frameworks, Klaimee's private certification could be displaced overnight.

Capital discipline is hard to evaluate at pre-seed with a two-person team and no public burn data. Klaimee appears lean. But insurance is inherently capital-hungry -- you need reserves, carrier relationships, and compliance infrastructure that a $500K YC check doesn't cover. The one verifiable condition that would flip me to Invest: if Klaimee demonstrated that its certification badge was already being required in enterprise procurement processes by named companies -- not hypothetically, but with signed LOIs or documented procurement gate requirements. That would prove the primitive is real and the demand isn't derivative of my skepticism about AI agent proliferation. Without that, Klaimee is a capable team building a logical product in a consensus-priced category. The mispricing dimension is the killer: 12/30 because Armilla, Testudo, and Corgi have already brought capital and carrier legitimacy to this space, and the structural durability question -- whether Klaimee's customers even exist at scale if my AI thesis is right -- compounds the concern.
