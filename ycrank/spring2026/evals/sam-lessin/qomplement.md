# qomplement -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's what jumps out at me immediately: qomplement is building an AI-powered document filling tool in a market where Nanonets is already doing $100 million in revenue, Rossum has raised $105 million, and Reducto just closed a $75 million Series B. "Filling" versus "extraction" is a product positioning choice, not a structural insight. Any of those companies adds a "fill" button in a quarter. Microsoft Power Platform already does some of this. When I look at this company, I see a feature dressed up as a business -- and I've been saying since September 2024 that this is exactly the category AI is incinerating. The entire value proposition is software processing documents using AI. Remove the AI and there is literally no company. That's not "cherry on top" -- that's cherry all the way down.

The founder-idea pairing doesn't activate my framework either. Kerim Taray graduated from Tec de Monterrey in 2024, co-founded a small bootstrapped sourcing venture, and spent time at Contalink -- a YC S21 accounting fintech. His CTO Andres Garza Garcia appears to have no prior ventures at all. These aren't bad credentials for a 23-year-old, but "sane person" in my vocabulary means someone whose background makes you take the crazy idea seriously specifically because of who they are. Brian Armstrong's Airbnb engineering experience made the Coinbase pitch land differently. The Mysten Labs team built crypto infrastructure at Meta. Here, I see early-career engineers from Monterrey building a document processing tool -- the founder credentials don't create tension with the idea because the idea itself isn't tense. Nobody hears "AI fills out PDF forms" and thinks that's insane. They think it's obvious. And obvious means every VC-backed document company is already building it.

The local-first architecture gets pitched as a differentiator, and I'll grant it's a thoughtful design choice for Latin American enterprises in regulated industries where cloud data residency is a real procurement barrier. But a deployment model is not a moat -- it's a configuration. Any competitor can ship a local version if the market demands it. And the trend line is moving toward cloud, not away from it. The 8-16 GB RAM requirement actually creates linear support complexity as you scale -- every customer machine becomes a deployment surface. This is the opposite of the physical-world operational moat I look for in companies like Craftwork or Metropolis, where you have real crews, real hardware, real regulatory positions that software cannot replicate. qomplement has none of that. It's pure software all the way through.

The strongest bull case I can construct: the "filling" niche really is less crowded than extraction, the LatAm enterprise market is genuinely underserved for AI document tools, and the testimonial of one transport client automating 10,000+ monthly invoices and saving $15K/month suggests real pain being solved. If they could own "document filling" as a category across Latin American regulated industries before extraction vendors extend downstream, and if the workflow builder creates genuine switching costs as customers embed qomplement into their operational processes, there's a path to a real business. The 15x cost advantage over cloud solutions matters in price-sensitive markets. But for this to work, you need to believe that well-funded extraction vendors won't add filling, that Microsoft and Google won't improve their existing form-filling capabilities, and that local-first deployment creates enough friction to prevent cloud competitors from entering -- all within a market where the SAM hasn't even been independently sized by any analyst. That's a lot of "ifs" stacked on top of each other, and none of them are structural -- they're all timing bets that the market stays fragmented long enough for qomplement to build switching costs.

The pivot history concerns me too. A year ago this was a general-purpose computer-using agent -- "the smartest AI built right into your operating system." Now it's a document filling specialist. Pivots are fine, founders learn, but pivoting from one AI-first product to another AI-first product tells me the team is searching for product-market fit within the exact category I think is most vulnerable to commoditization. The narrative ceiling is also capped -- "document filling" doesn't elastically expand into an infinity story the way Helium went from IoT hotspots to decentralized wireless infrastructure. At best, you grow from filling to all document workflows, which puts you squarely against every company in that competitive table with ten to fifty times your capital. This is a pass.
