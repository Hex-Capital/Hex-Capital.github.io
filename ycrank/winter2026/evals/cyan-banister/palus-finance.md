# Palus Finance -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Palus: this is the sixth company entering startup treasury management, offering a well-understood institutional finance strategy -- floating-rate agency MBS -- to a customer segment that already has Mercury, Brex, Arc, Rho, and Meow competing for their attention. When I look at my best investments, they share a quality of being genuinely unpopular and difficult to assess. SpaceX was considered lunacy. Crusoe Energy sounded like a joke. Uber was operating in legal gray zones that made every lawyer in Silicon Valley nervous. Palus is a yield optimization play that any institutional finance professional would immediately understand and any well-funded incumbent could replicate with a product team and a quarter of focused effort. There's nothing here that makes other investors uncomfortable, and that's actually what makes *me* uncomfortable.

When I think about the founders, I want to hear the story -- not the business. What I see here are two smart Caltech graduates who noticed that their YC startup cash was earning suboptimal yields and built a product around that observation. Sam Lushtak built investment research platforms for asset managers overseeing $3T+ in AUM, which gives him real domain exposure to fixed income strategies. That's relevant expertise, and I respect it. But it's not the same thing as biographical grit. Where's the hardship that makes this *their* problem to solve? The founders who stick through the inevitable crises -- and in fintech, the crises come from regulators, from market shifts, from incumbents waking up -- are the ones who are solving something that burned them personally. I backed Affirm because Max Levchin understood predatory lending at a visceral level and wanted to build transparent credit. The Palus founders noticed an interest rate spread while managing their YC checking account. That's founder-market *proximity*, but it's not the biographical commitment I need to see to write a check with my own money.

The bull case deserves real engagement, because the innovator's dilemma dynamic here is genuinely interesting. Mercury earns revenue from MMF fee-sharing and has built its entire treasury UX around same-day liquidity. Offering a 1-2 day settlement bond product would cannibalize their existing revenue and confuse their banking interface. That structural protection is real. And if rates keep declining, the 1-1.5% yield premium becomes proportionally more valuable -- at some point, earning 3.5% versus 5% on $10M matters enough to change behavior. If Palus can accumulate $500M+ in AUM through the YC network alone and build data advantages around startup cash drawdown patterns, they could become genuinely hard to displace. The Regan Capital partnership (largest floating-rate agency MBS ETF) is a credible institutional relationship. In a world where this team executes flawlessly and rates cooperate, this could be a solid mid-size fintech business.

But I keep coming back to what this company *doesn't* do that my best investments do: it doesn't create economic access for people who lack it. Startups sitting on $10M of post-Series A cash are not underserved. Earning them an extra $100-150K per year is nice, but it's not Uber putting money in drivers' pockets or Affirm giving transparent credit to people locked out of the system. And the moat question nags at me. The agency MBS strategy is textbook institutional finance -- any competitor with capital and compliance infrastructure could offer it. When Mercury decides the opportunity is large enough, they have 200K+ business customers and $650M in annual revenue to fund the buildout. The regulatory complexity of managing a bond portfolio provides some friction, but for a 2-person team, that same complexity is a burden, not just a moat.

The co-founder dynamic is a neutral signal -- two Caltech classmates building together, no red flags but no evidence of stress-tested conflict resolution either. I've learned from HQ Trivia that you can't assume college friendships translate into resilient co-founder partnerships. At this stage I'd want to sit across from them and ask how they handled the pivot from consumer savings to startup treasury -- was there disagreement? Who won? How? Those answers would tell me a lot.
