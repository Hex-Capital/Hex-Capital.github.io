# Maquoketa Research -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The competitive landscape tells you everything you need to know about whether this market window is still nonobvious. Helsing has raised €1.37B at a €12B valuation. Anduril sits at $14B+. Stark Defence raised $100M from Sequoia and won a €268M German contract. Neros has $121M and a U.S. Army PBAS selection. AeroVironment holds $990M+ in Army contracts for Switchblade production. When I backed Anduril, defense tech was considered uninvestable by Silicon Valley — that was the nonobvious window. That window opened roughly 2020-2022, and by 2026, loitering munitions is one of the most consensus categories in all of venture capital. The structural shift driving this market — Ukraine battlefield validation, incumbent production bottlenecks, bipartisan procurement urgency — is real and powerful. But it is no longer a shift that most investors have missed. It's a shift that has attracted billions in deployed capital. A pre-seed entrant arriving now faces a market where the structural opening has been recognized and heavily capitalized by competitors who already hold active military contracts.

The infrastructure-layer test is the second concern. Maquoketa builds a complete drone — airframe, electronics, autonomy stack. That's an end product, not an infrastructure layer. Compare this to Theseus (YC S24), which builds GPS-denied navigation modules that plug into multiple drone platforms — that's closer to picks-and-shovels positioning. Or compare to Anduril's Lattice OS, which aspires to be the operating system for autonomous defense systems writ large. Maquoketa is building the shovel, not the shovel factory. There's no platform dynamic here, no API that other defense companies integrate into, no compounding switching costs beyond the standard dynamics of a hardware procurement contract. When I look at my highest-conviction defense investment, the infrastructure layer — the software-defined autonomy platform — was the bet, not the specific munition.

The single-miracle test fails cleanly. I count at least four independent breakthroughs required: (1) the autonomous targeting and EW-resistance technology must actually work at mil-spec reliability, (2) they must win DoD procurement against five competitors with hundred-million-dollar head starts and active contracts, (3) the team must obtain ITAR compliance, facility clearances, and potentially personal security clearances — non-trivial operational overhead for a five-person team with no disclosed defense industry background, and (4) they must scale hardware manufacturing, which is a distinct engineering challenge from prototyping. Stark Defence won a €268M contract despite failing all four test strikes, which tells you something about procurement urgency — but it also tells you that incumbency in procurement relationships matters more than technical superiority. Compounding four independent probabilities gives you very long odds.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement. Demand is so acute that multiple vendors will be funded simultaneously — DoD policy explicitly favors multi-source procurement to avoid supply chain concentration. The in-house phased array and RF design capability from Berkeley Wireless Research Center is atypical for a pre-seed drone startup; most competitors assemble commercial off-the-shelf components rather than designing custom RF systems. If Maquoketa's EW resistance is genuinely a generation ahead — and Hernandez's BWRC background suggests the technical depth is real — they could win specific program competitions even against better-capitalized entrants. The AeroVironment production bottleneck (new factory not operational until late 2026/2027) creates a specific window where demand outstrips incumbent supply. And Germany's decision to award €9B+ to startups over Rheinmetall demonstrates that incumbency isn't destiny in this market. For this to be a great investment, you'd need the RF/EW-resistance capability to be demonstrably superior in competitive evaluations, a fast-track procurement pathway like OTA that compresses the 12-36 month contract cycle, and founders who can navigate the clearance and compliance infrastructure without it consuming their runway. That's plausible but it's three things that all need to go right.

On the team: Hernandez's phased array specialization at BWRC is the one genuinely differentiated technical credential here. That's real domain expertise in the core electronics challenge. Yeager's background at SpiderRock (trading tech) plus Berkeley EECS gives you software/systems capability. But neither founder shows evidence of defense industry experience, procurement navigation, or scaling a hardware organization. Building and shipping defense hardware at volume is an operational scaling challenge that's qualitatively different from software — you need manufacturing partnerships, supply chain management, quality assurance regimes, and facility security infrastructure. The High Growth Handbook was written for software companies scaling from 10 to 10,000; hardware defense companies face a harder version of every scaling problem with fewer second chances.

The margin dynamics are structural: this is a hardware business selling low-cost expendable munitions. "Low-cost" is literally the value proposition — you're competing to be cheaper than cruise missiles. That ceiling on unit economics is the opposite of the pricing power I look for. There's no SaaS-style negative churn here, no expansion revenue, no ability to raise prices repeatedly. You ship a drone, it explodes, and you need to sell another one. The recurring revenue comes from reorder volume, not from product stickiness. Defense hardware can generate healthy margins, but the "high volume, low cost" framing puts this in margin compression territory from day one.

This is a real market with a real structural catalyst, but the catalyst has been fully recognized and massively capitalized. The company builds an end product rather than an infrastructure layer, requires multiple simultaneous breakthroughs against billion-dollar competitors, and the founding team — while technically capable in RF design — lacks visible defense procurement experience or scaling credentials. I've seen this pattern before: strong market, wrong layer, wrong timing relative to consensus. Pass.
