# Bidflow -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I want to know about any founder is how they got to where they are -- not the business, the person. And the most striking thing about Jesse and Gautham isn't what they're building now, it's the gap between who they are and where they've landed. These are two genuinely brilliant young competitive programmers -- USACO Platinum, Jane Street at 18, ICML research papers as a teenager -- who six months ago were building AI QA agents for software testing, and now they're counting electrical symbols on circuit diagrams for contractors in the Tri-State Area. Their email address is still founders@tryghostship.dev. Their legal entity is still Ghostship AI Inc. When I ask myself the question I always ask -- "is this founder genuinely the right person to tackle this specific problem?" -- I can't find the thread that connects Jesse and Gautham's biography to electrical contracting. Nobody in the dossier grew up on job sites. Nobody watched a parent lose a bid because the takeoff was wrong. They found this market, they didn't come from it. And their direct competitor, Drawer AI, was founded by someone who spent 25 years doing exactly this work before deciding to automate it. That's the kind of biographical commitment I trust at the earliest stages.

The second problem is that this doesn't feel like a non-consensus bet. Construction AI is crowded and getting more crowded by the quarter. Beam AI just raised $48 million. Togal has over $17 million and claims 97% accuracy. Drawer AI raised $5 million specifically for the identical use case -- AI-powered electrical estimating. When I backed SpaceX, nobody else would touch private space launch. When I wrote my check into Uber, most of the Valley thought ride-sharing was illegal and doomed. Those were unpopular, difficult to assess, and genuinely weird. An AI copilot for construction estimating in 2026 is none of those things. It's obvious. It's the kind of application that every AI-focused fund has on its radar. The weirdness has been arbitraged away, and the price of being right here is competing against teams with more money, more domain expertise, and multi-year head starts.

I want to be honest about what's working, though, because there is a real bull case. If I imagine the world where Bidflow wins, it looks like this: two absurdly talented engineers with a decade of working together crack the accuracy problem faster than anyone expects -- their ML chops are legitimately elite -- and they embed themselves in the electrical contracting community so deeply that within two years they know this industry better than founders who came from it. Bass Electric's owner says they cut his estimate time by 5x. That's real. Small electrical contractors who couldn't afford to bid on data center projects because estimation alone would consume their capacity -- those shops could suddenly compete with the big firms. That's the economic access story I care about, the same pattern I saw with Uber creating income for drivers and Thumbtack connecting tradespeople with customers. If Bidflow genuinely levels the playing field for small electrical contractors in the data center boom, that matters. But the founders don't frame it that way, and I'm not sure they see it that way, which tells me something about whether this is a market they stumbled into or a mission they're committed to.

The one dimension where I'm genuinely impressed is the co-founder relationship. Jesse and Gautham met in a middle school coding class, built CerealCodes together, went through YC together with Ghostship, and now pivoted together to Bidflow. Gautham pulled an all-nighter to fix bugs during a CerealCodes crisis. That's roughly a decade of partnership across multiple ventures including at least one high-pressure failure and pivot. After what happened with HQ Trivia -- a company that had perfect timing, a transformative product, and still died because two co-founders couldn't resolve their conflict -- I never underweight this dimension. Jesse and Gautham have clearly stress-tested their working dynamic. That's real, and it's rare. But a strong co-founder bond isn't enough to override the absence of domain conviction and the competitive headwinds they're facing. At 83.5% accuracy against a competitor claiming 97%, with no construction background against founders with 25 years of it, in a market that's attracting serious capital from all directions -- I need to see the biographical thread that tells me these two will grind through the next five years in this specific industry when things get hard. I don't see it yet.
