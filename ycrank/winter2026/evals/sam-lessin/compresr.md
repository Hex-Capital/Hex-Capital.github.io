# Compresr -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the structural paradox that makes Compresr a textbook case of what I'd call "building a sand castle below the tide line": the entire business depends on LLM tokens being expensive, and LLM tokens are getting cheaper at roughly 10x per year. DeepSeek entered the market at 90% below incumbent pricing. The dossier itself cites a16z's data on this deflationary curve. So you have a company whose core value proposition -- "we'll save you money on tokens" -- is being solved by the market itself, on a timeline measured in quarters, not decades. The accuracy improvement claim on FinanceBench (72.3% to 74.5%) is a more durable angle, but it's one benchmark on one task, and it's not what leads their pitch. When the most prominent number on your website is "200x compression" and your benchmark shows 10x, the message is about cost savings, and cost savings on a rapidly deflating commodity is not an infinity story.

The founder-idea pairing here doesn't trip my filter in the right direction. Ivan Zakazov and his co-founders are clearly credentialed -- EPFL PhD track, a real peer-reviewed paper benchmarking 25 models, experience at Meta, Bell Labs, UBS. That's the sane part, and I buy it. But the idea isn't insane. It's the opposite of insane. Every developer building with LLMs has thought "I wish context were cheaper." This is the most consensus pain point in the AI ecosystem right now. When I say I want a sane person with an insane idea, I mean Brian Armstrong proposing a regulated crypto exchange when Bitcoin was a curiosity, or Teamshares proposing to buy hundreds of small businesses through employee ownership. "Let's compress LLM prompts to reduce costs" provokes no skepticism from anyone -- it provokes a shrug and "sure, but won't that solve itself?" These are sane people with a sane idea, and that combination doesn't produce venture-scale returns because the opportunity is already visible and priced in by every player in the ecosystem.

The AI-resistance test is where this investment falls apart for me completely. This is not a business that gets stronger as AI commoditizes software -- this is a business that gets *weaker*. The moat is a trained compression model, which is pure software IP. Microsoft Research already gives away LLMLingua for free, integrated into LlamaIndex and Azure Prompt Flow. AgentReady offers a competing API. The founding paper's techniques are published in the academic literature for anyone to reproduce. And the existential threat isn't even from competitors -- it's from the LLM providers themselves. Yes, there's the clever structural argument that OpenAI and Anthropic don't want to cannibalize token revenue by offering compression. But that argument only holds in a world where providers compete on capability. The moment the competitive axis shifts to efficiency -- which DeepSeek's pricing already signals -- providers will optimize context handling natively. Google's ADK already documents context compaction. The Birchbox lesson I learned the hard way applies here with extra force: narrative without structural moat produces the worst venture outcome, and Compresr's moat is dissolving from multiple directions simultaneously.

The bull case I'd need to believe: context compression evolves beyond cost savings into a genuine "context intelligence" primitive -- a layer that doesn't just shrink context but makes AI systems fundamentally smarter about what information matters. If the accuracy improvement angle (not the cost angle) becomes the core value proposition, and if the Context Gateway becomes standard infrastructure that every agentic workflow routes through, there's a path to something more durable. The 117-star open-source gateway is the seed of a protocol play. If that gateway became the standard intermediary between agents and LLM APIs -- handling not just compression but routing, caching, context prioritization, memory management -- you could squint and see new infrastructure. But that's a different company than what's described in this dossier, and it requires pivoting the entire thesis before the cost-savings wave breaks. I don't see evidence the founders are thinking about this expansion yet.

My "cherry on top" test demolishes this one cleanly: would this business be good if AI didn't exist? It literally cannot exist without AI. That's not cherry-on-top -- that's cherry-is-the-whole-sundae. And when the cherry depends on a specific economic condition (tokens being expensive) that the market is actively eliminating, you're not building a business, you're arbitraging a temporary inefficiency. Temporary arbitrage can be profitable, but it's not what I write angel checks for. I want businesses that survive the next three platform shifts, and Compresr might not survive the next three quarters of token price deflation.
