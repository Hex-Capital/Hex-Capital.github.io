# Gojiberry AI -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is that this company was listed for sale on TrustMRR at $11.1 million on March 9, 2026 — one week after going #1 on Product Hunt and concurrent with entering YC S26. Signals are what people do, despite what they say. You don't put a price tag on a company you believe has venture-scale trajectory. You price what you're willing to walk away from. The asking price — 6x revenue — is the economics of a small SaaS flip, not an ownership stake in a compounding asset. This is the clearest agent signal I've encountered in a batch evaluation. These founders are talented operators playing the game of company creation. That's a legitimate way to make money. It's not the game I invest in.

The specific knowledge question confirms what the sale listing already told me. Three founders, all French Grande École graduates, with three prior exits between them — CoCo AI (WhatsApp Shopify marketing), MechaChain (Web3 gaming), Edusign (digital attendance). None of these are in B2B sales intelligence. They pivoted into this space after CoCo AI, initially building a CRM auto-fill tool that attracted a 2,000-person waitlist and zero paying customers. Zero. The current product — LinkedIn intent signal detection and automated outreach — was their second pivot. This is the pattern of founders who identify fundable categories through market analysis, not founders whose specific knowledge compelled them to build something the world didn't know it needed. Roman's 12-channel GTM playbook is impressive execution, but execution playbooks are the definition of trainable knowledge. Society can teach this.

The leverage audit reveals rented infrastructure disguised as a product. Gojiberry monitors LinkedIn signals, uses LinkedIn for outreach, and enriches contacts through 15+ third-party data providers. Strip away the integrations and you have an orchestration layer sitting on someone else's data, someone else's communication channel, and someone else's AI models. LinkedIn can restrict automated messaging tomorrow — they've done it before. Apollo acquired Pocus in March 2026, signaling that the signal-to-sequence workflow is becoming a feature, not a company. When the platform you depend on or the incumbent above you decides to absorb your functionality, orchestration layers evaporate. This isn't Perplexity building a fundamentally new retrieval architecture — it's a well-assembled pipeline of API calls.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement because the execution metrics are striking. $175K MRR with three founders and $380/month in spend is exceptional team-to-value leverage. Sub-5% churn, $127 CAC, 1.3-month payback, 75% profit margins — these are the unit economics of a machine that works. The democratization angle is real: enterprise intent data at $99/month instead of $25K–$150K annually gives small sales teams access to signals that were structurally gatekept. If I believed the orchestration layer could compound into proprietary intelligence — that the data flywheel from 1,000+ customers would create defensibility faster than Apollo or Clay can converge on this feature set — this becomes an interesting position. The question is whether GTM execution speed can outrun incumbent product convergence long enough to build a durable moat. Given that HeyReach, Instantly, and Expandi all reached $10M+ ARR in adjacent categories, the evidence suggests this market accommodates many competitors at moderate scale but doesn't produce power-law winners from the execution layer.

The compound interest test fails for me. What compounds here? Not the data — Apollo has 275 million contacts. Not the signal detection — LinkedIn's data is equally accessible to every competitor. Not the brand — the space is crowded with look-alike tools. The outreach performance data from 1,000 customers is a mild flywheel, but it's the kind of advantage that erodes in 18 months as every competitor accumulates the same signal-to-conversion mappings. Compound interest requires an asset that gets more valuable the longer you hold it. I see efficient extraction, not compounding.

I respect what these founders have built. Three exits before 30, near-profitable SaaS at $175K MRR with essentially no capital deployed — that's a rare operational skillset. But the architecture of this business is borrowed leverage operated by agents who have already priced their exit. I pass.
