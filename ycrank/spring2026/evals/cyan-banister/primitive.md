# primitive -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what hits me first: Ethan Byrd built Actual AI, raised $3.2M, had 32 active pilots with a co-founder — and now he's solo-founding something entirely different, while his LinkedIn still reads "Founder & CTO @ Actual AI." His GitHub bio says "something new ;)" with a wink. I don't know what happened with Actual AI, and that matters enormously to me, because the story between the last company and this one is often where the real founding narrative lives. If he built agents, hit the wall of broken communication infrastructure, and decided to rip out the plumbing problem rather than keep building on top of it — that's a Postmates-to-Serve-Robotics thread, and I respect that deeply. But I don't have that story. I'm constructing a plausible narrative in the absence of one, and I've learned not to invest in stories I write myself. When I ask "tell me your story," I need to hear something that makes me feel why this person couldn't walk away from this specific problem. A resume of Google, Meta, AWS, Microsoft — that's career velocity, not biographical inevitability.

The competitive picture is what would keep me up at night. AgentMail came out of YC S25, raised $6M from General Catalyst, has Paul Graham and Dharmesh Shah as angels, reports 500+ B2B customers, and saw usage quadruple in a single month. Primitive is a team of one with three unfilled job postings. I've invested early against funded competitors before — I wrote my Uber check when 300 investors had passed — but the signal there was that nobody else believed. Here, everybody believes. This space is consensus-hot. General Catalyst is already in. VCs aren't shifting uncomfortably in their chairs when you pitch "email infrastructure for AI agents" — they're reaching for their checkbooks. That's the opposite of the signal I look for. My biggest wins have come from places that made the room nervous: rockets exploding, defense tech in pacifist San Francisco, male birth control. Agent email tooling at the peak of the AI wave doesn't make anyone nervous. It makes them nod.

When I run my invisible system test, I come up empty. Legacy email providers — Gmail, SendGrid, SES — aren't extractive or opaque. They weren't designed for agents, sure, but that's a technical gap, not an information asymmetry exploiting people who don't understand it. Nobody's being harmed by the taxi medallion cartel equivalent here. There's no hidden tax being exposed. It's honest plumbing that needs updating. That's a legitimate business — Twilio solved a similar plumbing problem for SMS — but it doesn't activate the part of my brain that lights up when I see a system that was quietly taking from people who couldn't see it. The companies I've built the deepest conviction around are the ones where I can point to who was being exploited and how the product makes that exploitation visible.

Let me give the strongest bull case its due, because it deserves it. If agent-to-agent communication becomes as fundamental as human-to-human communication, the company that owns that primitive layer could be enormously valuable — genuinely infrastructure-grade, genuinely at an inflection. Byrd's webhook-first architecture is a different bet than AgentMail's mailbox model; it treats email as an API rather than an inbox, which is a subtly different mental model that might turn out to be more developer-native. His four-company tour through the largest cloud platforms means he's seen email infrastructure at inhuman scale. And if Actual AI was the crucible that taught him where the real infrastructure gap was, then he's building from earned insight, not market research. Gartner's projection of 40% agent penetration by 2026 means the timing window is genuinely narrow. The question is whether a solo founder with no visible traction can outrun a funded team with 500+ customers in a race where speed matters.

I don't think this is my bet. Not because it's a bad company — it might be a perfectly good company — but because nothing in my framework fires. The biographical forge is a resume, not a crucible. The space is consensus, not contrarian. The system being disrupted isn't hidden or extractive. The defensibility against a funded competitor and an open Google protocol is unproven. And I can't get comfortable with the Actual AI ambiguity — I'd need that story before I could even begin to build conviction. I invest in people whose lives left them no option but to build exactly this thing, in spaces where most investors can't see what I see. Here, everyone can see it, someone else got there first with more resources, and I'm still waiting to hear why Ethan Byrd specifically had to be the one.
