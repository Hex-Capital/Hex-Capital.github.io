# screenpipe -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Louis Beaumont's tagline for screenpipe -- "help your team find the work worth automating" -- is the sentence that ends the meeting for me. That is not a labor-displacement thesis; that is a labor-discovery thesis, the SOP-writing layer that sits one abstraction above the actual $15T of GDP I care about (Fortune 2026-03-06). Screenpipe's success-state is a Fortune 500 process-mining tool with 48+ integrations and a Pipes runtime, priced at $150/seat Enterprise -- a workflow that Salesforce or ServiceNow ships as a feature in 2027. That is the signature: highest dimension is a stretched consequence claim that still lands below DoorDash territory, and the lowest is the required-capital match, where a pre-seed YC SaaS ticket is a check I am structurally over-resourced to write.

**OpenAI** (2019) is the HIGH rung on consequence, and the shared mechanism people will pitch me is "always-on capture of every knowledge-worker action creates the substrate for labor automation." That analogy is load-bearing only if screenpipe owns the automation, not the discovery. The dossier is explicit: Pipes "write back to connected tools," but the value proposition Louis Beaumont leads with is helping teams *find* the work worth automating. If screenpipe hits its maximum ambition, McKinsey process-mining consultants and UiPath's discovery product get displaced -- call it a $10-30B category. That is closer to my "GPT-4 summarizes sales calls" pass example lifted one rung than to OpenAI's civilizational redirect. The 250k users citation from Louis Beaumont's LinkedIn and the Microsoft/Google/NVIDIA logos on the homepage are distribution, not consequence, and I do not confuse the two.

**Range Fuels** (~2007) is the LOW rung on capital-intensity match for me -- it failed because the capital plan understated what the science required, and I lost publicly for holding the framework. Screenpipe's failure mode is the mirror image: the capital plan is *appropriately* small because there is no science to fund. A Rust agent, 48 SaaS connectors, and an on-device PII scrubber are $2-5M of engineering, not $50M of foundation-model training or $115M of tokamak physics per Commonwealth Fusion (2019). When a company's roadmap fits inside a YC pre-seed and a Series A, any Tier-1 generalist writes the check and I am contributing negative value per my own TechCrunch Disrupt (2013) framing -- 70-80% of VCs. The absence of a capital moat is not a neutral feature at Khosla check size; it is a structural mismatch.

Technical risk in screenpipe compounds the wrong way over 18 months. Microsoft Recall ships natively on Copilot+ PCs at $0 marginal cost; Meta acquired Rewind and disabled its desktop app on Dec 19, 2025 (GeekWire 2026); source-available forks -- atzamis/screen-pipe, EvolvingSoftware/screen-pipe -- already exist on GitHub. Every quarter the local-first privacy positioning gets less differentiated as Recall patches its security incidents and as Apple ships an on-device equivalent through the OS layer. Screenpipe's "moat" is a Rust codebase and 19k+ GitHub stars, both of which are execution artifacts, not science miracles. Compare to **QuantumScape** (early, IPO 2020), where a solid-state electrolyte was falsifiable and defensible for a decade -- screenpipe has nothing of that shape.

The founder-defies-experts dimension flips tiers only if Louis Beaumont has publicly staked a controversial position his field has rejected -- and the dossier gives me nothing. He is Techstars '22, OrangeDAO F1, a "French CIA" prior, a solo founder ex-Mediar. That is a competent builder profile, not the Pat Brown at Impossible Foods (2011) shape or the Chelsea Finn / Sergey Levine shape at Physical Intelligence (2024). What would flip this? A named research contribution -- a paper on on-device continual learning that the field ignored, or a public bet against the "cloud-scale wins" orthodoxy that Rewind's investors made. Absent that, screenpipe reads as domain-expert continuity from Mediar's SAP data-entry automation into a screen-capture-plus-Pipes wrapper.

Louis Beaumont's Mediar (2024, SAP legacy-software automation with Matt Diakonov) is a single anchor for the learning-rate dimension, and the comparison that matters is **Slash** (2024). Slash earned my $100M Series C partly because the Kanye pivot was a documented, public, non-linear thesis update -- founder learning made visible. Louis Beaumont's transition from Mediar to screenpipe is not a pivot; it is a continuation of the same screen-input-to-automation thesis, one abstraction layer higher and cross-app instead of SAP-only. That is competent iteration, not the sale-and-jump-to-harder-problem pattern I reward. I give him partial credit for being on his second company at all -- most YC S26 founders are on their first -- but I do not confuse continuity for learning rate.

My 36-month forecast: screenpipe is acquired by Salesforce, ServiceNow, or Atlassian for $80-200M as a process-mining and workflow-discovery feature, at $3-8M ARR, or it is dead from Microsoft Recall + Apple-Intelligence commoditization of always-on capture. The mechanism is the signature: the discovery-layer consequence is not large enough to sustain an independent public company, and the capital profile is not deep enough to build a moat before the OS vendors close the gap. Either outcome is inconsistent with a Khosla check.

The bull case that stings is the **Glean** exception. Glean's Arvind Jain was ex-Google search, a paradigmatic domain-expert profile -- exactly the anti-pattern -- and I still wrote the check because the consequence on Fortune-500 information asymmetry was large and the enterprise distribution moat was real. Screenpipe has 250k users, logos at Microsoft, Google, NVIDIA, Datadog, Adobe, Salesforce, and Thomson Reuters, and a local-first architecture that Recall's security failures and Rewind's Meta-acquired shutdown make genuinely differentiated for regulated buyers. The bull argues screenpipe is Glean-for-actions, not Glean-for-search, and the Pipes runtime captures the labor-displacement revenue, not just the discovery revenue. What dismantles this is that Glean sold information retrieval to buyers who had already paid consultants tens of millions to fix search; screenpipe is selling "find work worth automating" to buyers who have not agreed the problem is worth $150/seat. The condition that flips my verdict is one named signed enterprise contract above $1M ARR within 12 months plus published evidence that Pipes -- not the discovery layer -- is where more than half the ACV lands. Absent that, screenpipe passes.
