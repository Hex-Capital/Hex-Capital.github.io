# 10x Science -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's my problem with 10x Science: if it succeeds completely -- captures the market, automates every mass spectrometry workflow in biopharma -- the world gets slightly faster drug characterization. That's not nothing. But it's a $5 billion market growing at 5% a year. The software-addressable slice is a fraction of that. The consequence of total success is a solid enterprise SaaS company that saves analytical chemistry teams time and money. Nobody's life changes. No industry becomes structurally obsolete. The incumbents sell slightly fewer software licenses bundled with their instruments. This is precisely the kind of bet where the team has reduced the probability of failure -- good domain expertise, real bottleneck, clear customers -- to the point where the consequence of success becomes, for my purposes, inconsequential.

The strongest counterargument is timing. AI-powered drug discovery is flooding the pipeline with candidates that all need characterization, and the legacy tools can't keep up. If you believe that pipeline volume will grow exponentially while characterization remains the bottleneck, then the company that owns the automation layer is positioned at a critical chokepoint. That's a real argument. And the incumbent dynamics are favorable -- Genedata absorbed by Danaher in 2024, Protein Metrics swallowed by Dotmatics in 2021 -- creating exactly the kind of innovation vacuum where a startup can move fast. If this team builds something truly vendor-neutral that decouples analytical software from instrument hardware lock-in, they could accelerate the entire drug development process at scale. The 74% drug rejection rate for quality control deficiencies is a genuinely important statistic. But the company isn't framing itself as "we're going to fundamentally change how drugs get approved." They're framing it as "10x faster processing" and "$150K savings per team." That's the language of workflow optimization, not industry disruption.

The team composition is the strongest element here, and it makes me pause. David Roberts is a postdoc in Carolyn Bertozzi's lab -- a 2022 Nobel laureate in Chemistry -- with 37+ publications in the exact analytical technique the software automates. Andrew Reiter brings Broad Institute proteomics platform experience. And Vishnu Tejus is the critical third piece: a 2x YC founder, founding engineer at Nooks (which raised a $43M Series B from Kleiner), CMU XPRIZE finalist, with real ML chops. That domain-AI intersection is genuinely rare and deliberately assembled. This is good gene pool engineering for the specific problem they're attacking. But it also flags my concern about expert-driven companies: three people deeply embedded in the proteomics community, building software for the proteomics community. They know this world intimately, which means they also know what the field considers possible and what it considers ambitious. That knowledge constrains as much as it enables. Nobody working in mass spectrometry analysis thinks automating mass spectrometry analysis is crazy or impossible -- they think it's overdue. When the experts nod along with your vision, you're probably not attempting anything the consensus considers undoable.

What I'd need to get excited about this is a fundamentally different ambition frame. Not "we automate protein characterization" but "we make it so cheap and fast that characterization stops being a gating factor in drug development entirely, and the entire economics of bringing a biologic to market shifts." That version of the company is attacking a trillion-dollar pharma system, not a $5 billion analytical tools market. If Roberts came to me and said "we're going to compress the characterization step from months to hours, which means we can test 100x more candidates, which means the cost of drug development drops by half" -- that's a consequence of success I'd write a check for. But the current framing suggests a team that's building an excellent product in a manageable niche, exactly calibrated to succeed modestly.
