# Panacea -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Panacea is a real schlep with a thin animal record. Haris Javed-Akhtar and Yaman Ziadeh picked FDA submissions -- 510(k), IND, NDA, BLA -- which is exactly the kind of regulatory grind that pure-software YC applicants unconsciously route around, the same pattern that turned **Coinbase** (2012) into the company nobody else in its batch wanted to build. But when I scan the dossier for the pre-funding artifact -- the Armstrong wallet, the Houston demo, the cereal boxes -- I find a cal.com booking link and a "12x faster" marketing claim. That high/low pairing is the whole story of Panacea: the problem is on-thesis, the founders haven't yet shown me they're on-thesis.

**Coinbase** (2012) is the right rung on the schlep axis, and the analogy is load-bearing because Armstrong's appetite for compliance grind was itself the moat -- competitors flinched, and Brian kept walking toward the regulator. Panacea is choosing the same kind of work: FDA user fees up 20% year-over-year per Cruxi, 295 AI/ML 510(k) clearances in 2025, fixed-milestone pricing in a market where RQM+ has raised $301M to bill hourly. That last detail is the one I keep returning to -- Panacea is pricing in a way that incumbents structurally cannot copy without cannibalizing their own P&L. Where Panacea diverges from Coinbase, though, is that Armstrong shipped a working Android wallet to overturn his rejection; the Panacea dossier names no analogous artifact, no shipped submission, no named ex-FDA reviewer despite the central marketing claim of "ex-FDA, decades of experience."

The pass example I keep in my head is the polished AI-wrapper pitched by MBAs with a TAM slide and no demo. That failure mechanism is *no pre-funding tracks* -- animals leave tracks, and when the dossier is silent on what founders did before the check, I assume they did nothing remarkable. Panacea's dossier on the animal-resourcefulness axis is mostly silent: no Hacker News post, no public submission count, no scrappy first-customer anecdote, no GitHub for either founder, no Twitter for either founder. Haris ran Partnerships at Anterior; Yaman built AI platform at Anterior and before that at Bloomberg. These are competent adjacent operators, not yet the relentlessly resourceful pattern from my March 2009 essay. The website's claim of clients exists without a named one. I don't see the moment.

On organic-idea fit, the mechanism I want to see compound over 18 months is the founder having personally suffered FDA submission pain inside Anterior or an earlier role, then building Panacea as the tool they wished existed -- the Houston USB-stick origin from **Dropbox** (2007). Panacea has adjacency: an MBBS from Imperial College London on the CEO side, regulated-healthcare-AI engineering on the CTO side. But Anterior is utilization-management AI for payors, not 510(k) submissions, and Haris was Partnerships rather than Regulatory Affairs. The fit is one step removed -- they understand the customer's world without having lived the specific pain. Eighteen months of running submissions will either close that gap or expose it.

For hacker genealogy, the counterfactual is what would have to be true to flip Panacea up a tier: a public GitHub from Yaman with a non-trivial open-source artifact, a Lisp-or-equivalent technical signal, a shipped tool used by other biotechs. The dossier shows Bloomberg engineer plus AI Platform Engineer at a healthcare AI startup -- credentialed but not in the tribal-marker sense that flagged Patrick Collison's Young Scientist prize before **Stripe** (2010). Yaman co-founded Baqi previously, which is a positive prior-founder signal I weight, but no shipped public artifact bridges the gap from "competent enterprise engineer" to "hacker."

Cofounder configuration is Panacea's quietly solid dimension -- comparable to the **Stripe** (2010) brothers in mechanism if not in length: two people who actually worked together at Anterior before starting Panacea, with complementary coverage (medical domain plus AI platform engineering). The pair is real, the relationship predates the company, and the division of labor maps to the work. This is one of two dimensions where I am not penalizing absence.

My 36-month forecast for Panacea: alive but constrained, $2-5M ARR in service revenue, out-positioned at the high end by Weave Bio (now $30M raised with the Parexel partnership announced October 2025) and out-cheaped at the low end by Complizen's freemium tool, ending in either a sub-$50M tuck-in to RQM+ or ProPharma or a forced pivot from consulting-plus-AI into a thinner SaaS product. The mechanism producing that outcome is the high/low signature: a real schlep keeps Panacea from dying, an unproven animal profile keeps it from breaking out.

The bull case worth taking seriously: FDA regulatory consulting is a relationship business where reputation compounds, and Weave Bio's SaaS lane is genuinely different from Panacea's consultant-plus-tool lane -- buyers in biotech want a human accountable for the submission, not a dashboard. If Panacea's ex-FDA bench is real and Haris's medical credential opens biotech-founder doors, the service revenue could compound into a proprietary submissions dataset that no SaaS competitor can replicate, the same way **Coinbase** (2012)'s compliance investment became the moat. The dossier weakness that wins for me, though, is that no ex-FDA reviewer is named publicly despite being the central marketing claim -- in regulated sales cycles, an unverifiable bench is a sales-cycle risk, not just a marketing one. The verifiable condition that would flip my verdict: a named ex-FDA reviewer joining publicly plus a named biotech or medtech customer disclosing a Panacea-led 510(k) within six months.
