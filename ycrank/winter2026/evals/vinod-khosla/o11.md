# o11 -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The tagline tells me everything I need to know: "Microsoft Copilot, But It Actually Works." That's not a company thesis — that's a feature improvement. When Pradeep Sindhu and I spent six months incubating Juniper, we weren't building "Cisco's router, but it actually works." We were building on a fundamentally different technology architecture — TCP/IP routing — that would make Cisco's legacy approach structurally obsolete. Cisco's CTO told me they'd never build a TCP/IP router, and that dismissal was the signal. Here, Microsoft isn't dismissing AI-in-Office — they built Copilot, priced it at $30/user/month, and are pouring billions into making it better. o11 isn't attacking from a different architectural direction; it's polishing Microsoft's approach from inside Microsoft's own platform. That's the definition of incremental.

Let me apply my primary test. If o11 succeeds completely — wildest dreams — what changes? Bankers make pitch decks 40% faster. Consultants generate Mekko charts without manually formatting. Lawyers draft reports with more citations. The world still runs on PowerPoint, Excel, and Word. The same industries operate the same way, with the same tools, producing the same outputs. The documents just arrive sooner. Compare that to what happens if Commonwealth Fusion Systems succeeds (fossil fuels become economically obsolete) or if Impossible Foods succeeds (hundreds of millions of acres of agricultural land get reclaimed). The magnitude isn't in the same universe. o11's ceiling is a good business — a $5-10 billion productivity company if everything goes right. That's not nothing, but it's not the kind of outcome I invest my own money to pursue. These founders have optimized for probability of success — validated market, proven distribution channel, existing user demand — and in doing so, they've constrained the consequence of success to something that doesn't keep me up at night with excitement.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. Ajay Misra led AI at Cal AI, which hit $40M ARR with 10 million users before being acquired by MyFitnessPal. That's a genuinely extraordinary signal at pre-seed — you rarely see a 20-something founder who's already shipped AI at that scale. If you pair that execution velocity with o11's enterprise traction — Wells Fargo, Bank of America, Mayo Clinic on the logo wall, "hundreds of companies" using the product, 4.8-star ratings from 150 reviews — there's a real argument that this team can build a very profitable business. The $200/month MAX tier with enterprise custom pricing suggests the revenue per user could be substantial. Microsoft's $30/month Copilot pricing creates a price umbrella underneath which a more capable, vertically specialized alternative can thrive. And the "unlimited context" claim — processing thousands of documents with full citations — could represent genuine technical differentiation for finance professionals drowning in data. If o11 captures 2% of Microsoft 365's 400 million paid seats at even $20/month, that's $1.9 billion in ARR. The math works. But the math working is exactly the problem. When the investment thesis is a spreadsheet exercise about market share capture, I know the ambition is calibrated wrong.

The platform dependency is existential and insufficiently acknowledged. o11 distributes through Microsoft AppSource and Google Workspace Marketplace — channels that Microsoft and Google control completely. The product's value proposition is explicitly "we do what your platform owner's product should do, but better." History shows this works until it doesn't. Microsoft has every incentive to improve Copilot for finance-specific workflows, and they have the engineering capacity, the data access, and the distribution to do it. When I backed Square against traditional payment processors, Square owned its own hardware and its own relationship with merchants. When I backed Juniper against Cisco, Juniper didn't operate as a plug-in inside Cisco's router. Building your entire company as an add-in inside your competitor's platform isn't contrarian courage — it's a calculated risk that the competitor will remain mediocre in your niche forever. That assumption almost never holds.

These are talented founders executing competently in a real market. But talent is wasted when it's aimed at a problem whose consequence of success is inconsequential relative to the talent applied. Ajay Misra shipped AI to 10 million users. Aryah Oztanir is founding companies at 19 with a Morehead-Cain scholarship behind him. These are the kind of founders I'd want to back — on a problem that, if solved, makes an entire system of doing things obsolete. Making PowerPoint presentations 5x faster isn't that problem. I'd tell these founders what I've told many talented teams: you're too capable for a problem this bounded. Find something that scares you and matters.
