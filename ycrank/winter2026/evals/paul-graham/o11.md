# o11 -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice is a gap between who's building this and who it's for. The founders are 19-year-old college students from UNC targeting bankers, lawyers, and consultants. When I ask my first question — how did they find this problem? — the answer is revealing: "bankers, lawyers, and consultants told us they were tired of chat bots that couldn't do real work." That's second-hand discovery. They heard about the pain; they didn't live it. Neither founder has worked a single day in finance, consulting, or law. Compare this to Stripe, where the Collisons were hackers who personally endured the misery of integrating payment APIs. They were the user. Here, the founders are building for a user they've only interviewed. Ideas that come from conversations with potential customers rather than from your own frustration have a different character — they tend to optimize for what people say they want rather than what they actually need. The tagline "Microsoft Copilot, But It Actually Works" confirms this: it's a competitive positioning statement, not an expression of organic pain. Nobody who discovered a problem through lived experience describes their company as "X, but better."

That said, there is real schlep here, and I don't want to dismiss it. Building add-ins that manipulate native Office objects — 3D charts, Gantt charts, Excel formulas, citations across thousands of documents — inside Microsoft 365 and Google Workspace simultaneously is genuinely tedious work. It requires wrestling with Microsoft's add-in APIs, maintaining compatibility across six different applications, and handling enterprise-grade output quality for finance professionals who notice every misaligned cell. Most AI startups avoid this integration pain and build their own canvas instead, which is exactly what Gamma did. The schlep of operating inside someone else's application rather than building your own is real and does structurally deter some competition. But the schlep is technical, not domain-specific — you don't need to understand structured finance to build an Office add-in. That's a weaker form of the moat than what Stripe had, where the schlep was banking regulation that required genuine domain expertise.

The strongest signal in this company is the CTO. Ajay Misra led AI at Cal AI, which reached $40M ARR and 10M users before being acquired by MyFitnessPal. That is an extraordinarily rare credential for a pre-seed founder. Most pre-seed CTOs have built prototypes; this one has shipped an AI product at real scale. If I were evaluating on technical hacker quality alone, this would score high. The two-person team has already shipped a working product across six platforms with integrations into PitchBook, OneDrive, Box, and Dropbox. That's prolific output. Aryah at 19 has already founded ERA-0, CitySprout, and KlubSpace — the serial founder energy is there even if the wins are small. These are clearly builders, not PowerPoint founders.

The bull case is that the in-app approach is genuinely differentiated and that execution speed will matter more than domain expertise. Gamma built a standalone presentation tool and reached $100M ARR, but it requires users to leave their existing workflow. If o11 can deliver comparable AI capability without leaving PowerPoint or Excel, finance professionals — who live in these apps — might prefer it. The "hundreds of companies" claim, the 150 ratings at 4.8/5, and the featured logos (Wells Fargo, Bank of America) suggest at least surface-level adoption. If these are real enterprise deployments rather than individual free-tier users, the company is further along than it appears. And if Ajay can apply what he learned scaling Cal AI to this new context, the growth trajectory could be steep.

But I keep returning to the competitive dynamics. This is a consensus category — AI productivity tools are what everyone in Silicon Valley is building right now. Gamma has $91M in funding and $100M in ARR. Microsoft Copilot is the direct incumbent, and Microsoft controls the very platform o11 runs on. The "pricing umbrella" argument — that Copilot at $30/month creates room for alternatives — is the kind of reasoning that sounds good in a pitch but ignores the fact that Microsoft can change its pricing, restrict add-in APIs, or simply improve Copilot for finance use cases at any time. Building your entire company as a third-party add-in inside a competitor's product is a precarious position. Plus AI already does the Google Slides/PowerPoint piece. Templafy has $147M for enterprise document management. This is not a market where competition is structurally low because of schlep blindness — it's a market where dozens of well-funded teams are fighting for the same users.

The absence of organic founder-problem fit in a crowded, consensus market is a combination I find hard to get past. The founders are technically capable and clearly energetic. But when I apply my sequence of tests — organic test, schlep test, relentlessly resourceful test — they pass the schlep test partially and the technical-hacker test strongly, but fail the organic test. In a market with less competition, strong technical founders can compensate for manufactured problem discovery by iterating quickly. In a market this crowded, with this much capital deployed, you need every advantage — including the deep domain intuition that comes from having personally lived inside the problem. I'd watch these founders closely. Ajay in particular is someone I'd want to fund doing something else — something closer to his own experience. But for o11 as it stands, the thesis fit isn't strong enough for my money.
