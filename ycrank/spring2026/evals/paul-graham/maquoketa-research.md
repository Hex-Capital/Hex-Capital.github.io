# Maquoketa Research -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice is what's missing. When I look at a company, I start with a simple question: how did these founders find this problem? Did they stumble into it through their own life, or did they sit down and decide to start a startup? The dossier tells me Yeager came from trading technology at SpiderRock. Hernandez was doing phased array research at Berkeley's Wireless Research Center. Williams is a mechanical engineer with no public background at all. Nowhere in any of this do I see the moment of organic discovery -- the equivalent of Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive, or the Airbnb founders not being able to make rent. What I see instead is a team of technically capable Berkeley engineers who noticed that defense drones are the hottest category in startups right now and said "we could build that." That pattern -- smart people picking a trending market -- is exactly the failure mode I described years ago. The idea could have been conceived by anyone reading Defense News.

The schlep factor here is genuinely enormous. ITAR compliance, security clearances, DoD procurement cycles that run 12-36 months, hardware manufacturing at scale, autonomous targeting regulations under DoD Directive 3000.09 -- this is ugly, painful work that would make most founders run the other direction. In theory, that's exactly what I look for. Stripe worked because thousands of hackers knew payments were broken but nobody wanted to deal with the banks. The problem is that defense drones are no longer schlep-blind. Ukraine cured the blindness. When Germany hands €9B+ in drone contracts to startups, when every defense VC is writing checks, when five competitors have raised between $100M and $3.5B each, the unconscious filtering mechanism that usually protects a market from competition has been completely disabled. Schlep blindness is only a moat when other people are still blind.

The competitive landscape here is the hardest version of the "requires more than one miracle" problem. AeroVironment has $990M+ in Army contracts. Anduril has raised $3.5B. Helsing is valued at €12B. Even Stark Defence, which failed all four test strikes on its German contract, has raised $100M at a $500M valuation. And Neros, probably the closest peer, has $121M and a U.S. Army PBAS selection. Maquoketa has five people, YC funding, and a website that returns a 403 error. To win from this position, you need the product to be dramatically better than anything else in the market, AND you need to navigate procurement relationships that favor established players, AND you need to build manufacturing capacity for hardware. That's three miracles.

The strongest bull case is Hernandez's phased array and RF design specialization. This is legitimately rare at the startup level -- most defense drone companies are either assembling commercial off-the-shelf components or building software layers on top of existing hardware. If Maquoketa's in-house RF capability enables meaningfully better guidance, communications resilience, or electronic warfare resistance, that could be a genuine technical moat. The team covering all three core disciplines -- software (Yeager), RF/electronics (Hernandez), mechanical (Williams) -- is the right structure for building a complete drone system. And there is a real structural opening: defense primes like Lockheed and Raytheon can't easily pivot from high-margin, low-volume platforms to high-volume expendable drones without cannibalizing their own business. If I believed this team had discovered something specific about the RF problem through personal experience -- if Hernandez had been trying to solve EW resistance for years before deciding to start a company -- this could be a different evaluation entirely.

But I don't have that evidence. What I have is strong academic credentials, zero traction, zero contracts, zero public presence, and a hot market with well-funded incumbents. The founders are from Berkeley, not from the battlefield. The YC page says they build "autonomous one-way attack drones with in-house advanced electronics design," but I've seen no prototype, no demonstration, no customer letters of intent, and no SBIR awards. At pre-seed I don't demand much -- but I need something. A working demo. A meeting with a procurement officer that went well. An advisor with deep DoD relationships. Even a GitHub repo showing the founders actually writing code. The total absence of any signal beyond the YC listing itself is not what I'd expect from founders who are genuinely animated by this problem.

I keep coming back to a basic test: would these founders have built this product if they hadn't decided to start a startup? If Hernandez was going to work on phased array systems for autonomous drones regardless of whether it was a company, that's one thing. But the evidence points more toward "we have relevant skills and found a market" than "we've been obsessed with this problem and finally couldn't stand not solving it." That distinction matters. The years of painful execution required to win in defense -- the clearances, the procurement cycles, the manufacturing scaling -- are only survivable if the founders' motivation runs deeper than opportunity recognition.
