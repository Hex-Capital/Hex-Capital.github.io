# Saffron -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what jumps out at me immediately: this is a market where HackerRank is doing $221 million in revenue, CodeSignal has raised $90 million, and Karat hit a billion-dollar valuation. And both HackerRank and CodeSignal already offer configurable AI tools in their assessments. The dossier itself says the core concept — let candidates code with AI on a real repo and record everything — is replicable, and that any IDE provider or AI coding tool could build a similar layer. When the research memo written about your company identifies the commoditization risk this clearly, that's not a nuance I'm discovering — it's the obvious structural challenge. I backed Flexport because once you route a container through the network, the data and relationships compound and nobody can replicate that from outside. I don't see what compounds here that a well-funded incumbent couldn't build as a feature within two quarters.

When I sit down with founders, the first thing I want to hear is their story — not the pitch, not the TAM slide. What happened in your life that made this problem yours to solve? With Saffron, I see three genuinely talented young people from MIT, Stanford, and Harvey Mudd. Two of them went through Jane Street, which is famous for its hiring bar, so they have firsthand experience with rigorous technical evaluation. That's relevant domain exposure. But it's not a forge. "I went through elite technical interviews and saw they were broken" is an observation shared by essentially every engineer in Silicon Valley. It's the single most-discussed topic on every developer forum. Where's the moment this problem grabbed them by the throat and wouldn't let go? Jerry Yao built edtech products reaching 50,000 users and assistive technology for blind people — that tells me he knows how to build and he cares about people. Robert Chondro ran a nonprofit serving underprivileged kids. These are good humans. But the connection between who they are and why technical assessments must be reimagined for the AI era doesn't read as biographical inevitability. It reads as smart people who spotted a timely market gap.

The invisible system test is where this really falls short for me. Technical hiring is imperfect — deeply, annoyingly imperfect — but it's not opaque. It's arguably the most transparent broken system in technology. Every engineer has written the blog post about why leetcode interviews fail. Every hiring manager has complained about false negatives. The dysfunction is fully visible; it's just hard to fix. Compare that to property tax assessments, where most homeowners don't even know they're being overcharged, or credit card interest structures designed to obscure true costs from borrowers. When I backed Affirm, people literally didn't understand what they were paying. In technical hiring, everyone understands the problem. There's no hidden extraction mechanism being revealed. This is process improvement in a well-lit room, not a flashlight shone into a dark one.

Let me give the bull case its full weight, because the timing angle is genuine. AI-assisted coding is becoming how software actually gets written, and there is a real structural mismatch — companies evaluate candidates on a skill (unassisted algorithmic problem-solving) that no longer reflects the actual work. If you believe, as I do, that AI coding agents will be the default mode of software development within a few years, then the entire $1.2-billion assessment market needs to be rebuilt for that reality. HackerRank's moat is their puzzle library — thousands of algorithmic questions with standardized scoring. Pivoting to real-codebase, AI-native assessments doesn't just add a feature; it undermines the content asset they've spent a decade building. And the human-AI interaction dataset — every prompt, every accepted and rejected suggestion, every iteration pattern — could become genuinely unique training data for scoring models. If Saffron captured enough volume before incumbents moved, that dataset would be proprietary infrastructure. There's also a platform dependency angle that cuts both ways: yes, building on Claude Code creates Anthropic risk, but it also means the product works right now while competitors would need to build or license that integration. If these founders can execute fast enough to build that data moat before the window closes, there's a real company here.

But "execute fast enough before well-funded incumbents notice" is a race I don't love funding. The concept doesn't make VCs shift in their chairs — it makes them nod enthusiastically. Every investor understands that AI is changing how we code and therefore how we hire. This is a consensus thesis in a consensus-attractive market. Nobody is going to tell you at a dinner party that this idea is crazy. And the Anthropic dependency isn't a small thing — if Claude Code's pricing changes, or Anthropic decides assessment is a natural extension of their developer tools business, or a competing model becomes dominant, the foundation moves beneath you. I've learned the hard way that building your core product on someone else's infrastructure platform is different from building infrastructure yourself. This team would benefit from a more traditional SaaS investor who can help them navigate enterprise sales cycles and compete on distribution. It's not a Long Journey bet — there's no hidden system beneath the surface, no uncomfortable space being entered, no biographical inevitability pulling these founders toward this specific problem. I wish them well.
