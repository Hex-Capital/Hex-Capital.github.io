# Advocate -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most analytically interesting feature of Advocate is what I'd call a "billing gap" play: care coordination staff at skilled nursing facilities and behavioral health centers already perform reimbursable work -- scheduling, follow-ups, social determinant screenings -- under CMS codes like CPT 99490 (~$62/patient/month), but the documentation burden prevents most facilities from capturing that revenue. Advocate's pitch is dead simple: we find money you're leaving on the table. That's one of the most compelling sales narratives in B2B software, and it maps to my "capture existing behavior in a new medium" framework -- the behavior already exists, the billing codes already exist, and the AI layer bridges the gap between activity and reimbursement. The question is whether this structural observation constitutes a genuinely nonobvious market window or a narrow feature inside a thoroughly consensus category.

Start with market structure timing. Two shifts are converging: CMS expanded care management codes (CCM, PCM, behavioral health integration) over recent years, and speech-to-text plus LLM accuracy crossed the threshold required for automated code matching in 2024-2025. Both are real shifts. But "AI automates medical coding" is one of the most well-funded theses in healthcare right now. CodaMetrix raised $109M, Sully.ai has $35M and ~$2M cARR, Waystar is a public company doing $1.1B in revenue. Advocate's niche -- non-physician care coordination billing at smaller facilities -- is modestly less obvious than the physician encounter coding market, but it's a subspecialty of a consensus thesis, not a market most investors have dismissed. I'd compare this to investing in a payroll feature rather than investing in Gusto. When I backed Gusto early, the insight was that SMB payroll was a boring, critical market with no prestige-seeking competition -- the entire category was underserved. Here, the broader category (AI-powered medical coding) is swarming with capital. Advocate's specific niche may be temporarily protected by channel mismatch -- CodaMetrix's enterprise sales team won't chase $62/patient/month at nursing homes -- but that protection is fragile.

The infrastructure-layer test is where Advocate falls short of my framework. This is an application-layer tool: voice notes go in, billing packets come out. There's no API that other healthcare businesses build on, no developer ecosystem, no compounding network effect. The dossier itself notes "no defensibility signals found in public sources." The core technology -- ASR plus LLM-based code mapping -- is assembled from widely available components. Abridge, which I've backed and which already captures clinical voice and is expanding into coding workflows, could extend into care coordination codes as a natural product adjacency. DeepScribe and Suki face the same low barrier. Advocate is building an application on top of an infrastructure layer (foundation models, ASR services) that it doesn't own and can't control. That's the exact pattern I flag as platform risk.

The single-miracle test reveals a subtlety that matters at this stage. The technology miracle -- accurate speech-to-text and code matching -- is happening exogenously through foundation model improvement, which is favorable. But Advocate faces a compliance accuracy miracle that's independent: automated billing packet generation touches CMS fraud and abuse regulations (False Claims Act, Anti-Kickback Statute). The tolerance for coding errors is near zero because upcoding exposes facilities to legal liability. Getting accuracy high enough that facility administrators trust automated billing submissions to CMS is a distinct challenge from general AI coding accuracy. Layer on a distribution miracle -- selling into fragmented skilled nursing facilities and behavioral health centers, which are notoriously low-tech and relationship-driven buyer segments -- and Advocate needs two to three things to go right simultaneously. That's compounding odds.

The strongest bull case: Advocate maps to my "boring but critical" pattern. Care coordination billing at post-acute facilities is exactly the kind of unglamorous, operationally essential category where the absence of prestige-seeking competition IS the competitive advantage. The $3.2B care coordination software market (projected $8.1B by 2033) has a clear payer with budget authority -- facility administrators who directly benefit from captured reimbursement. Andrew Baran's personal connection to the problem (mother worked as a care coordinator) and Justinas Banys's experience building data systems at Garner Health (a $1.35B care navigation company) together cover domain and technical credibility. If Advocate nails the accuracy requirements and builds audit trail lock-in at enough facilities, it could become the default billing capture layer for this underserved segment, generating sticky revenue with minimal competition. The percentage-of-recovery pricing model aligns revenue with value delivered. For this bull case to work, the compliance accuracy hurdle needs to be solved with minimal human review, and the channel into fragmented facilities needs to be cracked -- potentially through partnerships with care coordination software vendors rather than direct sales. Neither is impossible, but both remain unproven.

The founder profile is serviceable but not distinctive by my standards. Baran's background spans McKinsey, Radian Capital (healthcare-focused VC), and a co-founding claim at Leilo that couldn't be independently verified -- public sources name different founders, which is a minor but real credibility flag. Banys joined Garner Health at Series A and built data systems there, which is relevant healthcare data experience, but at a Senior Data Science Engineer level rather than in a leadership role that demonstrates team scaling ability. Neither founder has operated at a company during its hypergrowth phase in a capacity where they were building and pruning teams. I've seen strong markets compensate for execution gaps, but the market here isn't strong enough to provide that cushion.

The dimension that locks Advocate into the Neutral tier rather than Invest is infrastructure-layer positioning. A billing capture application assembled from commodity components -- ASR, LLMs, CPT code databases -- without compounding switching costs or platform effects doesn't match the kind of bet I make. I invest in the infrastructure layers themselves, not applications sitting atop them. The nonobvious market timing dimension confirms: the niche is real but the broader thesis is consensus. If Advocate were building the compliance infrastructure layer that every healthcare AI billing company needed to use -- the audit trail platform, the CMS submission API -- I'd be materially more interested. As it stands, Advocate is a well-targeted application in a specific vertical gap, but not the kind of structural position I look for.
