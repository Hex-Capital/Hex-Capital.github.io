# Aster -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Emmett Bicker is eighteen years old, dropped out of college three weeks in, moved to San Francisco alone, and single-authored an arXiv paper claiming state-of-the-art on 4 of 5 benchmarks with 20x speed improvement over existing autonomous discovery methods. He's been doing ML since he was fifteen. Before Aster, he was a post-training researcher at Magic working on long-context coding LLMs — a real AI lab, not a resume line. He co-authored a paper in Nature Communications on hippocampal imaging. He contributed to the NanoGPT speedrun and claimed a world record training GPT-2 equivalent in 95.2 seconds. The raw output from age fifteen to eighteen — robotics team captain, neuroscience publication, AI lab researcher, solo research paper, YC founder — is the kind of trajectory that makes me pay attention. I've backed teenagers before. Patrick Collison was a teenager when I first funded Auctomatic, and the Bindwell founders were eighteen and nineteen when I invested in their AI pesticide company last year. Bicker has that same quality of doing too much too young, which is usually the earliest visible marker of the animal instinct I look for. The question with Aster isn't whether Bicker is talented. He obviously is. The question is whether this is the right bet at the right time in the right structure.

Aster's origin story has the right shape. Bicker wasn't reading market reports about MLOps tooling — he was inside the ML research loop at Magic, running experiments, iterating on training approaches, experiencing firsthand the bottleneck of manual hypothesis generation and testing. When someone who has been doing ML research since fifteen builds a tool to automate ML research, that's "live in the future, then build what's missing." The problem discovery is organic. But here's what bothers me: this is the obvious next thing for anyone inside that world to build. Every ML researcher who has spent a week running ablation studies has thought "why can't AI do this part for me?" Bicker got there fast and executed well, but the insight isn't the kind of hidden, schlep-blind discovery that creates natural moats. Stripe worked because payments infrastructure was a problem that repelled developers — the regulatory complexity, the bank integrations, the PCI compliance. Nobody looked at that mess and thought "I want to build there." Aster is the opposite. Everyone wants to build automated AI research. That's the problem.

Sakana AI has $479 million in total funding, published the first AI-authored paper in Nature, and open-sourced their AI Scientist on GitHub. Autoscience raised $14 million from General Catalyst in March 2026 with positioning so similar to Aster's that you could swap their descriptions and nobody would notice. FutureHouse has $250 million in philanthropic funding plus a commercial spinout with $70 million at a $250 million valuation. Google is building the same capability internally on Gemini. When I invested in Stripe, nobody else wanted to do that work. When I look at Aster, everyone wants to do this work, and several of them have a hundred times more capital to do it with. Aster's 20x speed claim is the primary differentiator, but it comes from Bicker's own single-author preprint without independent verification or peer review — that's a data point, not a moat. The core technique, using LLMs to iteratively generate and test hypotheses, is published and reproduced. Sakana's implementation is open source. In my framework from Black Swan Farming, if a good idea were obviously good, someone else would already have done it. Multiple well-funded someones are already doing it. Aster's idea is consensus-good, which in my experience means the returns have been competed away before the race starts.

Bicker is a solo founder. I've written explicitly about why this is dangerous — you need someone to brainstorm with, to talk you out of stupid decisions, to cheer you up when things go wrong. Aster's website says they're "looking for one extraordinarily talented person," and the YC page shows zero open jobs. At pre-seed, solo founding isn't disqualifying — plenty of founders are solo when they enter YC and find co-founders during the batch. But Aster isn't a simple product. It's an autonomous research system competing with teams of dozens or hundreds at Sakana, Google, and Autoscience. The bandwidth constraint of one person building research-grade AI, a commercial product, and a go-to-market simultaneously is real. The dossier also shows no evidence of creative workarounds under resource constraint — no Airbnb-cereal-box stories, no bootstrapping revenue, no scrappy pivots. Bicker's path from college dropout to Magic to YC was smooth and credential-forward. That demonstrates talent and ambition, which matter, but they're not the same as resourcefulness when everything goes wrong.

The bull case an advocate would make: Bicker at eighteen is the next Patrick Collison. Collison was a teenager at Auctomatic, which sold for $5 million, and then built Stripe into one of the most valuable private companies in the world. Bicker's technical output — Nature Communications, NanoGPT world record, SOTA benchmarks, single-author arXiv paper — already exceeds what most founders produce in a career. The 20x speed advantage, if it holds, is a genuine technical edge. The "consensus idea" critique is wrong because the actual contrarian bet is that a solo eighteen-year-old can outperform Google and $479M Sakana — most people would call that insane. And his earnestness is real: he's been doing ML since before ChatGPT existed, he dropped out in three weeks because he couldn't wait, and his hiring post reads like a builder looking for another builder, not an executive building a deck. I take this seriously. Bicker is someone I'd want to track across multiple companies. But Collison's genius was picking payments — a schlep that nobody else wanted. Bicker picked the idea everyone wants. That's a fundamental difference in competitive dynamics, and it's the reason I'm not writing a check today.

Aster scores high on founder learning rate — the trajectory from fifteen to eighteen is as steep as any I've seen, and the output is verifiable through publications, the speedrun, and the arXiv paper. Aster also scores high on earnest builder identity — Bicker isn't chasing a trend; he was in this field before it was a trend. But Aster scores low on the dimension I care about most after founder quality: the idea needs to repel before it attracts, and Aster's idea attracts everyone immediately. The one verifiable condition that would flip me to Invest: if Bicker finds a strong co-founder and Aster demonstrates that a small group of ML researchers have built their entire workflow around the platform and can't go back — the kind of obsessive early usage where people use it even in its crappiest form because the alternative is unbearable — then the competitive moat shifts from "we're faster" to "we understand researchers' workflows better than anyone." Right now, I see an exceptional eighteen-year-old alone in a crowded gold rush. I'd rather back him the second time around, on a problem that makes everyone else say "ugh, that sounds terrible."
