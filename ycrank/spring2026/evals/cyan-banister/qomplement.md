# qomplement -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

$60,000 per month on AWS infrastructure, with a $200,000 proprietary model training investment — those are the numbers qomplement leads with as its defensibility story. And I keep coming back to a hard question: what does $200K of training cost buy you when OpenAI, Anthropic, and an open-source project with 50,000 GitHub stars are all building the same core capability? BrowserUse raised $17M. Extend AI is already cash-flow positive serving Brex and Square. Rossum has $114.5M and $44.9M in annual revenue. qomplement is entering a space where capital is flowing freely and competitors are well-established — this is consensus AI, not contrarian conviction. When I think about the investments where I wrote the first check — SpaceX when rockets were exploding, Anduril when defense tech was politically radioactive — those were spaces that made other investors physically uncomfortable. Document processing automation makes investors excited. That's exactly the wrong signal for my framework.

Kerim Taray describes himself as "engineer, techno-optimist, contrarian" on his personal site. Andrés Fernando Garza García is described as a "recently graduated engineer" by Milenio and a "former CTO from high-growth startups" by Semilla Ventures — and those two descriptions sit uneasily together. When I meet founders, my opening question is always the same: tell me your story, not your business. qomplement's dossier gives me no story. No biographical crucible. No personal encounter with the pain of processing 5,000 invoices a month in a logistics company. No evidence that this problem found these founders rather than the reverse. Both attended Tec de Monterrey, Mexico's top private engineering school — that's a credential, not a forge. I've backed founders who taught themselves to code while surviving circumstances that would break most people. The absence of a founding story here isn't neutral to me — it's data. It tells me these are competent engineers who spotted a market opportunity in CUA technology, and that's a fundamentally different animal than someone who can't sleep because this problem is personal.

qomplement's local-first architecture — running a proprietary model on machines with 8-16 GB of RAM — is the one element that genuinely catches my attention. Let me make the bull case honestly: cloud AI providers have revenue models built on per-API-call economics, and going local-first in regulated industries where data can't leave the building is a structural bet that conflicts with how Google, Microsoft, and OpenAI make money. qomplement claims its agents cost 15x less than cloud alternatives at $3 per interaction. That transportation customer automating 10,000+ invoices monthly and saving $15,000 is real pre-seed traction. If you squint, there's a version of this where qomplement becomes the on-device AI infrastructure layer for every legacy enterprise system in Latin America — and LatAm's enterprise software landscape is genuinely underserved by Silicon Valley. The AWS GAIA 2025 cohort and Semilla Ventures backing suggest real network in a market most US investors ignore. I've made infrastructure bets at inflection points before — Crusoe Energy started as something most people dismissed too.

But here's where that parallel breaks down. Crusoe's founders had built portable data centers to capture energy that was literally being burned and wasted — an invisible system of extraction that nobody in Silicon Valley could see because it was happening in North Dakota oil fields. qomplement is automating invoice entry. Invoice processing is expensive and manual, but it's not opaque. Companies know exactly what they're spending on data entry. There's no hidden tax, no information asymmetry, no extractive structure being revealed. When Affirm attacked credit card opacity or Ownwell attacked property tax appeals, the act of making the system visible was itself the product. qomplement makes a known inefficiency faster — that's valuable, but it's optimization, not revelation. And the local-first positioning, while architecturally interesting, creates scaling constraints: enterprise customers with thin clients or locked-down IT environments face adoption barriers, and supporting custom model deployments across thousands of heterogeneous machines is an operational nightmare that cloud competitors simply don't have.

qomplement's product positioning tells its own story — "OS-level AI agent for legacy software automation" from Semilla Ventures, "AI Agents for Document Filling" from YC, "Turn invoices and POs into system-ready data" from their Twitter bio, and a website that shows nothing but the company name. That ambiguity at pre-seed is forgivable, but combined with the commoditization trajectory of CUA technology, it suggests qomplement hasn't yet found the wedge that makes it structurally irreplaceable rather than temporarily faster. The one condition that would flip me: if Kerim Taray or Andrés Garza García walked me through a biographical story where they personally lived inside the legacy-system pain — maybe running operations at one of those LatAm logistics companies, watching people manually enter thousands of invoices while knowing the technology to fix it was being built for the wrong market — and if qomplement had three to five paying customers with signed contracts proving the local-first architecture creates genuine lock-in that cloud competitors structurally cannot match, I'd reconsider. Right now, qomplement is a competent team building in a consensus space without the biographical inevitability or structural contrarianism that defines my highest-conviction bets.
