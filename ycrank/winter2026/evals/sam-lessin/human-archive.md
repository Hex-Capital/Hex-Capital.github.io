# Human Archive -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the problem I can't get past: Human Archive is a company that ceases to exist the moment you remove AI from the equation. That's the exact inverse of what I want to fund. My "cherry on top" test asks whether a business would be good if AI didn't exist -- and if removing AI makes the business worthless, you're not building a company, you're building a feature of someone else's stack. A multimodal robotics data provider has zero customers, zero revenue, and zero reason to exist in a world without foundation models that consume training data. Compare that to Craftwork -- take away AI and you still have a painting company with crews, trucks, and homeowners who need their walls done. Take away AI from Human Archive and you have four Berkeley and Stanford kids sitting in Ahmedabad with cameras and no one to sell footage to.

The sane-person-insane-idea filter doesn't fire here either. Four college dropouts with ML research backgrounds and a stint at Coinbase are smart and energetic, but they don't carry the operational credibility of someone who built robotics infrastructure at scale. Samay's ML@B work and published papers show technical competence; Shloke's CHI '25 publication and Stanford ME background add engineering depth. But "we'll build custom sensor hardware and collect the world's largest annotated multimodal dataset" requires a level of hardware and operational expertise that I don't see evidenced in the founders' track records. More critically, the idea itself isn't insane -- it's obvious. Everyone in the robotics space knows that foundation models are data-hungry. Goldman projects 50,000-100,000 humanoid robot shipments in 2026. The market is projected at $30B+ by 2035. When every VC on Sand Hill Road can articulate why robotics needs training data, you've lost the non-consensus premium entirely.

The competitive dynamics are what really concern me, and they echo the Birchbox lesson I carry everywhere. Scale AI entered robotics data collection in September 2025 -- contractor-based, sure, but with $2B in annual revenue, existing relationships with Physical Intelligence and every frontier lab, and the operational muscle to scale globally. Scale's entry doesn't just validate the market; it compresses the window for a startup to build a defensible position before the incumbent catches up. And here's the structural trap: Human Archive's customers are the very labs that have the strongest incentive to vertically integrate data collection. Generalist AI is already collecting 270,000 hours of manipulation data internally, growing at 10,000 hours per week. When your TAM consists of maybe fifteen frontier labs, and those fifteen labs are simultaneously your customers and your future competitors, you're building a business with a shrinking addressable market disguised as an expanding one.

The bull case deserves a fair hearing. If robotics is where language AI was in 2019 -- right before the data flywheel became everything -- then whoever builds the canonical, high-quality multimodal dataset could become the foundational input layer for the entire industry. The custom hardware angle is genuinely differentiating from Scale's GoPro-to-contractor approach: force-feedback and teleoperation data with proper modality alignment is technically demanding, and the operational infrastructure in India creates a cost structure that's hard to match from San Francisco. The team's willingness to relocate to Asia demonstrates exactly the kind of operational intensity -- the "tizz" -- that I value. If the quality gap between custom-hardware-captured multimodal data and contractor-filmed video is as wide as the founders believe, and if the window before model companies vertically integrate is long enough, Human Archive could accumulate a data asset that compounds into genuine lock-in. That's a real scenario. I just don't think the founders have the specific domain credibility to execute it faster than better-resourced competitors, and the structural dynamics of the customer base working against them make this a race I wouldn't bet on.

From my protocol-versus-application lens, data is a consumable commodity, not a primitive. Nobody builds on top of Human Archive's dataset the way developers build on Helium's wireless network or applications build on Solana's chain. You consume training data, train a model, and the dataset becomes a sunk input. There's no compounding network effect, no ecosystem that grows around it, no winner-take-most dynamic from being the canonical data layer. The narrative of "we power all robotics learning" sounds expansive, but structurally it's a supplier relationship, not a platform position -- and suppliers get squeezed.
