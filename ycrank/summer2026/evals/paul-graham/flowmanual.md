# FlowManual -- Paul Graham Evaluation

FlowManual is a schlep bet wearing a market-pick costume: David Shijoon Bae and Michael Lin picked the hardest distribution path in construction software — on-prem, air-gapped, AES-256-GCM with SHA-256 audit digests, the works — for a problem neither of them has personally lived through. That pairing is the analytical signature. The schlep is real and unfashionable; the founder-problem fit is a market scan from Harvard. When I see that combination my hand does not move toward the checkbook the way it did when Brian Armstrong rebuilt his Android wallet to overturn his **Coinbase** (2012) rejection.

**Coinbase** (2012) is the load-bearing analogy for the one dimension where FlowManual scores well. Armstrong's moat was nobody else wanting to do KYC/BSA compliance grunt-work in 2012; FlowManual's moat candidate is nobody else wanting to ship containerized air-gapped deployments to security-sensitive GCs while Trunk Tools ($70M raised, cloud-first) and Document Crunch ($30.5M raised, cloud-first) optimize for the easy half of the market. That is genuine schlep blindness in the **Schlep Blindness** (January 2012) sense — Procore is structurally conflicted on multi-tenant and the AI-native peers are venture-funded into cloud-first defaults. Where FlowManual diverges from the Coinbase rung: Armstrong was the schlep specialist who'd already wrestled bitcoin compliance personally before YC; the FlowManual dossier shows zero construction-industry employment for either founder, so the schlep is chosen, not survived.

The Pass Example I keep on my desk is the MBA-AI-wrapper team — glossy deck, no shipped artifact, no founder who lived the pain. FlowManual is not that company, but it rhymes on the dimension that matters most to me. Drew Houston forgot his USB stick at MIT before he built **Dropbox** (2007); the Collisons had been hacking commerce since **Auctomatic** (2007) before they built **Stripe** (2010). FlowManual's dossier names Cedar Ridge Mechanical as a customer and says nothing about either founder ever having reviewed a submittal, sat through a bid-leveling meeting, or chased a change-order discrepancy. The product is plausible. The biography is not. When I read "How to Get Startup Ideas" (November 2012) back to myself the test is "something the founders themselves want" — FlowManual is something the founders inferred contractors want.

Animal resourcefulness — David Shijoon Bae was 2024-25 National President of the California Association of Student Councils at eighteen, which is a real operational stunt for a teenager and not nothing in the **Loopt** (2005) force-of-personality sense; if there were a cereal-box moment in the FlowManual dossier I'd weight it heavily, but CASC is student-government leverage, not commercial leverage, and the dossier surfaces no pre-funding revenue stunt, no manual-onboarding war story, no unscalable founder-driven sale to Cedar Ridge Mechanical. The mechanism worry: on-prem AI deployments compound on founder grit over 18 months — installation services, security reviews, IT-team hand-holding — and a 2-person team without an industry rolodex will either eat that schlep personally (in which case I want to see them doing it now) or get crushed by it.

Hacker genealogy is where FlowManual is quietly thin in a way the dossier almost hides. Counterfactually: if Michael Lin had a visible GitHub with a shipped LLM-retrieval library, or if either founder had a Hacker News footprint predating FlowManual, the score flips a tier and the air-gapped deployment claim becomes credible-by-construction. The dossier reports "no GitHub repos found" for Bae and "no public account confirmed" for Lin, with HCS Tech for Social Good as Lin's only technical signal. Patrick Collison won Ireland's 41st Young Scientist for Lisp work at sixteen; Aaron Swartz co-authored RSS before **Reddit** (2005). FlowManual's hacker trail is absent rather than weak, and absence is itself a signal — animals leave tracks.

Co-founder configuration is the most neutral cell on the page. FlowManual has a pair, not a solo, which clears the **Reddit** (2005) Huffman/Ohanian dorm-roommate floor on configuration but not on chemistry — the dossier explicitly says "no public data on specific shared classes, clubs, or prior employment." Two Harvard dropouts is a story shape; two people who built three projects together for two years before YC is a story. The dossier offers the former.

My 36-month forecast: FlowManual stays under $1.5M ARR through Q2 2029 and gets acquihired into Trunk Tools or Document Crunch for the air-gapped deployment stack rather than the customer base, or shuts down. The mechanism is straightforward — Trunk Tools' Series B war chest plus Document Crunch's clause-level head start means they replicate bid-vs-contract diff inside 12 months, while FlowManual's two-Harvard-dropout team without construction-industry scar tissue runs aground on the enterprise sales cycle that the on-prem motion forces them into.

The strongest case against my pass is the **Airbnb** (2009) precedent: I rejected that idea silently in my head and was wrong because the cereal boxes overrode the idea. FlowManual's bull case is that the air-gapped + AES-256-GCM stance is a cereal-box-equivalent unscalable choice — a deliberate schlep that scares off Trunk Tools and Document Crunch from the security-sensitive GC segment, with Cedar Ridge Mechanical as the **Wufoo**-style first paying handshake. That case stings because **Coinbase** (2012) is exactly this shape and I almost passed on it. What dismantles it: Armstrong personally lived the compliance schlep before YC and shipped a working wallet to prove it; FlowManual's founders chose the schlep from the outside and the dossier surfaces no equivalent shipped artifact. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: a second and third named on-prem customer of Cedar Ridge's size signed within 90 days of YC Demo Day, with one founder personally running the installation. That is the FlowManual cereal box I'd need to see.
