# Scheduling Wizard -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The managed-service model is the most interesting thing about this company, and it's also what tells me this is the wrong kind of bet. Scheduling Wizard takes scheduling off the plate of residency program directors by doing it for them -- 19 departments across 14 hospitals, including Mass General and Johns Hopkins. That's real execution from a team of three recent JHU graduates, and I give them credit for it. But a managed service that produces physician schedules is a consulting operation with a software backbone, not a technology company that makes an industry structurally obsolete. The constraint-satisfaction problem at the heart of physician scheduling is well-understood computer science wrapped in domain-specific rules. That's optimization, not disruption.

The consequence of success here is what stops me. If Scheduling Wizard captures every residency program and fellowship in the country -- all ~1,100 ACGME-accredited institutions -- you've built a business worth perhaps a few hundred million dollars serving a niche within a $350M-$731M market. The dossier cites $760B in hospital inefficiency, but that's the broadest possible framing for what is, in practice, a physician scheduling tool for academic medical centers. When I backed Juniper, the consequence was replacing how the entire internet was routed. When I backed Square, it was replacing how every small business in America accepted payments. Here, the consequence is that chief residents save a few hundred hours a year on spreadsheets. It's a genuine pain point. It's not a problem whose consequence of success justifies my kind of bet.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. QGenda was acquired for $2.5B, proving that physician scheduling can generate significant enterprise value. The managed-service approach is somewhat contrarian -- every competitor sells self-serve software, but hospitals are drowning in software tools they don't have time to operate. If the "Scheduling Programming Language" accumulates enough department-specific logic, and if AI-driven automation reduces the labor intensity of the managed service to near-zero marginal cost, you could get software economics with white-glove experience. The incumbents can't easily replicate this without cannibalizing their per-seat revenue model. And these founders -- barely out of JHU -- already closed Mass General, Johns Hopkins, UT Southwestern, HCA, and UCSF. That sales velocity from 2024 graduates is genuinely impressive. If this team channels that execution capacity toward a larger target, they could build something significant. But right now, the ambition is calibrated to a niche, not a system.

The founding team is smart and complementary -- Oberly brings constraint optimization from Cambridge and DoD war games, Dermody brings Amazon and McMaster-Carr operations DNA, Hamimi brings the engineering stack. But they're JHU insiders building for JHU-adjacent customers. They know the residency scheduling world because they lived in it, which means they know exactly what the industry considers possible and impossible. That's the opposite of the outsider signal I look for. When Cisco's CTO told me TCP/IP routing would never work in telecom, that was the signal to invest in Juniper. Here, the experts -- QGenda, Lightning Bolt, Amion -- all validate that physician scheduling is a real market. When incumbents nod along with your thesis, you're probably not threatening them.

The technology curve argument is weak. Physician scheduling software has existed for decades. LLMs and modern constraint-solving make it cheaper for a small team to build competitive optimization, but that's a tailwind available to every competitor, including YouShift (YC W25, backed by Bain Capital Ventures) pursuing the identical space with a self-serve model. The timing isn't "skating to where the puck will be" -- the puck arrived years ago with QGenda and Lightning Bolt. The AI layer makes the product slightly better and slightly cheaper to build, but it doesn't create the kind of exponential trajectory I tracked when I wrote the OpenAI check in 2019, four years before the world noticed. This team is arriving at a well-attended party, not an empty field.

I pass. These founders are too capable to spend their careers making residency scheduling 30% faster. The problem isn't that it won't work -- it probably will. The problem is that success doesn't change how hospitals fundamentally operate. It makes one administrative workflow smoother in one corner of medicine. Some problems are too important not to work on. Physician scheduling is not one of them.
