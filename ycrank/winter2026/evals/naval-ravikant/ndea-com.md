# Ndea -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

François Chollet didn't just study intelligence — he built the instruments that measure it and the tools that produce it. Keras has 63.9K GitHub stars and powers deep learning at YouTube, Waymo, Spotify, ByteDance. The ARC-AGI benchmark is the closest thing the field has to a ground truth for what separates interpolation from genuine reasoning. Chollet created both. That's not domain expertise — domain expertise you can hire for. This is knowledge that emerged from a decade of building at the frontier, testing what works, observing what fails, and forming a thesis about why. When Chollet says publicly that LLMs will not produce AGI, he's not making a philosophical argument — he's reporting empirical observations from inside the machinery. I can count on one hand the number of people alive who have both built widely-adopted AI infrastructure and simultaneously designed the benchmark that exposes its fundamental limitations. That combination of builder-knowledge and measurement-knowledge, directed at the same problem, is the deepest specific knowledge profile I've encountered in an AI investment.

The leverage architecture here is unusual because it's recursive. Most leverage I invest in is multiplicative — one engineer's code serves a million users, one creator's podcast reaches a million listeners. Ndea's thesis is about creating a system that generates leverage itself: a program synthesis engine that independently discovers and invents. If it works, the leverage isn't 1000x — it's unbounded. One system producing novel scientific discoveries across drugs, energy, robotics, materials. That's not just infrastructure; it's a leverage factory. The obvious caveat: this leverage exists entirely as theory right now. No product. No revenue model. No pricing page. The "factory for rapid scientific advancement" is an aspiration, not a business. But I've learned not to ask "what's the business model?" at this stage of a genuine research breakthrough. The business model for general intelligence will be self-evident.

The contrarian positioning is what activates my framework most strongly. The entire AI industry — hundreds of billions in capital, tens of thousands of researchers — has bet that scaling transformer architectures with more data and compute is the path to AGI. Chollet is saying they're all wrong. Not slightly wrong. Architecturally wrong. His claim that "the state of program synthesis in 2025 looks a lot like deep learning circa 2012" is either delusional or the most important observation in AI right now. That's precisely the kind of bet I want to be in. When I invested in Bitcoin in 2014, smart people dismissed it as nerd money. When I invested in Uber at $4M, ride-sharing was illegal. The pattern is always the same: a small number of people with deep specific knowledge see something the consensus doesn't, and the consensus explains why they're wrong using logic that sounds compelling but misses the fundamental shift. OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, and xAI have collectively deployed more capital into scaling transformers than the GDP of some countries. Pivoting to program synthesis would mean admitting their core thesis is incomplete. That's the structural reason the consensus can't see what Chollet sees — their capital commitments have become intellectual commitments.

The Knoop pairing is strong and somewhat unusual. Zapier is one of the purest automation-leverage companies ever built — it literally multiplies individual productivity by connecting software systems without code. Knoop understands how tools create leverage for individuals at scale. He's also a YC alum (S12) who has operated as President and CPO of a company serving millions. The researcher-plus-operator pairing mirrors patterns I've seen work in AI labs, and their pre-existing collaboration through the ARC Prize Foundation means this isn't a convenience marriage — they've already demonstrated they can work together on a shared mission. Chollet leaving a Senior Staff position at Google to bet his career on this thesis, and Knoop stepping back from Zapier to make Ndea primary, both signal costly commitment over career optimization.

The real risk isn't competition — it's physics. Or rather, computation theory. Program synthesis may not scale. The ARC-AGI benchmark showed progress from 33% to 55.5%, which is meaningful, but still far from the 85% human target. The "circa 2012" analogy cuts both ways: deep learning circa 2012 required another eight years and exponential compute scaling before it became commercially dominant, and the winners of that scaling race were the companies with the most capital. If program synthesis follows the same trajectory, Google DeepMind — which already has formal reasoning expertise — could redirect thousands of researchers toward it once Ndea demonstrates viability. The $43M from Coatue and Factorial is serious money for a research lab but rounds to zero against frontier lab budgets. The key-person dependency on Chollet is extreme. And AGI bets have a near-perfect historical failure rate. These are real risks, not theoretical ones.

But here's why I lean in despite all of that: this maps to a pattern I've been following since Extropic and Atomic Semi. When someone with non-replicable specific knowledge identifies a computational paradigm shift that the consensus hasn't priced, and the technology would compound in ways that create entirely new categories of leverage, the right move is to make the bet and accept the failure probability. My portfolio is optimized for the case where nine investments go to zero and the tenth goes 1000x. Ndea has the specific knowledge profile, the contrarian positioning, and the theoretical leverage architecture that earns it a place in that portfolio. The benchmark ownership — controlling the very measurement system that defines AGI progress — is a subtle but powerful form of protocol-level positioning. If program synthesis is the right paradigm, Ndea will have had years of accumulated tacit knowledge before any well-funded competitor can redirect. The technology compounds because each program synthesis insight builds on the last in ways that pure capital cannot shortcut. I'd write a check.
