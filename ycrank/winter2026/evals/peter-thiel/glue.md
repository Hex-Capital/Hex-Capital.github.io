# Glue -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The AI developer tools market is, at this moment, the most Girardian space in all of technology. Cursor has raised $2.3 billion. Lovable has raised over half a billion. Bolt, Vercel's v0, and Figma collectively represent billions more in capitalization, and every one of them is building toward the same consensus: AI will transform how software is designed and built. Into this arena walks a two-person pre-seed team with no public repository, no traction data, and what appears to be a recent pivot from a product called "Willow." This is not a company entering a market. This is a company entering a war — and wars destroy value for everyone involved. When I look at a competitive landscape this crowded and this well-funded, my first question is not "can they win?" but rather "is there a secret here that justifies entering at all?"

The claimed insight — that AI coding agents need a visual design canvas rather than a code editor or a prompt-to-app generator — is not a secret. It is a product design hypothesis within a consensus thesis. Everyone in Silicon Valley agrees that AI agents will reshape development workflows. The question of *which interface paradigm wins* — code editor, natural language prompt, visual canvas — is an open design question, not a contrarian truth that most experts reject. A genuine secret would be something like "visual interfaces for software creation are structurally superior to text-based ones for AI agents because of [some deep insight about how transformer architectures process spatial information]." Instead, what I see is a feature bundle — a comment system, component sticking, framework auto-detection — packaged as a canvas. These are competent product ideas, but they do not constitute the kind of non-obvious truth around which a monopoly gets built. Stripe's secret was that developer experience in payments was so terrible that a clean API could capture the entire market. Glue's thesis appears to be "we will be the Figma for agents," which is a positioning statement, not a secret.

The founders have solid big-company credentials — Amazon, Microsoft, Meta — and relevant domain experience in UI engineering and AI agent architectures. Perbhat Kumar's work on enterprise RAG agents at Copilot Studio is genuinely relevant. But I see no evidence of the kind of definite, singular vision that characterizes the founders I back. The pivot signal — Tejas Priyadarshi's GitHub still reads "Founder @ Willow (YC W26)" — suggests the current product direction may be weeks old. Iteration is the opposite of definite optimism. When Zuckerberg sat across from me, he had already built the product and could tell me exactly what Facebook would become. When Musk started SpaceX, he had a specific plan for reusable rockets before the first test. I do not see that kind of conviction here — I see two capable engineers searching for the right product in a hot market, which is the lean startup methodology dressed in agent-native clothing.

Let me articulate the strongest bull case, because intellectual honesty demands it. If coding agents truly become the dominant mode of software creation — and the trajectory suggests they will — then the visual layer through which humans direct and review agent work could become as important as Figma was for the designer-to-engineer handoff. Figma captured $12.5 billion in value by owning a workflow transition point. If the entire EPD workflow collapses into a single "builder" role as Glue posits, whoever defines the canonical agent-builder canvas could capture analogous value. The timing argument is real: production-quality coding agents only emerged in 2024-2025, and no established player has yet built a purpose-designed visual collaboration surface for them. Being early to a genuinely new category — before incumbents react — is precisely how Figma defeated Adobe Illustrator and Sketch. If Glue achieves open-source adoption velocity comparable to Supabase or GitLab, the community itself could become a moat. This is the world in which Glue becomes a defining company.

But that bull case requires several things to be simultaneously true: that the visual canvas is the correct abstraction (rather than inline code review, natural language chat, or something else entirely), that Cursor and Lovable and Vercel do not simply add canvas features with their billions in funding, that open-source adoption materializes despite the repo not yet being public, and that the founding team develops the kind of extreme conviction necessary to hold a singular vision against well-funded competitors offering them acqui-hire deals. Each of these is possible. All of them together strike me as unlikely, and crucially, the path to monopoly is invisible. There are no network effects in an individual design canvas tied to a private codebase. There are no meaningful switching costs. There is no proprietary technology described that could not be replicated within eighteen months by any of the five competitors listed in this dossier, each of whom has at minimum a hundred times more capital. The open-source strategy that is supposed to provide distribution has not been activated — there is no public repository. A company cannot claim open-source as its moat when the source is not open.

This is a pass. Not because the founders lack capability — they clearly have relevant technical skills — but because the market structure is antithetical to monopoly creation. Competition in AI dev tools is not an incidental feature of the landscape; it is the defining characteristic. Billions of dollars are being deployed by multiple well-resourced players who share overlapping visions for how AI transforms software creation. There is no secret here that most smart people disagree with. There is no 10x technological discontinuity. There is no small market to monopolize first. What exists is a two-person team entering the most capital-intensive competitive arena in technology with a product hypothesis that may be weeks old. I have spent my career avoiding exactly this configuration.
