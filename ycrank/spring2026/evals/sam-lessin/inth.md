# Inth -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Guillermo Rauch calling Inth "@shadcn for Consent Management" is the most revealing data point in this entire dossier — and not for the reason the team thinks. Rauch runs Vercel. Vercel is the dominant platform for Next.js applications. If consent management is important enough for Vercel's CEO to tweet about, it's important enough for Vercel to build natively into their platform. Every time a platform CEO endorses your startup, you should ask: is this a customer or a future competitor? Inth has 1.8M npm downloads and impressive open-source traction, but the structural question isn't whether developers love c15t — it's whether the platform layer above them decides to absorb it. This is the "Adobe for AI" problem applied to developer tools: Vercel, Cloudflare, and Netlify all have the distribution, the framework integration points, and the engineering talent to ship a consent SDK as a platform feature. Inth's best champion is also its most plausible executioner.

Christopher Burns is clearly competent — second-time founder, previously CTO of Everfund processing payments across 33+ countries, decade of TypeScript experience, established in the Vercel/Next.js ecosystem. But competence is not the signal I'm looking for. I want a sane person pitching an insane idea. Burns is sane, and "faster cookie consent banners for developers" is... also sane. Extremely sane. Nobody hears "headless consent management platform" and thinks "that's fucking crazy, but what if he's right?" They think "yeah, that makes sense." A reasonable person building a reasonable thing in a validated market is the definition of a boring bet. Inth's sanity-to-insanity ratio is inverted from what I need — the idea should be the part that sounds crazy, not the part that sounds obvious.

OneTrust has $500M in revenue and raised $1.13B. Usercentrics does $117M. The consent management market is $732M to $1.8B depending on who's counting. This is not a category that VCs have dismissed — it's a category they've already funded to the tune of billions. The "mispricing" Inth is implicitly claiming is that incumbents are selling to compliance officers while developers are the real buyer. Fine, that's a wedge, not a mispricing. Developer-tools-for-regulated-infrastructure is one of the most consensus-attractive categories in venture right now — Snyk, HashiCorp, Vanta, Drata all proved this playbook. Every seed fund in the world is comfortable writing this check. When every investor at the demo day understands your thesis, the asymmetry is gone. Inth's 388K monthly npm downloads and listed customers including Vercel, Expo, and Zed are genuinely impressive pre-seed traction — but impressive traction in a consensus category means the price already reflects the upside.

The primitive test is where Inth definitively falls short for me. Venmo created a verb — people "Venmo" each other. Patreon created a behavior — paying creators directly — that didn't exist before. Solana created a new consensus mechanism. Inth makes consent management faster (30ms vs 445-709ms for incumbents) and more developer-native. That's a 15x performance improvement on an existing function, not a new primitive. People were already consenting to cookies; websites were already managing compliance. c15t is a dramatically better implementation of something OneTrust already does. The DSAR SDK in alpha hints at a broader privacy infrastructure play, but "privacy infrastructure for developers" at scale is exactly what OneTrust will become once they build a developer SDK — and they have $500M in revenue funding that effort. Inth's 0.6KB bundle size versus OneTrust's 50-100KB is a technical achievement, not a category creation event.

The bull case deserves honest construction: Inth has real developer love (1.8M npm downloads, 1,678 GitHub stars), a second-time founder with relevant regulatory payment experience at Everfund, endorsement from the most important person in the Next.js ecosystem, and regulatory tailwinds that are only accelerating (EU DMA, India DPDP, Canada CPPA). The open-source wedge into a market dominated by $10K+ ACV enterprise vendors could work the way Supabase worked against Firebase or the way Clerk worked against Auth0. If Inth becomes the default consent layer for every Next.js and React application, that's millions of sites generating usage-based revenue. The regulatory complexity itself — maintaining compliance across GDPR, CCPA, IAB TCF 2.3, UK GDPR, Quebec Law 25, and more — is a genuine barrier to casual replication. I checked my Known Exceptions: Slack overrode my consensus mispricing criterion because it was genuinely creating a new behavioral primitive (real-time workplace messaging replacing email). Inth does not meet that threshold — consent management is an existing behavior, not a new one. No exception applies.

Inth is a well-executed developer tool in a boring, well-funded, consensus-understood market. Burns and his team have built something developers genuinely prefer — the npm numbers don't lie. But my framework requires mispricing, primitive creation, and insanity in the idea. Inth has none of the three. The one condition that flips me: show me $500K+ ARR from enterprise customers paying 5-10x what CookieYes charges, proving that "developer-first" is a wedge into OneTrust's $10K+ budget line, not just a free open-source alternative. Revenue would prove the monetization model and suggest the market is underpricing the developer-buyer channel in compliance. Until then, Inth is a good product, not a good bet — the two are different things, and most investors never learn that distinction.
