# Lance -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The competitive landscape tells you everything you need to know. Canary Technologies has raised $180 million and serves 20,000 hotels across 100 countries. Duve has raised $85 million. HiJiffy automates 85% of guest interactions across 2,500 hotels. Asksuite runs an omnichannel platform with an estimated $17.5 million in annual revenue. Akia serves the Ritz-Carlton. And now Lance enters the arena, a pre-seed company with three Berkeley graduates and 50 hotels, proposing to solve a problem that half a dozen funded companies are already solving with substantially similar approaches. This is the textbook structure of mimetic competition -- multiple players imitating each other into the same market, each claiming marginal differentiation while converging on identical outcomes. Hotels need AI to handle phone calls. Everyone knows this. It is not a secret. It is a consensus dressed in slightly different clothes by each competitor. When I ask "what important truth do very few people agree with you on?" and the answer is "hotels should use AI for phone calls during a staffing shortage," I know I am looking at a market where no one has a genuine insight and everyone will compete on execution until margins approach zero.

The Computer Use Agent approach -- vision-based screen navigation of legacy PMS systems -- is the one element that arrests my attention. The thesis embedded in this architectural choice is more interesting than the company's surface-level positioning: the idea that legacy hotel software will never provide adequate APIs, so the correct strategy is to bypass the integration layer entirely by having AI agents see and interact with interfaces the way a human employee would. If true, this would be a meaningful insight about the structure of hospitality technology -- that the integration problem is permanently unsolvable through conventional means, and that visual navigation is not a hack but the correct long-term architecture. This would give Lance a deployment speed advantage over API-dependent competitors and reduce the leverage that PMS vendors hold over the ecosystem. But I see two problems. First, this is not Lance's unique technology -- Computer Use Agents are being commoditized by the foundation model companies themselves. Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google are all building these capabilities into their base models. Lance is building on a technology layer that will become infrastructure within 18 months, not proprietary capability. Second, the fragility is structural: every PMS UI update, every redesign, every version change breaks Lance's automation and requires maintenance that scales linearly with the number of PMS interfaces supported. An API, once built, is stable. A visual scraper requires perpetual re-calibration. The cleverness of the approach conceals its operational fragility.

The bull case deserves serious engagement. Gavin Brennen grew up around Marriott operations -- this is not a tourist entering hospitality, it is someone with genuine embodied knowledge of how hotels actually function. Caleb Chan was a founding engineer at a company with a nine-figure exit and built voice and AI agent technology at Google and Salesforce. Gatik Trivedi brings Stanford speech research, a prior exit, and agent infrastructure experience from Knowtex, a YC company. This is a capable, complementary team, and 50 hotels across Marriott, Hilton, and Hyatt brands at pre-seed represents real enterprise sales execution that most early-stage companies never achieve. The Lance Pad concept -- a tablet-based operations hub that replaces walkie-talkies -- hints at something potentially more defensible than voice handling alone: becoming the operational coordination layer for the entire hotel. If Lance can embed itself not just in phone answering but in staff dispatch, task fulfillment, and cross-departmental coordination, the switching costs compound meaningfully. A hotel that runs its entire internal operations through Lance is structurally different from a hotel that merely routes phone calls through it. If the team executes on that operational depth, the durability picture improves considerably.

But this bull case requires Lance to become something it is not yet describing itself as. The positioning is "your hotel's best employees" -- plural, suggesting breadth -- but the primary differentiation remains voice call handling, which is a feature that Canary Technologies could ship as a product update next quarter. The distance between "we handle your phone calls" and "we are the operational nervous system of your property" is enormous, and Canary has a 400-to-1 hotel count advantage and orders-of-magnitude more capital to traverse that distance first. More fundamentally, this is not a company built around a secret about the world. It is built around a real problem with a competent solution in a market that a dozen other companies have identified and funded. The founders would need to articulate why every other player in this space is wrong about something fundamental -- not just technically inferior -- for me to see a path to monopoly. I do not see that articulation here. The market is large, the problem is real, the team is capable, and none of that matters if the company cannot escape competition.

The power law demands that I invest only where the upside, if the company succeeds, is extreme. Even in the best case -- Lance becomes the dominant voice and operations platform for hotels -- the ceiling is a vertical SaaS company in hospitality serving a serviceable market that is materially smaller than the headline TAM. This is a good business if executed well, not a civilization-scale technology company. The technology is an application of commoditizing capabilities to a specific vertical, not a discontinuity that makes the prior world obsolete. I pass.
