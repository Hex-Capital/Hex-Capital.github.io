# Grove Tax -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that strikes me first about Grove Tax is how many people agree it's a good idea. Canopy has raised $292 million. TaxGPT just closed $4.6 million. Corvee pulled in $10 million. Taxnova -- doing something nearly identical -- is in the same a16z Speedrun batch. The accountant shortage statistics are real: 300,000 professionals gone since 2020, CPA exam candidates down 37%. Every trend report says AI will transform tax preparation. When I see that kind of consensus, my instinct is to run the other direction. The best startup ideas look like bad ideas to most people. "AI workflow automation for tax firms" looks like a good idea to *everyone*. That's the tell. The return premium has already been competed away before Grove Tax even ships its core product.

Uday Nandam's background is the strongest thing here. He was a PM at Intuit across ProConnect, Lacerte, and Intuit Link, and claims Head of Product at Thomson Reuters UltraTax. Those are literally the two dominant professional tax platforms. He's seen the workflow problem from inside the machine. That's worth something -- it's closer to organic discovery than someone who read a McKinsey report about CPA shortages. But there's a crucial distinction between observing a problem from the vendor's product management suite and feeling it in your hands while preparing returns at 2 AM during tax season. Drew Houston built Dropbox because he kept forgetting his USB drive. Did Uday Nandam build Grove Tax because he was personally drowning in document intake workflows, or because he identified a market gap while managing product roadmaps? The dossier's language -- "65% of time on non-tax tasks," "75% of CPAs retired" -- reads like the statistics of market research, not the frustration of lived experience.

The founder profiles concern me in a specific way. Uday is a product manager. Gaurav Mathur is described as an engineer at Indeed, Airbnb, and Cloud Kitchens -- large, established companies. No public GitHub repos were found for Gaurav. No prior startup exits for either. No stories of creative workarounds, scrappy early wins, or building revenue before fundraising. Uday did found CometBooks.net in college and built a gaming community at age 12, which shows early builder instincts, but his career arc has been managing products at companies that other people built. I've backed repeat founders across multiple companies -- Patrick Collison shipped Auctomatic as a teenager before Stripe. The Airbnb founders sold cereal boxes when no one would fund them. I need to see the animal quality, the person who does what they do so well they cross from professional into obsessive. Grove Tax's dossier gives me two competent operators with relevant resumes. That's not the same thing.

The product itself reveals an uncomfortable truth: the central capability -- automated return preparation -- is listed as "Coming Soon" on the pricing page. Grove Tax currently sells intake ($25/return) and delivery ($10/return). Those are real products that work, and the per-return pricing model is thoughtfully designed to remove per-seat friction. But intake and delivery are the easy parts. The hard part -- the part where you need tax-form-specific AI accuracy across thousands of form variants and state rules, where errors carry legal liability for preparers -- that's the part that doesn't exist yet. Grove Tax is selling the appetizer and dessert while the entrée is still in development. The incumbents, meanwhile, have the data and the distribution. Thomson Reuters has already announced AI-powered compliance solutions. Intuit has enormous ML resources. When the schlep is well-known rather than hidden, and the incumbents can see it too, the moat from schlep tolerance shrinks considerably. Compare this to Stripe: banks and payment processors knew online payments were a mess, but they couldn't solve it because the solution required a developer-first API design philosophy that was alien to their DNA. Grove Tax's workflow automation doesn't require that kind of paradigm shift from Intuit or Thomson Reuters -- it requires feature development.

The strongest bull case for Grove Tax: the workforce crisis is structural and accelerating, not cyclical. If Uday can ship the preparation module with sufficient accuracy, the land-and-expand motion from Collect/Deliver into full return automation is elegant. The per-return model genuinely conflicts with incumbents' per-seat economics, creating a window where Thomson Reuters and Intuit face business model paralysis. And Uday's insider knowledge of exactly how UltraTax and ProConnect are architected -- their technical debt, their integration points, their limitations -- could be a genuine information advantage that outsiders lack. For this to work, Grove Tax needs the preparation module to achieve accuracy that withstands the legal scrutiny of tax compliance, and they need to do it before Intuit ships something comparable as a feature update. That's a narrow window with high execution requirements for a two-person team.

My verdict comes down to two dimensions. First, the consensus problem: when $300 million in aggregate funding is already deployed against variations of "AI for tax workflows," you need an extraordinary founder signal to justify another bet in this space, and I don't see one. Second, the founder profile: competent operators from large companies, but no evidence of the relentlessly resourceful quality that separates startup founders from product managers who start companies. Uday's domain expertise is real, and in a less crowded field it might be enough. But Grove Tax is bringing a product management sensibility to a founder's fight, in a market where everyone already sees the opportunity.
