# ProjectX -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

ProjectX's core architecture -- per-app dedicated GPU allocation in the cloud -- reveals an inversion of my leverage hierarchy that the "cloud OS" branding obscures. Every application instance ProjectX runs consumes real GPU-hours rented from AWS, GCP, or Azure. That $204K Google Cloud grant and the ~$60K in Microsoft and AWS credits aren't revenue -- they're subsidized compute that masks the true marginal cost of serving each user. When those credits expire, ProjectX faces full hyperscaler pricing on every session. Notion scales to millions of users without proportional cost because a collaborative document is bytes, not GPU cycles. ProjectX scales like an infrastructure company: more users, proportionally more compute spend. This is capital leverage wearing a software company's clothing. The most dangerous startups to back are the ones where the pitch sounds like code leverage but the P&L looks like a services business.

Rounak Adhikary started ProjectX at age 20 in Kalyani, West Bengal, and has been building it for four years. That sustained effort is real -- he funded early development from his web agency Woldix's revenue, which is a bootstrapper's move I respect. But when I apply the specific knowledge test, I have to ask: what non-replicable expertise does Rounak bring to building a cloud operating system with GPU orchestration and multi-OS container isolation? His background is a BSc in Computer Science from LPU and a web development agency. Bishal Karmakar, the CTO, has Go, Rust, and Kubernetes skills -- competent systems engineering, but trainable. Neither founder spent years inside VMware architecting hypervisors, or at NVIDIA building GPU scheduling systems, or at Google building Borg. Compare this to Aravind Srinivas at Perplexity, whose Google Brain and OpenAI experience meant he understood transformer-based search at a level competitors couldn't hire for. ProjectX's founders are learning GPU orchestration on the job. That's admirable hustle, not specific knowledge. If Rounak disappeared tomorrow, a competent systems architect could continue building this product -- the core insight (per-app isolation with dedicated GPUs) is an architectural choice, not a non-replicable knowledge advantage.

Two of ProjectX's three original co-founders -- Ronit Banerjee and Suvrakamal Das, listed on Crunchbase from the 2022 founding -- are no longer on the YC page, replaced by Bishal Karmakar and Sourya Majumder. Pre-seed co-founder turnover is one of my clearest red flags. I evaluate co-founder relationships through the principal-agent lens: co-founders without deep pre-existing trust create internal agency problems before the company even faces external ones. The dossier identifies no shared prior employer or university overlap between the current founders -- they appear connected primarily by geography (Kalyani, West Bengal). This doesn't necessarily doom ProjectX, but it demands a higher bar of evidence on principal authenticity, and I don't see it. Rounak's four-year commitment is the strongest principal signal, and it's genuine. But "I've been working on this for years" is necessary, not sufficient. The question is whether he was compelled by the problem or by the opportunity to build a company. A web development agency founder pivoting to cloud infrastructure reads more like ambition than obsession.

The bull case for ProjectX is real and worth articulating honestly. The per-app isolation model with simultaneous Windows and Linux execution in one browser session is architecturally novel -- no incumbent (Microsoft Azure Virtual Desktop, Amazon WorkSpaces, Citrix) or funded competitor (Neverinstall at $1.21M, Vagon at $542K) offers this. The AI agent co-working angle is genuinely timely: as agents proliferate in 2025-2026, environments where human and AI workflows run side by side with independent compute become necessary. ProjectX won Tiger Launch at Princeton and the WTC Innovation Award at IIT Bombay against 17,000 startups, demonstrating competitive validation. And the incumbents face a real principal-agent problem: Microsoft's Windows 365 is architecturally committed to streaming full Windows desktops, and re-architecting for per-app Linux+Windows compute would cannibalize Azure Virtual Desktop's licensing model. A six-person team with no legacy revenue can move where Microsoft's agents cannot. If ProjectX's GPU orchestration layer achieves dramatically better utilization rates than industry standard -- turning the unit economics from liability to advantage -- the leverage equation transforms entirely.

But I've seen this pattern before. ProjectX's structural position reminds me of Wish -- not in product category, but in the absence of specific knowledge defending against incumbents with overwhelming resources. Wish competed with Amazon on price in e-commerce without a founder-specific-knowledge moat; ProjectX competes with Microsoft, Amazon, and Google on cloud desktop infrastructure without one. Wish's marketplace network effects were supposed to compensate; they didn't -- the stock collapsed 95% post-IPO. ProjectX's architectural novelty is supposed to compensate for the knowledge gap, but architectural choices are replicable given sufficient engineering investment. Google Cloud is simultaneously ProjectX's $204K benefactor and a potential competitor -- the same entity funding your infrastructure can build the feature that makes you obsolete. No amount of execution speed from a six-person team in Kalyani overcomes the structural fact that your three largest suppliers are your three most dangerous competitors. The condition that would flip me to Invest: demonstrated GPU utilization rates above 70% across heterogeneous workloads at meaningful scale, proving that ProjectX's orchestration layer has become a genuine technical moat rather than an architectural choice any well-funded team could replicate.

ProjectX democratizes access to high-performance computing -- a browser URL replacing a $3,000 GPU workstation -- and that pattern resonates with my portfolio's deepest thread from AngelList to CoinList to Polymarket. But the access expansion here is incremental over existing cloud desktops, not structural. Azure Virtual Desktop and Amazon WorkSpaces already put computing in the cloud; ProjectX adds per-app isolation and multi-OS, which is an improvement, not a category creation. The decisive dimensions are specific knowledge (absent) and leverage architecture (capital-intensive, not permissionless). A cloud OS that costs more to serve each additional user, built by founders without non-replicable domain expertise, in a market where the three largest cloud providers are both suppliers and potential competitors -- this is a bet on execution speed against entities with 10,000x the resources, without the specific knowledge asymmetry that makes such bets rational. I pass.
