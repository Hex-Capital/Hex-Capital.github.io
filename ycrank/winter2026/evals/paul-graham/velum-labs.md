# Velum Labs -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Three different product framings before a single paying customer. That's the signal I can't get past. Velum started as a homomorphic-encryption firewall for content-level access control. Then it became "the operating system for data quality." Now the website says "the ontology engine for enterprise AI." The GitHub repos are still full of forks from the encryption era. The flagship Firewall repo has one star. This isn't the kind of pivot where founders discover something unexpected about their users — like Justin.tv discovering that game streaming was where the real engagement was. This is a team shopping for a problem before they've shipped anything. When a company changes its identity three times pre-launch, the most likely explanation is that none of the problems were discovered organically. They were selected.

How did these founders find this problem? I see no evidence they lived it. Benjamin comes from physics and quantum computing at Stanford. Alen comes from ML research and a role at Dataroot, an ML platform company. Neither appears to have been a data engineer drowning in broken schemas and manual contracts at an enterprise with a federated data stack. That's who feels this pain — the person who gets paged at 2am because a schema changed upstream and broke a downstream dashboard. The schlep in enterprise data quality is real and substantial: regulatory compliance, integrating with 50 legacy systems, dealing with the organizational politics of data ownership across teams. It's exactly the kind of ugly, tedious work that causes schlep blindness. But the schlep has to find you. You can't read about schlep blindness and then go looking for schleps to tackle — that inverts the whole logic.

The strongest bull case is Alen's technical ability. Eighty GitHub repos, CalHacks overall winner, principal software engineer at an ML platform, research in topological neural networks and privacy-preserving inference. His pinned "ontology" repo in TypeScript maps directly to the product's core. This is a genuine hacker-builder, not someone who plans to hire engineers after raising money. If Alen encountered data quality chaos firsthand while building ML applications at Dataroot — if he watched models fail because the training data was garbage and nobody could trace where the corruption entered — then the organic connection might exist and the dossier simply doesn't surface it. The ontology-first approach is architecturally distinct from what Monte Carlo or Atlan does: deriving contracts automatically from observed data traffic instead of requiring engineers to write rules. If that works reliably, it's a real product. And the timing argument — LLMs making zero-shot NER and relationship extraction feasible at enterprise scale — is legitimate. For this to be a great investment, you'd need to believe the pivot sequence represents rapid learning by technically exceptional founders who are converging on the right problem, and that Alen's ML infrastructure background gave him genuine domain insight that hasn't been articulated publicly. I can construct that story, but the evidence doesn't tell it for me.

What concerns me most beyond the idea origin is the claim of 50+ pre-built enterprise extractors from a two-person team that hasn't launched. Building reliable integrations for SAP, Oracle, Salesforce, and ServiceNow is not weekend work — each of those systems has its own authentication model, API quirks, rate limits, and data schemas. Either these extractors are thin wrappers that won't survive contact with production enterprise data, or this claim is aspirational. Combined with the SOC 2 Type II certification claim — which typically requires months of documented compliance processes — there's a pattern of presenting enterprise-ready packaging before the core product has been validated by a single customer. Enterprise buyers will see through packaging fast.

The market is real but crowded and well-capitalized. Monte Carlo at $1.6B valuation, Atlan at $750M, Great Expectations with $65M, Bigeye with $73M. And the existential risk isn't these startups — it's Snowflake and Databricks building ontology features natively into their platforms. When you're selling a standalone data quality tool to enterprises that already pay millions to their data platform vendor, you're fighting gravity. The winner in this space will likely be whoever gets deep enough into enterprise workflows to create switching costs before the platforms absorb the category. A two-person team recruiting design partners hasn't started that clock yet.

I keep coming back to the origin question. The best data infrastructure companies were built by people who suffered through the problem themselves. Drew Houston forgot his USB drive and built Dropbox. The Collison brothers wrote code to accept payments and were so frustrated they built Stripe. I need to see that connection here — the moment these founders couldn't stand the status quo any longer — and I don't.
