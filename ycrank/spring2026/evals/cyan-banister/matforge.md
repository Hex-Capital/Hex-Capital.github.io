# matforge -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what caught me: a pre-seed founder whose PhD research is already on Intel and TSMC interconnect roadmaps. Not in a pitch deck. Not in a "we're in conversations with" slide. On the actual roadmaps. Akash Ramdas screened over 15,000 materials during his Stanford PhD and discovered novel compound metals that outperform ruthenium for semiconductor interconnects — and the two largest logic foundries in the world said yes, we'll build with these. That's an extraordinarily rare credential at any stage. At pre-seed, with $500K and two people? It's the kind of signal that makes me lean forward, because it means the product has already escaped the lab in some real sense before the company even formally exists.

But when I ask the question I always ask — tell me their story, not the business — what I get back is a credentialed path: IIT Madras metallurgical engineering, Stanford PhD, postdoc. That's impressive, deeply impressive. And I can see that Akash chose this domain early — metallurgical engineering is not what you study if you're optimizing for career optionality. He went narrow and deep before it was fashionable, before the AI-for-materials wave made it venture-backable. That counts for something. But it's not the biographical forge I feel most deeply about. It's not "this problem found me because I lived inside the broken system." It's a brilliant scientist who spent years mastering a domain and realized he could build a company around what he'd already proven. His co-founder Advaith Sridhar brings something complementary and real — he built autonomous AI agents at Persona AI before it was acquired by Luma Labs, and his AI4Bharat sign language dataset work reveals genuine impact orientation. Together they cover the two halves of this problem precisely. Clear CEO/CTO delineation, seven-year relationship from their IIT Madras days, co-authored a book together. No governance red flag here.

What I notice that others might miss: semiconductor materials discovery is one of the most opaque, gatekept systems in all of technology. A handful of chemical suppliers — Entegris, Shin-Etsu, BASF Electronic Materials — sell materials to a handful of foundries, and the discovery process is locked behind decade-long R&D cycles, PhD-level expertise, and corporate secrecy. And here's the structural dynamic that activates my pattern recognition: the incumbents profit from selling existing materials at scale. They have no incentive to discover their own replacements. That's the hidden tax — the entire semiconductor industry is paying a materials innovation penalty because the entities best positioned to discover new materials are economically motivated not to. Matforge is making that invisible bottleneck visible and compressible. This connects directly to my Substrate conviction — semiconductor infrastructure at an inflection point driven by the CHIPS Act, the AI compute buildout, and the physics wall at sub-3nm nodes. When I run the ten-year question, I can see it: if Matforge builds a compounding dataset of validated material candidates with embedded fab relationships, they're not a consulting company — they're the discovery infrastructure layer for the entire semiconductor materials stack.

The bear case is real and I have to name it. Periodic Labs raised $300 million. Lila Sciences has $550 million. CuspAI has $154 million and already lists semiconductors as a focus area with Samsung Ventures backing. Matforge has two people and YC money. The argument for Matforge is that semiconductor materials discovery requires domain depth that capital alone can't buy — and Akash has it while the mega-funded platforms are spread across multiple verticals. The argument against is that those platforms can hire semiconductor PhDs and throw resources at the vertical whenever they decide to. MIT Technology Review noted there's been no breakthrough moment in materials discovery and the real bottleneck is bridging AI prediction to physical synthesis. Matforge's AI-first approach has to cross that gap, likely needing lab partnerships or wet-lab capabilities that aren't yet evident. And the customer base is terrifyingly concentrated — fewer than ten major foundries globally. One delayed contract could be existential.

If this were going to be a great investment, here's what would have to be true: Akash's existing Intel and TSMC relationships convert from academic roadmap citations into paying contracts. The domain specificity that makes Matforge small also makes it the trusted partner for semiconductor R&D organizations that would never share proprietary process data with a broad-platform competitor. The AI agents actually compress discovery timelines enough that fabs see Matforge as critical infrastructure rather than optional tooling. And the prediction-to-synthesis gap turns out to be crossable with the right domain expertise orchestrating the AI — which is exactly what this team is built for. If all of that holds, this becomes the materials intelligence layer for the semiconductor industry, and the concentrated customer base flips from a risk to a moat because each fab relationship deepens the data advantage. I can see the path. I'm just not yet certain the founders are biographically incapable of doing anything else — and that's the conviction threshold for my biggest bets.

This lives in the space where I'd write a check and watch closely. The domain expertise is genuine and rare. The inflection is real. The opaque system being disrupted maps to my deepest investment pattern. But the biographical forge is academic rather than existential, and the competitive landscape demands that this team execute with a precision that two people and pre-seed capital make difficult against nine-figure-funded rivals. I'm interested, not compelled — which at pre-seed, with this specific founder credential, means I'm in, but sized modestly and watching for the synthesis bridge to materialize.
