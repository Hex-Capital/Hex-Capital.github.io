# ProjectX -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The founding team restructuring is the first thing I'd want to understand. The July 2024 press lists four co-founders: Adhikari, Das, Banerjee, and Kuncham. The YC Spring 2026 page lists three different people: Adhikary, Karmakar, and Majumder. That's three of four co-founders replaced in under two years, at pre-seed, before the company has meaningful revenue. I've seen co-founder changes work out — Parker Conrad rebuilt from scratch after Zenefits — but that was one founder with a decade of operating context starting over on his own terms. Here, the entire founding team rotated except the CEO, who was 22 at the time. At minimum, this signals alignment problems during the easiest phase of a company's life. If you can't hold a founding team together when all you have is upside and vision, the organizational challenges of scaling become exponentially harder.

Now to market structure timing, which is where I always anchor. The question is: what structural shift is making browser-native GPU streaming viable NOW that wasn't viable three years ago? The dossier infers three catalysts — GPU cost curve declines, WebGPU/WebCodecs maturity, and AI-driven demand for on-demand compute. These are all real, but they are aggressively consensus. There are 95 tracked companies in cloud GPU platforms with $20.8B raised collectively. Google acquired Cameyo for app virtualization in May 2024. Shadow, CoreWeave, Paperspace — this is one of the most crowded infrastructure categories in tech right now. When I backed Coinbase, nobody was building crypto infrastructure. When I wrote the Perplexity seed check, AI search was a punchline. The ProjectX market is the opposite of nonobvious — it's where every ambitious infrastructure founder in the world is currently competing. The India education angle — 75% of students lacking adequate devices — adds geographic specificity, but solving compute access for Indian students via cloud streaming is a well-understood problem that multiple incumbents address.

The infrastructure-layer test produces a more interesting result. The "cloud OS" framing suggests genuine platform ambitions: per-app GPU compute streamed in browser tabs, with sub-3-second cold starts and no VM overhead. If this works as described, it sits between raw GPU cloud (AWS, CoreWeave) and full desktop virtualization (Shadow, Citrix), creating a new abstraction layer. That's the kind of positioning I look for — Stripe built a payments API layer between banks and internet businesses; Infinity could build a compute access layer between GPU providers and end users. The problem is dependency: this layer sits entirely on top of hyperscaler GPU infrastructure, which means AWS or GCP can replicate the abstraction while owning the underlying compute. Stripe survived because banks couldn't build developer-friendly APIs. Can ProjectX survive when the GPU providers themselves are actively investing in streaming and virtualization? Google buying Cameyo suggests they're already moving up the stack.

The single-miracle test is where the investment case breaks down for me. ProjectX needs the streaming technology to work at scale (sub-3-second cold starts for GPU-heavy apps like Unreal Engine in a browser tab is a genuine systems engineering challenge). They also need to procure GPU infrastructure at competitive prices — and they have $204K in total funding against Shadow's $105-169M and CoreWeave's $5B+. And they need enterprise distribution in a market where procurement cycles are long and switching costs for existing VDI solutions are high. That's three independent breakthroughs. Compounding those probabilities produces odds I can't underwrite.

The bull case I'd need to believe: India-first is a wedge strategy, not a limitation. The $2.4M in signed LOIs from Indian institutions is a genuinely strong signal for a pre-seed company — most YC companies at this stage have zero signed revenue commitments. If the IIT Bombay pilot converts and the per-app model proves dramatically cheaper than full desktop VDI, ProjectX could own Indian institutional compute access before hyperscalers prioritize that market. Indian education procurement moves differently than US enterprise — relationships and government programs (the Ministry of Electronics & IT grants suggest political connections) matter more than brand. In this scenario, India becomes what Southeast Asia was for Grab: a market the global incumbents underserve, where a local team builds enough scale to become defensible before they notice. For this to work, the founders would need to convert LOIs to recurring revenue, demonstrate retention, and prove the technology works at institutional scale — all within an 18-month window before GPU streaming becomes a commodity feature of every cloud provider. I'd want to see those LOIs converting to paid contracts and the streaming latency data at scale before getting involved.

The founder profile fits a pattern I recognize: young, resourceful, accelerator-credentialed builders who've won pitch competitions and secured government grants. Rounak Adhikary's trajectory — founding a consulting company at 19, EIR at the Ministry of Electronics & IT at 21, Stanford ASES, now YC at 23 — shows hustle and pattern recognition. The CTO's Golang/Rust/C++ stack is exactly right for GPU streaming infrastructure. But I've seen no evidence of operating at a scaling company, and the co-founder turnover suggests the team hasn't yet found its stable configuration. The COO's $120K MRR consulting experience is relevant operational context but not the kind of hypergrowth scaling that prepares you for what comes after product-market fit.
