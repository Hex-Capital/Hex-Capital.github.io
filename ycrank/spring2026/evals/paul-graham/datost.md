# Datost -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first question I always ask is: how did the founders find this problem? And with Datost, I can't find the answer. Both Maceo and Jason were software engineers at Traba, a labor marketplace. Jason spent time on Confluent's data governance team. Somewhere along the way, they decided to build an AI data analyst that lives in Slack. But there's no visible moment of organic discovery -- no story about Jason drowning in ad-hoc SQL requests from the ops team at Traba, no account of Maceo watching a finance colleague wait three days for a query that took five minutes to write. The connection between their experience and this specific product is the kind of connection you can construct after the fact, not the kind that keeps you up at night before you've even decided to start a company. This feels like an idea that was assembled from components -- "AI is powerful now" plus "Slack is where people work" plus "non-technical people can't write SQL" -- rather than one that grew from a wound.

The schlep test makes it worse, not better. When I talk about schlep blindness, I mean problems that are obvious but ugly enough that your unconscious mind steers you away from them. Payment processing before Stripe. Trust between strangers before Airbnb. "AI analyst in Slack" is not that kind of problem. It's the opposite -- it's the kind of idea that sounds exciting in a brainstorming session precisely because there's no obvious schlep to scare you off. The API integrations are well-documented, the LLM capabilities are off the shelf, and the Slack developer ecosystem is mature. The real schlep in enterprise analytics -- navigating undocumented schemas, earning SOC 2 compliance, handling the political dynamics of who gets access to what data -- exists, but it's not unique to Datost. Every competitor faces identical challenges, and several of them have years of head start and millions more in capital: Scoop Analytics occupies nearly the same positioning with $3.5M already raised, Julius AI has 2M+ users and $11M, Chata.ai has $6.3M in actual revenue. This is a crowded category where the idea is consensus. When I noted in 2025 that the most impressive YC companies were not working on AI, this is the pattern I was worried about.

The strongest argument for Datost is the team composition itself. Both founders are genuine engineers -- not "technical co-founders" who learned to code at a bootcamp, but people who've shipped production code at real companies. Jason's work on Red Hat's Eclipse IDE and JDT Language Server, plus his Confluent data governance tenure, suggests he understands data infrastructure at a level deeper than API wrappers. Maceo, at 21, has already worked at three engineering roles and co-founded a non-profit, which at minimum shows initiative. They worked together at Traba, so the co-founder relationship is real -- not the bolted-on partnership I've seen destroy companies. If the question were purely "can these two build the product?" the answer is almost certainly yes. The GitHub org shows a demo repo in TypeScript updated as recently as March 2026. They're building.

But building capability is necessary, not sufficient. What would have to be true for this to work? Datost would need to find that the Slack-native form factor creates such a dramatically better user experience that enterprises choose it over established tools despite the security concerns of granting a pre-seed startup's bot access to production databases. They'd need the "broader connectivity" -- codebases, docs, data lakes -- to translate into answers that single-source competitors can't match. And they'd need to move fast enough that Scoop Analytics doesn't simply add the same integrations. It's possible. The "AI analyst with its own computer" framing suggests ambition beyond simple text-to-SQL. But I see no evidence yet that any of this is happening -- no users, no revenue, no waitlist, no organic sharing signal, no Product Hunt launch, zero GitHub stars. At pre-seed I don't demand traction, but I do look for any signal that someone out there is pulling for this product, and I can't find one.

The fundamental issue is that this company needs to outexecute well-funded competitors in a category where the core technology -- LLM-powered natural language to SQL -- is rapidly commoditizing. That's a race where the prize goes to whoever builds the deepest enterprise relationships and the most reliable query engine across messy real-world schemas. Two young engineers can potentially win that race, but not because they're in Slack and the others aren't. Slack is a distribution channel, not a moat. And Salesforce, which owns Slack, has every incentive to build this themselves as an extension of Einstein AI. When your entire value proposition depends on a platform owned by a company that sells analytics software, you're one product announcement away from irrelevance.
