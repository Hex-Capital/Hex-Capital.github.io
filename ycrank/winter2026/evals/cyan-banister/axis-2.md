# Axis -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Axis is that it's an AI tool for institutional commodity trading desks — which means it's a product built to serve some of the wealthiest, most sophisticated private companies on the planet. Vitol, Trafigura, Glencore — these firms print billions in profit. When I look at my best investments, there's always a thread of economic access running through them. Uber created livelihoods for drivers. Postmates put money in delivery workers' pockets. Affirm gave people honest credit. Axis creates marginal analytical efficiency for institutions that already have enormous resources. That doesn't disqualify a company from being a good business, but it tells me immediately that this doesn't sit in the part of the world where I do my best work.

I don't have a founder story here, and that's my biggest problem. Ian Wang is described as "interested in trading" from Yale, Class of 2025. Eric Zhu studied math at UChicago and was a quantitative trader — possibly an intern at TransMarket Group. These are smart, credentialed people. But when I ask my opening question — tell me your story, not the business — I get silence. There's no hardship that forged a commitment to commodity markets. No biographical thread that makes these two the inevitable founders of this specific company. Maybe that story exists and it just isn't public yet, but the signal I rely on most heavily to make investment decisions is simply absent. I backed Contraline because the founder had spent years in academic research that no one else cared about. I recognized Travis Kalanick's intensity because it came from somewhere deep and personal. Here, I see two recent graduates from top universities building in a category adjacent to their studies. That's competent, but it's not the kind of biographical inevitability I need to feel conviction.

"AI for finance" is about as consensus as investing gets right now. Every fund in the Valley is looking for AI applications in financial services. The competitive landscape confirms this — Kpler has $200 million in backing, Vortexa has Morgan Stanley behind it at a $500 million enterprise value, Enverus was acquired by Blackstone, and CommodityAI came through YC just two batches ago. When I look at the companies that have defined my portfolio, they were all deeply unpopular at the time I wrote the check. SpaceX was considered a vanity project. Crusoe Energy was mining Bitcoin on gas flares in North Dakota — people thought it was a joke. The whole reason I was able to get into those deals is that nobody else wanted them. Axis is building in a space where well-capitalized incumbents are already circling, and where the general thesis — LLMs can parse unstructured information for traders — is so broadly accepted that it's practically a category on every accelerator's application form. There's nothing magically weird here.

The strongest bull case I can construct: physical commodity trading genuinely drowns in unstructured information — broker commentary, geopolitical analysis, refinery data, weather patterns — and current tools are decades behind. If Axis nails the product, per-seat pricing at institutional trading desks could be extraordinary. The concentrated buyer base (maybe 50-100 major trading houses globally) means you don't need viral growth, you need 15 enterprise contracts. And Zhu's quantitative trading background, while thin, is at least directionally relevant. The "why now" is also real — LLMs crossed a capability threshold for parsing the kind of messy, domain-specific text that commodity markets generate. If these founders have genuine relationships at major trading houses and can land two or three lighthouse customers during YC, the business could work. But even this bull case describes a solid enterprise SaaS company, not the kind of category-defining, world-reshaping bet that gets me out of bed. Commodity trading desk analytics doesn't create new economic opportunity for people who currently lack it — it optimizes existing wealth extraction.

I also note the complete absence of information about the co-founders' relationship. Different universities, no shared employer, no public history together. After HQ Trivia, I never invest without understanding how co-founders fight and make up. Two recent graduates entering the brutally long enterprise sales cycles of institutional commodity trading — where a single deal can take 12-18 months — will face enormous pressure. I have no evidence about whether this team can sustain that pressure together.
