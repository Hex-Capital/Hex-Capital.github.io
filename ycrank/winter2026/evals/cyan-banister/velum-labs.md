# Velum Labs -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that jumps out to me here isn't the technology or the market -- it's the pivots. Three distinct product identities before a single customer: a homomorphic encryption firewall, an operating system for data quality, and now an ontology engine for enterprise AI. Their GitHub is full of forks from the encryption era, their YC page title still references the firewall, and the website says something else entirely. I've watched founders iterate, and I've watched founders search. Iteration is narrowing in on something you deeply understand. Searching is trying on identities until one sticks. When I backed Crusoe, they had one weird idea -- turn waste gas into compute -- and they held that thread through a near-death experience. Here, I see technically gifted people casting about for the right framing, which tells me the biographical thread connecting these founders to this specific problem either doesn't exist or hasn't been articulated yet.

And that's really where this falls apart for me. I always start with the founder's story -- not the pitch, not the architecture. Tell me how you got here. Benjamin is an undergraduate at Stanford in physics and math with quantum computing interests. Alen is a Minerva CS student who won CalHacks and did ML work at a couple of companies. Both clearly brilliant. The research affiliations with Max Planck and Harvard are impressive on paper. But I can't find the biographical thread. Why data quality? Why ontologies? Why enterprise data contracts specifically? There's no lived experience connecting these founders to this problem. Nobody suffered through broken data pipelines at a hospital and said "never again." Nobody watched their family's business fail because of data integrity issues. This reads like talented researchers who identified a technically interesting problem, not founders whose identity is inseparable from the solution. That distinction matters enormously to me at the pre-seed stage, because when the company nearly dies -- and it will -- the founders who persist are the ones solving their own problem.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. The ontology-first approach to data quality is genuinely architecturally distinct from what Monte Carlo or Atlan do. If LLM-powered entity extraction and automatic contract derivation work reliably at enterprise scale, this could become the semantic layer that every data stack needs. The timing argument is real -- this wasn't technically feasible before foundation models reached their current capability. Alen's GitHub shows serious depth: topological neural networks, privacy-preserving inference, an actual ontology implementation pinned to his profile. The 50+ pre-built enterprise extractors, if real, represent meaningful integration work. And SOC 2 Type II certification at this stage signals enterprise seriousness that most two-person pre-seed teams don't bother with. If these founders find their conviction and stop pivoting, there's a version of this that becomes important infrastructure. But that's a lot of "ifs" for my money.

What I don't see is anything that activates my core thesis. Enterprise data quality is not an unpopular category -- it's attracted over $600 million in venture funding across Monte Carlo, Atlan, Great Expectations, Bigeye, and Soda. This is consensus investing territory. The product doesn't create economic access for underserved populations; it serves data engineering teams at banks and hospitals. It doesn't make an invisible system visible to people who need transparency -- it makes data lineage visible to people who already have six-figure salaries and Snowflake accounts. The co-founders may share a familial connection given their Chilean-origin surnames, which I'd normally find encouraging -- I've seen family-run businesses resolve conflict in ways that 50/50 partnerships can't. But the multiple pivots could equally signal that they haven't aligned on what this company actually is. I'd want to sit with them and understand how they decided to abandon the encryption firewall direction and what that conversation looked like.

These are clearly smart, technically capable people, and I wish them well. But smart isn't enough for me to write a check from my own money. I need the biographical thread, the anti-consensus weirdness, and the conviction that comes from solving your own problem. This feels like a well-executed technical exploration in a crowded market, not the kind of magically weird, founder-destiny bet that defines my portfolio.
