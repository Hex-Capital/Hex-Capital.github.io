# Harbor -- Paul Graham Primary Source Research: Structured Quote Record Evaluation

I wrote once that "your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps." I used Stripe as my example — online payments had been broken for a decade, and nobody touched it because the regulatory and financial infrastructure work was so intimidating. EDC systems for clinical trials are the same breed of invisible problem. The software hasn't fundamentally changed since the 1990s. Site coordinators spend hours per subject per day on manual data entry. The market is worth billions. And yet the incumbents — Medidata, Veeva, Oracle — have coasted on architectures built when Bill Clinton was president. That's not because the problem is unsolvable. It's because building the replacement requires 21 CFR Part 11 validation, HIPAA compliance, GDPR compliance, immutable audit trails, and the stomach to sell into a domain where a single data extraction error in a pivotal trial could sink your company. Most founders' unconscious minds won't let them see this opportunity. Albert Cai saw it because he was standing inside it.

What I care about most is founders, and Albert's path is the kind I find most convincing. Four years at Biolinq doing clinical trial operations, taking a wearable biosensor from prototype through first-in-human studies to FDA De Novo authorization — that's not market research, that's living the problem. He watched coordinators struggle with legacy EDC systems during actual FDA-track studies, and he declined medical school to fix it. "The component of entrepreneurship that really matters is domain expertise." Zuckerberg didn't succeed because he understood startups; he succeeded because he understood what college students wanted. Albert understands what clinical trial coordinators need because he sat next to them. Nathan brings the complementary technical depth — employee number one at Jupiter (YC S19), production engineering at Google and Ramp, 74 public repos. They share a University of Michigan connection, which usually means a real relationship, not a co-founder matching service pairing. The fact that two people have shipped a compliant EDC platform with 5 live clinical studies and 6 named customers tells me something about their resourcefulness. "Startups take off because the founders make them take off."

The traction signals here are genuinely striking for a two-person pre-seed company. A 1,600-subject randomized trial configured in one week versus the legacy vendor's typical timeline. A customer CEO saying Harbor cut a two-month CRF build to two days. Another CEO reporting nine days versus the 10-12 weeks quoted elsewhere. These aren't incremental improvements — they're order-of-magnitude compressions. When I see 10x speed improvements with real customers on regulated products, I pay attention. "When a startup grows fast, it's usually because the product hits a nerve." Harbor seems to have hit a nerve. They have named customers — Biolinq, BioDynamik, NOVA Genomics, University Hospitals — not just waitlist signups. And the flat monthly pricing versus the incumbents' per-subject, per-site, per-CRF pricing creates exactly the kind of business model disruption where the incumbents can't easily respond without cannibalizing their own revenue. Medidata was acquired for $5.8 billion precisely because of that complex usage-based pricing. They're not going to flatten it voluntarily.

The strongest argument against is the incumbent AI response. Both Medidata and Veeva have announced AI features rolling out in 2025-2026. They have thousands of existing customers and can bundle AI at marginal cost. But this is the classic innovator's dilemma, and I've seen it play out before. Adding AI to a 25-year-old architecture is fundamentally different from building AI-native from the ground up. The incumbents' AI additions will be constrained by their existing data models, their existing pricing structures, and the organizational incentive to protect high-margin professional services revenue. "It's exceptionally rare for startups to be killed by competitors." The real risk isn't that Medidata copies the feature — it's whether Harbor can accumulate enough regulatory validation artifacts and customer trust before the market catches up. That's a race, and races favor the determined. The commoditization risk from general-purpose LLMs is more theoretical than practical in this domain — anyone can build a document extractor, but getting it validated for 21 CFR Part 11 audit trails in clinical submissions is a different undertaking entirely.

I do have concerns. Two people maintaining a compliant regulated product while simultaneously selling and supporting 5 live studies is stretched thin. The brand confusion with Harbor Clinical is the kind of annoying practical problem that wastes founder energy. And no public third-party audit of the AI-extracted data quality exists yet — in this domain, one bad regulatory finding could be devastating. But these are problems of execution and growth, not problems of fundamental direction. The job postings for an SDR and forward-deployed engineer suggest they know what they need. The free academic tier as a conversion funnel is clever distribution thinking.

This maps onto several of my frameworks simultaneously: schlep blindness (painful regulated domain that repels most founders), doing things that don't scale (6 customers with 2 people means they're doing the work themselves), frighteningly ambitious (taking on a $5.8B incumbent ecosystem), and the kind of idea that sounds bad initially ("two guys are going to replace Medidata?"). The Stripe analogy is apt — Stripe attacked a broken, intimidating infrastructure problem with better technology and simpler pricing, and the incumbents couldn't respond without destroying their own business models. Harbor is running the same play in clinical trials. I'd want to sit across from Albert and Nathan and see if they pass the animal test — "someone who does what they do so well that they pass right through professional and cross over into obsessive." The dossier evidence suggests they would.
