# 83 Sciences -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

83 Sciences is the rare pre-seed dossier where the contrarian secret is stated cleanly — 90% of experimental data never gets published, and Uncountable and Citrine are structurally the wrong shape to capture it because they sell tools rather than embed in labs — but where no multi-year definite plan appears anywhere in the artifact. That high-secret / low-plan pairing is the signature. It tells me Eric Riesel and Ian Naccarella have identified a real blind spot but have not yet committed to the ten-year architectural bet that would let 83 Sciences hold that blind spot against a $2.6B-valued CuspAI that can copy the on-site capture playbook the moment it becomes fashionable.

Stripe (2010) is the load-bearing analogy for 83 Sciences on the contrarian-secret axis. Stripe named a specific incumbent failure mode — PayPal's merchant onboarding was hostile to developers, and no one at PayPal could ship the seven-lines-of-code integration because the org was structured around consumer trust arbitrage, not developer experience. 83 Sciences names its failure mode with comparable specificity: Uncountable and Citrine ingest whatever customers upload, which by construction excludes the negative results labs are structurally embarrassed to share, and the fix requires in-person deployment that a cloud SaaS org will not authorize headcount for. Where 83 Sciences diverges from the Stripe rung is that Patrick Collison could articulate the developer-payments secret in one sentence and defend it against every incumbent; the 83 Sciences dossier still leaves the moat pinned on "[Inferred]: data network effects" with no dataset scale disclosed, which is the difference between a secret and a hypothesis about a secret.

Quora (2010) is the failure pattern I have to test against on the definite-plan axis, because Quora consumed Adam D'Angelo's Facebook-CTO pedigree on a roadmap that was constitutively indefinite — "let the community tell us what knowledge is." Does 83 Sciences repeat this? The dossier gives me no named 24-month milestones, no architectural commitment Riesel refuses to pivot from, no stated sequencing of which vertical (batteries, semis, pharma) gets captured first. The industries-served list on 83sciences.ai reads as an option chain, not a plan. Quora at least had a network-effect thesis it could name; 83 Sciences has "novel collaboration operating models with our academic and industry partners," which is the language of indefinite optimism dressed in materials-science vocabulary. This is where 83 Sciences most clearly fails my Zero to One filter.

CuspAI at a reported $2.6B and Citrine at $81.3M raised are the monopoly-trajectory problem, and the mechanism is compression rather than direct competition. 83 Sciences's pricing-power hypothesis over 18 months depends on the on-site-capture channel remaining unfashionable long enough for the negative-results corpus to compound past a defensibility threshold. But the moment CuspAI's $400M round closes, hiring twenty PhDs to embed in labs is a line item, not a strategy shift. 83 Sciences has no regulated-customer switching cost equivalent to Palantir's clearance-and-integration depth to slow that compression — the R&D-lab customer can defect on a one-year renewal. The moat compounds if and only if IP-sensitive data-sharing agreements are the actual chokepoint, which is unverified.

For 83 Sciences to flip a tier on founder lineage, Riesel would need to convert his JACS 2024 Crystalyze lead-authorship (67% accuracy on RRUFF experimental patterns per MIT News) into a second shipped artifact — a named national-lab partnership, a benchmark result, or a working Lab Brain deployment at a Sila-tier customer that Naccarella's operator network could unlock. The credentialed-outsider signal is present in the Freedman-group MIT chemistry pedigree and the JACS byline; what is absent is the Palmer Luckey shipped-Oculus equivalent. Yang running BCG's internal AI program to 30,000 users is real distribution muscle, but BCG-internal is not the same as shipping consumer or enterprise product to external customers, so 83 Sciences lands closer to credentialed-technical than to shipped-operator.

83 Sciences on the atoms-and-regulation axis parallels Palantir (2003) only in aspiration. Palantir's moat was the classified customer and the integration depth that took eight years to build; 83 Sciences's customer set — chemicals, batteries, semiconductors, pharma CDMOs — is atoms-adjacent through the labs it serves, but the product 83 Sciences ships is software layered on top of proprietary data. There is no capex barrier, no regulatory certification depth, no clearance requirement. Materials informatics at $251M today growing to a low-single-digit-billion market by 2035 (Precedence Research) is not the Palantir-scale regulated-customer vacuum; it is a specialty enterprise SaaS market with well-capitalized incumbents.

My 36-month forecast for 83 Sciences: acquihire by Citrine, Schrödinger, or a chemicals major (BASF, Merck KGaA) at sub-$75M, with the Riesel-group ML-for-crystallography IP as the actual asset transferred, or a pivot to a single vertical — most likely battery cathode formulation — as CuspAI's capital gravity forces specialization. Revenue at exit under $2M ARR. The mechanism is compression: the secret is real but not defensible at three-person scale against a $2.6B-valued rival with the same insight one product-marketing cycle away.

The strongest bull case against my forecast is that 83 Sciences is Palantir-shaped in disguise — that IP-sensitivity around unpublished experimental data is the actual moat, not the marketing, and that once a labor-intensive trust relationship with a top-tier academic lab or industrial R&D group is signed, that relationship compounds for a decade because negative-results corpora are non-transferable and non-replicable at any capital level. Cuspair's $400M does not buy the trust of an MIT PI who already knows Riesel from the Freedman group. This case stings because it names the one thing CuspAI's capital genuinely cannot short-circuit. But the dossier gives me zero signed partners, zero LOIs, zero named counterparties — the trust-moat thesis is asserted, not evidenced, and Naccarella's Sila-Nano network and Riesel's MIT network are conversion targets, not conversions. The single verifiable condition that flips my verdict is a signed multi-year data-sharing agreement with a top-five chemicals company or a DOE national lab, disclosed within twelve months, at which point 83 Sciences has a Palantir-shaped moat and I re-underwrite.
