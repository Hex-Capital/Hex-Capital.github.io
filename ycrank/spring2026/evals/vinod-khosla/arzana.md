# Arzana -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

William Alexander taught himself discus technique from YouTube and reached a state ranking. He taught himself Mandarin in a semester in Hong Kong. He's 21 years old, from Okoboji, Iowa, selling AI automation to the manufacturing executives he grew up around. Marshall Kools is a D1 wrestler who earned $10,000 in personal salary last year building Arzana. These are exactly the founder signals I respond to — naive outsiders with high learning rates who don't know what can't be done. The Midwest roots are real, the manufacturing exposure is firsthand, and the pain they're solving they watched with their own eyes in Iowa and Wisconsin. If I were evaluating founders in isolation, Arzana's team would score well. But I don't invest in founders in isolation — I invest in consequences.

Arzana's consequence magnitude is where the thesis breaks down. If Arzana fully succeeds — every U.S. manufacturer and distributor uses its AI agents for quoting, order entry, estimating, and customer updates — what changes? Quotes go out in one click instead of fifteen minutes. Purchase orders auto-populate into ERPs. The front office runs leaner. Arzana's own customer testimonial from Milltown Paper's CEO frames the value as saving 15 minutes per quote. That's the pitch: operational efficiency in a specific business function within a specific industry. Nobody's life fundamentally changes. Arzana is not making manufactured goods cheaper for consumers, not making a basic human need affordable, not creating a new market. The consequence of success is a well-run SaaS company with strong manufacturing vertical penetration — which is fine for most investors but does not generate the asymmetric return profile I require. I want to either lose my money or make 50 times my money. A manufacturing front-office automation tool is designed to produce 3-5x returns.

Arzana's technical approach compounds the concern. The underlying technology — LLMs reading emails and PDFs, extracting structured data, writing to ERP systems — is commodity capability in 2026. The dossier itself flags this: "The underlying technology is accessible to well-funded competitors." Conexiom, with $170M raised and $28.7M in 2024 revenue, has already launched an AI Platform for sales order automation. Paperless Parts has AI-powered quoting with 500+ customers. DigiFabster has an AI Quote Agent. Arzana's per-customer models trained on historical job costs provide some differentiation, but this is a data accumulation advantage, not a technology discontinuity. Arzana is not building a new engine — Arzana is brokering cheaper launches on existing rockets, to use the Rocket Lab analogy in reverse. The Rutherford engine used electric turbopumps and 3D-printed combustion chambers — a genuinely non-linear approach to cost. Arzana is applying GPT-class models to PDFs. The 10x faster processing Arzana claims on its website is speed improvement, not a step-function change in what's possible.

The bull case deserves honest construction. Eight named customers at pre-seed — including Zeiss, a global precision manufacturer — is exceptional traction for a five-person team. A New York Times feature before Demo Day is unusual signal. The forward-deployed engineering model creates genuine switching costs: once Arzana's agents are configured across a customer's ERP, CRM, and email systems and trained on historical job costs, ripping it out is expensive. The $17.46B manufacturing operations management market growing at 19.1% CAGR is real. And if Arzana's AI agents truly replace front-office headcount entirely rather than assisting existing staff, the value proposition strengthens — a manufacturer spending $500K annually on order processing staff could cut that to near-zero. I checked my Known Exceptions: DoorDash violated the non-linear technical approach criterion but was overridden because the consequence magnitude was transforming food access for hundreds of millions of people. Arzana's consequence — more efficient manufacturing offices — does not reach that threshold. The DoorDash exception doesn't apply.

Arzana's forward-deployed model raises a separate structural concern. Engineers onsite at customer facilities for weeks before system configuration is a services business wearing a software mask. For a five-person team, this caps the number of simultaneous deployments at perhaps two or three. Scaling requires either hiring aggressively — engineering the gene pool with founder-quality people on meaningful equity — or productizing deployment. Neither path is visible yet, and the labor intensity means Arzana's early growth rate is constrained by bodies, not technology. This is the opposite of the option-value structure I want: a $1M bet that either expires or creates a company that scales without linear cost increases.

Arzana would flip to an invest for me under one condition: if William and Marshall demonstrate that their AI agents can fully eliminate front-office roles — not assist them, replace them — and show a customer whose administrative headcount dropped by 80% with Arzana deployed. That would change the consequence calculus from "faster quotes" to "manufacturing labor cost structure fundamentally altered." Until that evidence exists, Arzana is a competent team building a well-executed AI efficiency tool in a competitive market with commodity technology. The founders are the best part of this company — young, hungry, authentic to the vertical, with clear learning velocity. But the product they're building is the exact AI copilot for a specific enterprise vertical that I pass on every batch. The consequence of Arzana's success is a moderately profitable company, not a changed world. The dimension that locks the verdict is consequence magnitude: manufacturing front-office automation, even done perfectly, does not produce the asymmetric payoff my framework requires.
