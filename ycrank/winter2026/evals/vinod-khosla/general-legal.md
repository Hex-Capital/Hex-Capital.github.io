# General Legal -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Let me start with the tension that defines this company. We backed Spellbook at Khosla Ventures -- $50M Series B at $350M valuation -- because AI contract review is a real technology disruption. Spellbook sells tools to lawyers. General Legal looked at that model and said: why augment the law firm when you can *be* the law firm? That's a genuinely contrarian inversion. Most legal tech founders worship at the altar of "sell picks and shovels." These founders decided to dig the mine themselves. The problem is, a mine is still a mine -- it scales with shovels and miners, not with software.

The consequence of success here is what stops me. If General Legal wins completely -- captures every growth-stage startup's commercial contracting work -- you get a very profitable law firm. Maybe a billion-dollar business. But the world doesn't operate differently. Startups pay $500 instead of $2,000 for contract review. That's a pricing disruption, not a structural one. Compare that to what I funded at Impossible Foods, where success meant reclaiming half the planet's arable land, or at Commonwealth Fusion, where success means displacing fossil fuel power generation. At General Legal, success means cheaper legal bills. The ambition is calibrated to the efficiency of an existing system, not the replacement of it. When someone tells me they're building a better version of an existing service at lower cost, I ask: are you too smart to spend your career on this problem?

The founding team is the strongest possible team for this specific company -- and that's partly the problem. Three ex-Casetext co-workers, all attorneys-turned-AI-engineers, all from the same ecosystem (Harvard Law, Cooley, Fenwick, Casetext, Thomson Reuters). They've already built and exited from this exact intersection. That is the textbook insider pattern I've warned against for decades. Industry experience gives you a set of biases that prevents you from making mistakes -- but it also prevents you from inventing. These founders know precisely what a law firm can and cannot do. That knowledge constrains them to making a better law firm rather than imagining what replaces law firms entirely. The transition from "sell tools to lawyers" at Casetext to "be the law firm" at General Legal shows some evolution, but it's an adjacent move within their comfort zone, not a leap into unknown territory.

Now, the bull case I owe this team: if General Legal expands beyond commercial contracts to become the default AI-native firm for all startup legal work -- formation, financing, IP, employment, M&A -- it could structurally displace Cooley, Fenwick, and Wilson Sonsini. BigLaw can't match $500 flat-fee pricing without cannibalizing their hourly billing model, which is their entire business. That business model judo is real. And the switching costs compound -- as General Legal accumulates a client's full legal history, risk tolerances, and contract patterns, switching becomes painful. If this team executes that expansion, and if the AI workflows genuinely deliver 10x attorney efficiency at scale, you could get a network-effects legal platform that looks more like Square disrupting POS than like a boutique law firm. That's the scenario where I'd be wrong to pass.

But I don't think that scenario is likely, because the model has fundamental scaling constraints that software businesses don't face. Every contract requires a licensed U.S.-barred attorney. The 3-hour turnaround guarantee means you can't just queue work infinitely -- you need attorney capacity to match demand in real time. At $500 per contract, the unit economics require enormous AI leverage per attorney, and that leverage depends on LLM capabilities that every competitor can access. Lawhive has $60M and is entering the U.S. market. Harvey has $750M+ in funding. The proprietary AI workflows are built on the same foundation models everyone else uses. This is a space where the technology is commoditizing faster than any single firm can build defensible moats around it. And when YC explicitly calls for AI law firms in their Request for Startups, and multiple well-funded competitors already exist, you're not ahead of the curve -- you've arrived at the party when the trend is consensus.

The team construction is strong in domain coverage but homogeneous in perspective. All three founders share essentially the same trajectory: elite law school, BigLaw practice, pivot to AI/ML, years at Casetext. Nobody on this team has built a services business from scratch, managed high-volume client operations, or brought a go-to-market perspective from outside legal tech. For the specific risks this venture faces -- scaling a human-dependent service while maintaining quality and margins -- the team composition feels incomplete. This is a law firm that needs to figure out operations at scale, and nobody here has done that.
