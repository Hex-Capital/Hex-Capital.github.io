# Arzana -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's what jumped off the page: a 21-year-old from Okoboji, Iowa who taught himself Mandarin in Hong Kong, learned discus throwing from YouTube to a state ranking, took one coding class in his life, and is now building AI agents for manufacturing. William Alexander is exactly the kind of outsider athlete I look for -- high learning rate, no respect for conventional prerequisites, Midwestern grit. If I were casting the founder of a company that could change the world, this kid would get a callback. The problem is what he's building.

Let me start where I always start: what happens if Arzana fully succeeds? Every manufacturing front office in America runs on their "Office Execution System." Quoting goes from five days to one. Order entry, purchasing, AP/AR -- all automated. And then what? Manufacturing companies save money on office staff. Their margins improve by a few points. That is a fine business. But nobody's life fundamentally changes. A faster RFQ process at Schneider National does not reshape how billions of people live. This is the enterprise vertical AI copilot pattern I see fifty times a year at YC Demo Day -- large TAM, underserved by technology, automates 40-60% of a workflow. The consequence of success is a moderately profitable SaaS company generating 3-5x returns, which is a waste of my attention. I want to lose my money or make fifty times my money. The middle is where capital goes to die slowly.

The technical approach compounds my concern. The memo itself acknowledges it plainly: "the core technology -- LLM-based document parsing and workflow automation -- is buildable by any team with AI engineering capability." This is not Impossible Foods synthesizing heme protein. This is not Rocket Lab designing electric turbopumps. This is applying generally available LLM APIs to parse PDFs and emails in a specific vertical. Endeavor AI raised $7M from Craft Ventures to do roughly the same thing. Canals AI is in the same space. When I invest, I want to see a technical discontinuity that creates an unfair advantage -- a step-function improvement that competitors cannot replicate by hiring five engineers and reading the same API documentation. I do not see that here. Eleven ERP integrations are real work, but they are not a moat. They are plumbing.

The strongest bull case: Alexander's Iowa roots give him authentic access to a market that San Francisco founders cannot penetrate. Manufacturing is a $2.3 trillion sector run by people who distrust coastal technology companies. A kid from Okoboji with a "Founding Field Engineer" also from Okoboji has a distribution advantage that is genuine and hard to replicate. The Schneider National and Zeiss logos are not trivial for a pre-seed company -- those are real enterprises making real purchasing decisions. If Arzana compounds this distribution edge and the ERP integration depth becomes genuinely sticky, there is a path to a $500M-$1B outcome. For most investors, that is exciting. For me, it is not sufficient. I am not looking for companies that can become successful SaaS businesses. I am looking for companies where success means the world is visibly different.

What I would tell Alexander: you have the right founder DNA working on the wrong problem magnitude. The learning velocity, the outsider irreverence, the willingness to operate from Iowa while your peers chase consumer AI in San Francisco -- these are rare and valuable traits. But you are applying them to office workflow automation, which is incremental by construction. If you ever decide to swing at something where success means manufactured goods cost one-tenth what they cost today, or where small manufacturers can compete with multinationals because their entire operation runs autonomously -- call me. Right now, you are building a better tool for the existing system. I fund the people who replace the system entirely.
