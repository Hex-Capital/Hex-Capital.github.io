# Squid -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most striking signal here is that a two-person pre-seed company is already powering a publicly launched product at National Grid DSO -- not a pilot, not a POC, but an interactive FlexPortal that went live in February 2026 with trade press coverage in Utility Week and Energy Live News. For context, National Grid is one of the UK's largest distribution network operators. Getting embedded infrastructure deployed at that scale typically takes incumbents like GE Vernova months of enterprise sales cycles. Squid did it because both founders previously worked inside these organizations -- Conor Jones at National Grid and BCG, George Kolokotronis as Head of Technology at Octopus Energy -- and they co-founded VoltQuant together selling to those same customers before starting Squid. The customer relationships survived a company transition, which tells me these are real commercial relationships, not friendly pilots. That's an execution signal I weight heavily.

Grid planning software is exactly the kind of market most investors dismiss as "too boring, too slow-moving, too enterprise." And they're not entirely wrong about the surface characteristics -- utility procurement cycles run 6-18 months, the customer base is conservative, and the TAM for grid planning specifically is a subset of the $3.76 billion smart grid software market. But I've made my best returns in markets with this exact profile. PagerDuty faced funding resistance because operations alerting seemed niche and unglamorous -- until every company running software needed it. Gusto looked like "just payroll" until it became the HR platform for every small business. The question is whether grid planning follows the same pattern, where a seemingly narrow tool becomes essential infrastructure for a much larger set of workflows. The structural shift here is electrification: EVs, heat pumps, and distributed energy resources are fundamentally changing what grids need to handle, and the UK's RIIO-ED2 regulatory framework (2023-2028) is forcing distribution network operators to modernize their planning processes within a specific window. That's a real "why now," though it's more of a gradual acceleration than the step-function inflection I saw with GPT scaling curves before investing in Harvey. The market is genuinely boring and non-obvious -- but the inflection could be sharper.

What concerns me is the product-to-distribution trajectory. Squid's positioning as "a single source of truth for the network model" has the right structural DNA to become a platform -- once you're the versioned record of every substation and line, you can expand into connections management, capacity forecasting, asset strategy, and policy simulation. That's the Stripe pattern: start with one integration (payments/grid model), then distribute additional products through that same relationship. But I don't see evidence the founders have articulated this multi-product vision yet. The pricing page shows "Coming Soon" with a $0 price point, which at pre-seed is fine -- but for a company selling to utilities, the inability to point to a clear monetization path is a gap. The competitive landscape also gives me pause: Envelio, now E.ON-owned, provides overlapping digital twin SaaS and already serves major European utilities. Camus Energy has $25 million and 500% ARR growth in grid orchestration. Plexigrid has $11.6 million. This isn't a vacant market -- it's contested, even if the specific combination of browser-native delivery and collaborative versioning is differentiated.

The bull case I'd need to believe: grid planning is about to undergo the same transformation that happened to design tools (Figma), developer tools (GitHub), and operations tools (PagerDuty) -- where a browser-native, collaborative approach displaces fragmented desktop software and becomes the default infrastructure layer for an entire industry. If Squid becomes the system of record for how distribution networks are planned across the UK and then Europe, the TAM expands dramatically beyond the static market sizing. The electrification wave means more grid decisions need to be made, faster, by more people -- which is exactly when collaborative browser-native tools beat desktop engineering suites. And the regulatory compliance moat (SOC 2 Type II, ISO 27001, GDPR for a two-person team) deters new entrants in a way I find structurally attractive. If Octopus Energy and National Grid represent the beginning of a broader adoption curve among UK DNOs, and the RIIO-ED2 window forces a procurement cycle that advantages modern tools, Squid could capture meaningful market share before the incumbents unbundle their offerings.

Where I land: this is a genuinely "boring but critical" infrastructure play with impressive early execution and strong founder-market fit. The team has shipped real product to real enterprise customers, maintained relationships across a company transition, and built compliance infrastructure that most pre-seed companies wouldn't touch. The single miracle is clear -- become the system of record for grid planning at major utilities -- and the team is organized around it. But the market's structural inflection, while real, isn't as sharp as the technology threshold crossings I look for in my highest-conviction investments. The product-to-distribution trajectory is structurally possible but not yet articulated. And the competitive landscape includes a corporate-backed direct competitor (E.ON/Envelio) with existing European utility relationships. I'm interested, not convicted. This is a company I'd track closely and revisit after they demonstrate customer expansion beyond the founders' prior employer network.
