# Hub.xyz -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the ghost on the cap table. Léonie Nguyen is listed as co-founder and Chief Business Officer, but she was appointed CEO of Meey Group — a Vietnamese proptech company — in October 2025, and she doesn't appear on the YC page at all. Meanwhile, Armin is CPO. Tim is COO. Nobody is CEO. I broke my co-founder rule once, with HQ Trivia, and I've told this story a hundred times since: the product was right, the timing was right, and it died because there was no clear hand on the wheel. Here, it's worse than a 50/50 split — it looks like a founding team in mid-dissolution, with the leadership question unresolved while they enter YC. That's not a crack in the foundation. That's a missing foundation.

When I ask "tell me their story" — my first and most important question — I get résumés. Armin studied industrial engineering in Brussels, became an LP at a couple of venture funds, had "early crypto experience." Tim went to École Polytechnique, HEC Paris, Berkeley, worked at Tiffany & Co. on financial data systems. These are credentialed, capable people. But nothing in their biographies says this problem found them. Nothing says they were up at 3 AM obsessing over the brokenness of AI training data collection before they saw the market opportunity. This reads like smart operators who spotted the AI-meets-crypto intersection and positioned themselves there. That's career optimization, not a biographical forge. The founder who built Crusoe Energy had been staring at gas flares in North Dakota fields. The founders at Affirm understood credit card opacity from the inside. I need that connection, and I don't see it here.

Now, the bull case — because I want to be honest about what would have to be true for this to work. AI training data is genuinely becoming scarce and legally contested. Every major AI lab is fighting lawsuits over scraping. If centralized data collection becomes legally untenable, a distributed, user-consented network that collects and structures real-time multimodal data could be the infrastructure answer. 500,000 nodes is not nothing — that's a real network. The SwissBorg partnership opens a pipeline to over a million KYC-verified users. And if Hub.xyz can differentiate on data structuring quality — turning raw bandwidth into enterprise-grade training sets — rather than competing on raw node count, they could carve out a genuine position. The ten-year question would be: what does a global distributed data infrastructure look like when every AI system needs continuous fresh data? If Hub.xyz becomes the API layer between a million-node collection network and enterprise AI teams, that's infrastructure worth owning. YC adds credibility for the enterprise sales motion they'll need.

But here's what kills it for me: this isn't contrarian. It's the opposite. AI training data plus DePIN tokens is the most consensus-hot intersection in tech right now. There are 600-plus DePIN projects. Grass has already raised $17.5 million and sits at roughly a billion-dollar valuation doing essentially the same thing. Nodepay has $7 million and 1.5 million active users. When I backed Anduril, nobody else would touch defense tech. When I wrote my check into Contraline, male birth control made VCs physically uncomfortable. This space makes VCs salivate, not squirm. The "magically weird" test fails completely — there's nothing weird about this at all. It's the most obvious bet in the most crowded sector, arriving later than competitors with more capital.

And there's no invisible system being disrupted. AI training data isn't an opaque structure extracting value from people who don't understand it. It's an expensive, labor-intensive market that's actually becoming more transparent through litigation and licensing debates. Uber attacked the taxi medallion cartel. Affirm exposed credit card opacity. Ownwell made property tax appeals accessible. Hub.xyz is... making data collection cheaper through distributed bandwidth? That's an efficiency improvement, not a structural revelation. The token-incentivized 500K users earning IQ Points for contributing bandwidth aren't being liberated from an extractive system — they're participating in a speculative token economy. When I see airdrop campaigns and Discord engagement quizzes as the primary user acquisition mechanism, I see a narrative running ahead of substance. I've been burned by that pattern before.

The defensibility question seals the pass. The DePIN bandwidth-sharing architecture is explicitly commodity — the dossier says so directly, and the competitor landscape confirms it. The data Hub.xyz collects comes from the public internet, so there's no proprietary data moat. The differentiation claim is "structured, multimodal output" versus raw bandwidth, but there's no evidence this structuring capability exists yet, no enterprise customers, no LOIs, no case studies. A well-funded competitor — and Grass is exactly that — could replicate whatever API layer Hub.xyz builds. This is the pattern of my failures, not my wins: attention and incentive-driven user acquisition without the infrastructure moat that makes you irreplaceable.
