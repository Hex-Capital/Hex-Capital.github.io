# Traverse -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the pivot. These founders were building AI tools for mortgage brokers three months ago. Now they're building RL environments for medicine, law, and business strategy -- domains where, by their own admission, verification is "largely unsolved." A mid-batch pivot isn't inherently damning. But it tells you something about the nature of the knowledge driving this company. When I backed Twitter, Evan Williams had spent the better part of a decade thinking about how humans communicate in public. When I invested in Notion, Ivan Zhao had been obsessing over the architecture of personal thought for years before writing a line of product code. Traverse's founders identified a market gap -- probably a real one -- but there's a difference between spotting an opportunity and possessing the non-transferable understanding required to execute on it. Building verifiers for subjective professional judgment in medicine and law isn't a coding problem. It's an epistemological problem. And I see no evidence that either founder has spent meaningful time thinking about how doctors reason, how lawyers construct arguments, or how any non-deterministic professional domain actually encodes quality.

The strongest signal here is Zachary Yu's stint at Mercor. He worked inside what is now a $10B company building RL environments for frontier labs. That gives him operational knowledge -- how labs buy, what they value, where the delivery pipeline breaks. That's useful. It's also the kind of knowledge any competent engineer at Mercor would absorb in a year. The question I ask is: what did Yu learn at Mercor that Mercor itself doesn't know? If his insight is that Mercor's crowd-sourced workforce model structurally can't handle subjective verification -- that you need a fundamentally different architecture for non-deterministic domains -- that could be genuine specific knowledge worth investing behind. But I haven't seen him articulate that thesis anywhere. His announcement tweet invites "researchers who've been thinking about long-horizon agents" to reach out, which is outbound recruiting, not a founder who already possesses the insight and is building from it.

The leverage architecture concerns me. I systematically back infrastructure and picks-and-shovels plays -- Stack Overflow, Alchemy, Replit, Notion -- because platforms create nonlinear output from linear input. Traverse is selling RL environments to a buyer set of maybe five frontier labs. Each new domain -- medicine, law, finance -- likely requires recruiting specialized domain experts and building bespoke verification systems. That's a services business with a technical wrapper. Where's the code multiplier? Where does one engineer's work serve a million users? The RL environment space can produce platform-scale businesses -- Mercor and Scale AI have demonstrated that -- but they did it by building workforce management infrastructure that scales horizontally. Traverse is positioning on the quality end, which means scaling vertically with expertise. Vertical scaling means headcount. Headcount means you're renting out people's time, not creating leverage.

The bull case deserves a full hearing. If non-deterministic verification turns out to be a genuinely different problem -- not just harder labeling but a fundamentally different technical architecture -- then the incumbents' strengths become liabilities. Mercor's crowd-sourced workforce, Surge AI's annotation pipelines, Scale AI's operational machine -- all optimized for the wrong paradigm. A small team that cracks the verification problem for subjective domains would own the most valuable layer of AI infrastructure for the next decade, because every frontier lab needs it and none of the existing vendors can provide it. Yu's Mercor experience would be the inside view that identified the structural gap. The concentration risk (few buyers) flips too -- if Anthropic alone is spending tens of millions annually on RL environments with 3-5x growth expected, one contract could make this company. For this to work, Traverse would need to actually solve the verification problem they've identified, attract domain experts despite having no funding advantage, and sell to labs that already have relationships with their former employer. Each of those is possible. All three together is a parlay I'm not comfortable with at this stage.

The competitive dynamics are the wrong kind of consensus. Everyone with capital and ML talent is rushing into RL environments right now. Applied Compute raised $100M with an ex-OpenAI team. Mercor is at $10B. Surge AI is seeking a billion-dollar raise. This isn't a market where smart people think the category is stupid -- it's a market where everyone agrees it's brilliant and is pricing accordingly. My best returns have always come from the opposite setup. The non-deterministic angle provides some differentiation, but Mercor already lists healthcare and law among its target domains, which means the niche is contrarian within the category but the category itself is peak consensus. That's not the asymmetric setup I look for.

Two young, technically capable founders who ship fast and identified a real gap. I respect the pattern recognition and the willingness to pivot toward a harder problem. But I need founders whose knowledge exists outside the standard pipeline -- insight that emerged from genuine curiosity rather than market analysis. What I see here is smart generalists who correctly identified that non-deterministic RL environments are undersupplied. What I don't see is the years of accumulated understanding that would make their solution qualitatively different from what Mercor or Applied Compute will build once they decide to focus there.
