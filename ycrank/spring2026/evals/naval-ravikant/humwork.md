# Humwork -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Humwork's core proposition contains a structural inversion that determines everything else about this bet. The product exists because AI agents fail — they "loop on the same bug, make the same bad architectural guess five times" (YC Launch page). The fix is routing to a human expert within 30 seconds via MCP. But this means Humwork's unit economics are human-denominated. Every resolution requires paying an expert. The MCP server is code leverage — permissionless, zero marginal cost to integrate — but the actual product delivery is labor leverage, the form I rank lowest. Notion scales to millions of users without proportional headcount. Humwork scales to millions of resolutions only by scaling its expert bench proportionally. At 1,000+ verified experts today (humwork.ai), the business needs 10,000+ to serve meaningfully larger demand. The software dispatch layer is thin; the human layer is thick. This is closer to the Uber marketplace structure than to the Notion software structure, and it inherits the capital-intensive supply-side dynamics that come with it.

Yash Goenka brings relevant but transferable knowledge. He's built AI/LLM products since 2021, shipped Qualify.bot (YC S25) with real-time AI-to-human interaction in loan qualification, and co-built an AI voice platform handling 1M+ call minutes. Rohan Datta adds five years of data science at Microgrid Labs and ML research depth. Their Qualify.bot architecture — real-time matching of AI conversations to human judgment in time-sensitive contexts — is structurally similar to Humwork's agent-to-expert routing. That's genuine architectural knowledge. But it's the kind of knowledge a strong engineering team at Toloka could replicate in a quarter. Compare to Aravind Srinivas building Perplexity: his years inside Google Brain and OpenAI constituted knowledge so deep that competitors couldn't hire their way to parity. Humwork's founders identified a real problem through proximity to the AI agent wave, not through years of obsessive immersion in agent failure modes. The insight that "AI agents get stuck" is visible to anyone using Claude Code or Cursor daily.

Tendem, backed by $72M from Bezos Expeditions and Nebius, already operates a functionally identical product — MCP-based human expert access for AI agents — with 10,000+ experts versus Humwork's 1,000+ (toloka.ai; Nebius blog, 2025). Tendem has existing relationships with Anthropic and Shopify. Humwork's claimed differentiation — narrower focus on real-time agent debugging versus Tendem's broader task orientation — is a positioning choice, not a structural advantage. The MCP integration layer is technically replicable; the dossier itself acknowledges this. In my framework, "escape competition through authenticity" requires specific knowledge that competitors cannot acquire through hiring or capital. Humwork is competing on execution speed in a category where a well-funded incumbent has a 10x supply advantage. That's a race won by capital, not by knowledge.

Yash's trajectory raises the principal-agent question I weight heavily. He pivoted from Qualify.bot to Humwork within approximately nine months, both under Orange AI Inc. His portfolio includes Instawrite.ai, Jarvys.ai, Iris BCI, Phonecall.bot, Qualify.bot, and now Humwork. Serial building can signal relentless energy — or it can signal someone more passionate about founding than about solving any particular problem. The 16-year friendship with Rohan eliminates my co-founder trust concern entirely, and getting into YC twice demonstrates real capability. But the pivot velocity from Qualify.bot (launched July 2025) to Humwork (launched April 2026) suggests this was opportunity-driven, not obsession-driven. A true principal would have been wrestling with agent failure modes for years before deciding to build a company around it. Humwork feels like a sharp founder who spotted a wave, not a principal whose identity is fused with the problem.

The bull case deserves construction. Humwork's 87% resolution rate and 130% net retention in software engineering (humwork.ai) are legitimately strong pre-seed signals — retention above 100% means customers are expanding usage, which is the earliest indicator of product-market fit. The timing is exceptional: MCP released late 2024 created a standardized integration channel, and YC Spring 2026 was "dominated by autonomous agent pitches" (Analytics Drift), meaning Humwork rides a structural demand wave. The access democratization angle is real — compressing expert availability from hours (Upwork, Braintrust) to 30 seconds removes a genuine time gatekeeper. And the Qualify.bot experience provides directly transferable architecture for real-time AI-to-human routing. An advocate would argue Humwork is to expert knowledge what AngelList was to startup investing — democratizing access through a software layer. I grant the parallel has shape. But AngelList had zero marginal cost per syndicate. Humwork has human cost per resolution. The parallel breaks precisely where leverage matters most.

One verifiable condition would flip me to Invest: evidence that Humwork's resolution data creates a compounding AI layer that progressively reduces the need for human experts — a learning system where the ratio of automated-to-human resolutions improves measurably over time, turning labor leverage into AI leverage. If 87% of resolutions require humans today but the architecture is designed so that figure drops to 50% in twelve months through accumulated resolution data training an agent, then the leverage architecture transforms. No evidence of this exists in the dossier. Without it, Humwork scales linearly with human supply, and the decisive dimension — Permissionless Leverage Architecture at 10/25 — keeps this below my threshold. The second decisive factor is the Tendem overhang: $72M and 10x expert supply in a category where the integration layer is replicable makes this a capital race, not a knowledge race. I pass.
