# BeeSafe AI -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The thing that caught my attention immediately is the approach itself -- these founders built AI agents that pretend to be scam victims, engage criminals in weeks-long conversations, and extract the actual infrastructure of fraud: mule account numbers, crypto wallets, the whole operation. "Victim as a Service" is the name of their academic paper. That title alone has a quality I respond to -- it's provocative, a little uncomfortable, and describes something nobody else is doing. When I think about my pattern of backing companies that make invisible systems visible -- the way Flock Safety surfaced crime data for communities, or Carta replaced opaque spreadsheets with transparent cap tables -- BeeSafe fits. They're exposing the hidden plumbing of scam networks that currently operates in complete darkness. Financial institutions and law enforcement can't fight what they can't see, and these founders are turning the lights on.

But here's where I get stuck. When I sit down with founders, the first thing I want to hear is their story -- not the business, not the technology. I want to understand how they got here, what forged them, why this problem and not any other. What I see in the BeeSafe team are three exceptionally credentialed PhD researchers from CMU and UCSD who converged through shared academic advisors in systems security. They're clearly brilliant. They have publications at IEEE S&P and SIGCOMM, research stints at Google and Microsoft, and they literally invented this product category in an academic paper. But I can't find the biographical thread that ties them to this specific problem in a way that goes beyond intellectual fascination. Did one of them watch a family member lose their savings to a pig-butchering scam? Did they grow up in a community devastated by fraud? The dossier doesn't tell me, and that absence matters to me more than it would to most investors. The founders I've backed who went the distance -- the ones who survived the crises that kill most companies -- were solving problems that were personal to them in a way that made quitting unthinkable.

The technical moat is real and it compounds. Every scammer conversation BeeSafe runs generates proprietary data that no competitor has -- tens of thousands of adversarial interactions, thousands of extracted mule accounts. That's not something you can replicate by spinning up GPT-4 and pointing it at Telegram. The operational know-how to convincingly impersonate a victim for weeks without getting burned, across multiple platforms and languages, is hard-won expertise refined through years of research. And the incumbents face a genuine structural problem: BioCatch, Hawk AI, Featurespace -- they all sell transaction-layer monitoring to fraud-ops teams at banks. Pivoting to "we'll run offensive intelligence operations against criminal networks" requires a completely different product, a different buyer within the organization, and a different liability framework. That's the kind of business-model moat I learned to appreciate during my years at IronPort, where we sold at the infrastructure layer in ways that application-security companies couldn't easily replicate.

The bull case for this company is genuinely strong, and I want to be honest about that. Pig-butchering losses grew 66% year-over-year to $10 billion in the U.S. alone. The U.S. government is standing up dedicated strike forces. Financial institutions are desperate. LLMs just crossed the threshold where automated scammer engagement became feasible -- this wasn't possible two years ago. And nobody else is doing this. If I imagine the full potential, I can see a world where BeeSafe becomes the intelligence backbone for global anti-fraud operations, where every financial institution subscribes to their feed the way they subscribe to threat intelligence services today. The data flywheel would make them nearly impossible to displace. That's the kind of outcome where writing the first check matters most.

What holds me back from a higher conviction level is a combination of things I can't fully resolve from the dossier. The biographical depth isn't there -- or at least isn't visible to me yet. Three academic co-founders who share an advisor network is intellectually aligned but hasn't been stress-tested by the kind of operational crisis that breaks companies. Fraud prevention as a category is consensus-important even if the method is novel, which means this doesn't trigger my "everyone thinks this is crazy" signal the way SpaceX or Crusoe did. And the product scope is narrow enough that I worry about their ability to command budget ownership inside an enterprise -- are they a must-have intelligence feed, or a nice-to-have supplement to the BioCatch platform the bank already bought? The answer to that question determines whether this is a $50M outcome or a $5B outcome, and it depends entirely on how well these founders can sell, navigate enterprise procurement, and expand their surface area. All of which loops back to: I need to hear their stories. Not the business. The story.
