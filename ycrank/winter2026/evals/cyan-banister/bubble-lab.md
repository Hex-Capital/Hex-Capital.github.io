# Bubble Lab -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I notice first about Bubble Lab: this is one of the most consensus categories in tech right now. AI agents that automate workflows across SaaS tools -- every investor in San Francisco can immediately picture this, immediately nod along, immediately understand the TAM slide. n8n has raised $253 million. Gumloop just closed $24.5 million. Dust has Sequoia behind it. Salesforce itself is building Agentforce 360 directly into Slack, the very platform Bubble Lab depends on. When I look at the landscape of my best investments, the common thread is that other investors thought the idea was absurd -- Elon wanting to launch rockets, Travis wanting to replace taxis with an app on your phone, a team in North Dakota wanting to mine Bitcoin on gas flares. Those companies were not popular. They were difficult to assess. Bubble Lab is popular and easy to assess, and that's actually a problem in my framework. The weirdness has already been arbitraged away by better-capitalized players.

When I sit down with founders, I always start the same way: tell me your story, not the business. And what I see here are two talented Cornell Tech graduates -- Selina Li with a UPenn degree and JPMorgan quant background, Zach Zhong with UCSD CS and a "3x founding engineer" description. Smart people, clearly capable builders. But the biographical thread that connects them to operations automation specifically? I can't find it. They pivoted from gymii.ai, a consumer nutrition tracking app with LPGA partnerships, to B2B ops tooling. That's a significant domain shift that reads as market-seeking rather than problem-solving from lived experience. I'm not hearing the story of someone who spent years drowning in CRM updates and Jira ticket hell and finally snapped. The founders I back hardest -- the ones I'd drop everything to work for -- are the ones where the problem and the person are inseparable. Here, two capable engineers identified a hot market. That's a perfectly rational decision, but it's not the signal I invest on.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement because the execution signals are real for a pre-seed company. Four thousand users, 1,100 GitHub stars, SOC 2 certification this early, named customers reporting 10-plus hours saved weekly -- that's not nothing. The Slack-native positioning is a genuine UX insight: meeting people where they already work rather than forcing them into another dashboard. The freemium model with workspace virality is a smart distribution mechanic. And if Salesforce keeps Agentforce locked behind Business+ and Enterprise+ pricing tiers, there's real pricing space for a tool-agnostic alternative at the startup and mid-market level. If these founders can capture meaningful share before the window closes, they could build something acquirable. The prior co-founder relationship -- Cornell Tech together, gymii.ai together -- gives me some comfort that the team has been tested, even if only lightly.

But I keep coming back to two structural problems. First, the platform dependency is existential. Building your entire value proposition inside Slack while Salesforce is actively building the same capability natively is like opening a restaurant inside someone else's house. The open-source engine and visual workflow builder are hedges, but they undercut the very thing that makes Bubble Lab distinctive -- being Slack-native. Second, nothing about this company creates economic access for people who currently lack it. Uber gave drivers income. Postmates gave delivery workers flexibility. Affirm gave consumers transparent credit. Bubble Lab saves operations teams at companies running Jira and Stripe a few hours a week. That's productivity software for already-privileged knowledge workers. It's useful, but it doesn't make an invisible system visible or pull anyone into economic participation who was previously excluded. For my portfolio, that matters.

I respect the hustle and the early traction. These founders are builders who ship -- gymii.ai to Bubble Lab, 25 integrations, SOC 2 certification, all at pre-seed. But this is a consensus bet in a crowded field with massive platform risk, and the founders, while capable, don't carry the biographical weight that tells me they'll fight through the specific storm coming at them from Salesforce, n8n, and the rest. This isn't my kind of company.
