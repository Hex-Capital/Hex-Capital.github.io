# Nine Fives -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Noah Levy spent approximately a decade as a Senior RF Engineer at SpaceX designing satellite hardware. Andrew Kurtz spent nine years in aerospace including test automation on the Falcon 9 program at SpaceX and staff mechanical engineering at Aurora Flight Sciences, both with MIT and Cornell pedigrees respectively. When I look at Nine Fives, I see two engineers who lived inside the pain of GPIB cables, proprietary driver installations, and vendor-locked control software for years — and decided to build the instruments they wished existed. That's specific knowledge productized. You cannot hire a product manager to understand why a headless Linux test bench needs a REST API instead of a Windows-only GPIB driver. That intuition comes from thousands of hours fighting Keysight's software stack at 2 AM during a satellite integration campaign. The co-founder relationship — forged during overlapping SpaceX tenures, not at a startup weekend — adds signal. Levy brings the RF domain expertise to design instruments that perform to 6 GHz with sub-dB accuracy; Kurtz brings the mechanical engineering and test automation knowledge to understand how these instruments fit into automated production workflows. The specific knowledge is real, and it maps directly to the product.

Keysight's $5.4B revenue and National Instruments' $8.2B acquisition price rest on a business model that bundles hardware with proprietary software licenses and driver ecosystems. Nine Fives' "Simple, Networked, Driverless" positioning attacks the exact profit center that funds incumbent R&D. This structural parallel — a principal attacking an incumbent constrained by its own P&L — is the same dynamic I identified at Perplexity versus Google's ad-revenue dependency. Keysight's engineers have the specific knowledge to build ethernet-native, driver-free instruments. But shipping them would cannibalize the software licensing revenue that justifies their headcount. Mini-Circuits ships 60,000+ catalog products and structurally deprioritizes software UX investment on any single SKU. Nine Fives, as a two-person principal team with no legacy revenue to protect, can move where these agents cannot. The bull case writes itself: real specific knowledge, genuine principal-agent asymmetry against incumbents, two founders shipping two real products with datasheets and reference manuals before raising meaningful capital.

Nine Fives hits a hard constraint in my framework: this is a hardware business, and hardware businesses require proportional capital to scale. Every POE-ATTEN-6G unit requires component procurement, RF calibration, assembly, and inventory. Revenue grows linearly with units shipped, not exponentially with users acquired. There is no zero-marginal-cost replication here — no code leverage, no network effect, no data flywheel. The REST API and browser UI are excellent product features, but they're firmware on a physical device, not a platform that compounds. Compare Nine Fives to Notion — a seed-stage investment where a small team built software used by millions with near-zero marginal cost per user. Nine Fives cannot reach millions of users because there aren't millions of RF test benches, and each one requires a physical instrument to be manufactured and shipped. The serviceable market for networked programmable attenuators and switches sits in the low hundreds of millions — a solid niche business, but not the power-law outcome space where angel bets generate asymmetric returns. My framework penalizes businesses where "fortunes require leverage" and the leverage available is labor and capital rather than code and media.

Nine Fives' two-person team shipping two calibrated RF instruments is genuinely impressive team-to-value leverage for a hardware company — most hardware startups at this stage are still prototyping. The Varda Space exception in my portfolio proves I will back hardware when physics creates leverage impossible to replicate on Earth. But Varda manufactures pharmaceutical compounds in microgravity that literally cannot exist at 1G. Nine Fives builds better-interfaced versions of instruments that Mini-Circuits, Vaunix, and Adaura already sell. The dossier itself identifies the risk plainly: "Adding a REST API to an existing product line is an engineering project, not a fundamental barrier." Vaunix could ship a firmware update tomorrow adding browser-based control to their Lab Brick line. No patents protect Nine Fives' approach. The moat at this stage depends entirely on execution speed and developer experience quality — real advantages, but not structural ones. The 6 GHz frequency ceiling versus Vaunix's 40 GHz and Keysight's 50+ GHz range compounds this vulnerability.

Nine Fives does fit my access democratization pattern — removing proprietary driver dependencies gives any engineer with an Ethernet cable access to programmatic RF test control previously locked behind vendor-specific Windows software and GPIB interfaces. But the population being liberated is narrow: RF test engineers in aerospace, defense, and telecom. AngelList democratized startup investing for thousands of founders and investors who were previously excluded entirely. Nine Fives makes existing RF engineers' workflows 10x faster, which is valuable, but it's convenience democratization rather than structural access expansion. The condition that would flip me to Invest: evidence that Nine Fives is building a software orchestration platform — a test bench operating system that controls any vendor's instruments via a unified API, with Nine Fives hardware as the entry point but the software layer as the real product. That would shift the leverage from atoms to bits, create network effects as more instruments get integrated, and open a platform play with zero-marginal-cost scaling. Without that, Nine Fives is a well-executed hardware company in a niche market, built by founders with genuine specific knowledge and authentic principal commitment — but structurally capped by the physics of manufacturing and the arithmetic of a sub-billion-dollar serviceable market.
