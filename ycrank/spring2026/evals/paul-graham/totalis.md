# Totalis -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Totalis is that Kalshi — a $22 billion company with $1.5 billion in annualized revenue — has already built the core feature Totalis wants to sell. Kalshi launched RFQ-based parlays in late 2025 and licensed the technology to Robinhood within weeks. Polymarket acquired a derivatives platform. The exchanges aren't sleeping through the derivatives opportunity the way banks slept through developer-friendly payment APIs. They're sprinting toward it. When I backed Patrick Collison at Stripe, the schlep that repelled everyone was regulatory and integration complexity that banks had no economic incentive to solve for developers. Here, the prediction market exchanges have every incentive to build parlays themselves — parlays already account for over 20% of Kalshi's weekly sports volume. Building a "neutral derivative layer" across platforms that are actively building proprietary derivatives isn't schlep blindness. It's running toward a door that's closing.

The founders are technically capable — that much is clear. Pravesh Mansharamani has five DeFi engineering roles across two years, 11+ hackathon wins totaling $55K, and built EigenBets, an actual prediction market platform. Eric Liu has ML engineering experience and strong blockchain credentials. They co-built Goddid.Money at ETHGlobal Buenos Aires and won $7,600 across three prizes. These two can ship code fast. But hackathon wins are a different muscle than startup resourcefulness. A hackathon gives you a problem, a weekend, and judges. A startup gives you no problem definition, no timeline, and customers who don't return your emails. I look for stories of founders who did something creative when they were blocked — not when they were competing in a structured format. The dossier contains no such stories. That's not fatal at pre-seed, but it means I'm scoring on priors rather than evidence.

The problem discovery feels more like market observation than lived frustration. Both founders are genuinely immersed in the prediction market ecosystem — Waterloo Blockchain Club, ETHDenver, multiple DeFi companies. That immersion is real. But "derivative layer for prediction markets" is infrastructure language mapped onto a hot trend. When Drew Houston built Dropbox, he could tell you the exact moment he forgot his USB drive on the bus. I want to hear the specific moment when Eric or Pravesh tried to combine a Polymarket sports bet with a Kalshi politics position and couldn't, and that frustration kept them up at night. The dossier doesn't contain that story. What it contains is a clean market thesis about fragmented parlay infrastructure — which is the kind of thing you get from analyzing the competitive landscape, not from living in the future and building what's missing.

The bull case deserves serious consideration. Prediction market volume grew 17x in one year. The CFTC is formalizing regulation. If prediction markets become a mainstream asset class — and the Robinhood integration suggests they might — then derivatives infrastructure is genuinely a massive opportunity. A neutral cross-platform layer has structural advantages that individual exchanges can't replicate because of competitive conflicts. And these founders have the domain knowledge to build it. If they can bootstrap market maker liquidity and get one or two exchanges to integrate before incumbents lock down proprietary standards, they could become the connective tissue of a new asset class. The timing window is real. But "timing window" is a bet I'm constitutionally bad at evaluating — I told Airbnb investors the founders were great and the idea might be crazy, and I was wrong about the idea being crazy. Here, I think the founders are good and the timing might be right, but the competitive position is genuinely worse than it appears. The exchanges aren't ignoring this problem. They're solving it.

What bothers me most is that this idea sounds good to everyone. Say "derivative layer for prediction markets" to any crypto-native investor and they nod. Say "cross-platform parlays" to any prediction market trader and they want it. That universal head-nodding is exactly the signal I've learned to distrust. The best investments I've made were in companies where smart people's first reaction was confusion or skepticism — Airbnb ("strangers sleeping in your house?"), Bitcoin-related businesses in 2012, classroom behavior management software. Totalis generates no such reaction. It's an obvious infrastructure play on an obvious trend, which means the returns are already being competed away by the dozens of well-funded teams with the same insight.

The founders are young, fast, and domain-native. At Waterloo, the blockchain community clearly functions as a talent incubator the way MIT's AI lab did in an earlier era. I could see Pravesh and Eric building something exceptional — but I suspect this particular idea is a stepping stone rather than a destination. If they come back with a different take on the prediction market space — something that sounds weird or wrong, something that makes other builders in the space say "that'll never work" — I'd want to talk again.
