# Arzana -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Eight named customers including Zeiss at pre-seed with a 5-person team -- that's the number that stops me on Arzana's dossier. For a company founded in 2025 by a 21-year-old CEO from Okoboji, Iowa, landing an enterprise like Zeiss while running a forward-deployed engineering model speaks to either exceptional sales instincts or authentic manufacturing network access that most SF-based startups can't replicate. Arzana sits squarely in the "boring but critical" infrastructure pattern that has produced some of my best returns -- Gusto in payroll, Checkr in background checks, Samsara in industrial IoT operations. Manufacturing front-office automation (quoting, order entry, estimating) is exactly the unglamorous, operationally essential category where fewer ambitious founders compete and sticky revenue compounds through deep workflow integration. The structural parallel to Gusto is real: both target operationally essential, regulation-adjacent workflows that manufacturers and employers can't avoid, creating retention dynamics that outlast any individual product feature.

The market timing question for Arzana maps directly to what I've been calling the GPT Ladder -- at specific capability thresholds, specific markets crack open. Manufacturing document processing (reading messy RFQs and POs in hundreds of formats from different senders) required a level of LLM capability that OCR and rule-based NLP couldn't deliver. That threshold crossed in 2024-2025, and Arzana is building in the window. But I have to be honest about the degree of nonobviousness. When I backed Harvey in early 2022, legal AI had been abandoned -- there were zero credible competitors after a decade of failures. Arzana faces Conexiom at $170M raised and $28.7M ARR already shipping an "AI Platform," Paperless Parts with 500+ customers and AI-powered quoting, Esker with 3,000+ enterprise clients and Bridgepoint/General Atlantic resources behind it, and DigiFabster launching its own AI Quote Agent. The structural shift is genuine, but it's increasingly recognized by incumbents who are already adapting. That distinction -- between a market that's been abandoned (Harvey's legal AI in 2022) and a market where incumbents are actively investing in the same capability (Arzana's manufacturing automation in 2026) -- materially affects my conviction on timing.

Arzana's positioning is application-layer, not infrastructure-layer, and that's where my framework applies the sharpest discount. The company integrates with 10+ ERP systems (Epicor, Infor, JobBoss, NetSuite, SAP, Sage, and others listed on arzana.com) and embeds per-customer AI models trained on historical job cost data -- that creates real switching costs, which I value. But Arzana doesn't build a platform that other businesses depend on or that developers build on top of. Stripe became the payments API; Arzana becomes a tool that manufacturers use directly. The ERP integration creates stickiness per customer, and the estimating module's custom AI models create a per-customer data flywheel, but there's no network effect across customers and no API-first architecture that compounds the way infrastructure layers do. Arzana is closer to a vertical SaaS application with strong retention mechanics than to the infrastructure plays I weight highest.

William Alexander (21, Stanford Econ+CS, Iowa manufacturing background) and Marshall Kools (24, Stanford MS Engineering & Policy, D1 wrestler, prior COO at Brilla) have a clear CEO/COO split -- which I explicitly look for, since equal co-founder structures without a designated decision-maker predict deadlock during scaling. Marshall earning roughly $10,000 in personal salary signals genuine commitment. Their Midwest manufacturing roots (Okoboji IA, Neenah/Appleton WI) and the fact that Arzana maintains offices in both regions alongside San Francisco gives them authentic access to the industrial customer base. But neither founder has operated at a company during a scaling phase -- the 10-to-1,000 transition that I wrote the High Growth Handbook about. Manufacturing sales cycles are trust-based and relationship-driven, and while their backgrounds partially mitigate the youth credibility gap, selling AI process automation to traditionally conservative manufacturing executives at 21 and 24 remains a real friction point. I've seen mediocre operators do exceptionally well when product-market fit is strong enough, so I don't overweight this -- but combined with the forward-deployed model's scaling constraints (onsite engineering for weeks per customer at a 5-person team), Arzana's path to the 100th customer is not yet clear.

The single-miracle test is where Arzana scores best in my framework. The core technology miracle -- LLMs reliably extracting structured data from unstructured manufacturing documents -- is happening exogenously on its own capability curve. Manufacturers are proven buyers of automation that saves labor hours; the dossier cites Milltown Paper's CEO confirming Arzana reduced a 15-minute quoting process to one click. No regulatory miracle is needed (ITAR compliance is a feature Arzana already supports, not a barrier to overcome). The remaining challenge is execution: scaling distribution and productizing the forward-deployed model. That's standard startup execution, not a miracle. Compare this to a company needing simultaneous breakthroughs in technology AND regulation AND distribution -- Arzana needs the technology to keep working (already happening) and the go-to-market to scale (hard but standard). One miracle, already underway.

The strongest bull case for Arzana: this is a Gusto-shaped opportunity in manufacturing, a massive fragmented market ($17.46B in 2024, projected $76.71B by 2033 per Grand View Research) where thousands of small-to-mid-size manufacturers are underserved by enterprise solutions like Conexiom and Esker. The founders have authentic domain access, 8 named customers at pre-seed demonstrates real pull, the LLM structural shift is genuine, and the "boring but critical" pattern has historically been where my highest-conviction investments cluster because prestige-seeking founders avoid these categories. The NYT feature in February 2026 suggests narrative momentum. But the bull case breaks down on two facts: Conexiom's $170M in funding and existing AI platform means the "absence of competition" advantage -- the key ingredient in the boring-but-critical pattern -- doesn't fully apply here, and Arzana's forward-deployed model at 5 people creates a scaling constraint that well-resourced competitors don't face. The condition that would flip me to Invest: evidence that Arzana has displaced Conexiom at a specific customer, demonstrating competitive differentiation against the strongest incumbent, combined with deployment time compression from weeks to days showing the forward-deployed model is becoming productized.

Arzana lands in the space between "boring but critical" thesis fit and competitive reality. The market timing is real but not nonobvious enough to generate alpha -- the incumbents see it too. The application-layer positioning creates customer-level switching costs but not the compounding infrastructure dynamics that produce my highest-conviction bets. The single-miracle viability is strong, and the founders' authentic manufacturing connections are a genuine distribution advantage at this stage. I'd watch Arzana closely for evidence of productized deployment and competitive wins against Conexiom, but today I don't have the conviction to write a check from my own money into a market where the structural window is being contested by well-funded incumbents already adapting to the same LLM capability shift.
