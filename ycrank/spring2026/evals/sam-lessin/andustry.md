# Andustry -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's what jumped out at me immediately: Matchory raised $8.86M. LightSource Labs raised $33M at a $130M valuation. Cavela raised $8.6M. MESH Works has $884M in RFQs running through their platform. AI procurement is not a contrarian category — it's a category where every B2B-focused fund in the world is writing checks right now. The dossier itself cites that AI adoption in procurement is expected to hit 86% by end of 2026. So the first question I always ask — "is the market wrong about this?" — has a pretty clear answer: no. The market is enthusiastically right about AI procurement. Which means whatever upside exists here is already in the price. You're not buying SOL at four cents. You're buying into a category where well-capitalized competitors have a multi-year head start and the narrative tailwinds are consensus.

The industrial machinery vertical focus is the only thing that gives me pause on the mispricing question. Maybe there's a narrow argument that *general* AI procurement is hot but *industrial machinery specifically* is underserved — Johann Sturken's family is in industrial goods trading, he's got the mechanical engineering credentials from RWTH and TUM, the McKinsey aerospace work. That's real domain knowledge. And EUR 1M+ in beta transaction volume from a two-person team is not nothing in a category where average order values are presumably high. But vertical specificity within a hot horizontal category is not the same as a contrarian bet. It's just segmentation. Xometry does $686M in revenue and owns Thomasnet. If they decide off-the-shelf industrial goods sourcing is worth pursuing, they have the supplier relationships and the distribution to build this in a quarter.

The AI kill test is where this really falls apart for me. The core product is LLM-based RFQ generation, supplier matching, and quote comparison. The dossier says it plainly: "LLM-based RFQ generation and supplier matching are reproducible." This is exactly the kind of product I'm talking about when I say AI is sustaining technology for incumbents. SAP Ariba has procurement workflows and supplier networks. Coupa has procurement workflows and supplier networks. Even Alibaba has an industrial sourcing play. Any of these companies can bolt on an AI agent layer to automate RFQ generation tomorrow. The 500K "verified" supplier database sounds impressive until you ask whether it's proprietary or scraped from public directories — and at pre-seed with two people, I have a strong prior on which one it is. If the data is commodity, the AI is commodity, and the marketplace hasn't hit liquidity yet, what exactly is the moat?

Now, the bull case I'll genuinely engage with: industrial B2B marketplaces, when they work, are incredible businesses. The network effects are real and durable — once you're the place where industrial buyers go to source machinery, switching costs are high because the trust scores, supplier relationships, and quoting history create genuine lock-in. Think about what Xometry built in custom manufacturing. If Andustry could become the Xometry of off-the-shelf industrial goods — a marketplace where the network itself is the product, not the AI layer — that's a massive outcome in an $810B market. And Johann's background is almost too perfect for this specific problem: family in the business, engineering degrees, aerospace consulting experience, understands both the technical specifications and the commercial dynamics. If these two execute on marketplace liquidity before the well-funded competitors pivot into their niche, the winner-take-most dynamics of B2B marketplaces would protect them. That's the dream.

But I don't invest in dreams that require you to outrun capital advantages in a consensus category. These are sane founders with a sane idea — the ratio is wrong. I want the sane person telling me something that sounds fucking crazy. "We're going to use AI to make procurement more efficient" doesn't make anyone's eyes go wide. It makes them nod and say "yeah, obviously." The idea needs to be insane for the mispricing to exist, and this idea is deeply sensible. Johann left McKinsey to build an AI procurement platform in 2026 — that's the most predictable career move a smart German mechanical engineer could make right now. There's nothing about this that the market has written off, nothing that makes sophisticated investors uncomfortable, nothing where I'd be picking up a hundred-dollar bill that everyone else walked past. It's a well-executed pitch in a well-understood category. Pass.
