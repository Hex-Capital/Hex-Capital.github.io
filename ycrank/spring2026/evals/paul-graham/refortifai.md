# Refortifai -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Refortifai's YC page says "runtime security for AI agents" — tool-call interception, governance policies, blocking attacks. Refortifai's own website says "anti-cheat for AI models" — obfuscating model weights before distribution, a secured runtime that keeps original weight values off disk and out of RAM. These are not two features of one product. They are two different companies targeting two different buyers solving two different problems. When I see this kind of ambiguity at the pre-seed stage, it tells me the founders haven't yet found the problem that grips them by the throat. The best founding stories start with a frustration so specific the founders can tell you the exact moment they felt it. Refortifai's story starts with what looks like a market opportunity identified from the outside — and then maybe a pivot toward a hotter one.

Atman Kar, Refortifai's CTO, has the most legible background: RTL digital design at Texas Instruments building automotive RADAR accelerators, a CPU engineering internship at Tenstorrent, an MS from NC State. His GitHub tells me something real — a 6502 emulator in C, an operating system for i386 called LameOS, a Radon Transform implementation in Rust. These are the projects of someone who builds things because building things is what they do. That hacker identity matters to me. But here's the problem: Atman's deep expertise is in hardware design and low-level systems, which maps naturally to the model-weight obfuscation product on Refortifai's website — not to the agent runtime security product on Refortifai's YC page. Rithik Jain shifted from Economics and History to completing 21 CS courses at UW-Madison, which shows intellectual range but no visible security domain experience. Sayan Mitra's background is essentially a blank in the dossier. No confirmed cybersecurity expertise appears anywhere in Refortifai's founding team. When the Collison brothers built Stripe, they were developers who had personally suffered through the pain of integrating payments — the schlep was theirs. Refortifai's founders appear to be hardware and systems engineers who chose an AI security problem from outside it.

Microsoft released the Agent Governance Toolkit as free open-source software under MIT license on April 2, 2026 — the same quarter Refortifai is in YC. This toolkit provides runtime policy enforcement across Python, TypeScript, Rust, Go, and .NET with sub-0.1ms latency and integrations into LangChain, CrewAI, and Google ADK. It covers all ten OWASP agentic AI risks. Refortifai's YC-page product sits in the direct path of this release. Noma Security has raised $132M and reports 1,300% ARR growth. Capsule Security raised a $7M seed in April 2026 with an open-source component. PromptArmor, a YC W24 company, already has $3M and backing from Accel and Lightspeed. Refortifai is entering a market where nobody's first reaction is "that will never work." Everyone agrees AI agent security is important and necessary. That universal agreement is exactly what makes me nervous — when the consensus is that an idea is good, the returns have already been competed away, or worse, a well-resourced incumbent gives the solution away for free.

The strongest bull case for Refortifai goes something like this: the agent security market is genuinely nascent with a 50.4% CAGR projected to 2033, Atman Kar's low-level hardware expertise could enable uniquely performant runtime instrumentation that software-only competitors can't match, the model-weight obfuscation product could represent genuine IP if it achieves full inference speed without homomorphic encryption overhead, and Nicolas Dessaigne — who built Algolia from developer tool to infrastructure company — is their YC partner. Getting into YC at all is a filter. I'll grant all of that. But none of it addresses my core concern: I see no evidence that Refortifai's founders have the specific relationship to this problem that separates organic discovery from market opportunity selection. There are no stories of resourcefulness under constraint — no cereal boxes, no weekend prototypes, no users obsessively using a crappy v1. The dossier is almost entirely absent of the signals I weight highest. I checked my Known Exceptions: Boom Supersonic overrode my usual criteria on "frighteningly ambitious" conviction, Watsi on pure earnestness, ClassDojo on extreme schlep blindness. Refortifai doesn't trigger any of these overrides — the ambition scale is standard for a security startup, the earnestness signal is limited to Atman's hobby projects, and agent runtime security is the opposite of a schlep that repels founders (it's attracting them in droves this quarter).

The condition that would flip me to Invest: if Sayan Mitra turned out to have deep offensive security or threat research experience — a background in red-teaming AI systems, published vulnerability disclosures, or years at a security company where they personally discovered the agent tool-call attack surface — that would change Refortifai's story from "market opportunity" to "organic discovery." Alternatively, if Refortifai had even five enterprise customers using an early version despite its rough edges, that would demonstrate the "small number of people want a large amount" signal. Right now neither exists in the public record. Refortifai has a hacker on the team (Atman) building in a hot market without visible domain expertise, visible traction, or a settled product direction, against a free Microsoft open-source alternative and $170M+ in funded competitors. The two dimensions that lock this verdict are Organic Problem Discovery — where the pivot from model-weight obfuscation to agent security actively suggests idea-shopping rather than lived frustration — and Idea That Repels Before It Attracts, where the market consensus that AI security is critical eliminates exactly the return premium I'm looking for.
