# InLoop Robotics -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The idea here — deploying robots that openly admit they don't know what they're doing and ask a human for help — is exactly the kind of thing that makes other robotics founders cringe. Every well-funded competitor in this space is racing toward full autonomy, because that's the story that raises $200M. InLoop is saying the opposite: we'll ship the imperfect thing, route failures to a teleoperator, and turn every mistake into training data. That's architecturally interesting. It's the robotics equivalent of "do things that don't scale" — the teleoperator is the manual, unscalable work that seeds the data flywheel. The approach sits in that Thiel Venn diagram overlap: it sounds like an admission of weakness ("your robots need help?"), but if the learning loop actually works, they'll converge on autonomy faster than companies training exclusively in simulation. I like the shape of this idea.

But when I try to evaluate the founders, I hit fog. Two of the three — Rizali and Sharfeddine — met at RoboTUM in Munich, which Rizali co-founded as Germany's largest student robotics initiative. That's a real organizational feat. Sharfeddine is the strongest signal: BMW and Bosch on his resume, a BEHAVIOR Challenge win in 2025, and GitHub repos that show he's working directly with the frontier manipulation frameworks — he forked Physical Intelligence's openpi, he built teleoperation tools in Isaac Sim. This is someone who understands where the boundary of robot capability actually sits, which matters because the whole human-in-the-loop architecture depends on accurately knowing when the AI doesn't know. But the third founder, Feduniak, is a blank page. No retrievable education, no work history, no public repos. The most I found is a FIDE chess profile for someone born in 2007, which would make him eighteen. I've bet on teenagers before — the Bindwell founders were 18 and 19 — but there I could see what they'd built and what they'd pivoted away from. Here I see nothing, and that gap creates genuine diligence discomfort.

What I'm missing are the stories. When I interview founders, I'm listening for specific moments — the creative hack that got them past a wall, the weekend they spent doing something insane to acquire their first user, the time they ran out of money and figured it out anyway. The dossier gives me "multiple robots deployed" with "zero breakage," which at pre-seed is genuinely impressive execution — most robotics startups at this stage are still demo-ing in controlled labs. Getting hardware into actual warehouses is hard, and it tells me someone on this team can ship. But I don't know the story behind it. Did they bootstrap the first robot from spare parts? Did they personally drive it to the customer site and set it up themselves? Or did they have a smooth path through YC with standard funding? The absence of these stories isn't proof of their absence in reality, but truly resourceful founders generate notable anecdotes almost involuntarily. I'd need to sit with them for ten minutes to know.

The bull case is real and I want to be honest about it. If the data flywheel works — if every teleoperator intervention genuinely improves the shared model at a rate that compounds with each new deployment — then InLoop has stumbled onto something with superlinear returns baked in. More customers means more edge cases means better AI means fewer interventions means better unit economics means more customers. That's the virtuous cycle. And the RaaS model with zero CapEx is the right key for the lock that incumbents like Amazon Robotics and Symbotic aren't designed to open — the small-to-mid warehouse that can't write a $500K check for fixed automation. Nimble and Dexterity are chasing FedEx and UPS; InLoop could own the long tail. If Sharfeddine's technical depth is as real as his GitHub suggests, and if these founders have the determination I can't yet see evidence of, this could be the warehouse robotics version of what Stripe did to payments — making the hard thing simple for the segment everyone else ignored.

But I keep coming back to the competitive landscape, which is genuinely brutal. Nimble has $221M and a billion-dollar valuation. Dexterity has $291M and customers moving real freight. Pickle Robot has $87M and 30+ units ordered. These aren't paper competitors — they're deploying real robots into real warehouses with real revenue. The human-in-the-loop concept is elegant but not patentable. Any of these companies could add a teleoperator fallback in a quarter if InLoop proves the approach works. The moat has to be the accumulated deployment data, and that moat takes years to build while your competitors can buy time with capital. Hardware companies that need to outrun well-funded incumbents need either an unfair distribution advantage or an unfair technical advantage, and I don't see either yet. What I see is smart students who understand the technology building in a space where the incumbents also understand the technology and have a hundred times more money.

The problem discovery also worries me. These founders are roboticists who noticed warehouses need robots — not warehouse operators who realized robotics could solve their pain. Rizali's LinkedIn headline still says "Aspiring Robotics Engineer" with a "Passion for Innovation." That's student language, not founder language. The path from "we're good at building robots" to "warehouses need robots" is market analysis, not lived frustration. Compare this to Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive, or the Octopart founders searching for electronic parts in their physics lab. Where's the moment of personal pain that makes this team uniquely suited to this problem? I don't see it, and that matters because the deepest understanding of what warehouse operators actually need comes from having been one, not from having studied them.
