# Alt-X -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Let me be direct about what I see here: Alt-X is building a tool that helps private equity analysts copy numbers from PDFs into Excel 80% faster. If they succeed -- completely, wildly, beyond anyone's expectations -- the world has slightly more efficient spreadsheet jockeys. Private markets still function identically. The same people make the same decisions through the same structures. The deals still get done. The capital still flows through the same channels. Nobody's life changes. No industry gets displaced. The consequence of success is, in my framework, inconsequential. I look at this and think: you are two smart people who could be working on something that matters, and instead you're optimizing a workflow.

The $30/month price point tells me everything I need to know about how the founders themselves have calibrated their ambition. Compare this to when I backed Square -- Jack Dorsey wasn't building a marginally better cash register. He was making the existing payment infrastructure structurally obsolete. Every small business that couldn't accept cards could suddenly accept cards. The system changed. Alt-X's explicit design principle -- stay inside Excel, remain "fully editable and auditable," never threaten the existing workflow -- is the opposite of disruption. They've architected their product to be maximally non-threatening to incumbent processes. That's great for adoption probability. It's terrible for consequence magnitude. They've reduced the probability of failure to the point where success means a nice SaaS business, and that's it.

Ryan Samadi is a Citadel analyst, BofA trading desk, PE analyst, Stanford finance. He is the industry itself. He knows exactly how the current system works, which means he'll optimize it rather than reimagine it. My best investments come from people who don't know what an industry considers impossible -- Pat Brown didn't know the food industry's constraints, Peter Beck didn't know aerospace's rules, Pradeep Sindhu came from Xerox PARC, not from Cisco. Samadi is building precisely what his former employers would have asked for: a faster version of their existing process. There's no contrarian thesis here, no willingness to attempt something the establishment dismisses as crazy. The establishment would love this product. That's the problem.

The competitive landscape makes this even harder to justify. Endex has $14M and backing from the OpenAI Startup Fund. Shortcut has $78M and Sequoia. Datarails has $175M and is approaching $100M in revenue. Alt-X is a two-person team entering a space where well-capitalized competitors already exist. When I backed Juniper against Cisco, there was no well-funded competitor building TCP/IP routers -- Cisco's own CTO said they'd never do it. Here, the opposite is true: multiple well-funded teams are already building Excel-native AI agents, and any of them can add alternative-asset-specific templates in a quarter. The vertical specialization in PE and RE underwriting is a positioning choice, not a technology moat. Foundation models for document extraction are broadly available. The core capability is commoditizing in real time.

The bull case would require believing that Alt-X's vertical focus on alternative assets creates a compounding data advantage that horizontal competitors cannot replicate -- that deal-specific KPI extraction for cap rates, NOI, IRR, and MOIC builds a flywheel of training data that becomes insurmountable. And that the $30/month wedge expands into six-figure enterprise contracts as the tool becomes essential infrastructure for IC decision-making at major funds. If private market AUM continues growing while analyst headcount stays flat, the demand is real. Datarails' trajectory to $100M revenue validates that Excel-native AI can build a significant business. But even this best case produces a Datarails-scale outcome -- a $500M-$1B company serving a workflow niche. That's a fine venture outcome for someone else's portfolio. It's not the kind of bet where the consequence of success justifies my attention. I don't invest in faster spreadsheets. I invest in problems too important not to work on.
