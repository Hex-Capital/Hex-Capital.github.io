# Chamber -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I keep coming back to with Chamber: this is a company that's built to be popular. GPU utilization is low, enterprises are bleeding money, NVIDIA just acquired the leading independent player for $700 million and is open-sourcing it — everyone in the room can see the gap. And the founders are four Amazon infrastructure engineers who built exactly this kind of optimization system at Amazon. It's clean, it's legible, and every B2B SaaS investor I know would nod along to this pitch. That's precisely why it doesn't activate anything for me. The most promising companies I've backed have been the ones that make people uncomfortable or confused. SpaceX made people think Elon was delusional. Crusoe Energy made people ask "wait, you want to mine Bitcoin on gas flares?" Chamber makes people say "oh, that makes sense" — and in my experience, that's a different kind of company entirely.

When I sit with a founder, I want to hear how they got here — not the business, the person. And what I see in this dossier is four talented engineers who identified a problem at their employer, recognized a market window, and assembled a well-credentialed team to go after it. Charles Ding is a second-time founder with an exit, an MBA from Booth, stints at Meta, Amazon, and Microsoft. That's an impressive resume. But a resume isn't a story. There's no biographical thread connecting these founders to GPU waste in a way that goes deeper than professional observation. Jason Ong's immigration from Malaysia to the U.S. — that's the kind of detail I'd want to pull on in a conversation, because sometimes there's something underneath that explains everything. But based on what's here, the founding narrative is "we built this at Amazon and we're building it again as a startup." That's rational, not obsessive. And at the earliest stages, I'm looking for obsession rooted in something personal.

The bull case is genuinely strong, and I want to be honest about that. Run:ai's $700M acquisition validates the category at a price point that makes this a meaningful market. NVIDIA open-sourcing Run:ai commoditizes the incumbent while tying its future development to NVIDIA's hardware stack — a structural gift to any vendor-neutral alternative. The team's domain expertise is not generic: Jason Ong specifically worked on GPU-related scheduling tooling at Amazon, Shaocheng Wang spent nine-plus years building AWS observability products, and the team collectively claims hundreds of millions in cost savings at Amazon. If you believe that the right team in a validated market with a clear structural opening is the formula, then Chamber is a textbook execution bet. The free monitoring tier as a PLG wedge into enterprise orchestration contracts is a clean go-to-market. A competent investor could make a strong case for this company.

But I'm not that investor. When I look at Chamber through my lens, I see enterprise cost optimization for companies that are already resource-rich enough to operate GPU clusters. There's no economic access story here — no driver getting income through Uber, no tradesperson finding customers through Thumbtack. The people who benefit from Chamber are platform engineering teams at companies spending millions on compute infrastructure. That's a valid customer, but it's not the kind of impact that gets me out of bed. And the "agentic AI" positioning — I've seen enough cycles to know that when your primary differentiator is the industry's hottest buzzword, that differentiator has a half-life measured in months. Kueue and Volcano will add ML-based scheduling. Cloud providers will build native tools. Well-funded startups will bolt on autonomous features. The moat here is really about speed of execution and data accumulation from production deployments, which is real but not the kind of defensibility that keeps me up at night with excitement.

The co-founder dynamic is the one dimension where I feel genuinely good. Four former Amazon colleagues who built infrastructure together under pressure — that's a stress-tested working relationship. It's not quite a family business, which is my gold standard for conflict resolution, but it's materially better than co-founders who met at a conference and decided to start a company. Having navigated Amazon's internal politics and performance culture together, they likely have established patterns for disagreement and decision-making. That matters more than most investors realize.

Chamber is a well-assembled team attacking a validated opportunity with relevant domain expertise. In the hands of a different investor — someone who optimizes for team-market fit and execution probability in large addressable markets — this could be a strong bet. But I invest my own money based on whether I feel that jolt, whether the founder's story and the company's mission are inseparable, whether the idea is strange enough that most of the room has already said no. I don't feel it here. This is a consensus play in a hot market, and the best version of my portfolio has always been built on the opposite.
