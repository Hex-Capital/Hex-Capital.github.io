# 9 Mothers -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about 9 Mothers is the schlep. Building an autonomous counter-drone weapon system means dealing with ITAR export controls, DoD procurement cycles that stretch years, acoustic signal processing, real-time AI inference on edge hardware, hardware manufacturing, and a policy environment where the Department of Defense has an entire directive (3000.09) governing when autonomous weapons can pull a trigger. That's not one schlep -- it's five simultaneous schleps stacked on top of each other. Most founders who read about the Ukraine drone revolution and thought "I should build something for this" would unconsciously stop at the first conversation with a defense procurement officer. The fact that these founders are actually building it -- 14 job postings for senior engineers in acoustic ML, DSP, robotics, and electrical engineering -- tells me they've at least walked into the schlep with their eyes open.

The question I can't fully answer is how they found this problem. Roman Khomenko holds a master's from Kharkiv National University of Radioelectronics and served in the Police of Ukraine. Kharkiv has been under sustained drone and missile attack since 2022. If Khomenko experienced the drone warfare threat firsthand, that's one of the most powerful organic discovery stories I've seen -- a Ukrainian AI/ML engineer who watched his city get attacked by the exact weapons his company now builds defenses against. But the dossier doesn't confirm this directly. Russell Smith's background is British, web-based QA tools, Ruby on Rails. His path from Rainforest QA to autonomous weapons is a big leap. Without hearing the founders tell me how they landed on this problem, I'm working with a suggestive signal rather than a confirmed one. The difference matters. An idea born from watching drones destroy your city is fundamentally different from an idea born from reading about drones destroying cities.

What works is the team composition. Smith and Khomenko worked together for eight years at Rainforest QA -- Smith as co-founder and CTO, Khomenko rising from data scientist to lead AI. That's exactly the kind of co-founder relationship that survives the stress of a hard startup. When I've seen co-founders who met recently just to start a company, it almost always falls apart under pressure. Eight years of working together is real. Smith is also a second-time YC founder, which means he knows what matters in the early stages and won't waste time on things that don't. Khomenko's production ML pipeline experience and computer vision work at Rainforest translate more directly than you might think -- detection and classification of drone signatures isn't that far from automated visual QA. Pyzh adds cybersecurity from Leviathan Security Group, which is relevant for defense systems but is the weakest link in terms of builder credentials.

The bull case here is genuinely interesting. If the acoustic-AI approach works as a passive detection modality, it could undercut radar-based systems on cost, power, and detectability simultaneously. Defense primes sell $10M+ integrated platforms because that's what their business model demands -- they can't cannibalize those margins to compete at a price point a pre-seed startup would target. Anduril just won a $20B Army framework contract, which means the DoD has committed to buying counter-drone systems at scale. That contract validates the market and potentially creates subcontracting or complementary procurement opportunities. A small, portable, autonomous system that costs a fraction of Anduril's Sentry Tower could slot into the tactical edge where big platforms don't fit. The timing -- Ukraine proving the threat, DoD budgets expanding, edge AI hardware getting cheaper -- is genuinely good. If 9 Mothers can get a working prototype in front of a military unit and it detects and neutralizes a Group 1 drone, the procurement machine will find a way to buy it.

But I keep coming back to two problems. First, there's zero evidence of user pull. No field tests, no LOIs, no named military contacts, no revenue, no prototype demonstrations. At pre-seed I don't expect much, but for a defense company, even a single connection to a program manager or a scheduled demonstration at a military base would be meaningful signal. The 14 job postings and investor backing tell me someone believes in the team, but investors believing in a team is not the same as a soldier pulling the product toward them. Second, the competitive landscape is genuinely terrifying. Anduril at $60B, Epirus at $550M+, DroneShield doing A$216.5M in annual revenue. These aren't hypothetical competitors -- they have deployed systems, established government relationships, and the capital to iterate fast. When I invested in Boom, the ambition magnitude was so extreme that competition was structurally limited. Counter-drone systems are the opposite: every defense company on the planet is chasing this market right now.

The founders are technical and they clearly build things -- the Rust UDP proxy on GitHub, Khomenko's Kaggle background, Smith's decade as a startup CTO. But their domain expertise is web infrastructure and ML, not defense hardware. The 14 job postings spanning acoustic engineering, robotics, DSP, and electrical engineering reveal exactly what's missing from the founding team. They're hiring the domain knowledge rather than bringing it. This isn't fatal -- plenty of great companies are built by smart generalists who hire specialists -- but at pre-seed with autonomous weapon systems, the gap between "we can write ML models" and "we can build hardware that works in a contested electromagnetic environment" is enormous.

I respect the schlep and the timing. The co-founder relationship is strong. Khomenko's Ukrainian background is a potentially powerful organic signal. But too much of the thesis rests on inference rather than evidence. I'd want to hear the founders tell me how they found this problem, see a working prototype, and understand their first path into DoD procurement before writing a check.
