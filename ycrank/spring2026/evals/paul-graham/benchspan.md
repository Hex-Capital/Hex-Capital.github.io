# Benchspan -- Paul Graham Primary Source Research: Structured Quote Record Evaluation

The most interesting thing about Benchspan is a specific line on Avi Arora's resume: ML Engineer II at Microsoft, building infrastructure for scalable agentic evaluations and agent benchmarks on GitHub Copilot. "The verb you want to be using with respect to startup ideas is not 'think up' but 'notice.'" Avi didn't think up the agent benchmarking problem. He lived inside it. He watched engineering teams at one of the world's largest AI organizations struggle with the exact plumbing Benchspan now proposes to sell. That's the right origin story. And the pivot from Oddpool isn't as jarring as it first appears — PredictionMarketBench was literally "a SWE-bench-style framework" for backtesting trading agents. They were already building benchmark infrastructure; they just realized the general version was more valuable than the vertical one. The running back found a better lane.

But here's where I get stuck. "The best ideas look initially like bad ideas." Agent benchmarking infrastructure doesn't look like a bad idea. It looks like an obviously useful tool that a well-funded platform company adds as a feature on a Thursday afternoon. Braintrust has $121M. LangChain has $260M. Galileo has $68M and 834% revenue growth. These companies already do evaluation and observability. Adding parallel benchmark execution to their existing platforms is not a moonshot engineering challenge — it's a product increment. When I think about Stripe, the schlep that kept everyone away was regulatory complexity, financial infrastructure, compliance nightmares. Years of work that nobody wanted to do. Docker containers running in parallel is a schlep, sure, but it's a solvable-in-weeks schlep, not a solvable-in-years schlep. The depth of the moat has to match the proximity of the incumbents, and here the incumbents are very close and the moat is shallow.

I want to give these founders credit where it's due. Avi's domain fit is genuine — Copilot agent evals, Prompt Shields processing 20 billion classifications a year, ML research at Georgia Tech. Ritesh architected systems handling 100K+ TPS at Bloomberg. They complement each other well, and they share a Georgia Tech CS background that likely means the relationship is real, not a co-founder dating exercise. The "formidable" part is there — these are serious technical people. But I'm missing the "earnest" signal. Where's the cockroach moment? The Airbnbs sold cereal boxes to fund themselves. The Collisons grabbed your laptop and installed Stripe on the spot. Zero public traction for Benchspan — no users, no revenue, no community, no Product Hunt launch, no Twitter presence. The product page exists and has a cal.com booking link. At pre-seed I don't penalize the absence of scale, but I need *some* evidence of obsessive early user engagement, and I can't find any.

The bull case is real and I want to articulate it clearly. If you believe that every AI engineering team in the world will need to run agent benchmarks as routinely as they run unit tests — and that the benchmark landscape will keep fragmenting rather than consolidating — then Benchspan is building CI/CD for agent evals, and the first company to own that layer captures an enormous wedge of the agent tooling stack. "The way to do really big things seems to be to start with deceptively small things." Benchmark execution is the small thing; the big thing is becoming the infrastructure of record for how companies measure AI agent quality. If the team can move fast enough to build catalog breadth and accumulate result history before incumbents wake up, the switching costs could compound. And Avi's Copilot pedigree gives them credibility with exactly the buyer persona they need to reach. If I squint, I can see this as Datadog for agent benchmarks — observability infrastructure that starts narrow and expands.

But I don't invest on squinting. The commoditization risk is too present. SWE-bench ships its own open-source harness. The UK AISI's Inspect framework exists. Individual benchmarks increasingly include execution scaffolds. The execution layer Benchspan provides — Docker containers, parallelism, partial re-runs — is engineering work, not intellectual property. "It's exceptionally rare for startups to be killed by competitors," I've written, and I believe that. But I was talking about startups in markets where the product-market fit is so strong that users pull the product forward. Here I see no pull at all. A mid-batch pivot, a website, and a booking link. I need to see the founders making Benchspan take off by force of will — going out and getting users, doing things that don't scale. Right now I'm evaluating credentials, not traction, and credentials alone have never been what I select for. "Good people can fix bad ideas, but good ideas can't save bad people." These are good people. I'm just not yet convinced this is more than a feature dressed as a company, and I haven't seen the determination signals that would override that concern.
