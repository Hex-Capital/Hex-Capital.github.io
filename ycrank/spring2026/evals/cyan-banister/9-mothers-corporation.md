# 9 Mothers -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Roman Khomenko earned his MSc at Kharkiv National University of Radioelectronics, lectured there in cybersecurity, and served in the Police of Ukraine — and Kharkiv has been under relentless drone bombardment since 2022, with the exact cheap FPV suicide drones that 9 Mothers' EDDA turret is designed to kill. I don't need to ask Roman "tell me your story" to feel the biographical weight here. When someone's hometown is being shattered by the weapon their company was built to defeat, that's not a market opportunity — that's a wound that won't close until the problem is solved. His technical career reads like a blueprint drawn by fate: eight years building computer vision and ML pipelines at Rainforest QA, a first-place Kaggle finish in image classification, production experience with PyTorch and YOLO — the exact stack you need for real-time autonomous drone tracking. Roman didn't pivot to defense; defense came to find him because his entire life built the skillset this moment demands.

Russell Smith co-founded Rainforest QA through YC S12 and scaled it to $25.7M in revenue over roughly a decade, raising $41.2M along the way. That's not a founder who flinches at long timelines — and defense hardware companies live and die by their ability to survive multi-year procurement cycles before a dollar of contract revenue arrives. Smith's embedded systems work in Rust and ESP32 signals he's been migrating toward hardware even before 9 Mothers, and his GitHub shows 212 repos and real engineering depth. The Smith-Khomenko working relationship spans approximately eight years at Rainforest QA. I've seen founding teams assembled for market convenience disintegrate under the first real pressure — but 9 Mothers' core technical duo has already weathered a decade of building together, which is rarer than people appreciate. Bogdan Pyzh, the third co-founder and COO, brings cybersecurity competition credentials from Baylor's team that advanced to nationals in 2018. Clear role delineation — CEO, CTO, COO — avoids the 50/50 co-founder trap that destroyed HQ Trivia despite having the right product at the right time.

9 Mothers builds autonomous AI-powered weapon turrets — the words alone make people shift in their chairs, and that's a signal I've learned to pay attention to. When I backed Anduril, defense tech was politically toxic in Silicon Valley; Palmer Luckey was persona non grata. 9 Mothers occupies an even more uncomfortable position: not just defense tech, but autonomous kinetic engagement systems that fall under DoD Directive 3000.09's evolving policy on lethal autonomy. The counter-UAS space itself, though, is becoming consensus — Anduril's $20B Army contract, Epirus pulling in $550M+, the DoD's $1.86B in committed counter-drone spending — which means 9 Mothers gets the tailwind of institutional demand without the purity of being truly pre-consensus. The contrarian edge isn't the *market* (everyone agrees drones are a threat); it's the *approach* — a tiny startup building cheap, portable, kinetic AI turrets while the primes build million-dollar integrated systems. That specific bet, that a $500 FPV drone should be defeated by something closer to its own cost rather than by a system that costs 1,000x more, is the kind of unit economics argument that large defense contractors structurally cannot make because it cannibalizes their business model.

Anduril at $60B valuation and ~$2B revenue is the elephant that 9 Mothers must navigate around, not compete against. Fortem Technologies ($69M raised, $28.5M revenue) and Epirus ($550M+) crowd the space further. 9 Mothers' defensibility argument rests on form factor and cost — EDDA targets the small, portable, man-carry or vehicle-mount segment that sits below what Anduril's Lattice platform optimizes for. ITAR compliance and security clearance requirements create barriers to entry but also create barriers for 9 Mothers itself: two of three founders have international backgrounds, and clearance timelines for Ukrainian nationals in the current geopolitical environment could stretch unpredictably. The technical integration challenge is real — fusing acoustic ML, computer vision, target tracking, and weapons control at the edge in a small form factor — but it's replicable by well-funded competitors with robotics expertise. 9 Mothers' moat, if it develops, will come from deployment data and iteration speed, not from any current structural lock-in. The inflection point is genuine: Ukraine proved cheap drones reshape battlefields, edge AI compute has matured enough to run inference in small packages, and DoD procurement is moving faster than historical norms on C-UAS. 9 Mothers is building at the right moment, but the window is crowded.

The invisible system I look for isn't as clean here as it was with Affirm attacking credit card opacity or Ownwell attacking property tax extraction. But there is a structural access problem buried inside the defense industrial base: soldiers and forward-deployed units face a lethal asymmetric threat from $500 drones while the procurement system delivers expensive, complex, slow-to-deploy systems designed for a different era. 9 Mothers' EDDA — if it delivers on the promise of a small, cheap, autonomous point-defense turret — would make protection accessible to the units that currently can't get it through traditional procurement channels. That's not transparency in the Affirm sense, but it's democratization of protection in a way that connects to what I care about: expanding access to something that's currently gatekept by cost and institutional structure. The cascading impact is literal survival — soldiers and potentially civilians at events like FIFA 2026 (which already has $115M in DHS counter-drone funding allocated) gaining protection against a threat that's growing exponentially.

The strongest bull case for 9 Mothers: a proven founding team with 8+ years of collaboration history, led by a founder who scaled a prior YC company to $25.7M revenue, building alongside a Ukrainian ML expert whose hometown IS the battlefield that proved this technology is existentially necessary, entering a market with $20B+ in validated DoD demand, at precisely the moment when edge AI makes their product technically feasible. The bear case is equally real: Anduril could build a small turret product in a quarter with their existing Lattice platform and $4B in fresh capital; the clearance issues for international founders could delay government contracts by years; the autonomous weapons policy landscape under DoD Directive 3000.09 could restrict the fully autonomous capability that differentiates EDDA; and no evidence of any DoD engagement, LOI, or field testing exists in the public record. What would flip me to Pass: if Anduril announces a small-form-factor C-UAS turret product, or if clearance issues force a founder restructuring. What keeps me in: the Khomenko biographical thread is the kind of founder-problem inevitability I've built my career betting on, the team has a proven decade of building together, and the low-cost portable segment is structurally unattractive for the primes to chase aggressively.

9 Mothers earns an Invest from me, driven primarily by Khomenko's biographical forge — a Kharkiv-trained ML expert with police service and Kaggle-champion-level CV skills building counter-drone weapons while his hometown burns under the exact threat he's solving — and by the team's 8-year working relationship and Smith's demonstrated ability to build a company through long timelines. The contrarian conviction score is real but tempered by the fact that C-UAS is becoming consensus even if autonomous kinetic turrets remain uncomfortable. The competitive risk from Anduril and funded peers is the primary drag, and the absence of any public traction with DoD customers means I'm betting on the founders' ability to navigate a brutally long procurement cycle on pre-seed capital. This is a Long Journey bet in the most literal sense: I'd write the check and treat it like I threw the money away, the same way I treated SpaceX, knowing the founders have the forge to survive the valley if the thesis holds.
