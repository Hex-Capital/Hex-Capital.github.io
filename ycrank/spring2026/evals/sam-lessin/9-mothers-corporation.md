# 9 Mothers -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Anduril just raised $4 billion at a $60 billion valuation and locked down a $20 billion Army framework contract. Epirus has pulled in $550 million-plus. Fortem just took $25 million from Lockheed Martin. Counter-UAS is not a category the market has dismissed -- it's a category the market is throwing money at with both hands. So when I look at 9 Mothers building an "AI-powered C-sUAS point defense turret," my first question isn't "is this a good product?" It's "where's the mispricing?" And I'm struggling to find it. Defense tech in Spring 2026 is one of the hottest categories in venture. YC is backing it with Garry Tan personally partnering. DoD has committed $1.86 billion-plus in counter-drone spending. Every sophisticated investor I know has a defense thesis right now. 9 Mothers is building in a space where the upside is already in the price -- the opposite of buying Solana at four cents when everyone said Ethereum won.

Russell Smith scaling Rainforest QA to $25.7 million in revenue over a decade, Roman Khomenko winning first place in iMaterialist on Kaggle and running ML pipelines for eight years alongside Smith -- these are serious, competent people. The eight-year working relationship between Smith and Khomenko at Rainforest QA is exactly the kind of repeat-founder signal I pay attention to. When I backed Raj on Solana, it was because I'd watched him through three companies. 9 Mothers has that same DNA: a CEO who's operated at scale and a CTO with directly applicable computer vision and ML chops who's been in the trenches with him for nearly a decade. Bogdan Pyzh's cybersecurity background from Baylor rounds it out. The "sane" side of the equation is strong. But here's the problem: is building a counter-drone turret in 2026 actually insane? It would have been in 2019. In 2026, after Ukraine proved FPV drones change warfare, after the DoD made C-UAS a top-three priority, this is a rational idea pursued by rational people. Sane person, sane idea -- that's just boring.

9 Mothers' EDDA turret is a smaller, cheaper, more portable counter-drone system than what Anduril or Raytheon builds. That's a real product insight -- military units do need point defense that doesn't require a prime contractor installation. But "smaller and cheaper version of existing defense capability" is not a new primitive. Venmo created peer-to-peer mobile payments as a behavior that didn't exist. Solana created a new consensus mechanism. 9 Mothers is creating... a better-form-factor C-UAS system. It's a "better X" competing in an existing market, not a new verb. The kinetic-defeat approach via turret-mounted shotgun versus Fortem's net-capture drone or Epirus' directed energy or D-Fend's RF takeover -- these are engineering trade-offs within a defined category, not category creation. Nobody's going to say "we got 9 Mothers'd" the way people say "Venmo me." 9 Mothers competes on execution and unit economics, which is fine but caps the upside to market-share capture rather than market creation.

Metropolis is the portfolio anchor here -- AI-powered physical infrastructure where the hardware creates a moat software can't replicate. 9 Mothers' turrets have that same quality: you can't replace a mounted kinetic defense system with an API call. The AI kill test works in their favor. Adobe can't add a feature that shoots down drones. ITAR compliance and security clearances create genuine regulatory barriers to entry. The physical-world moat is real, and this is where 9 Mothers scores highest in my framework. But Metropolis had geographic lock-in -- once you manage a parking garage's infrastructure, switching costs are enormous. 9 Mothers' turrets are hardware units that compete on specs and price against Anduril's manufacturing capacity and Lockheed Martin-backed Fortem. Physical doesn't automatically mean defensible if a $60 billion competitor can manufacture the same form factor with better government relationships and production infrastructure.

The bull case someone would make: Smith is a proven YC founder who built and operated a $25.7 million revenue company, Khomenko has world-class ML credentials directly applicable to autonomous targeting, they've worked together for eight years, and the low-cost portable C-UAS segment is genuinely underserved by the big players who optimize for large integrated systems. Incumbents face business model cannibalization building a cheap turret when they sell expensive platforms. ITAR barriers protect the moat once established. The market is $6.64 billion growing to $20 billion by 2030 at 25% CAGR. My Slack exception shows I'll pay consensus prices when a genuine primitive is being created. But that exception required a new behavioral primitive -- real-time workplace messaging replacing email -- and 9 Mothers isn't creating a new behavior. It's a hardware product in a defined procurement category. The bull case is "great team in a big growing market," which is a perfectly fine thesis for somebody, but it's not mine. What would flip me: if 9 Mothers demonstrated that autonomous point-defense turrets unlock a deployment pattern that's categorically new -- every infantry squad, every civilian facility, every vehicle as a defended node in a mesh network, creating a new "distributed autonomous defense" primitive that didn't exist before -- and if the pricing reflected contrarian skepticism rather than consensus enthusiasm. Show me that this is being dismissed by other defense VCs as too small or too weird, and show me the primitive, and I'll reconsider.

9 Mothers' 14 open positions at $150K-$250K salary ranges signal aggressive hiring for a pre-seed hardware company facing multi-year DoD procurement cycles. Hardware is inherently capital-intensive, defense sales cycles are long, and two of three founders have international backgrounds that may complicate ITAR clearance timelines -- a risk with no mitigation data in the dossier. Capital discipline is hard to assess at pre-seed, but the combination of hardware burn rate, defense procurement timelines, and ambitious hiring suggests 9 Mothers will need several more rounds to reach meaningful revenue, which is the factory-farm conveyor belt I think is dying. The two dimensions that lock my verdict: Consensus Mispricing scores low because defense C-UAS is consensus-hot with $60 billion Anduril and $1.86 billion DoD spending signaling the upside is priced in, and Primitive Creation scores low because 9 Mothers builds a better form factor in an existing category rather than creating a new behavioral or infrastructure primitive. The strong founder signal and physical-world durability aren't enough to overcome a category where I see no asymmetric risk/reward.
