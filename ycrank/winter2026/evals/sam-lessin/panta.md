# Panta -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the thing that jumps out at me immediately: Harper raised $47 million in February 2026 doing essentially the same thing. An AI-native licensed brokerage. Multiple YC companies -- Fernstone, Acolite -- are attacking adjacent problems. Newfront got acquired by WTW for $1.3 billion. When I look at the commercial insurance AI space right now, I see a category that every VC in San Francisco has already marked as "inevitable." And when something looks inevitable to everyone, the returns are already priced in. This is not a sane person with an insane idea. This is a sane person with a very, very sane idea. That's the worst category in my framework -- you're buying consensus at market price, and the execution risk is enormous because you're racing well-capitalized competitors who reached the same conclusion twelve months earlier.

The founders are legitimately strong -- I want to be clear about that. Vincent Chen shipped agentic AI to billions of users at Google through NotebookLM and Ask Photos. Frank Wang was a senior full-stack engineer at Apple with real open-source credibility in Rust and Gleam. And critically, both obtained commercial insurance broker licenses, which signals genuine domain immersion rather than tourist behavior. These are the kind of builders I respect. But credibility without contrarianism just means you're a well-qualified entrant to a crowded race. The "sane" half of my equation is satisfied; the "insane" half is completely absent. When your closest competitor has $47M, 5,000+ businesses, and a 12-month head start, being technically excellent is table stakes, not an edge.

Now, I'll give Panta credit where my framework actually likes what I see: this is NOT a SaaS company. It's a licensed brokerage earning commissions -- a service business where AI provides operational leverage rather than being the product you sell. That's structurally closer to my Craftwork thesis (painting company with a tech layer) than it is to the AI wrapper companies I pass on reflexively. The 50-state licensing is a genuine regulatory barrier. The carrier appetite data that accumulates with each placement creates a compounding information advantage. And the E&S focus -- construction, hazmat trucking, oil and gas -- targets the gnarliest, most complex risks where traditional brokers are slowest and most expensive, and where automation is genuinely difficult. These are real structural advantages. But here's where my "cherry on top" test cuts against Panta: would this business be good if AI didn't exist? The answer is clearly no. Without the autonomous agents, Panta is just another small brokerage competing against Marsh and Aon with no differentiation whatsoever. The AI isn't the cherry -- it's the entire sundae. And when AI IS the product, every competitor with equivalent models and sufficient capital can build the same thing.

The bull case I have to take seriously: maybe the specific execution -- fully autonomous agents on physical Mac Minis navigating carrier portals for the hardest E&S placements without any human in the loop -- is actually harder and more contrarian than it appears from the outside. Most competitors keep humans in the loop. Fernstone explicitly uses licensed brokers reviewing before binding. If Panta can reliably achieve full autonomy on complex placements where others can't, and if that reliability compounds through proprietary carrier data, the margins could be extraordinary -- brokerage commissions on a $135B market growing 12.5% annually with near-zero marginal cost per placement. The 127 reviews at 4.9/5 in roughly three months of operation is a real signal, not noise. The anecdotal 38% price improvement over a traditional broker suggests the model actually works in production. If Harper's broader SMB focus means they can't match Panta's depth on truly complex E&S risks -- if specialization beats generalization here -- there's a world where Panta carves out the most profitable niche in commercial insurance. That's a business I'd want to own.

But I keep coming back to the competitive dynamics. The underlying technology -- LLM agents navigating portals and processing documents -- is not proprietary. Harper has ten times the funding and an order-of-magnitude head start on customer acquisition. The dossier notes that Panta hasn't disclosed a single carrier name, which makes me nervous about the durability of those relationships when carriers have to choose between working with a three-month-old AI brokerage or an established human-relationship-driven firm on a complex E&S placement where errors create real liability. Insurance is a trust business. The regulatory question -- how state insurance commissioners will ultimately treat fully autonomous AI brokerages -- is genuinely unresolved and could be existential. And the narrative, while expansive in theory (E&S to all commercial to all insurance), is shared with every other company in this space. Panta doesn't own the story.

I'm landing at a neutral-to-pass. Strong founders, real market, actual early traction -- but a consensus opportunity in a crowded field where the AI layer that differentiates them is commoditizable and the best-funded competitor is already far ahead. The brokerage licensing and operational complexity provide a floor that pure software plays lack, which saves this from being a clear pass. But nothing here makes me feel like I'm seeing something the market hasn't already noticed.
