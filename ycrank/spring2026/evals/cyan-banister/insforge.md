# InsForge -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Supabase has $500M in the bank, 80K+ GitHub stars, and has already begun shipping MCP support for AI agent interaction. InsForge has 7,700 stars, $300/month in revenue at last public disclosure, and a six-person team building a "semantic layer" over the same backend primitives — Postgres, S3, OAuth — that Supabase already runs at scale. When I look at InsForge, the first question I have to answer isn't whether agent-native backend infrastructure is a real category (it is), but whether this is a company or a feature. The semantic layer that InsForge has built — context-optimized interfaces for AI coding agents — is a software abstraction, not a deep technical moat. Apache 2.0 licensing means anyone can fork it. InsForge's self-reported MCPMark benchmarks (1.6x faster task completion, 1.7x higher accuracy versus Supabase) have no third-party validation. When the incumbent has 65x your funding and has already started building your differentiator, the burden of proof shifts to you, and InsForge hasn't met it.

Hang Huang applied to Y Combinator six times before InsForge was accepted into P26. Tony Chang won ten hackathons and built infrastructure at Databricks and Meta. I respect the persistence — six rejections and still building is not nothing, and it tells me these founders don't quit easily. But when I ask my opening question — tell me your story, not the business — I hear "Yale MBA who worked at Amazon and Riot Games" and "UIUC CS grad who interned at Databricks and Meta." Those are credentials, not crucibles. Neither founder's biography reveals a personal encounter with the problem that precedes InsForge. Nobody was broken by the inability of AI agents to provision a Postgres database. The problem found them through professional observation, not lived necessity. Compare this to the Crusoe Energy founders, who witnessed gas flaring firsthand in North Dakota oil fields before building portable data centers to capture it — that's the difference between identifying a market gap and being unable to look away from a problem your life delivered to you.

InsForge operates in developer tools and BaaS — the single most consensus-attractive investment category in 2026. AI coding agents are on the cover of every tech publication. Every VC dinner I attend, someone is pitching their AI developer infrastructure thesis. This is the opposite of the spaces where my biggest wins have come from. When I wrote my check for Anduril, defense tech was politically toxic in Silicon Valley. When I backed Flock Safety after spotting a WiFi network name at a YC event, surveillance technology made civil liberties advocates nervous. When I backed Contraline, male birth control made VCs physically squirm. InsForge makes nobody uncomfortable. The idea — "backend infrastructure optimized for AI agents" — provokes enthusiastic nodding, not shifted chairs. That consensus signal tells me this is a space where capital will flood in, competition will intensify, and the winner will be determined by resources and distribution, not by conviction and weirdness.

The bull case deserves honest construction: InsForge is positioned at a genuine inflection point in how software gets built. The transition from human-developer to agent-developer workflows is structural, not cyclical. InsForge's 4,000+ databases, #1 Product Hunt placement, #1 GitHub Trending, and 1.5M+ X impressions during launch week demonstrate real developer attention. The "building in public" strategy doubled their database count from 2,300 to 4,000+ in two months. If Supabase's human-developer UX is architecturally difficult to retrofit for agents — if you really need to build agent-native from the ground up — then InsForge's head start matters. Flexport proved that being the first infrastructure player at a supply chain inflection could create durable network effects. Could InsForge be Flexport for the agent-developer stack? The argument has shape. But Flexport was attacking global freight logistics — one of the most opaque, Byzantine, document-heavy systems on earth, where information asymmetry was the business model. InsForge is optimizing an already-transparent developer tools market where Supabase publishes its documentation openly and Firebase has years of tutorials. There's no invisible tax being eliminated, no extractive structure being torn open, no information asymmetry shifting power from gatekeepers to users. InsForge makes a good workflow better. That's valuable, but it's not the structural thesis that drives my highest-conviction bets.

InsForge's defensibility story depends on execution speed against an incumbent that moves fast. The switching costs once teams deploy production databases are real but identical to any BaaS platform — that's table-stakes lock-in, not a compounding moat. The open-source community at 7,700 stars is healthy for pre-seed but faces the denominator problem against Supabase's 80K+. The $25/month Pro tier with generous free limits creates a long conversion funnel that $300/month revenue confirms hasn't yet converted. For InsForge to win, it needs to build a complete, production-grade backend platform while simultaneously maintaining its agent-optimization lead — and it needs to do this before Supabase, with fifty times the engineering headcount, closes the gap. That's a race I wouldn't bet my own money on without a founder whose biographical relationship with this problem makes them unable to lose. I don't see that founder here.

One condition would flip me to Invest: evidence that Hang Huang or Tony Chang has a specific, verifiable biographical experience that makes agent-native infrastructure personally urgent — not professionally interesting, but personally inescapable — combined with a signed enterprise customer that validates the semantic layer's value at production scale. Right now InsForge is a competent team building a logical product in a consensus market against a well-funded incumbent, with no invisible system to disrupt and no biographical forge driving the mission. The decisive dimensions are Founder's Biographical Forge (absent personal stakes) and Contrarian Conviction (consensus space), and those two together account for 55 of my 100 points. InsForge can't reach my threshold without them.
