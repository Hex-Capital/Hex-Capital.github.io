# Zolvo -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's what I see immediately: Salient raised $70M, hit $25M ARR with zero churn in two years, and they're doing AI loan servicing. Zolvo is doing AI loan servicing. The difference is "commercial" versus "consumer/auto." Now, category segmentation within a hot vertical is sometimes a real insight — the mid-market commercial lender with $200M AUM truly is underserved by nCino's enterprise sales motion. But "we're the same thing as the company that just raised $70M from a16z, except for a slightly different customer segment" is not a mispricing opportunity. That's a pricing confirmation. The market has already decided AI loan servicing is valuable. The question isn't whether Zolvo is building something useful — it probably is. The question is whether the upside is already in the price. And when 83% of lenders are actively planning to increase their GenAI budgets in 2026, you're not finding alpha. You're finding consensus.

The AI kill test here is brutal and I don't think the founders have reckoned with it. nCino does $541M in revenue selling to exactly these lenders. Finastra does $1.8B. The dossier's inference about "business model conflict" — that incumbents won't sell automation that eliminates their customers' headcount — is creative but wrong. Incumbents LOVE selling automation to mid-market because mid-market customers are cost-sensitive and don't have unions or political headcount commitments. The tech stack is Django plus OpenAI plus Gemini plus Claude. There is no proprietary model. There is no novel architecture. The moat signals row in the dossier literally reads "No public data found." When your entire value proposition is orchestrating commodity LLMs against a well-understood workflow, you're a feature waiting to be absorbed. nCino's product team is building this right now, I promise you.

The bull case, which I want to be honest about: commercial lending servicing really is the least digitized corner of the lending stack. Origination got all the love, servicing got none. If Zolvo moves fast enough to accumulate proprietary reconciliation and verification data from hundreds of mid-market lenders before incumbents wake up, that data flywheel could become a genuine moat. Rodriguez closed a million dollars at Domu including Chubb — she can clearly sell. The $6K in revenue within four weeks is real signal that someone is paying for this. And the channel conflict argument, while I think it's overstated, has a kernel of truth: large incumbents move slowly in new segments, and a startup with a focused wedge can sometimes establish itself before the elephant turns. If I'm wrong about the incumbents' speed, and if Zolvo's workflow integration creates real switching costs at the data layer, this could compound into something meaningful. That's the scenario where I'd regret passing.

But I keep coming back to the fundamental problem: this is a sensible idea executed by competent people in a validated market. That's the wrong ratio. Rodriguez and Montes seem smart and scrappy — relocating to SF with three grand, shipping revenue in weeks, publications at ACL and EMNLP at 22. Good credentials. But what they're pitching isn't insane. Nobody hears "AI automates commercial loan servicing back office" and thinks "that's fucking crazy." They think "oh yeah, obviously someone should do that." And when the idea is obvious, you need to ask who else thinks it's obvious. The answer is: Salient with $70M, LoanPro with $100M, Canopy with $34M, and every product manager at nCino. Competent execution in a crowded, consensus-validated category is a fine business. It's not my bet.

The founders are young, hungry, and have shipped together at Domu — that relationship depth matters and I respect the Domu investors putting money in. But founder-market fit in commercial lending requires deep domain knowledge of bank examination requirements, state lending regulations, and the specific pain of reconciliation workflows. A BBA and a dual engineering degree don't signal that. Selling AI to regulated financial institutions with extended compliance cycles when you're 24 years old and your SOC 2 isn't even done yet — that's a long road. Not impossible, but the patience required to close enterprise financial services deals creates a capital intensity that conflicts with the scrappy four-person team model.
