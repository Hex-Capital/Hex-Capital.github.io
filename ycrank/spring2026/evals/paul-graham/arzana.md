# Arzana -- Paul Graham Evaluation

William Alexander taught himself discus technique from YouTube and reached state ranking. Marshall Kools won a Wisconsin state wrestling championship and competed D1 at Stanford. These are the kind of biographical details that don't show up in pitch decks but tell you something about who you're dealing with — people who decide they want something, then figure out how to get it through brute-force learning and obsessive repetition. William is 21. Marshall is 24. Marshall made about $10,000 last year (NYT, February 2026). They're running three offices — San Francisco, Okoboji Iowa, Appleton Wisconsin — with five people. That's not a business plan for operational efficiency. That's founders who physically embed themselves in the world they're building for, which is exactly what I mean by doing things that don't scale. Arzana has landed 8 named customers including Zeiss — a $9 billion German industrial conglomerate — at pre-seed with a team of five. I don't have the cereal box story, but a 21-year-old convincing Zeiss to let his AI agents touch their ERP system is its own kind of signal.

The origin story is what pulls me in hardest. William grew up in Okoboji, Iowa, working at a manufacturing sourcing company (arzana.com/founders/william). Marshall worked manufacturing jobs in Wisconsin (arzana.com/about). Arzana didn't come from a brainstorming session or a TAM analysis or a trend piece about "AI for manufacturing." It came from two kids who grew up watching people spend 15 minutes hand-building quotes, re-keying purchase orders from PDFs into Epicor and JobBoss, and sending customer updates by manually toggling between email and ERP screens. They lived in the present — not the future — and built what was missing. That's the Dropbox pattern: Drew Houston didn't research the cloud storage market, he kept forgetting his USB drive. William didn't research the manufacturing automation market, he watched his neighbors do the same painful data entry every day of his childhood. Arzana's problem discovery is among the most organic I've seen in a YC batch. The fact that both founders maintain offices in their home towns, not just in San Francisco, tells you the connection to this customer base isn't performative.

Arzana operates in a domain that makes most Stanford CS graduates physically recoil. ERP integration with Epicor, Infor, JobBoss, QuickBooks, NetSuite, SAP, Sage, IFS, Dynamics, and Prophet 21 — that's ten different legacy systems, each with its own API quirks, data models, and update cycles. The forward-deployed model means Arzana engineers fly to factories in the Midwest and sit with operations staff for weeks before configuring anything. This is a schlep of the first order. Nobody at a YC Demo Day gets excited about integrating with Prophet 21. That disgust reaction is exactly what I look for — it's a competitive moat that no amount of funding can easily replicate because the work itself repels the kind of founders who typically raise venture capital. Stripe succeeded for a similar structural reason: PCI compliance and bank integrations were a schlep that repelled every developer who looked at payments. The parallel isn't exact — Stripe was building for developers, Arzana is building for manufacturing operations managers — but the mechanism is the same. The schlep filters out competitors who would otherwise crowd the space.

The bull case for Arzana is strong: Conexiom raised $170M and does $28.7M in revenue (Latka, 2024) focusing narrowly on touchless sales order processing. Paperless Parts raised $51M serving 500+ CNC and 3D printing job shops (Tracxn). Esker was acquired by Bridgepoint and General Atlantic with 3,000+ customers including NVIDIA and Sony (Edison Group). These competitors validate the market but each has a structural gap that Arzana can exploit — Conexiom is enterprise-priced and narrow-scoped, Paperless Parts serves only job shops, Esker is horizontal rather than manufacturing-specific. Arzana covers quoting, estimating, order entry, and a unified dashboard across a range from job shops to Fortune 500 (arzana.com). The per-customer AI models trained on historical job costs create a data flywheel that raises switching costs over time. The real competitive risk is that the underlying technology — LLMs reading emails and PDFs — is increasingly accessible. Conexiom has launched an AI Platform. DigiFabster has an AI Quote Agent (TCT Magazine). But commoditization of the base technology doesn't commoditize the customer relationships, the ERP integrations already built, or the manufacturing-specific training data already accumulated. Arzana's moat isn't the AI — it's the willingness to sit in a factory in Wisconsin for three weeks to understand how a mold maker actually processes purchase orders.

Where Arzana gives me less signal is on learning rate and iteration velocity. I don't have weekly growth numbers, pivot stories, or evidence that the product has evolved visibly from its initial form. The four-module architecture (Quoting, Estimating, Order Entry, Dashboard) suggests expansion from a simpler starting point, but the dossier doesn't tell me what Arzana looked like six months ago versus today. William's self-teaching pattern — state-ranking discus from YouTube, self-taught Mandarin in Hong Kong — suggests high general learning velocity, but I'm extrapolating from personal history rather than observing company-level iteration. At pre-seed with a 5-person team, I weight this gap less heavily than I would for a Series A company, but it's genuine missing evidence. The forward-deployed model also creates a scaling tension: with five people and three offices, Arzana can probably serve 10-15 customers before hitting a wall. The question is whether they're accumulating enough pattern recognition during this unscalable phase to productize the deployment — to turn three weeks onsite into three days, then three hours, then self-serve. That transition is the company's defining challenge.

Arzana's earnestness is visible in the details that are hardest to fake. Marshall's $10,000 annual salary. Three offices including two in the founders' home regions rather than the obvious choice of running everything from SF. A New York Times profile titled "These A.I. Dreamers Don't Fit the Stereotype" (February 8, 2026). Building ITAR compliance into the product for defense manufacturers (arzana.com). These aren't the choices of founders optimizing for status or valuation milestones. These are choices made by people who genuinely care about solving a problem for a community they belong to. The closest pattern in my portfolio is Bindwell — teenagers tackling pesticide chemistry — but Arzana's founders have a deeper, more personal connection to their domain. They didn't just stumble into manufacturing. They grew up inside it and chose to come back.

The bear case I take most seriously: Conexiom's $170M in funding and $28.7M in existing revenue means Arzana is entering a market where an established player can outspend them 100-to-1 on engineering and sales. If Conexiom decides to move downmarket or broaden its product scope, Arzana's entire positioning could be squeezed. The forward-deployed model, while providing deep integration, may cap Arzana's growth rate below the 5-7% weekly threshold that signals a true startup rather than a services business. If by the end of YC, Arzana hasn't demonstrated a path to reducing per-customer deployment time dramatically — say, from weeks to days — I'd downgrade my conviction. The condition that would flip me to Pass is evidence that the forward-deployed model isn't generating reusable product insights — that each customer deployment is bespoke rather than accumulating toward a scalable system. But right now, the organic problem discovery, the authentic schlep, and the early traction with Zeiss at pre-seed with five people tell me these founders have the right relationship to this problem. The idea is boring enough to protect them and real enough to build on.
