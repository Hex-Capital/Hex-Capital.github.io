# Modern Industrials -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I see when I look at Modern Industrials is a team that could build almost anything — and chose lumber distribution. Ankit Bhargava was a Member of Technical Staff at xAI building agentic AI systems. Vatsal Bhargava led ML for Google Ads Bidding. Austin Mao ranked in the top 5% at McKinsey and ran growth at a Sequoia-backed startup. These are frontier AI people. So when I ask my opening question — tell me your story, not the business — I'm listening for the biographical thread that pulled three people with these credentials into the world of building materials quoting. And I can't find it. What I find instead is a LinkedIn post describing the $450B+ LBM industry as "one of the largest and most under-digitized industries in North America." That's a market observation, not a biographical crucible. Nobody on this team grew up in a lumber yard, ran a distribution business, or experienced the pain of manual quoting as a lived reality. The problem didn't find them — they found the problem through market analysis. That distinction matters deeply to me.

What Modern Industrials does have is one of the strongest co-founder relationship signals I've seen at pre-seed: two brothers, Vatsal and Ankit Bhargava, plus their childhood best friend Austin Mao — a 20-year friendship. I've been burned badly by the co-founder red flag, most painfully with HQ Trivia, where the right product at the right time was destroyed by founder conflict. Modern Industrials won't have that problem. Brothers and a lifelong friend building together is the opposite of two strangers who met at a hackathon. But strong relationships are necessary, not sufficient. The forge I look for is about the founder's relationship to the PROBLEM, not just to each other.

When I apply my invisible system test — is there an opaque, extractive structure being made transparent? — Modern Industrials falls short in a specific way. The LBM distribution chain does contain opacity: pricing is relationship-dependent, quoting is manual, and information asymmetry exists between distributors and the contractors they serve. But Modern Industrials isn't making that system visible to the people being extracted from. Their AI Sales Engine automates quoting for the distributors themselves — it makes the gatekeepers faster, not the system more transparent. Compare this to Affirm, which made lending terms visible to consumers, or Ownwell, which gave property owners tools to fight opaque tax assessments. Those companies shifted power from institutions to individuals. Modern Industrials shifts efficiency within the institution. That's valuable as a business, but it's not the structural disruption that drives my highest-conviction bets.

The competitive landscape is what concerns me most. Ventura, a YC W26 company, is building a nearly identical product — AI quoting for industrial distributors — and its CEO has a prior exit in wholesale distribution SaaS. Ventura already has a customer processing over 1M quotes and orders annually. When I think about Flexport, one of my portfolio companies that digitized freight forwarding, what made it work was the combination of technology AND deep logistics domain knowledge. Ryan Petersen lived inside the freight system. Modern Industrials is bringing exceptional AI talent to a domain where the competitor has both the technical capability AND the domain fluency. Austin Mao's claim that they've "already gone live with national players" is encouraging but unnamed and unverified — and it sits against Ventura's specific customer claim. In a relationship-driven industry where distributors have been doing things the same way since DMSi was founded in 1976, the team that speaks the customer's language first usually wins the integration.

The bull case for Modern Industrials is real, and I want to be honest about it. The LLM capability threshold that crossed in 2024-2025 makes parsing unstructured trade documents — faxes, PDFs, spec sheets — commercially viable for the first time. The "why now" is genuine. If Ankit Bhargava's xAI experience in agentic AI systems translates into a genuinely superior product for document understanding and pricing logic, the team's technical edge could overcome the domain expertise gap. Vatsal's track record of building a side project to $10K MRR with 500+ users shows he can ship products that people use. And the LBM distribution market IS large enough that even a modest share would build a meaningful company. The switching costs once a distributor trains the AI on their specific pricing rules and catalogs could compound into a real moat. If Modern Industrials can sign three to five national distributors before Ventura or Flitch locks them up, they could win through execution speed rather than domain pedigree.

But this isn't a Long Journey bet for me. The dimensions that drive my strongest investments — biographical inevitability, contrarian conviction that makes VCs uncomfortable, hidden systems being torn open for the people trapped inside them — are absent here. Modern Industrials is a capable team applying AI to an underdigitized vertical. That's a good business thesis, and I genuinely wish them well. The co-founder bond is beautiful. But when I run my ten-year imagination exercise, I see an efficient quoting tool, not infrastructure that transforms what's possible. The Founder's Biographical Forge — 13/30 because the friendship is real but the connection to LBM is not — and Invisible System Made Visible — 8/20 because they serve the gatekeeper, not the gatekept — are the two dimensions that keep this in neutral territory.
