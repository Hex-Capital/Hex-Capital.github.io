# Hessian -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Bao Nguyen, James Smith, and Tom Watson are running a forward-deployed engineering shop with AI agents on top, and the first sentence of the Hessian dossier — "we forward deploy with AI agents to automate business operations" — tells me exactly which leverage quadrant Hessian lives in before I read the founder bios. Three Cambridge/Durham-credentialed engineers pointing real specific knowledge at a labor-leverage business model is the analytical signature here: Hessian is the inverse Stack Overflow — the team I would want building permissionless code leverage is instead selling implementation hours into Fazeshift and whoever else the YC network surfaces.

Twitter (2007) is the high rung on leverage architecture because anyone could post, the platform charged nothing, and the marginal cost of the next user was zero — that is what code and media share and what underwrites the 400x outcome. Hessian sits two rungs below that: the dossier says the team "forward-deploys engineers on-site to map a customer's workflow" and "continues to operate end-to-end for the customer," which is exactly the structural opposite — every new customer requires a human in the loop and gatekeeper permission from the customer's CIO to access their systems. The eleven integrations listed on hessian.sh (Claude, OpenAI, GitHub, Postgres, Slack, AWS) are platform-permissioned channels, not permissionless distribution. Even the demo path is a Google Calendar link, not an open-source repo with organic stars. This is labor leverage with code dressing, and Hessian's own framing — "3 engineers can scale forward-deployed engagements" — concedes that headcount is the binding constraint.

Single-vertical SaaS where every customer is economically independent of the next is the failure mode I anchor at the low rung of the network dimension — the Nth customer doesn't make the (N-1) prior customers more valuable. Does that pattern appear in Hessian? The dossier names exactly one customer (fellow YC company Fazeshift) and the "future moat" entry concedes that compounding workflow templates across engagements is "unproven now given 3-person team and no disclosed customer count." Sales proposals, invoice reconciliation, and month-end close are bespoke per-customer workflows; the agent built for Fazeshift's receivables flow does not increase the value of the agent built for the next customer's GL close. Hessian is single-sided B2B services dressed as a platform — the upside math does not have the 10,000x geometry I underwrote Uber and Twitter on.

Specific knowledge is where Hessian actually scores. Bao Nguyen co-founded Zettafleet (decentralized LLM training across hundreds of GPUs, out of stealth February 2026 per Sifted) and published ML systems work with Google DeepMind; Tom Watson migrated GoCardless's core payments database with zero downtime on a system processing over £1B on peak days; James Smith spent five years at Bloomberg on entity-disambiguation pipelines over billions of observations. This is real non-trainable depth in exactly the financial-ops and data-infrastructure substrate Hessian sells into. Aravind at Perplexity is the load-bearing parallel — a Berkeley ML PhD pointed at retrieval cleared my specific-knowledge gate even when the network-effects dimension was weak. Hessian has the founder depth; what Hessian does not have is Aravind's permissionless-API distribution wrapping it.

The AI agents market at $12.06B in 2026 growing 45.5% CAGR to $53.2B (Business Research Company) gives Hessian pivot room on paper — the team could plausibly abandon services and ship the runtime/observability platform as a self-serve product, which is the visible escape hatch. Eighteen months of compounding inside the services model, however, ossifies the GTM muscle around implementation contracts; Distyl AI raised $207M to a $1.8B valuation (SiliconAngle, September 2025) running this exact pattern and has not visibly pivoted to product. The market is big enough; the model is sticky in the wrong direction.

Buffett's trio reads clean on Hessian — three Durham CS classmates, Cambridge scholarships, prior employers (Bloomberg, GoCardless, DRW) that don't generate integrity drama, no founder volunteering unprompted statements about their own honesty. The 50/50/50 equity question and named CEO designation are resolved (Bao is CEO). Nothing in the Hessian dossier triggers the Epinions scar-tissue alarm. Default-neutral here, modestly above midpoint because the technical credentials are real.

Hessian in 36 months is either a sub-$50M acqui-hire into Distyl AI or a Big-4 consulting practice as the labor-arbitrage thesis collapses against a $1.8B incumbent with $207M in the bank, or — the better outcome — the team rips out the services scaffolding and ships the runtime/observability platform as a developer-self-serve product reaching $2-5M ARR by Q2 2029. The mechanism producing this fork is the high/low signature itself: Cambridge-grade ML founders eventually notice that their labor is the binding constraint and either accept an acquirer's balance sheet or pivot to the permissionless-leverage version of the same idea.

The strongest bull case invokes my own Anchorage Digital exception — sometimes permission-as-moat *is* the structural leverage, and Palantir built a $50B company on forward-deployed engineers over twenty years. If Hessian's runtime accumulates enough proprietary workflow data across engagements that the FDE motion becomes a wedge into a defensible agent platform — Sierra at $104M ARR and $10B valuation followed roughly this arc per Sacra (November 2025) — then the labor leverage flips into platform leverage by year four. The dossier evidence that defeats this: Distyl AI is twenty months and $207M ahead of Hessian on the identical strategy, Sierra was started by Bret Taylor with a different distribution wedge, and Anchorage worked because regulatory charter (OCC, January 2021) was a structural moat no faster-mover could replicate — a 3-person YC team racing Distyl has no analogous moat. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: Hessian ships an open-source agent runtime to GitHub with 5,000+ organic stars and a third of its revenue coming from self-serve platform fees rather than implementation contracts within 18 months. Until then, Hessian is the team I want building a different company.
