# Gojiberry AI -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The most revealing data point in this entire dossier is not the $175K MRR or the 44% month-over-month growth. It is the TrustMRR listing on March 9, 2026, offering the company for sale at $11.15 million -- a 6x revenue multiple -- posted one week after the Product Hunt launch and simultaneous with YC S26 participation. A founder who is simultaneously raising venture capital and listing the company for sale on a marketplace has answered the most important question I ask before any other: do you believe this company will be worth billions, or are you optimizing for a near-term exit? The Zuckerberg test is not whether you can build something impressive -- it is whether you refuse to sell it when someone offers. These founders are advertising the sale price before anyone has even asked.

This pattern is not anomalous. It is structural. All three founders have prior exits -- CoCo AI (a WhatsApp marketing Shopify app sold in 2025), Edusign (a paperless attendance SaaS sold in 2025), MechaChain (a Web3 game acquired by Kevuru). These are competent builders of small profitable software businesses, and I mean that without condescension -- bootstrapping to half a million in ARR and selling is genuinely difficult. But the revealed preference is clear: these are serial flippers, not empire builders. The pivot history confirms it. Gojiberry began as a CRM auto-fill tool with 2,000 waitlist users and zero revenue, and they pivoted to intent-based lead generation when the first idea didn't monetize. That is textbook lean methodology -- test, learn, iterate -- which is precisely what I call "code for unplanned." There is no definite vision here for what the world looks like in ten years. There is a well-executed playbook for finding product-market fit and selling before the market turns.

Now, where is the secret? I ask this of every company, and the answer here is that there isn't one. The thesis -- "small sales teams need affordable intent data and LinkedIn signals can provide it" -- is not contrarian. It is the most consensus take in B2B SaaS right now. Apollo acquired Pocus (a signal-based selling platform) in March 2026. Clay reached $100M ARR at a $3.1 billion valuation. Instantly, Lemlist, HeyReach, Amplemarket, and Expandi have collectively raised hundreds of millions and generated tens of millions in ARR in overlapping categories. The dossier itself lists five direct competitors with combined ARR exceeding $230 million. When I count the players in a market and the number exceeds three, I already know the structural outcome: margins compress to zero as mimetic rivals imitate each other into mutual destruction. The dossier's own language -- "LinkedIn signal detection and automated messaging are technically reproducible" and "low barriers to entry for execution-layer tools" -- articulates the problem more clearly than any analysis I could add.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. The execution metrics are genuinely exceptional: $175K MRR with 75% profit margins, sub-5% churn, $127 CAC with 1.3-month payback, built on a distribution engine costing $380 per month. If these numbers are real, this is one of the most capital-efficient customer acquisition machines I have seen at this stage. The argument would be that in a large, fragmented market, a team that executes this efficiently can carve out a durable SMB niche that enterprise incumbents cannot profitably serve -- that 6sense at $58K median ACV literally cannot build a sales motion for $99/month customers. This is the Spotify exception in my framework: when supply-side economics create a structural price floor that prevents downmarket competition, you can win a large market directly. For this to work, however, you need the moat to come from somewhere other than the product itself, because the product is reproducible. And the moat here -- CRM integrations, trained ICP models, campaign history -- is thin. These are switching costs measured in days, not years. A team of three people generating $175K MRR is an excellent small business. It is not a monopoly.

The technology dimension confirms the diagnosis. There is no proprietary breakthrough here -- the system aggregates LinkedIn signals (which LinkedIn controls) through third-party data providers (which anyone can access) and generates personalized messages using LLMs (which are commodity infrastructure). Every component is available to every competitor. The "data flywheel" claim -- that 1,000+ customers generate compounding signal-to-conversion data -- is the weakest form of moat because Apollo, with $150M ARR and orders of magnitude more data, already has a larger flywheel. This is a 1-to-n company: a better-packaged, cheaper version of capabilities that already exist in the market. The underlying technology is not discontinuous. It is incremental workflow automation layered on top of someone else's platform, someone else's data, and someone else's AI models.

I pass. The traction is real, the founders are skilled operators, and this will likely be a profitable business for some time. But I do not invest in profitable businesses -- I invest in monopolies. Every great company is built around a secret, and the secret here is missing. The market is a Red Ocean of funded competitors converging on identical capabilities. The founders' own behavior -- listing the company for sale while raising venture capital -- tells me they see this the same way I do. They are building to sell, not building to dominate. That is a perfectly rational strategy for them. It is not a strategy that produces power-law returns.
