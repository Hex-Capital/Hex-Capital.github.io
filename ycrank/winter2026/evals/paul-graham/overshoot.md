# Overshoot -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Overshoot is that Younes El Hjouji was literally the person building the old system when it broke. He was a founding engineer at Cosmonio, which Intel acquired, where he built a computer vision training and serving platform from scratch. Then he watched customers walk away from traditional CV because Vision Language Models could do what used to require custom-trained models. That's not someone who read a TechCrunch article about AI trends and decided to start a company. That's someone who was standing in the building when the earthquake hit. His cousin Zakaria, meanwhile, spent seven years at Uber building surge pricing -- one of the hardest low-latency, high-throughput systems in production anywhere -- and then went to Meta AI to write GPU kernels for inference engines. The combination is almost too perfect: one founder who understands the CV problem space, another who understands how to make inference fast. The idea clearly grew from their professional lives, not a brainstorming session.

The schlep here is real but bounded. Video inference is genuinely different from text inference. You're dealing with codecs, streaming protocols, frame extraction, temporal analysis, and latency budgets measured in milliseconds. General-purpose inference platforms like Fireworks and Together optimize for text because that's where the volume is -- video is an afterthought. That asymmetry is exactly the kind of thing schlep blindness is about: the problem is visible to anyone building vision applications, but solving it requires getting deep into video infrastructure plumbing that most AI teams unconsciously avoid. However, I need to be honest about the limits of this schlep. Stripe's schlep involved banks, regulations, and fraud systems -- barriers that compound and get harder for competitors to replicate over time. Overshoot's schlep is technical optimization. Important, but the kind of thing a $331 million company like Fireworks could invest in if this market proves large enough. The schlep deters people now, but it won't deter them forever.

The technical founder signal is strong. Both founders are builders. Zakaria wrote GPU kernels at Meta. Younes built a CV platform from scratch. They have a working SDK with 54 commits on GitHub, a live API delivering sub-200ms responses, support for multiple video input modes, and two distinct processing architectures (Clip Mode and Frame Mode). This isn't a PowerPoint startup -- it's two engineers who built the thing themselves because they could. The cousins relationship also matters. I've seen what happens when co-founders who barely know each other hit hard times -- nine companies in one batch added unknown co-founders and all nine fell apart. Family isn't a guarantee, but it's a bond that predates the startup by decades.

What worries me is the competitive landscape and the thin traction evidence. Three hundred developers is a number, but 14 GitHub stars and no revenue signal suggest those 300 may be casual rather than committed. I don't see evidence of the kind of pull where users would be genuinely harmed if this disappeared -- no organic sharing, no economic dependency, no signal that people found Overshoot without being told about it. And the competitors have raised, collectively, nearly a billion dollars. Together AI alone does $50 million in revenue. If Overshoot proves that specialized VLM inference on live video is a real market, these companies will come for it. The moat argument -- that focus enables technical leaps -- is true at the moment but not durable without network effects or switching costs.

The bull case is worth dwelling on. If VLM-powered video becomes a major application layer -- and the use cases are compelling: real-time security analysis, robotics vision, AR applications -- then the developer who learns Overshoot's three-line API first may stick with it. Developer tools have natural stickiness. Stripe won not because no one else could have built payment APIs, but because they were purpose-built for developers at the exact moment when web developers needed payments. If we're at a similar inflection point for video AI applications, Overshoot's positioning could be exactly right. The founders have the unusual combination of CV domain knowledge and low-latency infrastructure experience that this particular problem demands. If the market materializes at the pace VLM capabilities suggest, being the specialized platform when developers start building could create a meaningful lead.

But I keep coming back to the commoditization risk. The models they serve -- Qwen, InternVL -- are open-source. Their differentiation is the optimization layer. That's a real advantage today, but advantages built purely on performance optimization tend to erode. Stripe's moat wasn't just API design -- it was the entire banking relationship and compliance infrastructure underneath. I don't see the equivalent here yet. This is a competent team with genuine domain expertise attacking a real problem at the right time, but the structural moat is thin and the competitive environment is brutal.
