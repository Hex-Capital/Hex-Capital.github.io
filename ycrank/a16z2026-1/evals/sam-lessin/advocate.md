# Advocate -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's what I see when I look at Advocate: five of my red flags lit up simultaneously. AI company where the AI is the product. Dependent on regulatory reimbursement rates to generate value. Better version of an existing workflow, not a new category. Pure software with no physical moat or protocol lock-in. And the category — AI automating healthcare billing — is about as consensus-hot as it gets in 2026. CodaMetrix just raised $109M. Sully.ai pulled in $35M. Waystar is public doing $1.1B in revenue and growing 24% year-over-year. Every healthcare-focused VC on Sand Hill Road is pitching their LPs on "AI transforms RCM." When every sophisticated investor in the room is nodding, the upside is already in the price. There is no mispricing here — there's a crowd.

The primitive test kills this faster. Advocate converts voice notes from care coordination staff into CPT-coded billing packets. That's an automation of an existing billing workflow — it's not creating a new behavior, a new financial instrument, or a new category of economic activity. Nobody is going to say "the Advocate of X" to describe a future company. Compare this to Venmo, which created the verb and the behavior of peer-to-peer mobile payments, or to the Creator Fund ISAs, which invented people-as-investable-assets as a category. Advocate is doing something useful — capturing uncollected reimbursement under CPT 99490 and related codes — but "useful automation of existing process" is exactly the kind of thing incumbents eat for breakfast. This is the Adobe problem I keep talking about: every time Abridge or DeepScribe sees this demo, they think "we should add that feature to our existing voice-capture pipeline." And they can, because the core tech — speech-to-text plus LLM code mapping — is built on commodity components.

Andrew Baran has a credible background: McKinsey, Radian Capital healthcare investing, Columbia. Justinas Banys built data systems at Garner Health when it was Series A stage, and Garner is now a $1.35B company. These are competent, rational people. Baran's mother worked as a care coordinator — that's genuine domain proximity. But here's the thing: competent rational people with a sensible idea in a validated market is precisely the configuration that bores me. The sane/insane ratio requires insanity somewhere in the equation. "Use AI to capture missed billing codes" is the most rational pitch I've heard this week. Nothing about it makes me think "that's fucking crazy but what if they're right?" It makes me think "yes, obviously, and Waystar's product team is thinking the same thing."

Structural durability is where Advocate really falls apart under my framework. The dossier's own defensibility section is honest: "No defensibility signals found in public sources at this stage." The inferred moats — proprietary training data, switching costs, audit trail lock-in — are hopes, not facts. Contrast this with Metropolis, which I seeded early and is now worth roughly $5B. Metropolis works because AI amplifies physical parking infrastructure that no competitor can download or replicate with an API call. The parking garages are the moat. Advocate has no physical asset, no protocol-level network effect, no infrastructure that exists independent of the software layer. CMS could restructure care coordination billing codes tomorrow and the entire value proposition evaporates — that's the regulatory dependency pattern I've been burned on before. When your revenue ceiling is set by a government agency's reimbursement schedule, you're not building a company, you're building a derivative instrument on regulatory policy.

The strongest bull case: the specific niche of care coordination billing at skilled nursing facilities and behavioral health centers is genuinely fragmented, low-ASP, and structurally unattractive to the big RCM players who sell to hospital CFOs. Maybe Advocate owns this gap the way a cockroach survives where elephants won't go. The "found money" pitch — no new hires, no workflow changes, pure margin — is a clean sale to facility administrators. For this to work, Advocate would need to be so embedded in hundreds of facilities before Abridge or CodaMetrix bothers to look down-market that switching costs become real. That's possible. But it's a narrow path that requires the incumbents to stay asleep, CMS reimbursement rates to stay stable, and no other YC company (Careforce is already adjacent) to attack the same gap. I don't invest in narrow paths where the win condition requires everyone else to make mistakes.

The dimension that locks this as a pass isn't any single criterion — it's that Advocate triggers no positive signal on my framework while triggering multiple negatives. No mispricing (consensus-hot category), no primitive (workflow automation), no insanity in the idea (sensible to a fault), no structural moat (pure software on commodity tech), and regulatory dependency on CMS rates. Advocate may build a decent small business capturing lost billing revenue for post-acute facilities. But "decent small business in a consensus category" is the opposite of what I'm looking for with my own money.
