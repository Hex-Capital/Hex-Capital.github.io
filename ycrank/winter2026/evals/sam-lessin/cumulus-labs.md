# Cumulus Labs -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's my core problem with Cumulus Labs: this is the single most consensus investment category in tech right now. Serverless GPU inference? Every investor I know has looked at this market. Modal is valued at $1.1B and reportedly in talks at $2.5B. Baseten just raised $300M at a $5B valuation. RunPod is already at $120M+ ARR. Together AI is doing $300M in annualized revenue. When I run my first filter -- is this a sane person with an insane idea? -- I get "sane person, extremely sane idea." And that's the worst possible outcome for me as an investor. Sane-sane means the returns are already priced in. The market has already told you this is a massive opportunity, and the market is not wrong about the opportunity -- it's wrong about who captures it. My bet is that it won't be a two-person pre-seed startup competing against companies with half a billion dollars in the bank.

The founders deserve real credit. Suryaa built a distributed GPU marketplace at TensorDock, which is as directly relevant as domain experience gets. Veer ran mission-critical ML workloads for Space Force and NASA, so he's lived the customer pain. They've known each other since third grade -- that's the kind of co-founder bond I deeply respect, the kind that survives the brutal moments. This is an unusually strong founding pair for a pre-seed company. But domain expertise in a consensus category isn't the same as insight into a non-consensus one. Brian Armstrong wasn't just competent at engineering -- he was competent at engineering AND had the insight that cryptocurrency exchange infrastructure was a real business when most of Silicon Valley laughed at Bitcoin. Where's the non-consensus insight here? "GPU inference needs to be faster and cheaper" is something every infrastructure engineer on the planet agrees with.

Now let me run the AI kill test, and here it gets structurally ugly. This company doesn't just use AI as a cherry on top -- AI IS the sundae. Without AI models requiring inference, there is no Cumulus Labs. That's the exact opposite of what I look for. But worse: the moat is Ion, a proprietary inference engine. That's a software moat in a software market, which is the category I've spent two years arguing is dissolving. Open-source inference engines like vLLM, SGLang, and TensorRT-LLM have massive engineering teams and corporate backing. NVIDIA itself is investing heavily in inference optimization. A claimed 4x cold start advantage over Modal is a benchmark number that degrades with every competitor's next release. You're in a feature war where capital and headcount determine winners, and Cumulus has neither. They don't own the GPUs -- the dossier mentions "aggregating idle GPU capacity from public clouds, private data centers, and vetted individual hosts" -- so there's no physical-world moat either. Google Cloud Run with GPUs already launched. The structural disincentive argument for hyperscalers breaks down the moment the market is large enough that cannibalization math favors offering the feature.

To steelman the bull case: the GPU inference market is genuinely enormous and growing at 35%+ CAGR. RunPod went from nothing to $120M+ ARR, proving the market rewards execution even among multiple players. If Ion delivers a sustained, compounding performance advantage -- if inference optimization produces proprietary data that makes the next optimization better, creating a flywheel -- then there's a path where Cumulus captures meaningful share of a $50B market. The third-grade friendship, the supply-side/demand-side complementarity of the founders, the YC backing -- these are real assets. And the high-touch "forward-deployed custom optimizations" approach could build deep customer relationships early. If I'm wrong about software moats in this specific subdomain -- if inference engine performance is harder to replicate than I think -- this could compound.

But I don't think I'm wrong. This is middleware. It sits between the model layer (which developers choose) and the hardware layer (which they rent), and it's squeezable from both directions. There's no protocol position here -- Cumulus isn't creating a new primitive that others build on, it's optimizing an existing execution layer. There's no network effect. No data moat. The narrative is large but it's a shared narrative -- every competitor tells the same "infinity story" about GPU infrastructure. And there's no revenue signal, no named customers, no disclosed usage metrics to provide a downside cushion. This is pure narrative at pre-seed in the most competitive infrastructure category in tech. I'd rather fund a painting company with a scheduling algorithm than an inference middleware company competing against a combined $1.2 billion in competitor funding.
