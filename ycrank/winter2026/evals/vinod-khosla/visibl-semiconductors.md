# Visibl Semiconductors -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The most interesting structural observation about Visibl is also its most damning quality through my lens: the "coordination layer" framing is a deliberate choice to avoid the hard technical bet. ChipAgents is attempting to automate RTL design and verification — that is a bet on replacing engineering labor with AI agents. Silimate is attempting to predict PPA failures before they happen — that is a bet on making entire design review cycles obsolete. Visibl is attempting to help existing teams coordinate their existing workflows more efficiently. One of these approaches, if successful, leaves the semiconductor design process fundamentally intact. I have been backing companies for forty years, and the companies that build better coordination tools for existing teams are not the companies that change industries. They are the companies that become features of the platforms that do.

Let me be direct about consequence magnitude. If Visibl succeeds perfectly — if it becomes the coordination standard across every chip design team globally — what has changed? Teams ship chips faster, with fewer delays and less rework before tapeout. That is a genuinely valuable outcome for semiconductor companies, and it is the kind of outcome I pass on routinely. The EDA market is $21 billion. The AI-augmented expansion might add $6 billion. Visibl is targeting a subset of that subset — the coordination overhead portion. If everything goes right, this is a several-hundred-million-dollar business. That is not a bad business. But it is not the kind of outcome that justifies the risk of pre-seed investing. A company that made chip design possible with 10 engineers instead of 1,000 — that would be consequence-magnitude territory. A company that democratized custom silicon so that any software company could design its own chips — that would transform the semiconductor industry. A coordination dashboard that reduces tapeout delays is enterprise workflow software with semiconductor domain expertise bolted on.

The structural insight about incumbents is worth acknowledging because it is genuine: Synopsys will not build a coordination layer that orchestrates Cadence tools, and Cadence will not build one that orchestrates Synopsys tools. That creates a real opening for a neutral third party. But the existence of a structural opening does not make the prize worth pursuing at venture scale. Plenty of structural openings exist in enterprise software where incumbents have business model conflicts. The question is whether filling that gap produces a transformational outcome or a nice niche business. Here, I see the latter. And ChipAgents — with $74 million in funding, 50 deployed customers, and an advisory board of former CEOs and CTOs from Synopsys, Cadence, and Mentor Graphics — has already claimed the adjacent territory. A two-person team entering this space faces not just the incumbents but a well-capitalized, deeply connected startup that arrived first with more resources and industry relationships than Visibl can assemble in the next two years.

The strongest bull case requires believing that coordination, not automation, is the binding constraint on the semiconductor industry — that the exponential complexity of advanced nodes (3nm, 2nm) creates a superlinear growth in coordination overhead that eventually becomes more expensive than the design work itself. If that is true, then whoever owns the coordination layer owns the highest-value chokepoint in chip design. And if LLM capabilities continue improving at current rates, the coordination layer could evolve from tracking drift to actively resolving conflicts, effectively becoming the decision-making infrastructure for chip design. Under that scenario, Visibl's cross-vendor neutrality becomes a structural moat, and the company could capture enormous value as the operating system through which all chip design flows. The timing is genuinely favorable — advanced nodes are multiplying complexity while AI capabilities are reaching the threshold needed to interpret domain-specific EDA outputs. These two curves intersect right now. I give this scenario perhaps a 10-15% probability, which in my framework is not disqualifying — if the consequence of that scenario were large enough. But even in the optimistic case, this becomes a very successful enterprise software company, not a company that rewires how the physical world works.

The founding team is competent but constructed along industry-insider lines. Kashanchi has chip design experience at Microsoft's Azure Maia program, Arm, and Intel — exactly the kind of background that teaches you what the industry considers impossible rather than freeing you to attempt it. Neil brings software and data systems experience from Deloitte's AI consulting practice. Together, they identified a real pain point from inside the system and are building a tool to alleviate it. This is the archetype of an incremental innovation team: experienced enough to see the problem clearly, experienced enough to constrain their solution to what the existing system will accept. I look for the biochemist who does not know that the food industry thinks plant-based meat is impossible. I do not see that outsider energy here. The AI-for-chip-design space is already consensus — $74 million flowing to ChipAgents, multiple other startups funded — which means the contrarian window has closed. Visibl is not skating ahead of the puck; the puck arrived, and they are joining a crowded ice surface.
