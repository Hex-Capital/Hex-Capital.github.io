# InLoop Robotics -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The competitive landscape here tells me everything about where this market sits on the consensus curve. Nimble has raised $221M at a billion-dollar valuation. Dexterity has raised $291M at $1.65B. Pickle Robot pulled in nearly $100M. Amazon acqui-hired Covariant's entire team last August. Warehouse robotics isn't a market that investors are sleeping on -- it's a market where multiple well-capitalized companies are already executing, with enterprise customers like FedEx and UPS signed up. When I look at the structural timing question -- "what change is making this possible NOW that wasn't possible two years ago?" -- the answer is VLA models and foundation models crossing manipulation capability thresholds. But that answer is obvious to every growth investor writing $200M checks into this category. There's no timestamp mismatch here. The window opened, and the market noticed immediately. That's the opposite of the kind of nonobvious structural shift I look for.

The human-in-the-loop deployment strategy is the most interesting thing InLoop has going. Ship imperfect policies, use remote teleoperators as a recovery system, feed every intervention back as training data. It's a clever product insight about how to get production data faster than competitors chasing full autonomy before deployment. But I've seen this playbook before -- it's exactly what autonomous vehicle companies tried with remote safety operators. The core problem is that the data flywheel advantage is smaller than it looks on paper, because the teleoperator cost doesn't decline as fast as founders project it will. And InLoop has disclosed zero data on intervention frequency, teleoperator-to-robot ratios, or the rate at which their model's confidence improves over deployments. Without that data, I'm being asked to take the flywheel on faith. The concept is also straightforwardly replicable -- one of the founders literally forked Physical Intelligence's open-source manipulation framework on GitHub, which means the underlying technology building blocks are accessible to anyone. The defensibility story depends entirely on accumulated deployment data, and at pre-seed with "multiple robots" deployed and no named customers, that data advantage is negligible.

From an infrastructure-layer perspective, InLoop is building at the application layer, not the platform layer. They're assembling robots that go into warehouses -- they're not building the manipulation API, the training infrastructure, or the developer tools that other robotics companies use. The companies building the infrastructure that InLoop depends on (Physical Intelligence, Google DeepMind) are the picks-and-shovels plays in this ecosystem. InLoop is the miner. When I invested in Stripe, it wasn't because payments were interesting -- it was because Stripe was becoming the API layer that every other company built on top of. InLoop doesn't have that compounding dynamic. Their switching costs come from facility-specific model adaptation, which is real but modest compared to genuine infrastructure lock-in.

The single-miracle test concerns me. InLoop needs the AI improvement rate to be fast enough that teleoperator ratios decline (otherwise the RaaS unit economics break). They need hardware reliability in harsh warehouse environments across diverse deployments. They need a distribution and sales engine to compete against companies with 50-100x their capital and existing enterprise relationships. And they need the RaaS model to produce positive unit economics despite hardware depreciation and teleoperator staffing costs. That's at least three things that need to go right simultaneously. The AI capability improvement is partially exogenous -- VLA models are getting better on their own curve -- but the translation from general manipulation capability to InLoop's specific hardware in specific warehouse environments is not guaranteed.

The strongest bull case: InLoop could capture the long tail of small-to-mid-sized warehouses that Nimble and Dexterity are ignoring while they chase enterprise contracts. The zero-CapEx RaaS model removes the capital barrier that's kept SMB operators locked out of automation. If the founders can iterate faster than incumbents -- speed being the only startup advantage -- and if the data flywheel actually compounds, they could own the SMB segment before anyone notices. The founders are technically capable: Sharfeddine won the 2025 BEHAVIOR Challenge and has BMW and Bosch experience; Rizali co-founded Germany's largest student robotics initiative. They're not pretending to be something they're not. But these are students and recent graduates -- Rizali is still pursuing his MS, Feduniak has no retrievable public background at all (and the only matching name on public records suggests someone born in 2007). I've seen great founders do incredible things in strong markets, but warehouse robotics against competitors with billions in aggregate funding is a market where execution gaps get punished fast.

The margin architecture is the final concern. Hardware-plus-teleoperator cost structure means gross margins at scale will look more like a services business than a software business. RaaS models in industrial robotics typically run 40-55% gross margins in steady state -- a far cry from the 70%+ software margins that support venture-scale returns. The flat monthly subscription creates some retention stickiness, but it also caps expansion revenue unless they add premium tiers or new capability modules. Without evidence of pricing power or a path to margin expansion, I don't see the margin architecture that would support the kind of compounding returns I require.
