# IncidentFox -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the thing about IncidentFox that stops me cold: this is the single most consensus idea in enterprise AI right now. An AI agent that triages production incidents. There are *at least ten* startups doing exactly this -- Neubird with $22.5M from Microsoft, Cleric with $4.3M, Resolve, Causely, and half a dozen more catalogued in a landscape survey before IncidentFox even launched. Meanwhile, incident.io just raised $62M at a $400M valuation specifically to build AI agents that resolve incidents, and PagerDuty won "AIOps Platform of the Year." When I say I look for a sane person with an insane idea, the critical word is *insane*. The idea has to provoke genuine skepticism from most investors -- that's how you know the returns aren't already priced in. "AI should help debug production incidents" provokes zero skepticism from anyone. It's obvious. Everyone is racing toward it. That means even if you execute perfectly, you're fighting for market share in a commoditizing category against incumbents with vastly superior distribution.

The founders are credible -- I'll give them that. Jimmy Wei's Meta FAIR research on conversational AI and Long Yi's SRE work on Roblox's Stateful Infra team at 100M+ DAU is exactly the background you'd want for this product. They lived the on-call pain. They know the infrastructure. But credible founders pursuing a consensus idea is the wrong pairing in my framework -- it means the opportunity has already been identified and the market is efficiently pricing it. Compare this to Solana, where Anatoly Yakovenko's Qualcomm systems engineering background was the "sane" anchor, but building a new Layer 1 blockchain during crypto winter when everyone had written off the category was the "insane" part. IncidentFox's founders are sane. The idea is also sane. That's a problem.

My most fundamental objection, though, goes deeper than the crowding. IncidentFox fails my "cherry on top" test in the most direct way possible: if you remove the AI, there is literally no business. No physical operations, no proprietary infrastructure, no regulatory position, no protocol -- nothing. The entire value proposition IS the AI agent querying Datadog and Prometheus and summarizing what it finds. This is exactly the architecture I've been arguing is structurally indefensible. As foundation models improve -- and they will, rapidly -- the barrier to building "query logs, correlate alerts, and propose fixes" drops to near zero. The "auto-discovery of integrations" differentiator is a feature, not a moat; as MCP matures, any agent framework will auto-discover tools. The customer-specific learning that supposedly creates switching costs is just cached context about system topology that a competitor's agent can rebuild by reading the same codebases and incident history. Contrast this with Craftwork, where the painting crews and scheduling operations create a moat that no amount of AI progress can replicate. IncidentFox is software all the way down, and software moats are dissolving.

The strongest bull case would go something like this: the AI SRE category is going to produce a winner worth $5-10B, and IncidentFox's open-source approach (Apache 2.0 core, 413 stars, 1,003 commits) could create a developer-led distribution moat similar to GitLab or PostHog. The Slack-native interface meets teams where they already work, reducing adoption friction. Purpose-built AI-native architecture might genuinely outperform the bolt-on AI that PagerDuty and incident.io are layering onto human-coordination platforms. And the dual-license model (open core + BSL enterprise layer) is a proven monetization playbook. If IncidentFox wins the open-source standard for AI SRE agents, it could become the layer other tools integrate with, flipping from application to infrastructure. For this to work, though, you need the open-source community to rally around this specific project over ten competitors, the enterprise conversion funnel to materialize before the BSL 1.1 conversion date in 2030 opens the enterprise features to everyone, and you need PagerDuty and incident.io to stumble on execution despite having hundreds of millions in revenue and funding. That's a lot of things that need to go right in a market where the underlying AI capability is commoditizing monthly.

The company is pre-pilot -- "Accepting 2 Pilot Partners for Q1" -- with no revenue, no named customers, and no LOIs. That's normal at pre-seed, so I don't penalize it heavily, but it means the entire investment thesis rests on narrative conviction in a market where I have the opposite conviction. 413 GitHub stars is genuine developer interest but not the kind of breakout signal that suggests organic pull. There's no infinity story here that I can trace -- the narrative ceiling is "AI incident management tool," which is a real but bounded category, and the company sits on top of everyone else's infrastructure (AWS, Datadog, PagerDuty, Kubernetes) rather than creating a new layer that others build on. This is an application, not a protocol. It compounds linearly through sales effort, not exponentially through network effects.

I respect the founders' domain experience and technical chops. In a different market structure -- if this were 2019 and no one else was thinking about AI for incident management -- the pairing might be interesting. But in early 2026, this is the most crowded, most consensus, most obviously-commoditizable corner of enterprise AI. It hits three of my explicit avoid patterns simultaneously: AI-first company where AI is the core value proposition, a hot market where the pitch leads with TAM, and a SaaS-like business model as the primary monetization. Pass.
