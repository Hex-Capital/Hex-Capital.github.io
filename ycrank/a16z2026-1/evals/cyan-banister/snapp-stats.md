# Snapp Stats -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Shawn Tsao built Caviar from scratch and rode it through a $100M+ acquisition by Square and then a $410M sale to DoorDash. That's a real exit, real survival, real entrepreneurial forge. When I hear "co-founder of Caviar," my ears perk up — I know what it takes to build a consumer marketplace in food delivery against ruthless competition. But then I ask my question — the one that surprises founders — tell me your story, tell me why *this* problem, and the thread snaps. Caviar was food delivery. Munch On Me was daily food deals. Umai Hospitality Group is restaurants. Shawn's entire biographical arc is food and hospitality. And now he's building an AI sports video agent? His investment in The Town FC — an Oakland soccer club — is the only sports data point in the entire dossier. That's a hobby check, not a biographical forge. Alex Marshall built AI for the Pentagon's $160B allocation model and did data science at Genentech — impressive credentials, but those are healthcare and defense, not sports. Andrew Tamura's background is engineering across Karat, Scoop, Mixmax — general startup infrastructure. Nobody on this team has a story where sports video analysis found *them*. This looks like four capable people who spotted a market gap, and I've seen that movie before. The Caviar exit buys credibility but not inevitability.

When I run the invisible system test on Snapp Stats, I come up empty. Coaches scrubbing through game film is tedious — nobody disputes that. But tedious is not opaque. There's no hidden tax being levied on athletes who don't understand the system. There's no information asymmetry being exploited by gatekeepers. Compare this to what Affirm does to credit card opacity, or what Ownwell does to the property tax system — those companies make a structurally extractive system visible for the first time. Snapp Stats makes a slow workflow faster. That's a productivity tool, not a system disruption. The fan-facing "personalized sports feed" positioning is even further from my framework — fans already have ESPN, The Athletic, Twitter, YouTube highlights. The content isn't hidden; it's fragmented. Fragmentation is an inconvenience, not an injustice.

Sports plus AI is the kind of pitch that makes a room of VCs nod along enthusiastically — everyone watches sports, everyone understands AI is transforming media, the TAM slides write themselves ($10.6B growing to $49.9B per Grand View Research). That consensus enthusiasm is precisely what concerns me. My biggest wins came from spaces where VCs shifted in their chairs: defense tech with Anduril, surveillance cameras with Flock Safety, male birth control with Contraline. Nobody shifts in their chair when you say "AI for sports highlights." WSC Sports raised $136M doing AI highlight generation. Genius Sports is public at $670M revenue. Sportradar clears a billion. The money is already here, the incumbents are already building, and Microsoft just partnered with the English Premier League on an AI Companion. Snapp Stats argues these incumbents serve broadcasters and sportsbooks, not coaches and athletes — and that business model conflict creates an opening. That's a reasonable strategic argument, but it's a market positioning argument, not a contrarian conviction. The coach/athlete segment is underserved because it's smaller and harder to monetize, not because other investors lack the courage to enter it.

The bull case I have to confront honestly: Shawn Tsao knows how to build consumer products that get acquired. He did it once with Caviar, identifying an underserved niche (premium restaurant delivery) within a crowded market and building something acquirable. If Snapp Stats can own the coach-and-athlete video analysis niche the way Caviar owned high-end food delivery, the playbook is proven. The LVMM with persistent, searchable memory across footage *could* become a compounding data asset. The a16z Speedrun acceptance and at least one closed customer with 10x pipeline growth are real early signals. And the timing argument — multimodal AI crossed a performance threshold in 2024-2025, agentic frameworks like LangChain and Crew.AI matured, NCAA NIL rules increased demand for performance analytics — is plausible. For this to work, Snapp Stats would need to get deeply embedded in college coaching workflows before GPT-4o or Gemini adds sports video understanding as a feature, and before WSC Sports or Genius Sports decides the coach/athlete segment is worth building a new sales channel for. That's a narrow window requiring flawless execution.

The defensibility question makes me uncomfortable. Snapp Stats' tech stack — Python, TensorFlow, LangChain, Crew.AI on standard cloud infrastructure — is replicable. The dossier itself notes "the barrier is not structural." The dual B2C/B2B positioning with six people feels like Snapp Stats hasn't yet decided what it's building — a consumer sports feed or a coaching tool — and that ambiguity at pre-seed with four co-founders (whose equity split is undisclosed) recalls patterns I've been burned by. The CTO Andrew Tamura's LinkedIn still showing Rosie AI as his current role adds a note of uncertainty about full commitment. These aren't fatal individually, but they compound.

My decision on Snapp Stats comes down to two dimensions where the evidence is clear. The biographical forge is strong in general but absent for this specific problem — Shawn Tsao is a proven builder, but nothing in his life story made AI sports video the thing he couldn't walk away from. And the space is consensus-attractive in a way that directly conflicts with how I source my best bets. I need the room to be uncomfortable. I need the idea to sound weird on first hearing. "AI agent for sports video" sounds great on first hearing, and that's the problem. I wish this team well — they have the talent to build something real — but this isn't a Long Journey bet for me.
