# Silmaril -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

$1.4 billion. That's what Palo Alto Networks, Cisco, SentinelOne, Check Point, and F5 collectively paid to acquire five AI security startups in 2024-2025. And the pitch for Silmaril is essentially: "all those companies are now trapped inside big platform bureaucracies, the field is cleared, and we're the next independent player." Here's my problem with that narrative. When the market pays $1.4B for five companies in one category in eighteen months, that's not a contrarian opportunity -- that's the definition of consensus. Every cybersecurity-focused LP, every enterprise-focused seed fund, every YC partner is looking at that acquisition wave and thinking "fund the next Lakera." Garry Tan personally shared Silmaril's launch post. The YC LinkedIn post got 1,250 reactions and 235 comments. Nobody is uncomfortable with this bet. Nobody thinks AI security is a bad idea. And when nobody thinks you're wrong, the risk isn't in the price -- the upside is already in the price.

Silmaril's "multihead classifier" that evaluates user intent, application context, and execution states is technically sound -- but it's a better mousetrap, not a new verb. Lakera was doing 98%+ detection before Check Point bought them. Prompt Security had "multi-million ARR" before SentinelOne ate them. Straiker AI raised $21M from Lightspeed for essentially the same dual red-team-plus-runtime architecture. Silmaril claims 99% detection versus 50% for "leading guardrails," but security professionals in the LinkedIn comments already flagged "validation methodology and deterministic reproducibility" concerns. The self-healing RL-based approach is a technical improvement on static pattern matching, yes. But Venmo created peer-to-peer mobile payments as a behavior that didn't exist. Solana proposed a fundamentally new consensus mechanism. Silmaril proposes... faster, more adaptive input classification. That's execution improvement in an existing category, not primitive creation. Nobody will say "Silmaril me" or use Silmaril as shorthand for a new behavioral pattern.

Aum Upadhyay built AWS's security and privacy framework preventing "$1.8B in damages." Eduardo Velasco built low-latency ML models generating "$400M in annual revenue" at Amazon. These are serious credentials -- exactly the kind of people you want building a security classifier. But that's the issue with my sane/insane framework: Silmaril's founders are sane people pitching a sane idea. Two ex-Amazon engineers building enterprise AI security tools is the most obvious, sensible thing you could do in 2026. The ratio is off. I want the sane engineer who tells me they're going to create income-share agreements with YouTubers or build an employee-owned small business rollup or launch a new Layer 1 blockchain when everyone says Ethereum won. Silmaril's pitch makes immediate sense to everyone -- and that should worry them, not reassure them.

Silmaril fails my AI kill test from both directions simultaneously. Direction one: OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google are all investing in native safety layers. If model providers ship effective built-in prompt injection defense -- and they have every incentive to, because "our models are secure" is a selling point -- the third-party guardrail market shrinks. Direction two: Palo Alto Networks now owns Protect AI ($500M acquisition). SentinelOne owns Prompt Security ($180M). Check Point owns Lakera. CrowdStrike is inevitably next. Silmaril is pure software with a 5-line SDK integration -- and as the dossier notes, "the 5-line integration is lightweight in both directions." That's not a moat. That's a feature request. The core classification task is, by the dossier's own admission, "well-understood in ML and could be replicated by well-resourced teams." Silmaril has no physical-world operations, no protocol-level lock-in, no network effects that compound over time. The data flywheel from autonomous red-teaming is real but thin -- every customer deployment adds attack patterns, sure, but any well-funded competitor running their own red-team simulations gets to the same place.

The strongest bull case for Silmaril goes like this: post-acquisition innovation death is real, the five acquired companies will calcify inside their parent platforms, agentic AI creates fundamentally new attack surfaces (MCP, tool-calling, multi-agent confused-deputy patterns) that static guardrails can't handle, and Silmaril's RL-based self-healing approach is genuinely architecturally superior. The "$28M in damages prevented" testimonial in two months suggests real enterprise value. And the timing is perfect -- the market for standalone AI security vendors has been cleared of competition. I've checked my known exceptions: Slack overrode consensus mispricing because it was creating a new behavioral primitive. Silmaril doesn't qualify -- it's not creating a new behavior, it's defending against attacks on existing AI behaviors. No exception applies. The one condition that flips me: if Silmaril showed me $500K+ ARR in the next six months with 3+ enterprise contracts, proving that the post-acquisition gap is real and enterprises are actively buying standalone despite having Palo Alto and SentinelOne in their stack -- that would demonstrate mispricing I'm currently not seeing.

Silmaril is a well-built product from qualified founders in a market that has already been correctly priced by everyone. Four of my five red flags fire: AI as the product not a feature, better version of existing category, pure software with no structural moat, and consensus-exciting among other investors. The dimensions that kill this for me are Consensus Mispricing (AI security is the opposite of mispriced -- it's the consensus bet of 2026 cybersecurity) and Structural Durability (pure software classifier, low switching costs, squeezed between LLM providers building native defenses above and security platforms bundling acquired capabilities below). Pass.
