# Pulsent -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Toplyne raised $17.5 million from Tiger Global and Sequoia Capital to identify PLG conversion signals for B2B SaaS companies, then shut down and returned capital to investors after failing to reach product-market fit. Pulsent occupies the same category — watching user behavior in B2B SaaS products and converting that observation into sales action — with one modification: where Toplyne surfaced signals for human sales reps, Pulsent deploys AI agents that execute autonomously across email, LinkedIn, and WhatsApp. The question my framework demands is whether autonomous execution transforms a failed category into a monopoly, or whether Pulsent has simply attached a new engine to a car that already proved it has nowhere to drive. The structural problems that killed Toplyne — integration complexity across fragmented data sources, low buyer willingness to pay for conversion tooling, and the ever-present threat of incumbents adding the same feature — are not execution problems. They are category problems. Pulsent inherits every one of them.

Pulsent's stated secret is that "advances in visual AI (December 2025)" now allow agents to watch user sessions and understand behavior "at human or better level." This is not a secret. It is a capability announcement — a technological development available to every company with access to the same foundation models. Intercom's Fin AI agent is already approaching approximately $100 million in ARR. Pendo surpassed $200 million ARR and is actively shipping AI features. When I ask "what important truth do very few people agree with you on?", the answer cannot be "AI can improve customer engagement" — every investor, every incumbent, and every McKinsey slide deck agrees with that proposition. The unification of analytics and engagement platforms is similarly obvious; both Intercom and Pendo recognize the gap and have engineering teams a hundred times Pulsent's size building toward it. A correct observation that everyone shares is not a secret — it is consensus. And consensus cannot produce monopoly.

Nicolò Magnante and Arseniy Shishaev co-founded Bluco together in 2024, an AI recruitment startup that won a €25,000 AXA grant and secured clients including Porsche and Trenord railways. They then pivoted to Pulsent in early 2026 — the company was previously called "Swish" before another rename. The shared founding history is genuine; the YC description states they have worked together for three-plus years, which satisfies my requirement that co-founders share a prehistory forged under pressure. But the pivot sequence — AI recruitment to AI user engagement in under two years — reveals the opposite of definite conviction. Pulsent's founders are searching for a market that works, not executing a bold specific plan they have staked irreversible commitments on. The YC description's reference to "strong demand from PLG B2B Clients" is the language of market discovery, not the language of someone who knows exactly what the world looks like in ten years when they have won. Arseniy's Datadog experience handling hundreds of millions of data assets is directly relevant technical background, and I do not dismiss it — but technical competence deployed in service of an indefinite search is execution capacity without strategic conviction.

The bull case for Pulsent would proceed as follows: Toplyne's failure cleared the category of competitors and investor interest, creating precisely the conditions under which a contrarian bet could work. Pulsent's autonomous execution — agents that act, not dashboards that report — represents a genuine discontinuity enabled by the December 2025 step-function in multimodal AI capabilities. The three paying customers (Pletor, Leadbay from YC F25, Basalt) demonstrate early pull, and the claimed hundreds of millions of daily events suggest real infrastructure already built. If Pulsent accumulates per-customer behavioral models that compound over time, integration switching costs plus a learning data moat could produce defensibility that Toplyne never achieved. This is a legitimate argument. But it fails my framework at the structural level: even if Pulsent executes flawlessly, what prevents Intercom — with $343 million in revenue and an AI agent already generating close to $100 million ARR — from adding proactive conversion to its reactive support platform? What prevents Pendo — at $300 million revenue with deep product-analytics data — from shipping cross-channel engagement? Pulsent's core components — LLM-powered message generation, event stream ingestion, session replay analysis — are commercial APIs and open-source tools assembled into an integration layer. I check my Known Exceptions: the Lyft exception requires that the category generate such enormous total value that even the number-two player produces fund-relevant returns, but customer engagement software is not creating new economic value at ride-sharing scale. The Stemcentrx exception requires a genuinely contrarian scientific thesis — Pulsent has no such thesis. No exception applies.

Pulsent's power-law ceiling concerns me independently of the monopoly question. The customer engagement solutions market at $25 to $29 billion grows at ten to eleven percent CAGR — a large, mature, fragmented category structurally resistant to winner-take-all dynamics. Even if Pulsent captures the AI-powered PLG conversion niche, the realistic best case is a $200 to $500 million acquisition by Intercom, Pendo, or a CRM platform seeking to fill its engagement gap. That is a perfectly respectable outcome for the founders but structurally irrelevant to power-law portfolio construction. The company that returns the entire fund must be capable of reaching $10 billion or more in independent value, and Pulsent's category offers no path to that magnitude. Meanwhile, Pulsent is a pure software layer with zero physical-world technology barrier — no hardware, no regulatory moat, no government customer embedding. A well-funded competitor could rebuild Pulsent's integration layer in months with the same APIs and the same foundation models.

The condition that would flip me to Invest is specific and verifiable: Pulsent would need to demonstrate that its per-customer behavioral models create measurably superior conversion outcomes that cannot be replicated by Intercom or Pendo using the same underlying AI capabilities — evidence of a genuine proprietary data moat, not an integration advantage. Without that evidence, Pulsent is a competent team applying consensus AI capabilities to a competitive software category where a well-funded predecessor already failed. The monopoly secret is absent. The founder conviction reads as iterative rather than definite. The power-law magnitude is capped by category structure. Competition is for losers, and Pulsent has entered a market defined by competition.
