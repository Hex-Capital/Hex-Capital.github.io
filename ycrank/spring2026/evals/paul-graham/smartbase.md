# Smartbase -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Smartbase is a parachute-into-schlep company: the dimension where it places highest is the schlep zone — ITAR/CMMC/DFARS compliance, handwritten POs, faxes, and nine ERPs from SYSPRO to Infor to Sage X3 — and the dimension where it places lowest is organic founder-pain, because Sam Goldman ran a DevOps platform and Taira Fujioka was an SDE 2 on AWS SageMaker Inference, neither of which involves anyone re-keying a paper PO at a metal finishing shop. That high/low pairing tells me what Smartbase actually is: founders who went looking for a Stripe-shaped wedge and found one that is genuinely ugly, then tried to manufacture organic-idea fit by sleeping at a factory.

Coinbase (2012) is the right HIGH rung for Smartbase on the schlep dimension, because Brian Armstrong chose bitcoin compliance precisely because every other smart founder was unconsciously routing around it — the grind became the moat. Smartbase has the same shape on paper: handwritten PO extraction for defense-supply-chain shops requires a U.S.-hosted ITAR enclave, line-item accuracy under DFARS, and nine ERP integrations that no LLM-and-an-API-key competitor will bother to write. Where Smartbase diverges from the Coinbase analogy is that the schlep is named in the dossier but not yet survived — Smartbase has "first customers" on five-figure ACVs, not a thousand shops through ITAR audit. The Coinbase rung is earned by the grind one has already absorbed; Smartbase has identified the grind and walked toward it, which is two-thirds of the way there.

The Pass Example failure mechanism is "no founder has personally suffered the problem" — the MBA AI-wrapper team I'd reject for pitching me a market they read about. Smartbase shows that pattern partially: Sam Goldman's prior $2M DevOps platform and Taira Fujioka's SageMaker Inference role at AWS do not put either founder anywhere near a metal finishing shop's fax machine. The dossier's compensating evidence is the line that Smartbase founders "worked on factory floors, sat side-by-side with employees — even slept at a factory," which is the right unscalable move, but it is field research, not the Drew Houston USB stick. Houston forgot his USB stick because he was Drew Houston; the Collisons hacked commerce because they had been hacking commerce since they were teenagers. Smartbase parachuted in, which is honest and animal but not organic.

On animal resourcefulness, the mechanism that matters over 18 months is whether sleeping-at-a-factory generalizes into the next ten unpleasant tasks Smartbase will face — chasing a CFO at a 40-person shop in Ohio for an ACH, debugging a SYSPRO field mapping at 2am, getting a defense prime to sign a data-processing agreement. Sam Goldman and Taira Fujioka have already pivoted once — Anytrace YC S25 to Smartbase YC P26 — and got re-admitted, which is a smaller-scale Kan/Shear signal that the YC partners (Harj Taggar here) think these two will keep swinging. That is animal behavior. The factory-sleep stunt and the pivot-and-return together pin Smartbase comfortably above the MID rung on this axis.

On hacker genealogy, the counterfactual: for Smartbase to clear the HIGH rung on this dimension, I'd want a public GitHub trail, a Hacker News presence, or a competitive-programming marker from Taira Fujioka — none of which the dossier surfaces. "Youngest SDE 2 at AWS SageMaker Inference" is a credential of the kind Patrick Collison would have lapped at sixteen, not a hacker tell. Smartbase clears the floor (real infra background, prior shipped YC product in Anytrace) but does not clear the Collison/Swartz bar. This is mid-low and would only flip a tier if Taira Fujioka turned out to have shipped a non-trivial open-source inference library under another handle.

On co-founder configuration, Smartbase parallels Twitch (2007) on one specific axis: Justin Kan and Emmett Shear came through Kiko (2005), failed, sold it on eBay, and were re-funded as a known pair. Sam Goldman and Taira Fujioka came through Anytrace (YC S25) and pivoted into Smartbase (YC P26) as a known pair. That is the signal I care about more than chemistry — they have shipped together, raised together, and chose to keep going together. Two founders, complementary (CEO with a fundraising rep, CTO with infra credentials), prior co-working established. Solid on this axis.

My 36-month forecast for Smartbase: reaches $2–5M ARR concentrated in metal finishing and a small defense-supply tail, then either gets bought by Epicor or Acumatica for under $50M as a feature, or pivots horizontally into general SMB manufacturing PO automation where it collides head-on with Leverage AI ($14.9M raised) and Turian ($3.78M raised) and loses the wedge advantage. This is consistent with the signature: a real schlep produces real revenue, but parachuted founders rarely produce the No.-1-portfolio-company outcome that "Black Swan Farming" (September 2012) is hunting.

The strongest objection is the Coinbase Known Exception: Brian Armstrong wasn't a banker either — he was an Airbnb engineer who picked the bitcoin compliance schlep, and the schlep itself was the moat. By that read, Smartbase's parachute-in is not disqualifying — Sam Goldman and Taira Fujioka picked a regulated, unpleasant problem with a 9-ERP integration matrix, and the question is just whether they have the animal disposition Armstrong had. The dossier evidence that wins against this objection is that Armstrong was a bitcoin user before he was a Coinbase founder — he had organic exposure that Smartbase's two founders demonstrably lack on metal finishing. The verifiable condition that would flip my verdict: five paying metal-finishing customers at $40K+ ACV with one named defense-prime LOI within six months — at that point Smartbase has survived enough of the schlep to count as a Coinbase-shaped bet rather than a Coinbase-shaped pitch.
