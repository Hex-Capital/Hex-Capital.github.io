# Nine Fives -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Noah Levy spent years building satellite RF hardware at SpaceX. Andrew Kurtz worked on Falcon 9 payloads and test automation at the same company. Both founders then decided to channel that experience into manufacturing programmable attenuators and switches — instruments that sell for $500 to $5,000 apiece in a market the dossier itself estimates at "the low hundreds of millions." This is the central tension of Nine Fives: the founder profile activates nearly every pattern I look for — SpaceX alumni with shared prehistory, atoms-over-bits conviction, hardware engineering in a stagnant industry — while the market structure negates the one criterion that cannot be negotiated away. The power law governs all returns, and programmable RF attenuators are not a power-law category.

Nine Fives' implicit secret — that Keysight's $5.4 billion business and National Instruments' $8.2 billion acquisition price depend on proprietary driver ecosystems that cannot survive the shift to headless, API-first test automation — has real structural merit. When incumbents derive high margins from software licensing and vendor lock-in, unbundling with zero-install REST API instruments creates a genuine business model conflict: Keysight cannot respond without cannibalizing the proprietary stack that funds its R&D. Mini-Circuits, with 60,000+ catalog products, structurally deprioritizes software UX for any individual product line. These are real dynamics. But the insight fails my monopoly test at the critical juncture. Adding a REST API to an existing attenuator is, as the dossier notes, "an engineering project, not a fundamental barrier." Nine Fives' differentiation is a software layer atop commodity RF hardware — the very category of moat I find least durable. There are no network effects between instruments on a test bench, no data advantages from deployment scale, no platform dynamics that compound. Each sale is independent, each customer can be poached, and the switching cost — rewriting test automation scripts — is moderate, not structural. This is not a conspiracy to change the world; it is a better mousetrap in a market that can accommodate many mousetraps simultaneously.

Kurtz (MIT, Aurora Flight Sciences) and Levy (Cornell, SpaceX Senior RF Engineer) represent exactly the founder archetype I weight most heavily: deep technical operators from a high-intensity shared environment who experienced the problem firsthand and decided to build the solution. Two shipping products with published datasheets at pre-seed is genuine execution, not PowerPoint vaporware. The co-founder relationship — overlapping SpaceX tenures, complementary mechanical and RF engineering disciplines — satisfies my requirement that "founders should share a prehistory before they start a company together." If I were evaluating Nine Fives on founder quality alone, the signal would be strong. But my framework explicitly rejects the premise that brilliant, determined founders pursuing a competitive market can escape the structural logic of competition. The RF test instrument market has four named competitors in this dossier alone — Mini-Circuits, Vaunix, Adaura, Keysight — and none of them is going away. Palmer Luckey left Oculus to build Anduril because autonomous defense was a category where monopoly was structurally achievable through government embedding; Nine Fives' founders left SpaceX to enter a market that is definitionally fragmented.

The power-law math is the dispositive failure. Nine Fives operates in a serviceable market likely below $500 million. Even aggressive capture — 20% of a $500 million addressable market — produces $100 million in revenue, which at hardware multiples supports perhaps a $1-2 billion valuation in the best case. That outcome is irrelevant to fund-level returns. The broader RF test equipment market ($3.99 billion in 2025, projected $8.48 billion by 2034) does not change this arithmetic because Nine Fives sells individual instruments, not platforms — there is no mechanism by which dominating attenuators and switches expands into oscilloscopes or spectrum analyzers without becoming a different company entirely. The 6 GHz frequency ceiling on both current products, against Vaunix's 40 GHz and Keysight's 50+ GHz capabilities, further constrains the addressable slice. I could construct an argument that Nine Fives becomes a platform — "the Stripe of test equipment," where the API layer becomes the standard integration point — but the dossier contains no evidence the founders are articulating or pursuing that vision. Without a definite plan for platform monopoly, this remains an instrument company with linear revenue scaling.

The strongest bull case an advocate would make: Nine Fives is attacking an industry frozen since the 1970s (GPIB dates to 1965) with the DevOps-for-hardware-test thesis, led by SpaceX-caliber founders building real atoms in a sector adjacent to defense and aerospace. The Open RAN and 5G testbed buildout creates a time-sensitive window. If Nine Fives captures the "API-first networked instrument" category before incumbents modernize, it becomes the default specification in automated test benches across defense, telecom, and manufacturing — and from that beachhead, expands into a full instrument platform with government contracts as anchor revenue. The Palantir parallel would apply: embed the API standard into enough test systems that switching costs compound until displacement is structurally impossible. I take this seriously. But it requires Nine Fives to execute a platform transition from two instruments at 6 GHz to a comprehensive, multi-band, multi-instrument ecosystem — and to do so before Mini-Circuits ($111M-$400M estimated revenue) decides to add REST endpoints to its existing catalog. No evidence in the dossier suggests the founders have articulated this platform vision or that they possess the capital to execute it. The condition that would flip me to Invest: a signed government contract or defense prime LOI specifying Nine Fives instruments for an automated test program, combined with a public articulation of a platform roadmap extending to mmWave frequencies and additional instrument categories. Government embedding would change the monopoly calculus entirely.

Nine Fives scores well on exactly one dimension — physical-world technology with SpaceX-pedigreed founders building real hardware — and falls short on the two dimensions that determine whether I write a check: monopoly secret and power-law magnitude. The secret is real but replicable; the market is real but small. A company that could become a $500 million instrument business is not a company that returns a fund, and the power law does not grade on effort. Pass.
