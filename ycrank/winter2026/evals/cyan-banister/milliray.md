# Milliray (formerly DroneTector) -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that jumps out at me about Milliray isn't the technology or the team -- it's the market temperature. Counter-drone was a lonely, weird space three years ago. Today, DroneShield just posted 276% revenue growth to AUD 216 million. Dedrone got scooped up by Axon. Robin Radar secured PE backing and has 200 units deployed in Ukraine. Over a billion dollars in counter-drone purchases closed in a single month last December. When I see that kind of heat, my instinct is to move in the opposite direction. The most promising companies are not popular, and counter-drone has become very popular, very fast. Now -- the 94 GHz millimeter-wave niche is genuinely different from what the crowd is chasing. Most of these players are working at lower frequencies or relying on RF signal detection. Building purpose-designed hardware for nano-drones that slip past everything else is a specific, technical bet within a broad market. But I can't pretend the broader category still has that quality of being dismissed and misunderstood. The window of weirdness has mostly closed here.

When I try to do what I always do first -- ask these founders to tell me their story, not the business -- I come up empty. What I can see is three accomplished PhDs from elite UK universities. Matthew Moore built the exact 94 GHz radar system during his doctoral research at St Andrews and published peer-reviewed work on drone micro-Doppler simulation. That's genuinely impressive founder-technology alignment. Thomas Doherty brings deep optics and laser physics from Oxford's quantum computing group. Jordina Frances de Mas contributes automated reasoning and ML from her St Andrews PhD. These are the right technical minds for this problem. But I don't hear a survival story. I don't feel the biographical thread that tells me why these specific people will persist through the years of defense procurement hell, the manufacturing nightmares, the moments when the company nearly dies. The founders I back tend to be solving problems that were forged in their own lived experience -- Travis at Uber had that gravitas born from years of fighting, the Crusoe team had independently stumbled onto something that sounded insane because they'd been living in the weird intersection of energy waste and computation. Academic spin-outs can absolutely succeed, but they don't activate the part of my pattern recognition that's made my best investments.

The CTO situation gives me real pause, and it connects to my deepest scar as an investor. Thomas Doherty is listed as CEO of Quantum Fabrix, an ion trap quantum computing venture, while serving as CTO of a three-person startup. I learned from HQ Trivia that even perfect timing and a transformative product can't survive when the founding team's commitment is fractured. A three-person deep-tech hardware company needs every founder operating at full intensity. If Doherty is splitting attention between millimeter-wave radar and quantum computing, that's not a co-founder conflict in the HQ Trivia sense, but it's a commitment risk that sits in the same category. I'd need to hear directly from the team about how this is structured before I could get comfortable. And beyond the CTO issue, I have no evidence of how these three handle disagreement under pressure. An academic relationship at St Andrews and a shared fellowship program is not the same as having survived a crisis together.

I want to be honest about the bull case, because there is one. If I set aside my biographical filter, the technical moat here is real. Moore didn't just study drone detection theoretically -- he physically built and tested 94 GHz Doppler radar systems and published the results. The physics works. NATO DIANA and DASA backing provide institutional beachheads that most pre-seed startups would kill for, and in defense procurement, those relationships compound. European sovereignty concerns create structural demand for a UK-based radar provider that doesn't depend on American systems. And the specific problem they're targeting -- the smallest, lowest-signature drones that slip past existing detection -- is exactly where the gap is widest and growing fastest. If they can scale from research prototype to production hardware with competitive unit economics, they could become the detection layer of choice for the hardest targets. This is the kind of hard-space infrastructure play where navigating regulatory complexity becomes a moat, and that's a pattern I've backed before with Anduril and IronPort. The technology risk is present but bounded, not requiring multiple miracles.

Where Milliray doesn't fit my portfolio pattern is on economic access and real-world impact for underserved populations. This is a company selling sophisticated hardware to defense ministries and critical infrastructure operators -- important work, but not the kind of positive-sum economic opportunity that drew me to Uber creating income for drivers or Affirm providing transparent credit. There's a safety impact argument to be made, and I do see my pattern of making invisible systems visible at work here -- drone threats are invisible until you have the technology to detect them. But the beneficiaries are institutions with large budgets, not people who've been locked out of economic participation. That doesn't make it a bad company. It just means it doesn't pull on the thread that gets me most excited about writing a check with my own money.

This is a technically strong team building real hardware in a growing market with institutional validation. In many investors' frameworks, that's a compelling pre-seed bet. In mine, the absence of a founder survival narrative, the evaporation of anti-consensus positioning as the market heats up, the CTO's divided attention, and the lack of economic access impact all add up to a pass -- not because anything is broken, but because this isn't the kind of bet where my specific pattern recognition gives me an edge. I'd want to meet these founders and hear their stories directly before completely closing the door, because the dossier may be hiding biographical depth that doesn't show up in LinkedIn profiles and publication records. But based on what I can see, this is someone else's great deal, not mine.
