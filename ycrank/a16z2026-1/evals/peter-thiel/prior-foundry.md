# Prior Foundry -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The most structurally revealing fact about Prior Foundry is Keshav Sivakumar's tenure as a Forward Deployed Software Engineer at Palantir -- because it illuminates both the promise and the fundamental gap in this company's trajectory. Palantir's monopoly was never built on superior algorithms; it was built on embedding proprietary software so deeply into CIA and Army workflows that extraction became operationally unthinkable. The switching costs compound annually because Palantir becomes the institution's nervous system, not a vendor producing deliverables. Prior Foundry's three-step workflow -- Research, Simulate, Act -- terminates in "decision-ready deliverables (memos, policy notes, charts)." That is consulting output, not embedded infrastructure. Keshav knows the Palantir playbook intimately. The question is whether Prior Foundry is executing that playbook or merely borrowing its vocabulary. Memos can be replaced next quarter. Embedded operating systems cannot be replaced in a decade.

What is Prior Foundry's secret -- the important truth that few agree with? The claimed insight is that LLM agents anchored in probability survey data can simulate how entire populations respond to policy decisions, replacing months of consulting firm analysis. This is not a secret. It is a published Stanford paper (arxiv 2411.10109, November 2024) demonstrating 85% accuracy, built on an open-source codebase (github.com/joonspk-research/genagents) accessible to anyone with a GitHub account. The General Social Survey, the World Values Survey, the European Social Survey -- these are public datasets. The LLM APIs are commoditized. When the foundational research, the data, and the compute are all open, there is no proprietary technology creating a 10x barrier. Prior Foundry possesses a head start, not a moat. A head start in a race is not a monopoly -- it is a temporary advantage in a competitive market, and competition destroys value.

Where Prior Foundry genuinely surprises is distribution. The OECD partnership spanning 38+ member governments, $60K ARR within two months of operation, and a reported pipeline exceeding $1M through a distributor -- at pre-seed, this is exceptional evidence of institutional pull. Shirin Abrishami Kashani's direct experience as an OECD analyst is not decorative credentialing; it is a distribution advantage rooted in relationships that took years to build. Government buyers do not adopt tools from strangers, and Shirin is not a stranger. The Allegheny County pilot adds a domestic anchor. If I were evaluating Prior Foundry solely on distribution architecture, this would score near the top of any pre-seed company I have seen. But distribution without monopoly structure creates a profitable consulting business, not a company that captures its category. McKinsey has extraordinary distribution and zero monopoly characteristics -- Prior Foundry risks becoming a faster, cheaper McKinsey for policy simulation rather than the singular platform that governments cannot operate without.

The power law test is where Prior Foundry's case weakens most decisively. The addressable market -- AI governance at $228M-$620M in 2024, projected to $3.6B-$7.4B by the early 2030s -- does not have the structure that produces fund-returning outcomes. Policy simulation is a niche within government technology, which is itself a niche within enterprise software. Prior Foundry could become a respected, profitable company valued at several hundred million dollars. That outcome is irrelevant to my portfolio construction. The power law demands that a single investment could return the entire fund; a $500M exit in the best case does not satisfy that criterion. I do not see the concentric expansion path -- the way Facebook moved from Harvard to all universities to all humans, or the way Palantir moved from CIA to Army to every enterprise with complex data. What is Prior Foundry's second act after government policy simulation? The category ceiling appears real.

The strongest bull case requires believing three things simultaneously: that Prior Foundry will evolve from producing reports to becoming embedded decision infrastructure within government agencies (the Palantir trajectory), that the OECD channel will convert 38 member governments into paying customers at enterprise scale, and that the simulation methodology will prove accurate enough for high-stakes decisions that governments cannot reverse. If all three are true, the Palantir parallel becomes more than cosmetic -- Prior Foundry becomes the operating system for evidence-based governance worldwide, and the market size question dissolves because the company would have created its own category. Jonne Kamphorst's dual appointment as Sciences Po Assistant Professor starting January 2026, while the company is headquartered in San Francisco, cuts against this narrative. A co-founder who maintains an academic career is hedging, not committing -- the opposite of the irreversible conviction that definite optimism demands.

Prior Foundry's verdict turns on two dimensions where the evidence is clear. The monopoly secret is absent: published research, public data, and commoditized infrastructure do not create structural dominance, regardless of how well the team executes. The power law magnitude is insufficient: the policy simulation market does not produce the extreme outcomes my framework requires. The distribution evidence through the OECD and Shirin's government network is genuinely strong and prevents this from being a clear pass -- but distribution excellence in a competitive, ceiling-limited market builds a good business, not a monopoly. Prior Foundry has the team composition to build something valuable. It does not have the structural characteristics I require to write a check.
