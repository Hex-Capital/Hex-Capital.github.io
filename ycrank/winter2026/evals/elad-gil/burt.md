# Burt -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most important thing about Burt is that it's entering a market where a well-funded winner is already emerging. HappyRobot — a YC S23 company — has raised $62M, sits at a ~$500M valuation, reports eight-figure revenue, and counts DHL, Werner, and Schneider as customers. When I wrote about "market-ending moves," this is the dynamic I described: a company with compounding distribution advantages — enterprise logos, integration depth, trained models on real logistics data — creates a gravitational pull that makes it progressively harder for new entrants. Burt is building AI agents for freight broker back-office tasks. HappyRobot is building AI agents for freight broker back-office tasks. The difference is HappyRobot has a two-year head start, 100x the capital, and enterprise relationships that create switching costs. This isn't like entering search after ten search engines existed — Google had a structural technical advantage. I don't see Burt's structural advantage here.

The market inflection is real. LLMs crossed the fidelity threshold for parsing unstructured logistics communications — emails, PDFs, carrier check calls — sometime in 2024. HappyRobot's reported 10x revenue growth since its December 2024 Series A validates genuine demand. The cyclical freight downturn is pressuring brokerages to cut back-office costs. All of this is true. But here's the problem: this is no longer a non-obvious market. Three YC W26 companies target freight brokers in the same batch (Burt, Lanesurf, Lighthouz AI). General Catalyst is backing Lanesurf. HappyRobot's raise made FreightWaves headlines. When I look for markets that trigger dismissive reactions from other investors — the way PagerDuty's alerting software seemed boring in 2012 — AI for logistics in 2026 is the opposite. It's consensus. Everyone sees this opportunity now, which means the asymmetric returns have already been claimed by whoever got there first.

The bull case I have to engage with honestly: HappyRobot is going enterprise. DHL, Werner, Schneider — these are massive carriers. Thousands of small-to-midsize freight brokerages operating on spreadsheets and phone calls may be completely ignored by HappyRobot's enterprise sales motion. Burt's "configurable AI teammate" approach could be the Gusto to HappyRobot's ADP — capturing the fragmented long tail. The back-office tasks they're automating (POD chasing, shipment tracking, invoice validation, load quoting) are concrete, measurable, and genuinely painful for small brokerages. If Burt could establish itself as the default mid-market solution with deep TMS integrations, the switching costs could compound into a real moat. That's a legitimate path. But it requires (a) founders who can sell to logistics operators they've never met before, in a relationship-driven industry where domain credibility is table stakes, and (b) a focused enough product to nail one workflow before expanding — and the broad task surface suggests the opposite of focus.

Neither founder has documented experience in freight or logistics. Bobby Zhong's background is UC Irvine CS, founding engineer at Pirros (YC W23), engineer at Replo (YC S21), plus a student support platform (Educado, ~7 months) and a web development agency (Moonwish Studios). Kurt Sharma studied EECS at Berkeley and also worked at Replo. They clearly have engineering talent and LLM experience. But the YC page title still references "Easily fine tune and deploy specialized models" — suggesting Burt started as a general-purpose model fine-tuning platform and recently pivoted to logistics-specific AI agents. This is the pattern I flag as a red flag: founders entering a trendy sector they weren't in six months ago. When I backed Harvey, the founders had identified legal AI as their target because they understood the legal workflow deeply. When I backed Anduril, the team understood defense procurement. Logistics brokerage is a trust-intensive business where operator credibility matters for the first sale and every sale after. Two engineers who built coding agents at Replo will need to learn an entirely new domain language, sales motion, and customer psychology — and they'll need to do it while competing against teams that already have it.

The product scope concerns me through the lens of my single-miracle framework. Burt is trying to automate POD chasing, shipment tracking, invoice validation, AND load quoting — four distinct workflows, each with its own integration requirements, edge cases, and customer expectations. Compare this to Lanesurf, which is laser-focused on voice AI for carrier rate negotiation, or Lighthouz, which specializes exclusively in freight bill auditing. Those companies have identified their one miracle and organized around it. Burt's breadth suggests the team hasn't yet figured out which of these four workflows is their wedge — the one they can do better than anyone else. For a two-person team, trying to be excellent at four things simultaneously means being mediocre at all of them.

The timing within the technology cycle is correct but not differentiated. LLM capabilities for logistics document parsing reached viability in 2024-2025, and the cost curves continue to improve. But Burt isn't building ahead of a capability curve the way Harvey was — they're building after the curve has been identified and exploited by HappyRobot, which saw it two years earlier. Being positioned at the right moment matters enormously, but being positioned at the right moment after your primary competitor has already captured the structural advantage reduces it to a commodity timing thesis that any competent team could execute.
