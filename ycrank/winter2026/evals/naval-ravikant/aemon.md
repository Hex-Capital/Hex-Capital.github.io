# Aemon -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The most interesting thing about Aemon is also its deepest vulnerability: Google DeepMind built AlphaEvolve, published it, and validated that LLM-powered evolutionary search can make genuine discoveries. Aemon's bet is that DeepMind won't productize this for external customers, creating a window for a three-person startup to own the commercial layer. This is a real structural insight — Google's research lab incentives don't align with selling optimization tools to enterprise R&D teams. But it's the same structural insight that any smart person reading the AlphaEvolve paper in May 2025 could reach in an afternoon. When your founding thesis can be derived from a blog post, you're in a speed race, not a knowledge race. And OpenEvolve is already on GitHub.

The founders are clearly talented — ICLR and EMNLP publications before turning twenty, international medals in math and robotics. That's raw intellectual horsepower. But in my framework, intelligence is a necessary condition, not a distinguishing one. The question I always ask first: what do these founders know that can't be taught? The answer here is concerning. They know how to do ML research at a high level for their age. That's a trainable, replicable skill — there are thousands of people worldwide who publish at ICLR. What I don't see is the non-transferable domain knowledge that would make them uniquely suited to this specific problem. Evan Williams spent a decade on Blogger before understanding what Twitter needed to be. Amjad Masad built coding tools for years before Replit. These founders saw a validated category and moved fast. Fast execution is valuable, but it's not specific knowledge — it's energy. High intelligence, high energy. The specific knowledge dimension is where the case thins.

The leverage architecture is genuinely interesting. If Aemon works as described — feed in a problem and an evaluation metric, get back production-ready code that outperforms human experts — that's computational R&D leverage that didn't exist commercially before. One AI system exploring hundreds of solution variants replaces months of human researcher iteration. That's the kind of leverage multiplication I look for: disproportionate output from minimal marginal input. The legal retrieval case study (recall jumping from 0.540 to 0.912) suggests real practical value beyond benchmark stunts. But the leverage is partially borrowed — it sits on top of frontier LLMs that Aemon doesn't control, which means the leverage ratio shifts as the underlying models improve for everyone.

The contrarian timing test is where this falls apart for me. Everyone already believes AI will transform R&D. Lila Sciences raised $550M. Periodic Labs raised $300M. Sakana AI is at $2.6B valuation. Cognition is at $10B. This isn't a category where smart people think you're wrong — it's a category where smart people are writing nine-figure checks on the same thesis. The circle packing record is a clever marketing move, but beating AlphaEvolve on one benchmark with $10 of compute doesn't constitute a durable advantage — it constitutes a press release. When I invested in Uber at $4M, ride-sharing was illegal. When I backed Bitcoin in 2014, respected economists called it a fraud. Aemon is riding a wave of validated consensus, not swimming against a current.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement: twin brothers with elite research credentials, first to market in commercializing evolutionary code generation, targeting a multi-trillion-dollar R&D market where the most capable system (AlphaEvolve) is locked inside Google. If they can accumulate domain-specific solution patterns across customer engagements — learning what works for chip design versus drug discovery versus financial modeling — that flywheel could create the kind of compounding knowledge infrastructure I've backed before (Stack Overflow being the cleanest analogy). And Google's structural reluctance to productize research tools is a real, durable moat-by-proxy. If Aemon becomes the default interface between enterprise R&D teams and evolutionary AI optimization, the switching costs compound. For this to work, they'd need to move fast enough to own the category before the open-source implementations mature and before Cognition or Sakana expand into their niche. It's possible. But it requires winning a race on execution without the specific knowledge advantage that typically distinguishes winners from fast followers.

I'd pass. The founders have intelligence and energy, but the specific knowledge that makes a founder irreplaceable for this particular problem isn't evident. The category is consensus, not contrarian. The technology sits on published research with an open-source implementation already available. And the enterprise R&D market requires relationship depth and domain credibility that twenty-year-old dropouts will need years to build — years during which better-capitalized competitors will be moving into the same space. I want to back founders who know something that can't be taught, building leverage that didn't exist before, at a time when most people think they're wrong. Aemon has the middle piece but not the bookends.
