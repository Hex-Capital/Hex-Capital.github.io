# Menza -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The most interesting thing about Menza is the gap between the founder's demonstrated capability and the company's ambition level. Qasim Munye built ShortlyAI as a self-taught ML practitioner while still in medical school, then sold it to what became Jasper -- a real exit, not a soft landing. That's the kind of entrepreneurial signal I take seriously. A doctor who teaches himself machine learning and ships a product good enough to get acquired has something that can't be trained. But here's the tension: his specific knowledge is in building AI products and getting them to market, not in understanding how DTC brands make decisions with data. He pivoted from AI writing to AI analytics -- a lateral move into an opportunity, not a deepening of obsession. When I backed Evan Williams, his specific knowledge was about the medium itself, about how humans wanted to express themselves publicly. Qasim's specific knowledge is about building AI products generally, which is valuable but transferable across categories. Mariam Ahmed's Goldman Sachs experience in reporting and portfolio optimization is closer to the domain but still adjacent -- institutional finance reporting and DTC brand analytics are different problem spaces. I don't see evidence that either founder has been living inside the consumer brand data problem long enough to see what others can't.

The leverage test is where this falls short for me. Menza replaces a data analyst with software -- that's labor substitution, not leverage creation. A DTC brand that previously needed a £300K data team can now get reports and alerts from an AI. Useful. But it doesn't give the brand a new capability that didn't exist before. Compare this to what Replit does: it doesn't just make coding cheaper, it makes entirely new people into builders. Or what AngelList did: it didn't just make fundraising more efficient, it created a new category of investor. Menza makes existing workflows cheaper. That's a real business but it's not the kind of leverage that compounds into something disproportionate. It's an application sitting on top of other people's infrastructure -- Shopify's API, Meta's API, Google's API -- which means the leverage actually belongs to those platforms, not to Menza.

The timing is consensus, not contrarian. "AI will automate data analysis" is the most popular pitch in enterprise software right now. ThoughtSpot is doing it. Julius AI raised $11M to do it. Every BI tool from Tableau to Power BI is adding natural language querying. Shopify itself is building Sidekick. When I invested in Uber, ride-sharing was illegal. When I invested in Bitcoin, it was dismissed as nerd money. Those were bets against a gloomy crowd. Menza is a bet with an enthusiastic crowd. The vertical focus on DTC brands is differentiated but not contrarian -- it's a standard wedge strategy that any smart team could identify after six months of market research. The dossier lists four direct competitors and several indirect ones, and none of them are dismissing this category.

The strongest bull case: Qasim's prior exit is genuinely rare at pre-seed, and the 12 named customers with specific outcomes (60 hours saved monthly at Evolve Beauty, 10% revenue leak detected at The Keto Shop) demonstrate real product-market resonance. The business model conflict argument is legitimate -- ThoughtSpot at $4.2B valuation can't economically pursue SMB DTC brands, which creates a protected niche. If Menza owns the analytics layer for every growing DTC brand and expands vertically, the integration switching costs could compound into a real moat. For this to be a great investment, the DTC analytics vertical would need to be larger than it appears, the AI layer would need to become genuinely proprietary through accumulated customer data, and the founders would need to resist the temptation to sell early to Shopify or a BI incumbent. I don't see enough evidence that any of these conditions are likely, but I respect the execution speed -- 12 paying customers from a three-person team is real.

My core objection is structural: this is an application-layer company in a consensus category, dependent on third-party APIs, competing against well-funded teams building similar capabilities. The technology -- LLM-powered querying, data connectors, anomaly detection -- is accessible to any well-resourced competitor. The 650+ integrations represent engineering hours, not compounding technology. I systematically favor infrastructure over applications, picks-and-shovels over the gold miners. Menza is a gold miner. A competent one with a prior exit and real customers, but a gold miner nonetheless. The ceiling here is a solid vertical SaaS exit, not the kind of compounding platform that generates asymmetric returns.
