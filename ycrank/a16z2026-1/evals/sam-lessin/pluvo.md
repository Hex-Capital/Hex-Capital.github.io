# Pluvo -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Pluvo is the platonic ideal of the deal I pass on. "AI Decision Engine for Modern Enterprise" -- every word in that tagline is a red flag in my framework. AI? Sustaining technology for incumbents. Decision engine? Feature, not company. Modern enterprise? The enterprise already has Anaplan, Datarails, and Abacum fighting over these exact buyers. When I see a company operating in the precise category where every sophisticated investor is writing checks -- Datarails just closed $70M in January 2026, Abacum pulled in $60M six months before that -- I know the upside is already in the price. There is no mispricing here. This is consensus, full stop. The question "what if they're right?" doesn't generate any interesting tension because everyone already believes AI agents for FP&A are valuable. The interesting question would be "what if they're wrong?" -- what if the incumbents eat this category before Pluvo gets to scale?

The AI kill test is where Pluvo dies on my whiteboard. Anaplan already has PlanIQ and CoPlanner. Datarails has 400+ employees, $550M valuation, and 70% year-over-year revenue growth -- they're explicitly adding AI to their Excel-overlay FP&A workflows with that $70M Series C. Abacum is tripling revenue with AI-native positioning across 31 countries. Every single incumbent in this space is looking at Pluvo's demo and thinking the exact thing I always warn about: "Great, we should add that feature." The "deterministic computation vs. LLM prediction" distinction Pluvo emphasizes on their site is a technical implementation choice, not a structural moat. Any competent engineering team can combine computation with LLM orchestration. The "institutional knowledge codification" angle could theoretically create switching costs, but that requires years of customer entrenchment that Datarails and Abacum already have a head start on.

Alexandre Labrèche is a former financial analyst and ex-Ford. Vanessa Galarneau has 10+ years in ML for finance at Norton Rose Fulbright. Andrew Ingram built the prior product at Rain Technologies. These are competent, domain-relevant people -- and that's the problem. They're sane people doing a sane thing. The idea of applying AI agents to FP&A variance analysis is not fucking crazy. It's the most obvious application imaginable in 2025-2026. Sixty-nine percent of CFOs already say AI is integral to their finance transformation. This is a team building exactly what everyone expects someone to build, in exactly the way everyone expects it to be built. The sanity-to-insanity ratio is all sanity, no insanity.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. $430K ARR in three months of commercial launch is real velocity -- that's not nothing. CFO angel investors like Greg Henry from 1Password and Charly Kevers from Carta writing checks into the seed round signals that the target buyer finds the product genuinely useful, not just demo-impressive. The pivot from Rain Technologies' Budget Manager to Pluvo shows the team has been in financial software since 2019, which is more depth than most AI-wrapper founders bring. And "dozens of customers across North America" suggests repeatable sales motion early. If Pluvo can somehow build switching costs faster than the well-funded competitors can ship comparable features -- if the "governed model" that codifies how each company works becomes genuinely painful to migrate -- then maybe they carve out a defensible mid-market niche. But that's a bet on execution speed against competitors with 20-30x more capital and established customer bases, in a category with no structural barrier beyond running faster. That's not a contrarian bet. That's a footrace where you start behind.

The comparison that keeps surfacing for me is what Pluvo is not. Venmo created a behavior that didn't exist -- peer-to-peer mobile payments became a verb. Solana was a new consensus primitive in a category the market had written off. Pluvo is a better tool in an existing category that the market is enthusiastically funding. Nobody is going to say "Pluvo me" -- they're going to say "run the AI analysis" regardless of which platform they use. There's no new primitive here, just execution on an obvious application of widely available technology. The decisive dimensions are consensus mispricing and structural durability: Pluvo operates in the hottest AI enterprise category of 2026 with well-funded competitors already building the same thing, and the AI kill test is fatal because every incumbent with distribution can replicate agentic FP&A analysis as a feature addition. Pass.
