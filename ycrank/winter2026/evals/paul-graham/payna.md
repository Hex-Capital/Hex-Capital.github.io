# Payna -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice is that Brico already exists. This is not a problem hiding in plain sight — it's a problem that has already been found, funded to the tune of $21.6 million, recognized by Forbes, and is being solved for marquee customers like Marqeta and BitGo. When Stripe went after payment processing in 2010, every programmer knew payments were broken but nobody was building the fix. That was schlep blindness. State-by-state licensing compliance is genuinely schlepy — fifty jurisdictions, examiner playbooks, FinCEN requirements, the whole tedious regulatory apparatus — but the blindness has already been cured. Payna isn't walking into an empty field that everyone else unconsciously avoids. They're walking into a field where someone is already farming it successfully with a 600% year-over-year growth rate.

How did these founders find this problem? Aaron Chen was the sole designer at Axal, an a16z CSX-backed crypto startup. He won hackathon bounties. He was selected for the Ritual Foundation Fellowship. Two months before the YC batch started, the team built "Paena," a consumer DeFi earned-wage-access platform at ETHGlobal Buenos Aires. Then they pivoted to B2B compliance infrastructure. The trajectory is clear: they had a crypto product that didn't work, they looked around for what to build next, and they spotted a growing regulatory market. This is exactly the pattern I've warned about — the idea was conceived in a brainstorming session, not stumbled into through lived pain. Nobody on this team has documented experience navigating NMLS procedures, managing money transmitter license renewals, or sitting across from a state examiner. The problem is real. The discovery path is not organic.

The co-founder situation concerns me independently. He Song — one of two co-founders — has virtually no public footprint. No findable education, no work history, no GitHub, no indexable LinkedIn profile. I can't evaluate someone I can't see. And the relationship between the co-founders is opaque — no shared employer, no shared university, no visible history together. I've seen what happens when co-founders who don't know each other deeply try to survive the years of painful execution a startup requires. Nine companies in one YC batch added unfamiliar co-founders and all nine fell apart. I'm not saying that's what happened here, but the absence of any visible co-founder backstory is a gap I can't fill with optimism.

The technical signal is weak in a way that matters for this specific product. Aaron Chen's documented role is "designer," not engineer. His GitHub has 16 repos, 2 followers, and a maximum of 2 stars on any project. The team is hiring a Founding Engineer at $150K-$200K with 1% equity — which suggests the core technical product isn't being built by the founders themselves. For a compliance operating system that needs to encode the regulatory logic of fifty jurisdictions accurately, the person building it needs to be in the room making decisions about the company, not a hired engineer. This isn't a consumer app where you can iterate on design. Getting state examiner playbook logic wrong has real consequences for your customers.

The strongest bull case: the compliance schlep is genuinely enormous and expanding. The GENIUS Act creates new stablecoin licensing requirements. Compliance costs are projected to rise 25%. Every new crypto company that wants to operate legally across multiple states faces an ugly, manual, expensive process. If Payna executes exceptionally well, they could carve out a niche — perhaps by being more AI-native than Brico, or by serving a different segment of the market. Aaron Chen understands the crypto ecosystem from the inside, which means he understands his potential customers. And YC acceptance is a nonzero signal. But for this bull case to play out, the team would need to develop deep regulatory domain expertise they don't currently demonstrate, out-execute a competitor with a $21.6 million head start, and build a product whose core value depends on accuracy in a domain where errors create legal liability for clients. Each of those is achievable; all three together is a lot of miracles.

I keep coming back to the organic test. The founders who built Stripe didn't pivot from a social app into payments because they noticed the market was large. They'd been processing payments themselves and couldn't believe how bad it was. Drew Houston didn't pivot into file sync — he'd been forgetting his USB drive for years. When I look at Payna, I see smart people who noticed a market opportunity, not people who lived inside a compliance nightmare until they couldn't take it anymore. That distinction matters because the motivation to push through years of regulatory tedium — maintaining accuracy across fifty evolving jurisdictions — has to come from somewhere deeper than market analysis.
