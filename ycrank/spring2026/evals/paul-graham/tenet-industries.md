# Tenet Industries -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Emil Falk built a glass recycling machine, won Venture Cup's Startup of the Year in 2023, and deployed production units at four Ragn-Sells facilities in Sweden — all apparently before turning 22. That's the kind of resume line that makes me sit up. When I backed the Bindwell founders, what convinced me wasn't their AI pesticide pitch — it was that two teenagers had already demonstrated the capacity to ship something real into the physical world. Falk has that same signal: he took a hardware product from a student competition through European championships to actual commercial deployment. That's not a pitch deck founder. That's someone who knows what it feels like when a manufacturing run goes wrong at 2 AM. Tenet Industries needs exactly this quality, because their thesis — engineering drones around the factory rather than the reverse — is fundamentally a manufacturing bet, not a drone bet. The question is whether one founder's demonstrated shipping ability is enough to carry a team where the other two co-founders, Hugo Frisk and Fabian Andersson, have essentially no public track record.

Tenet Industries' core claim — a unified hardware and software platform for automated production of strike drones across multiple form factors — reminds me of what made Stripe interesting. The Collisions didn't build a better payment page; they rebuilt the infrastructure layer that made payment pages possible. Tenet Industries isn't trying to build a better drone; they're trying to build the factory that makes drones cheap and fast. That's a process innovation thesis, and if it works, it compounds: each production iteration drives down marginal cost and improves yield. Hugo Frisk's self-taught electronics background is relevant here — you need someone who understands component selection and PCB layout at the physical level to design for automated assembly. But "self-taught electronics designer" with no public GitHub repos and no shipped products is a thin signal. I've backed self-taught builders before, but usually because I could see what they'd built. With Frisk, I'm taking it on faith. Fabian Andersson is even harder to read — Lund-based, no detailed work history, no education credentials found. Two of three Tenet Industries co-founders are essentially opaque to me, and no shared prior institution connects the three. That absence of co-founder history makes me nervous — I've seen too many teams fracture under the specific stresses of defense contracting.

The bull case for Tenet Industries is genuinely strong, and I want to be honest about it. Sweden joined NATO in March 2024 and immediately allocated SEK 4B — roughly $440M — for unmanned combat systems through 2028. Sweden's Defence Minister Pål Jonson has called drone warfare "central to modern combat." A Swedish drone manufacturer has a structural advantage in Swedish procurement that Munich-based Helsing and Berlin-based Stark Defence simply don't have. Defense procurement is one of the worst schleps in existence: security clearances, export licensing under the Wassenaar Arrangement, multi-year qualification cycles, the ISP controlling military exports. Most founders I meet would rather build another developer tool than navigate that bureaucracy. The fact that Tenet Industries is choosing this domain — weapons manufacturing in a heavily regulated, compliance-heavy environment — is exactly the kind of schlep-blindness signal I look for. Bindwell chose pesticide chemistry; Stripe chose PCI compliance; Tenet Industries chose defense procurement. The pattern is founders who aren't repelled by the grunt work that repels everyone else.

But here's what stops me from writing a check: Helsing has raised €1.37 billion and holds a €269M Bundeswehr contract. Stark Defence raised ~$100M and is backed by Peter Thiel, Sequoia, and the NATO Innovation Fund, with a founder — Florian Seibel — who already built a unicorn in the drone space with Quantum Systems. Germany's €300M drone brigade contracts went to Helsing, Stark, and Rheinmetall. Tenet Industries has approximately $500K from YC against competitors with a thousand times more capital who already have government contracts and production lines delivering hundreds of units per month. This isn't Stripe entering a market of sleepy banks — this is a pre-seed company entering a market where well-funded, well-connected startups have already won the opening contracts. The "design-for-automation" thesis could theoretically deliver structural cost advantages, but it's a claim on a YC page, not a demonstrated capability. I don't know their unit economics, their production rate, or whether their kamikaze quadcopter has ever been tested beyond a bench prototype. Tenet Industries also lacks any battlefield validation — Ukrainian competitors like Fire Point and Terminal Autonomy have products proven in active combat, which matters enormously to procurement officers spending public money.

The idea itself — expendable strike drones manufactured at scale — has migrated from contrarian to consensus since Russia invaded Ukraine. In 2020, pitching "drones as ammunition" would have drawn skepticism from most investors. In 2026, it draws billion-dollar checks from Sequoia. Defense tech startups had their best funding year ever in 2025. That doesn't make Tenet Industries' idea bad, but it strips away the return premium I look for in "Black Swan" bets. When Helsing can raise €1.37B and Stark can attract Peter Thiel, the market has already voted that defense drones are a good idea. The upside is being competed away by better-capitalized players. Tenet Industries' specific angle — the manufacturing platform thesis and Swedish procurement positioning — preserves some differentiation, but these are execution bets, not insight bets. I don't see an origin story of personal frustration or organic discovery in the dossier. These founders may have deep personal reasons for building strike drones, but the dossier gives me nothing — no moment of frustration, no lived experience in defense, no "I was in the military and saw this problem firsthand" narrative. The way to get startup ideas is not to read about Ukraine in MIT Technology Review.

One verifiable condition would flip me to Invest: if Tenet Industries has a signed LOI or evaluation contract with the Swedish Armed Forces — or any NATO military — that I haven't seen in this dossier. That would prove both the manufacturing thesis (someone credible believes their production approach works) and the procurement navigation ability (they've gotten through the door that takes most defense startups years to open). Emil Falk's prior hardware deployment at Ragn-Sells shows he can sell to institutional buyers, and if he's replicated that in defense, the Bindwell parallel becomes much tighter. Without that evidence, what I see is one strong founder signal (Falk), one plausible but unverifiable signal (Frisk), one invisible co-founder (Andersson), a genuine schlep-blindness opportunity, and a competitive landscape that has already attracted billions in capital from investors with deeper defense networks than mine. Tenet Industries' Founder Resourcefulness Under Constraint scores above default primarily on the strength of Falk's WindowGlass Recycling deployment — real hardware at real commercial sites — but falls short of the top quartile because I lack the specific stories of creative problem-solving that reveal whether this team has the running back quality or merely the plow-straight-ahead quality.
