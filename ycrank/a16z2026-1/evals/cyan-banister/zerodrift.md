# ZeroDrift -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Kumesh Aroomoogan spent twelve years building AI products for financial services at Accern, raised $40M, made Forbes 30 Under 30, and saw the company acquired by Wand AI in 2025. Then he turned around and built ZeroDrift to solve a problem he watched compliance teams struggle with for over a decade. That's an impressive career arc — and it's exactly the kind of founding story that gives me pause. When I ask "tell me your story, not the business," I want to hear what broke you open, what system chewed you up and spit you out, what made you incapable of walking away from this fight. What I hear from ZeroDrift is "I was in financial services AI and saw the compliance bottleneck." That's pattern recognition from professional proximity. It's smart. But it's not a forge. The difference matters to me because the founders who were forged by their problem survive the valley of death — they don't pivot to something easier when Smarsh comes knocking with a bundled feature.

The $150K ARR with 50%+ month-over-month growth roughly two months after a February 2026 launch, the $2M qualified pipeline naming Franklin Templeton, Citi, Two Sigma, and Vanguard — these are legitimately strong pre-seed signals. SOC 2 Type II and ISO 27001 certifications already in hand show execution speed that most pre-seed companies can't match. If I were evaluating ZeroDrift purely on traction quality and founder-market fit in the conventional sense, I'd be leaning forward. Kumesh clearly knows how to sell enterprise software to financial institutions. He's done it before. But my framework isn't calibrated for conventional — it's calibrated for the things that conventional investors miss, and ZeroDrift is a company that conventional investors should love.

Here's what stops me cold: the invisible system test. When I look at Affirm — one of my portfolio companies I feel deeply about — Max Levchin built something that made credit card lending terms transparent to consumers, shifting power from institutions to the people those institutions were extracting from. ZeroDrift points in the opposite direction. It makes compliance enforcement more efficient for the compliance officers at $4B asset managers and Tier 1 banks. The people ZeroDrift serves — compliance teams at Citi, Vanguard — are not victims of an opaque system. They are the system. ZeroDrift doesn't expose a hidden tax or dismantle an information asymmetry that hurts ordinary people. It helps powerful institutions police themselves more smoothly. That's valuable work, but it's not the structural disruption I feel in my gut when I write a check.

ZeroDrift also sits squarely in consensus territory. Compliance software for regulated financial services is a space every institutional investor understands and most would eagerly fund. Smarsh has 6,500+ customers and Gartner Magic Quadrant leadership. Global Relay does $189M in revenue with 20,000+ customers. Greenboard raised $20.5M from General Catalyst. Nobody shifts in their chair when you pitch "AI compliance for banks" — they nod and ask about pricing. The pre-send versus post-send angle is a genuine product insight, and the AI content explosion (Global Relay's 3,000% year-over-year ChatGPT capture surge) creates real urgency. But a clever positioning within a well-served market isn't the same as entering a space that makes sophisticated investors uncomfortable. The 300-investor-pass test — which is how Uber came to me — doesn't apply here. ZeroDrift could raise from any number of enterprise-focused funds without difficulty.

The bull case I have to be honest about: ZeroDrift's rulepack library — encoding SEC, FINRA, and firm-specific policies into machine-readable enforcement rules — does compound with each customer. The multi-channel integration across Outlook, LinkedIn, ChatGPT, and Copilot creates genuine switching costs. And the inflection is real: AI-generated content at scale inside regulated industries is a category of risk that Smarsh and Global Relay's post-send archiving architecture wasn't built to address. If incumbents hesitate to cannibalize their archiving revenue by offering pre-send enforcement, ZeroDrift has a genuine window. The business model conflict the dossier identifies — Smarsh could build it but might resist undermining their core positioning — is the kind of structural advantage that creates real companies. I also note one concern: Kumesh claims he "led fundraising to a $1B valuation" at Wand AI, but Crunchbase shows Wand at $33M raised. That gap between narrative and verifiable fact is exactly the pattern I've learned to watch for since IRL.

ZeroDrift is a well-executed enterprise SaaS company built by a domain expert in a large, growing market with strong early traction. By most investment frameworks, it deserves attention. By mine, it doesn't reach the bar. The founder's biographical forge is professional, not personal — career optimization in a familiar vertical, not a life that made this problem inescapable. The space is consensus, not contrarian. The invisible system isn't being made visible to ordinary people — it's being made more manageable for institutional compliance departments. The two dimensions that locked my verdict are the biographical forge (Kumesh's twelve-year career is expertise without crucible) and the invisible system test (ZeroDrift serves power rather than disrupting it). I wish Kumesh well — he'll build a real business here — but this isn't a Long Journey bet.
