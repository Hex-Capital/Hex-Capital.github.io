# Taxnova -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that strikes me about Taxnova is the competitive landscape. SPRX has $46M in funding. Boast.ai has $30M and eight-figure revenue with over a thousand customers. Neo.Tax raised $13M. These are not sleepy incumbents — they are well-capitalized, focused players in R&D tax credit automation. Taxnova is entering this fight with $1M in pre-seed capital and a "US coming soon" badge on its website. When I look at this market, I don't see a space that other investors are afraid to touch. I see a space where money is flowing freely toward a known problem with a known buyer. a16z Speedrun accepted them — that's a signal of quality, but it's also a signal of consensus. Nobody shifts uncomfortably in their chair when you say "we automate R&D tax credits." This is the opposite of Anduril or Contraline or Crusoe. It's a rational, well-lit room, and there are already several well-funded companies sitting in it.

When I ask my first question — tell me about George Nichkov and Maria Malykh, their stories, not the business — the dossier gives me professional résumés. George went from Oliver Wyman consulting to product leadership at Gett. Maria managed R&D teams at Criteo and co-founded awaitly before this. These are competent, experienced builders. But I don't hear why George or Maria *must* solve the R&D tax problem. Where is the moment the problem found them? Maria's experience coordinating nine R&D teams at Criteo gives her insight into how engineering organizations work, which is the data source for R&D claims — that's a relevant professional thread. But "I managed engineering teams and realized their work product could be structured for tax purposes" is career pattern recognition, not biographical inevitability. The team hired people from EY, BDO, Grant Thornton, and the IRS to fill the domain gap, which is smart recruiting but confirms the founders themselves came to this problem from the outside.

Taxnova's relationship to the "invisible system" question is where I feel the most tension. R&D tax advisory *is* opaque — advisory firms derive significant revenue from process knowledge that companies can't easily replicate, and the 75% of claim preparation time spent on data collection is a structural inefficiency that benefits hourly billers. The HMRC inquiry rate jumping from 0.5% to 20% in three years is a genuine regulatory shock that raises the stakes for documentation quality. But here's what gives me pause: Taxnova's advisor-agnostic model explicitly preserves the intermediary layer. They're building infrastructure *for* the advisory firms rather than routing around them. Compare this to Carta, which I backed because it put cap table information directly into founders' hands — founders who literally couldn't understand their own equity because lawyers and accountants held the keys. Carta shifted power from gatekeepers to the people being gatekept. Taxnova makes the gatekeepers faster. That's a pragmatic go-to-market decision, and the completed pilot with a Top 5 advisory firm validates it commercially. But it means the extractive structure stays intact; it just runs more efficiently. The information asymmetry between tax advisors and their clients isn't what Taxnova is attacking — they're attacking the *operational burden* on the advisor side.

The defensibility signals here are the strongest part of the thesis, and I want to be honest about that. $100K ARR at pre-seed with named customers like incident.io and Paddle is real. The enterprise pilot at $200K+ ACV potential suggests genuine enterprise willingness to pay. Fifteen-plus tool integrations across GitHub, Jira, Linear, Xero, QuickBooks, and the rest create real switching costs once embedded. Multi-jurisdiction tax logic — live in the UK, launching in the US — compounds the replication barrier with each country added. The Revolut Head of Tax as an advisor adds credibility. If Taxnova executes on the global-first positioning while SPRX, Neo.Tax, and Boast remain US-focused, the multi-jurisdiction moat could become structurally significant. The timing is genuine: HMRC inquiry rates and US Section 174 changes create simultaneous compliance pressure across their two target markets.

The strongest bull case for Taxnova is that it becomes the Stripe of R&D tax — infrastructure that every advisory firm and every technology company plugs into, jurisdiction by jurisdiction, where each new country deepens the moat. The $100K ARR proves the product works. The advisor-channel strategy avoids the suicide mission of fighting Big 4 firms head-on. The regulatory inflections in both UK and US create urgency that wasn't there two years ago. If George and Maria can outrun their capital disadvantage against SPRX's $46M and capture the multi-jurisdiction layer before anyone else scales beyond a single country, this could be a meaningful business. But "meaningful business" and "Long Journey bet" are different things. I need founders whose biographical forge makes them unkillable in a space that scares other people. Taxnova has competent founders in a space that attracts other people — and a lot of their capital. This is a well-built company pursuing a rational opportunity, and I wish George and Maria well, but it doesn't carry the signal I look for when I'm writing my own check.
