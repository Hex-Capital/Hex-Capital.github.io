# Regbase -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

CUBE Global has raised $22 million, AscentAI raised $27 million before being acquired by Edgewater Capital, Thomson Reuters covers 750+ regulatory bodies across 130+ countries, and Vixio monitors 1,400 regulatory authorities. Regbase enters this landscape with three people and the claim that it will differentiate by indexing "obscure local websites and content behind QR codes." I start every evaluation with the same question: what important truth does this company know that the market has missed? Regbase's implicit answer is that the long tail of subnational, non-English regulatory content is where the real compliance risk lives, and incumbents are structurally unable to serve it. This is observationally correct — Thomson Reuters does optimize for established regulatory bodies, and CUBE does focus on financial services. But correctness is not the same as a secret. Every regtech founder in every Y Combinator batch and every compliance conference can identify this gap. The insight fails the most basic test: it does not make knowledgeable people uncomfortable or dismissive. It makes them nod. A secret that produces agreement is not a secret — it is consensus dressed in the vocabulary of differentiation.

Regbase's competitive landscape reveals the deeper structural problem. The dossier lists four funded competitors operating in the same category, with CUBE actively consolidating by acquiring Thomson Reuters' Regulatory Intelligence division. When Regbase lists seven verticals on its website — supply chain, PFAS, ESG, healthcare, private equity, water management, manufacturing — it signals breadth-seeking in a crowded market rather than monopoly of a narrow beachhead. Facebook began with 100% penetration at Harvard before expanding campus by campus. Regbase is attempting to serve Fortune 500 companies, global consultancies, and leading law firms across seven industries simultaneously with three people. This is not a conspiracy to change the world; it is a pitch to everyone at once, which means it is a pitch to no one in particular. The absence of named customers, logos, or quantified metrics behind the Fortune 500 claim makes the distribution story even harder to evaluate — at pre-seed I do not penalize the absence of traction, but I do penalize unverifiable claims of traction that substitute for a specific beachhead strategy.

Natalie Aresta-Katz and Isiah Godoy both worked at Paul, Weiss in the ESG practice, and their domain expertise is genuine — they tracked laws across dozens of countries and all U.S. states. Ethan Shea brings technical credibility from Microsoft, Citadel, and Vercel's agentic AI team. The Paul, Weiss co-founder prehistory satisfies one structural criterion I care about: shared intensity before the founding. But the founding story itself — "a big law firm research project that couldn't find a solution to track laws across 50 countries" — describes a pain point, not a vision. Where is the definite plan? Regbase's dossier contains no articulation of what the world looks like when they succeed, no irreversible commitment that would make no sense unless a specific thesis is correct, no indication that the founders have rejected the lean-startup methodology in favor of a bold, specific multi-year arc. The approach reads as "apply AI to regulatory monitoring and iterate toward product-market fit" — precisely the indefinite thinking that characterizes companies I avoid.

The power law calculation disqualifies Regbase before the other criteria even matter. The regulatory intelligence platform market is projected at $7.6 billion by 2034. Even granting Regbase implausible dominance of this entire category — which Thomson Reuters, CUBE, and others would prevent — the ceiling is a $5-7 billion company. That is a respectable enterprise SaaS outcome. It is not a fund-returner. The power law demands that a single investment could return the entire fund; Regbase's realistic best case, even with aggressive assumptions, is a $1-3 billion outcome in a fragmented market with no winner-take-all dynamics. Compare this to General Matter's $900 million DOE contract in nuclear fuel enrichment, where a single government relationship creates a monopoly pathway in a market with civilizational-scale demand. Regbase operates in a category where the #1 player and the #5 player might differ by 3x in revenue, not 100x.

The strongest bull case an advocate would construct runs as follows: Regbase's operational moat — the custom ingestion of thousands of fragmented government sources with human verification — compounds over time in ways that a new entrant cannot shortcut. Each new jurisdiction covered, each new language parsed, each new obscure source indexed adds to a data advantage that creates switching costs for compliance-sensitive customers. The Paul, Weiss network provides warm introductions to every AmLaw 100 firm. If Regbase can establish itself as the authoritative source for the long tail before incumbents notice, it could become the standard for cross-jurisdictional regulatory intelligence the way Palantir became the standard for intelligence community analytics. I find this argument structurally insufficient. Palantir's moat was created by embedding into CIA and Army operations where displacement required physical-world migration of classified systems. Regbase's moat is an editorial database of publicly available government documents — a moat that any well-funded competitor, including a general-purpose LLM provider, could erode by pointing crawlers at the same sources. The dossier itself acknowledges this: "General-purpose LLMs can increasingly parse government websites and translate content." The condition that would flip me to Invest: Regbase signs an exclusive multi-year contract with a government agency or major regulatory body that embeds its platform as the official monitoring infrastructure — creating Palantir-style switching costs rather than SaaS-style subscription relationships.

Regbase is a pure software layer with no physical-world technology barrier, operating in a competitive market where the consensus insight — "AI can improve regulatory monitoring" — is shared by every participant. The one public GitHub repository has zero stars. There is no network effect, no proprietary hardware, no government embedding, and no evidence of organic pull from users who discovered the product without marketing. The founders are competent domain practitioners solving a real problem, and the company may build a viable business. But viable businesses in competitive markets are not what I invest in. The two dimensions that lock this verdict are Monopoly Secret — the insight is correct but consensus, producing no structural dominance — and Power Law Return Magnitude — the category ceiling is too low and too fragmented for the extreme outcomes the power law demands.
