# StableBrowse -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

$96 million in aggregate competitor funding — Browserbase at $40M, Browser Use at $17M, Steel.dev at $17M, Kernel at $22M — all targeting the exact same buyer persona: developers building AI agents that need to interact with the web. StableBrowse enters this field with standard YC funding and a genuinely different architectural bet: strip the rendering engine entirely instead of wrapping Chromium. The bet is interesting. The question is whether the team making it possesses the specific knowledge to execute it. Building a browser engine — even one that removes the visual stack — requires deep expertise in JavaScript engine integration, DOM parsing, web standards compliance, and security sandboxing. This is knowledge that accumulates over years inside Chromium, Mozilla, or WebKit teams. It cannot be synthesized from internships and hackathons.

Somansh Shah's background is data science at Purdue with a Lenovo internship and ML stock-recommendation projects. Deepit Shah studied CS at UIUC with an Amazon backend internship. Neither founder shows visible experience with browser engines, rendering pipelines, or web standards — the exact domain where StableBrowse claims to have rebuilt the stack from the ground up. When I invested in Perplexity, Aravind Srinivas had spent years at Google Brain and OpenAI constructing the systems that make AI search work — his specific knowledge WAS the product. StableBrowse's claimed 130.6 MB per instance, 4ms cold start, and 85% token reduction are impressive numbers if real, but no GitHub repos, no open-source contributions, and no prior browser-infrastructure projects are visible for any of the four founders. Specific knowledge can be learned through obsessive building — my own framework insists on that — but I need to see the evidence of that obsession, and here I see credentials adjacent to the problem rather than embedded in it.

StableBrowse's leverage architecture is, in theory, exactly what I look for. A rendering-free browser engine is pure code leverage — zero marginal cost per agent session, scales horizontally without proportional headcount. The CDP compatibility with Playwright and Puppeteer is a clever migration path that lowers initial adoption friction. But two structural problems collapse the leverage advantage. First, StableBrowse is building leverage on top of someone else's leverage layer. OpenAI with Operator, Google with Gemini, and Anthropic are all constructing native browsing capabilities for their agents. When the foundation model provider ships browsing as a feature, the standalone browser-infrastructure layer gets compressed — the same dynamic that kills AI wrappers, just one layer deeper in the stack. Second, the CDP compatibility that eases adoption also means switching costs are nearly zero. A developer using StableBrowse's CDP-compatible API can migrate to any other CDP-compatible provider with minimal code changes. StableBrowse's leverage is real but rented, not owned.

Deepit Shah's LinkedIn indicated he was returning to Google full-time — a detail the dossier flags without resolution. Two of StableBrowse's four founders, Sarthak Awasthi and Jay Mehta, have common names that couldn't be confirmed as the same individuals listed on the YC page. No founder has a public Twitter/X account, no company LinkedIn page exists, and zero job postings are open. These are pre-seed norms individually, but the Deepit Shah signal is not a pre-seed norm — it's a principal-agent problem inside the founding team itself. A founder who may be returning to Google is, by definition, an agent weighing options rather than a principal whose identity is fused with the company. I look for founders who would be working on this problem without venture funding. StableBrowse offers no evidence of that.

The strongest bull case an advocate would make: StableBrowse's rendering-free architecture is structurally correct, and every dollar of that $96M in competitor funding is sunk into the wrong approach. Browserbase, Browser Use, Steel.dev, and Kernel all build on top of full Chromium — if rendering-free wins, they'd need to re-architect their core products. StableBrowse's 85% token reduction and 4ms cold start, if validated at scale, represent a 10x improvement that creates genuine switching incentives despite low switching costs. The Varda Space exception in my portfolio shows I'll back companies where the physics (or in this case, the architecture) creates leverage so structural that it overrides other concerns. Could StableBrowse be the architectural equivalent — a bet where being right about the fundamental approach matters more than the founders' resume pedigree? I'd need one verifiable condition to flip: evidence that StableBrowse's engine reliably handles the modern web at scale — a public benchmark against Browserbase across 1,000 diverse sites, or a named production customer processing significant session volume. The architectural bet is interesting enough that proven web compatibility would force me to re-evaluate the specific knowledge question — maybe they learned it by building. But claims on a website are not evidence, and right now, claims are all I have.

StableBrowse fails my framework at the foundational level: specific knowledge productization. The founders' backgrounds are tangential to the core technical challenge of building a browser engine. The access democratization angle is modest — developers already have Playwright and Puppeteer; StableBrowse makes agent browsing cheaper, not newly possible. The leverage architecture is structurally sound but vulnerable to platform compression from LLM providers and to the zero-switching-cost problem created by their own CDP compatibility. The principal authenticity signals — one founder possibly returning to Google, two unverifiable founders, no visible obsessive engagement with browser infrastructure — sit below my threshold. My Wish investment taught me that leverage without specific knowledge is insufficient. StableBrowse has a clever architectural thesis wrapped around a specific-knowledge vacuum. I pass.
