# Sirius Technology -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Let me start with the question I always start with: if Sirius Technology fully succeeds — dominates the AI retention market, processes every cancellation request for every subscription company on Earth — what changes? Subscription companies lose fewer customers. Their revenue goes up. The $650B subscription economy leaks less money to churn. That is a fine outcome for a SaaS investor. It is not a changed world. Nobody's healthcare gets cheaper. Nobody gains access to energy or education they couldn't afford. If anything, Sirius Technology's product makes it harder for consumers to cancel subscriptions — the exact inverse of what gets me out of bed. This is a textbook case of what I call targeting redistribution of existing spend rather than creation of new demand. The consequence ceiling is a profitable B2B SaaS company in a competitive vertical, and that ceiling disqualifies it from my framework before I evaluate anything else.

The technical approach confirms the concern. Sirius Technology claims a "proprietary AI model purpose-built for retention," but intercepting cancellation requests with personalized win-back offers is not a technology discontinuity. LiveX AI does it with conversational agents across chat, voice, and email. Churned.io does it with a "Co-Pilot" AI engine. Subsets does it with explainable AI experiments for subscription media. The dossier itself states the quiet part out loud: "the core concept of AI-driven cancellation interception is reproducible." When five funded competitors target overlapping use cases and the differentiation reduces to model performance and integration depth, you are in an execution race, not a technology monopoly. Benazir Toleubekova's claimed background architecting Alibaba's recommendation engine for 500M daily users is genuinely relevant to building prediction systems at scale — but relevance to the domain does not make the approach non-linear. A 20% retention improvement is a meaningful efficiency gain, not a step-function change.

The founders are the strongest signal here, and I want to be honest about that. Azamat Khamidov and Benazir Toleubekova are immigrant outsiders — Uzbekistan and what appears to be Kazakhstan — building in San Francisco. That matches a pattern in my portfolio that I don't talk about publicly: Apoorva Mehta, Tony Xu, Max Levchin, all immigrants who attacked industries with irreverence because they didn't know the rules. Azamat pivoted from PointAI (e-commerce support chatbot) to Sirius in 2025, which signals willingness to kill a company and start again — a form of learning velocity. Benazir's trajectory from Taobao at 17 to Momenta's autonomous driving algorithms shows range across domains. The claimed $3M ARR with 200% NRR and 75% month-over-month growth — if real — would represent extraordinary commercial execution. But these metrics are self-reported on the a16z Speedrun listing with no third-party verification, no named customers, and no press coverage. I don't invest based on spreadsheets, and I don't invest based on unverified self-reported metrics either. The pivot recency compounds this: the current product is less than a year old, and PointAI's metrics (80% query resolution) don't transfer.

The strongest bull case: these founders are learning machines who have already demonstrated the ability to pivot, scale quickly (the 5M MAU claim for a prior product), and build in a space where their technical backgrounds in recommendation systems and large-scale ML give them an edge. The data flywheel from millions of daily interactions could compound into a durable moat. And the subscription economy is genuinely massive — $650B in annualized recurring revenue means even a small percentage of retained revenue flowing through Sirius Technology represents a large business. If Sirius captures performance-based pricing at scale, the revenue model could be very attractive. For a different investor — one who optimizes for probability-weighted returns in known markets — this might be a compelling bet. But I don't make IRR calculations on spreadsheets. I either believe the world changes, or I don't. Sirius Technology could become a $500M company. That's a waste of my attention.

Ambition is the final filter. "AI Retention Platform for Subscription Companies" — no informed observer would say "that can't be done" or "that will take 20 years." It's a well-mapped problem with well-understood solutions being incrementally improved by AI. When I backed the 19-year-old at Mach Industries building weapons systems to compete with Lockheed Martin, the improbability was the point. When I wrote the check to OpenAI, I sent an apology letter to my LPs because the ambition looked absurd. Sirius Technology's ambition looks sensible, and sensible is the wrong adjective for my portfolio. The company is competently positioned in a growing market with capable founders and potentially strong traction — and none of that maps to the kind of bet where I either lose everything or change the world.
