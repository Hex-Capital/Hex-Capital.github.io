# CodeWisp -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Elvin Fu has been teaching people to make games for over a decade. He's a top-20 creator on Scratch out of 135 million users, he's taught 20 million students game development through YouTube and his academy, and he built two game engines from the ground up starting when he was ten years old. When someone like that builds an AI-powered game creation platform, I don't have to ask where the idea came from. He watched millions of students hit the wall between wanting to make a game and actually being able to. The organic discovery here is real -- this is someone who has spent more than a third of his life inside this exact problem. That part passes my test cleanly.

What concerns me is the schlep. Or rather, the absence of one. "Anyone can create real games with AI" is not a problem that's been hiding in plain sight because it's too painful to tackle. It's a problem that every AI company and their cousin is already working on. Rosebud AI has $9.2 million and $2.7 million in revenue. Series Entertainment raised $28 million from Netflix and a16z. Replit could add game-specific features tomorrow with its $222 million war chest. When I see a category with this many well-funded competitors doing similar things, I get nervous. The best startup ideas face structurally low competition because the schlep scares everyone away -- Stripe worked because nobody wanted to deal with banks and compliance. Here, the schlep is building an LLM wrapper with game-specific scaffolding. That's not the kind of ugly, regulatory, soul-crushing work that creates natural barriers to entry. The core technology is commodity. Any team with API access to Claude or GPT-4 can build prompt-to-game in a weekend hackathon.

The bull case is distribution. Fu's 84,000 YouTube subscribers and his reach into 20 million students represent a genuine audience wedge that Rosebud and Replit don't have. If CodeWisp becomes the default tool for the "I want to learn to make games" crowd -- the Scratch-to-real-code pipeline -- that's a defensible position built on trust and community rather than technology. The 1,513 published games on the platform are an early signal of community formation. This is the scenario where CodeWisp wins: not by being the best AI game generator, but by being the place where a generation of young creators first publish their work and discover each other. That would make it more like early Roblox than like another AI tool.

But I'm not convinced that scenario plays out. The engagement numbers are modest -- the top game has 1,355 views. There's no pricing page, no revenue signal, no evidence that users are pulling this product toward them with the intensity I look for. When Airbnb's founders visited NYC, they found hosts who needed the income to pay rent. When early Stripe users got the Collison installation, they immediately started processing payments. I don't see that kind of economic dependency or urgent need here. Game creation for beginners is a nice-to-have, not a must-have. And nice-to-have consumer products in competitive categories have a brutal failure rate.

The solo-founder signal also gives me pause. The YC page lists only Elvin Fu, with two unnamed team members. I required Drew Houston to find a co-founder before accepting Dropbox, and that was with a product that had overwhelming organic pull. Here the organic pull is thinner and the competitive landscape is more hostile. Who's handling the ML engineering? Who's building the community features? The strongest teams at this stage have complementary co-founders whose skills are visible. When I can't even identify who the other people are, that's information.

Fu is clearly a technical hacker who builds things -- the game engines, the shipped mobile games, the Stanford hackathon win. That's a real signal. If he were attacking a problem with more structural protection, I'd be more excited. But building an AI game creation platform in 2026 is like building yet another social network in 2012 -- the idea is obvious, the competition is fierce, and the winner will be determined by execution and capital, not by being first to see the opportunity. The "vibe coding" trend creates tailwind for the category, but tailwind blows on everyone equally.
