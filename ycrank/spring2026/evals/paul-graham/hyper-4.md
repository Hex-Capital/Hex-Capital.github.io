# Hyper -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Hyper is what you get when two Berkeley roommates who shipped real autonomy code at Matic Robots aim at a category Glean is already inside of at $200M ARR -- the team configuration is the strongest thing in the dossier, and the founder-pain narrative is the weakest. Shalin Shah and Kanyes Thaker are EECS class of 2021, worked together at Matic, lived together, and now run Hyper as a two-person team. That's the **Reddit** (2005) Huffman/Ohanian dorm pattern, not the hackathon-pair anti-pattern. But the dossier never tells me Hyper exists because Shalin or Kanyes themselves got tired of re-pasting context into Claude Code. That asymmetry is the whole evaluation.

The closest portfolio analogue on team configuration is **Reddit** (2005) -- two people who already lived together, deciding to build something together, with the social activation energy already paid years before the company existed. Hyper has that exact structural property: Berkeley 2021, Matic Robots coworkers, roommates. The Matic detail matters more than the Berkeley detail; people who debugged autonomy stacks together have already learned whether they can fight without quitting. Where Hyper diverges from **Reddit** (2005) is that Huffman and Ohanian were building the link aggregator they personally wanted to read, and the Hyper dossier gives me no equivalent sentence. So I'm getting the cofounder gift without the organic-idea gift that usually travels with it.

On organic idea fit, the **Dropbox** (2007) failure mode is the relevant one to invert: Drew Houston forgot his USB stick, built the thing for himself, and the Hacker News demo did the rest. Does that pattern appear in Hyper? The dossier says AI-first teams' context is fragmented across Notion, Cursor, Claude Code, email, LinkedIn DMs -- a market observation, not a Shalin-and-Kanyes observation. There's no anecdote that Hyper started because Kanyes kept re-explaining his Snorkel context to Claude Code on every chat turn. The product description reads as third-person market gap, which is the inverse of what I want. Default penalty applies.

The animal-resourcefulness dimension for Hyper has one genuine artifact and one gap. The artifact: a search snippet says Shalin "built an app for the blind in high school with over 3 million users." Three million users in high school is not nothing -- that's a kid who shipped something the world actually used, which is the same shape of signal as Drew Houston's pre-Dropbox shipping. The gap: the dossier names no Hyper-specific pre-funding stunt. No cereal boxes, no rejection-overturning prototype like Brian Armstrong's Android wallet that rescued **Coinbase** (2012). The high-school app raises Hyper above the default, but it's old evidence, not present-tense animal behavior for this company. I score this above the silence default but well below the Houston rung.

On schlep, Hyper is in a crowded schlep, which is almost worse than a clean one. Ingesting Notion, email, LinkedIn DMs, and Cursor sessions and keeping them clean in real time is genuine grinding work -- connectors rot, schemas drift, security review is brutal. Over 18 months that compounds either into a real moat or into a maintenance tax that two people cannot pay. The mechanism question is whether Hyper's grind is contrarian or consensus: Glean has spent $600M+ and six years on the same grind, Dust raised $40M from Sequoia on an adjacent grind, Notion AI and Slack AI grind it ecosystem-natively. **Coinbase** (2012)'s compliance grind worked as a moat because nobody else in YC W12 wanted to touch it. Hyper's grind is one many well-funded teams already want.

On hacker genealogy, Hyper passes the basic test and stops short of the Lisp-tier signal I over-index on. Kanyes has a public GitHub (@kanyesthaker) with Ferret at 60+ stars and was founding data engineer at Snorkel AI helping land multi-million-dollar deals; Shalin led autonomy at Matic Robots and shipped that high-school app. These are people who ship. What's missing is the Patrick Collison Young Scientist signal, the Aaron Swartz RSS-spec signal -- the recognized-pre-startup-hacker tribal marker that makes me lean in independent of the idea. Counterfactual: if Kanyes's GitHub had a widely-used OSS project rather than a 60-star flashcard tool, this dimension would clear the top quartile and I'd be writing a different memo.

My 36-month forecast for Hyper: acquihired by Cursor, Notion, or a mid-tier enterprise-search player for $15-40M after failing to differentiate from Glean and Dust on the enterprise sales motion, with ARR somewhere between $0 and $1.5M at exit. The mechanism: Hyper's value depends on injecting context into third-party AI tools, and the moment Anthropic ships native cross-tool memory or Cursor signs a Glean deal, Hyper's channel narrows to a feature. The cofounder strength gets the team to a soft landing rather than a wipeout, which is why I'm forecasting acquihire and not death.

The strongest case against my forecast runs through **Stripe** (2010): the Collisons were two Berkeley-adjacent hackers who'd already shipped Auctomatic together, and the prevailing read in 2010 was that payments was a crowded schlep PayPal and Braintree had locked. I funded them anyway because the founders compounded faster than the incumbents could move. Hyper could be that -- Shalin and Kanyes shipping iterations weekly against Glean's enterprise-sales bureaucracy, where two roommates eat a $200M ARR incumbent from below. What dismantles this bull case is that the Collisons had Auctomatic as a prior shipped exit before **Stripe** (2010); Hyper has no equivalent shipped Hyper-specific artifact in the dossier, just resumes plus a tagline. The verifiable condition that would flip my verdict: a working Hyper demo on Hacker News with engineering teams in the comments saying "I've been using this for six weeks and I can't go back" -- the **Dropbox** (2007) test, applied honestly.
