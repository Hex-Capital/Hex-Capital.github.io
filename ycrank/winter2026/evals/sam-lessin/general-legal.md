# General Legal -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The most striking thing about General Legal is the gap between founder quality and idea novelty. Three people who built CoCounsel at Casetext -- acquired by Thomson Reuters for $650M in cash -- all holding dual credentials as practicing attorneys and AI/ML engineers, reuniting to start a new company together. Ryan Walker was CTO, Javed Qadrud-Din directed machine learning, J.P. Mohler deployed AI in actual federal courtrooms. If I were assembling a founding team in a lab to build an AI-augmented law firm, I could not do better than this. These are maximally sane people. The problem is that Y Combinator literally published a Request for Startups asking founders to "start your own law firm, staff it with AI agents, and compete with existing law firms." Lawhive just raised $60M doing a version of this model. LegalOS, in the same YC batch, applies the identical structure to immigration law. When the accelerator is requesting the startup category by name, the "insane idea" premium has been fully arbitraged. I'm looking at sane people with a sane idea, and that's my least favorite configuration -- it means I'm buying at market price no matter how good the team is.

Now, there's a subtlety here that a surface reading misses. Within legal tech, the consensus play is building software tools for lawyers -- that's Harvey at $8B, Spellbook at $350M valuation, Robin AI before it cratered. The decision to actually *be* a law firm, to carry malpractice liability, to employ licensed attorneys, to do the ugly operational work rather than ship a SaaS product -- that is somewhat contrarian. It reminds me of what drew me to Craftwork: the operational complexity that makes software-focused investors recoil is precisely what creates a defensible position. BigLaw can't adopt $500 flat fees without cannibalizing their hourly billing model. Harvey and Spellbook can't become law firms without creating channel conflict with their own customers. Robin AI tried the pure-software approach and ended up acquired after financial difficulties. So there's a real innovator's dilemma protecting General Legal on two fronts simultaneously. That's worth something.

But here's where my framework gets uncomfortable with this business. I run the "cherry on top" test: would General Legal be a good business if AI didn't exist? The answer is clearly no. Without AI making attorneys 10x more efficient, a $500 flat-fee contract review is unprofitable -- you're just a cheap law firm, and cheap law firms have always existed and never built venture-scale outcomes. The AI isn't the cherry on top; it IS the sundae. And when I see a company whose entire value proposition depends on AI capabilities, I ask what happens when the next model generation makes those capabilities universally available. Thomson Reuters owns the founders' previous technology and is building agentic CoCounsel capabilities. Any team of attorneys with AI engineering chops can replicate the model -- the regulatory barrier requires licensed attorneys, but there are 1.3 million of them in the United States. The scarce resource is the law-plus-AI combination, and that combination is becoming less scarce by the quarter.

The structural ceiling is what concerns me most. Every contract reviewed requires a licensed attorney's involvement. This is linear scaling with human capital, not a compounding primitive. Venmo created a new payment layer that every application could build on -- network effects compound. Solana established a protocol where every new project increases the value of the existing ecosystem. General Legal processes contracts one at a time. My contract review doesn't make your contract review better. There's no flywheel, no network effect, no protocol dynamic. The accumulated client context creates mild switching costs, but contract review is fundamentally transactional -- a growth-stage company isn't locked in the way they'd be locked into core infrastructure. The narrative can expand from commercial contracts to broader legal services, but the ceiling is "very successful, tech-enabled law firm" -- a real business, potentially a large one, but not an infinity story. Firms like this get valued on revenue multiples, not narrative multiples.

The strongest bull case: these founders know this market better than anyone alive. They built and sold the definitive AI legal product. They understand exactly which workflows AI handles well and which require human judgment. The YC startup network gives them distribution into their exact target customer. If they execute, they could become the default legal layer for every venture-backed company, expanding from commercial contracts to full legal departments, and the compounding client data eventually creates a genuine information advantage that new entrants can't replicate. And Lawhive's $60M war chest is targeting consumers, not startups -- the growth-stage commercial contract niche might be defensible enough for General Legal to own it before anyone else arrives. If I believed law firms could achieve protocol-like dynamics -- where being the standard for startup legal becomes self-reinforcing through network referrals and ecosystem integration -- I'd be writing a check. But I've seen too many services businesses with tech layers convince themselves they're platforms. The Birchbox lesson stays with me: exceptional founders and elastic narratives don't compensate for a model that competitors can replicate once the playbook is visible.

I respect the founders enormously and I'd want to back them on something structurally wilder. If these three came to me with a genuinely non-consensus legal infrastructure play -- something that made other investors uncomfortable -- I'd lean in hard. But an AI-augmented contract review firm in a category YC explicitly solicited, with Lawhive carrying $60M into the same waters, doesn't trigger my conviction. The founders deserve better than a consensus idea.
