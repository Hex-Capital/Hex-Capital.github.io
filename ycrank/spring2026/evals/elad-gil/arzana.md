# Arzana -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing that catches my attention here is the traction relative to team size: 9 named customer logos including Schneider National (Fortune 500) and Zeiss, 20+ plants and distribution centers, published pricing at $2,500-$7,000/month — all from a 4-person team at pre-seed. That's an unusual signal. If even half of those are paying customers, this team is closing enterprise manufacturing deals at a pace that suggests they've found a genuine pain point with a willing buyer. The Tier One Technologies testimonial — "cut our TTQ from 5 days to 1" — is the kind of concrete operational impact that drives retention. I've seen companies with far more pedigreed teams struggle to land a single named logo at this stage, so the traction alone earns a closer look.

The market structure timing is real but no longer fully nonobvious. LLMs crossed the reliability threshold for parsing messy, unstructured manufacturing documents — handwritten notes, varied PDF formats, email chains — sometime in the 2023-2025 window. Before that, OCR and rule-based systems couldn't handle the format diversity of actual manufacturing RFQs. That's a genuine capability threshold being crossed, similar to what I saw with Harvey and legal document analysis. But here's where it gets less clean: Endeavor AI raised $7M from Craft Ventures in November 2024 targeting the same broad market. Canals AI has backing from Applico. The "AI for manufacturing" window is open, and multiple teams are climbing through it. What IS somewhat less obvious is Arzana's specific focus on front-office execution — quoting, order entry, AP/AR — rather than the factory-floor automation that attracts more prestige-seeking founders. This maps to a pattern I've seen repeatedly: Gusto won payroll, Checkr won background checks, PagerDuty won incident management — all in categories that most ambitious founders considered beneath them. Manufacturing front-office operations is that kind of boring, essential category.

The infrastructure-layer question is where I have the most nuanced read. Arzana isn't pure infrastructure — they don't offer an API that other companies build on, and there aren't network effects in the traditional sense. But the 11+ ERP integrations (Epicor, Infor, JobBoss, SAP, NetSuite, and six others) create something functionally similar: once you're processing a manufacturer's RFQs through their specific Epicor configuration and the AI has learned their pricing logic and catalog structure, ripping that out is operationally painful. Each ERP connector is a mini-moat. The "Office Execution System" framing — spanning quoting, order entry, purchasing, customer service, AP/AR, CRM, and outbound — suggests they're positioning as the connective tissue between the messy unstructured front office and the structured ERP backend. That's not infrastructure in the Stripe sense, but it's workflow embedding that compounds switching costs over time. The concern: maintaining 11+ ERP integrations with 4 people is either impressively capital-efficient or dangerously fragile. At this team size, one engineer leaving breaks the whole integration surface.

The single-miracle profile is actually one of the cleaner ones I've seen at pre-seed. The technology miracle — LLMs reliably parsing unstructured manufacturing documents — is happening exogenously. They don't need to build foundation models. The distribution miracle appears to be solving itself through the founders' Iowa manufacturing network (CEO from Okoboji, Field Engineer from Okoboji, physical office there). The regulatory environment for manufacturing front-office software is benign. What they need is execution: can they maintain parsing accuracy at production quality across diverse manufacturing document formats while scaling ERP integrations? That's one miracle, and the traction suggests they're already partway through it.

The founder profile is where my conviction drops. I weight market structure over founder narrative, and I've seen mediocre operators do extremely well in strong markets. But a 21-year-old CEO who has taken "only one coding class" building AI agents for manufacturing is a specific kind of risk. The pivot from Brilla (gourmet meal delivery on Stanford's campus) to manufacturing AI doesn't suggest the deep domain immersion I typically want to see. The Iowa roots provide genuine manufacturing network access — having a physical presence in Okoboji and a founding field engineer from the same community is authentically different from a SF team cold-calling manufacturers — but network access and operational scaling expertise are different capabilities. When I think about what happens at 50 customers, 200 customers — the hiring challenges, the ERP maintenance burden, the enterprise support demands — I want to see evidence that the founding team has navigated that kind of organizational complexity before. I don't see it here.

The bull case I want to be honest about: if this team continues executing at their current pace, the ERP integration library becomes a genuine competitive moat that Endeavor's broader approach can't easily replicate. Manufacturing is a market where trust is built through relationships, not marketing spend, and the Iowa-rooted go-to-market gives Arzana an authenticity advantage that a Craft Ventures-backed SF startup can't buy. The front-office niche is specific enough to defend but large enough to build a substantial business — $2,500-$7,000/month across the U.S. manufacturing base is a large addressable market. And the pricing architecture is solid: SaaS replacing manual headcount, with natural expansion as customers add modules. If the technology continues improving on its own curve and this team can recruit strong engineers, the boring-but-critical infrastructure thesis plays out. The reason I'm not quite at Invest is that the founding team's technical depth is genuinely thin for an AI company, the competitive window is narrowing with Endeavor well-funded, and the market timing — while real — isn't sufficiently nonobvious to generate the kind of structural alpha I look for.
