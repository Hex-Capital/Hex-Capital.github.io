# Miraka -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Three founders under 25, selling into NewYork-Presbyterian — a top-5 heart hospital — and generating $300K in annual run rate from insurance-reimbursed appointments. That's the signal worth examining. Nolan Abeyta and Kazuo Nakamura graduated Princeton in '24 and '23 respectively, built Overwatch Health on the same cardiac rehab problem starting in 2021, and have now iterated into Miraka with 71% month-over-month growth in billable appointments. Most 23-year-olds can't get a meeting with a hospital procurement office. These two closed one. That's not domain expertise — that's the kind of naive persistence I've seen work before, the "not understanding what couldn't be done" energy that drove my early bets. Jesse Abeyta's Amazon AI background and military exoskeleton biometrics work adds a technical leg the team otherwise lacks. The iteration from Overwatch Health to Miraka, including what appears to be at least one intermediate pivot through "Paralign," is evidence of learning velocity — they've been attacking this problem for four years and adapting their approach each time.

Where Miraka loses me is the consequence question. If Miraka fully succeeds — captures 94% of eligible cardiac patients and reduces readmissions by 90% — hundreds of thousands of patients avoid preventable hospital readmissions annually. Lives saved, billions in cost avoided. That matters. But the world doesn't look fundamentally different. Cardiac post-discharge care works the way it should have always worked. That's fixing a broken system, not replacing it with something new. Compare this to my bet on Curai Health, where AI delivers primary care at near-zero marginal cost — the end state there is that primary care itself becomes free. Miraka's end state is that cardiac rehab enrollment goes from 10% to 94%. Important, but bounded. The consequence of success is a well-functioning cardiac care system, not a transformation of how healthcare is delivered.

The technical approach compounds this concern. Miraka integrates EHR data and wearable signals into predictive risk models — that's applying existing machine learning to existing data sources. Biofourmis already has an FDA-cleared Biovitals analytics engine doing deterioration prediction. Cadence has $144M in funding and an AHA partnership doing RPM at scale. Health Recovery Solutions demonstrated 8% vs 28% readmission rates in a peer-reviewed AJMC study. Miraka's differentiation is operational, not technical: they provide the clinical care team, not just the monitoring platform. That's a business model insight — bundling the clinicians with the AI — but it's not a technology discontinuity. Nobody at Cadence or Biofourmis is saying "that's impossible." They're saying "we chose a different go-to-market." When the competitor's response is a shrug rather than disbelief, the technical moat is thin. The 94% capture rate versus the 10% industry average is genuinely impressive at 9.4x improvement, but I need to understand whether that gap is driven by proprietary AI or by the simple fact that Miraka shows up with clinicians while SaaS platforms hand hospitals software and walk away.

The structural parallel that keeps surfacing is Oscar Health. Both Miraka and Oscar operate clinical services in heavily regulated, insurance-reimbursed healthcare markets. Both depend on CMS billing rules — Miraka's revenue model runs on RPM billing codes that CMS adjusts annually. I accepted that regulatory risk with Oscar because the consequence of reinventing health insurance for millions of Americans was large enough to override my preference for technology risk over market risk. Miraka's scope is narrower: one specialty, one care transition. The 2026 Physician Fee Schedule lowered RPM billing thresholds in Miraka's favor, but a single adverse CMS rule change could collapse unit economics overnight. That's the kind of policy dependency I've learned to avoid after burning hundreds of millions in cleantech on companies that needed favorable regulation to survive.

The bull case I have to take seriously: Miraka's founders have been grinding on this specific problem since 2021 and are now generating real revenue from real patients at a real hospital. If the AI improves over time and progressively reduces the clinician-to-patient ratio, what starts as a clinical services business could evolve into a technology-leveraged platform where the marginal cost of monitoring each additional patient approaches zero. At that point, the affordability thesis kicks in — continuous cardiac monitoring for every discharged patient at a fraction of today's cost. The 94% capture rate would mean cardiac care after discharge becomes effectively universal rather than an afterthought serving one in ten patients. That's the trajectory that would make this exciting. But I don't see evidence that the AI is on that trajectory yet — the current model requires clinical staff to scale, which means linear costs. Without a concrete path to 10x improvement in clinician leverage, Miraka is a well-executed clinical operations company, not a technology bet with asymmetric upside.

Miraka scores on founder learning rate — the Overwatch-to-Miraka iteration, the hospital sales at 23, the domain persistence. But it falls short on non-linear technical approach (operational differentiation, not a technology breakthrough) and consequence magnitude (improving a care pathway, not reinventing one). I need either a 10x technical wedge or a consequence so large it overrides the technical gap, the way DoorDash's planetary-scale food access consequence overrode its lack of technology novelty. Miraka has neither at the scale required.
