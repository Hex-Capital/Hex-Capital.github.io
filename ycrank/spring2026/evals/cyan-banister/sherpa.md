# Sherpa -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I see when I look at Sherpa: an AI wrapper in a transparent market. My partner Lee has a phrase for this — he calls it out directly — and the pattern is exactly what we've trained ourselves to avoid. The core capability is generating marketing copy variants using LLMs and deploying them through a JavaScript snippet. Any team with access to foundation models and a weekend could build a comparable prototype. Mutiny has $72.6 million in funding and $20.7 million in revenue and already does AI-powered website optimization for B2B marketers — adding automated A/B testing is a feature sprint, not a new company. Optimizely sits at $400 million ARR with an enterprise customer base that could absorb this capability without blinking. When I run the ten-year question — where does this technology end up in a decade? — I can't find the infrastructure hiding beneath the surface. With Crusoe, Bitcoin mining was the weird shell and energy infrastructure was the truth underneath. With Sherpa, automated A/B testing is the surface... and it's also the bottom. There's no deeper layer.

Tell me the founders' story. Ethan Kinnan graduated from UW in 2024 with a finance degree, worked in partnerships at Lindy, had a prior startup called Finquire that lasted about a year. Norbu Sonam studied CS at RPI, did solid engineering work at Microsoft shipping Copilot's image generation feature to GA, then joined Lindy as a software engineer. They both worked at an AI agent company, saw that CRO workflows were manual and slow, and decided to automate them. That's a perfectly reasonable founding story — but it's a market observation, not a biographical forge. When I hear "I was at an AI agent company and spotted an automation opportunity," I hear career optimization, not inevitability. Nobody in this story was broken and rebuilt by the problem they're solving. Nobody was up at 3 AM unable to sleep because conversion rate optimization was eating them alive. The problem didn't find these founders — they found it through adjacency. I've been wrong about this before, but the pattern of my biggest wins is founders who physically could not stop working on their problem regardless of whether anyone funded them. I don't see that signal here.

The invisible system test is where this falls hardest for me. A/B testing is one of the most transparent, well-understood practices in digital marketing. Optimizely, VWO, Google Optimize (before it was sunsetted) — these tools have been available and widely adopted for over a decade. Marketing teams know exactly what they're paying, how the tools work, and what outcomes to expect. There's no hidden tax, no information asymmetry exploiting anyone, no opaque structure extracting value from people who don't understand the system. Sherpa makes an existing process faster. That's valuable — I'm not dismissing the utility — but it's an efficiency gain in a legible market, and that's fundamentally different from what drives my highest-conviction bets. When I backed Affirm, the thesis was that credit card companies were hiding the true cost of borrowing behind deliberately confusing terms. When we invested in Ownwell, the thesis was that property tax systems were designed to be opaque so municipalities could over-assess without challenge. Sherpa doesn't have an equivalent structural revelation. The companies it replaces aren't hiding anything — they're just slower.

Let me steelman this, because I owe every company that. The bull case: the CRO market is genuinely large, the manual workflow bottleneck is real, and these founders have legitimate technical chops from Microsoft and Lindy. If the data flywheel compounds — if learning from a million-plus website visits across customers creates a genuine cross-industry understanding of what converts — then the first mover who accumulates that dataset wins. The claimed 15-20% conversion lift in 30 days is compelling if it holds across diverse customer types. And the timing is right — LLMs crossed a quality threshold for generating plausible marketing copy precisely when this team was positioned to build. If Sherpa executes faster than Mutiny can pivot and faster than Optimizely can innovate, there's a window. But that window is narrow, the incumbents are well-resourced, and the only named customer is their former employer. The 1,047,213 visits learning base cannot be independently verified, and a single testimonial from the company where both founders previously worked doesn't count as market validation in my book — that's a warm referral dressed up as product-market fit.

Nothing about this makes me uncomfortable, and that's the problem. No VC would shift in their chair hearing "we automate A/B testing with AI." Nobody's been told no 300 times. This is consensus technology applied to a consensus problem in a consensus market. I wish them well — the founders are clearly smart and capable, and this might be a fine business. But this isn't a Long Journey bet. My money goes to the magically weird, the things that sound wrong until they're obviously right, the founders who were forged for a specific fight. Sherpa is a competent product in a crowded lane, and that's just not where I write checks.
