# Panta -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Commercial insurance brokerage is one of those problems that has been sitting in plain sight for decades, and almost nobody with the technical ability to fix it wants to go near it. Fifty-state licensing. Carrier portals that look like they were designed in 2004. Fifty-step workflows involving email threads, phone calls, and manual form re-keying. E&O liability if your system gets a coverage wrong. This is textbook schlep blindness -- the exact kind of ugly, regulatory, tedious complexity that causes smart technical people to unconsciously filter the problem out before they even consider working on it. In that respect, Panta's target reminds me of what Stripe looked at in payments: the pain was obvious to everyone, but the schlep kept people away. And the fact that both founders went and got commercial insurance broker licenses -- that's not a weekend project. That signals genuine willingness to walk into the schlep rather than around it.

But here's what I can't find anywhere: how did they discover this problem? Vincent Chen was building foundation models at Google. Frank Wang was a full-stack engineer at Apple. Neither of them worked in insurance. There's no mention of a family construction business that couldn't get coverage, no prior role at an insurance company, no personal anecdote about watching a broker spend two weeks placing a policy. When two ML engineers from Google and Apple decide to build an AI brokerage, the most natural explanation is that they identified a large, manual market susceptible to AI automation. That's the "startup idea machine" pattern -- start with "AI is transformative," then scan for industries with lots of manual labor, then pick one. The idea may be excellent, but it looks manufactured rather than organic. And manufactured ideas, however logical they appear on paper, lack the deep understanding of user psychology and market quirks that comes from lived experience.

The competitive picture concerns me too. Harper, from YC W25, has raised $47M and served 5,000+ businesses building essentially the same thing: an AI-native licensed brokerage. Fernstone and Acolite are also YC companies in adjacent insurance AI. When I'm seeing four YC companies in the same narrow vertical within two batches, that's no longer a hidden opportunity -- it's a consensus category. The best startup ideas provoke the reaction "that's crazy" or "why didn't someone do this already?" Insurance AI brokerage is now provoking the reaction "oh, another one of those." Panta's differentiation is focusing on the harder E&S/specialty market -- construction, oil & gas, hazmat -- and that's genuinely the schleppier end. But Harper has $47M and a twelve-month head start. If they decide to move upmarket into E&S, Panta's niche advantage evaporates.

What's genuinely strong here is the technical team and the early traction. Vincent built production AI systems at Google scale -- NotebookLM, Ask Photos, Vertex AI recommendation models. Frank is an open-source contributor in the Rust and Gleam ecosystems and won HackIllinois. These are hackers who build things. And the agents run on physical Mac Minis navigating real carrier portals, file systems, and phone systems -- that's unglamorous infrastructure work, not a demo wrapped around an API call. The early numbers are real too: 127 customer reviews at 4.9/5, "hundreds of businesses" switching in roughly three months of operation, and a specific case of undercutting a traditional broker by 38% on complex oil & gas coverage. For a pre-seed company, that's meaningful pull. Businesses don't switch brokers on a whim -- there's real friction involved in moving your policies, which means the speed and cost advantage is compelling enough to overcome inertia.

The bull case for Panta is that the E&S specialty niche is exactly right. Harper going broad into SMB is the easy path. Panta targeting construction, hazmat trucking, and oil & gas is the hard path -- and hard paths have less competition for a structural reason. If these founders can build proprietary carrier appetite data for hard-to-place risks, that becomes a compounding moat that neither Harper nor any SaaS tool can replicate. The brokerage model generates commissions from day one, and if AI replaces the human brokers who account for most of the cost structure, the margins really could look like software. In a $135B E&S market growing at 12.5% annually, even a small slice is enormous. And the team is technically strong enough to build something genuinely differentiated in agent reliability and placement speed.

What keeps me from a higher conviction score is the combination of a missing organic origin story and a crowded competitive landscape. I don't see evidence of the co-founders' shared history either -- Google and Apple, no visible university overlap, no public information on how they met. That's one of my stronger red flags. The schlep willingness is real and impressive, but the schlep alone isn't enough when I can't trace the idea back to something in the founders' own lives. Good founders can absolutely learn a domain deeply enough to win -- but at this price point, with Harper holding a $47M head start, I need to see either an unmistakable organic backstory or evidence of the kind of creative resourcefulness that suggests these founders will outmaneuver a much better-funded competitor. I see neither yet. This is a competent technical team in a genuine schlep market, but the signal composition puts them in the territory of "promising but not yet convincing."
