# OpenSpec -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about OpenSpec: this is a consensus play. When GitHub — backed by Microsoft — has already shipped Spec Kit with 71,000 stars, when Amazon launched Kiro with 250,000 developers, when Tessl raised $125 million at a $750 million valuation, the category is no longer difficult to assess. It's no longer unpopular. The biggest companies in tech have collectively said "yes, developers need structured specs for AI coding agents." That's the opposite of the signal I've trained myself to follow. The most promising companies I've backed — SpaceX, Uber, Crusoe Energy — were ones where the market had said "no" so loudly that the silence around them was deafening. OpenSpec is building in a space where the market is shouting "yes" from every direction, and when everybody agrees on the problem, what you're really competing on is execution and distribution against incumbents with functionally unlimited resources.

I want to know Tabish Bidiwale's story — not the business, the person. And what the dossier gives me is a solid professional narrative: computer science degree from the University of Sydney, senior software engineer at Q-CTRL through their growth to a $113 million Series B, six-plus years of building software. He saw AI coding agents recreate the same misalignment problems he'd watched human teams struggle with, and he built a tool to fix it. That's professional founder-market fit, and it's real. But it's not the biographical thread I listen for — the hardship that forges an unbreakable commitment, the reason someone will keep going when the company is in crisis and everyone says to quit. I don't see evidence that Tabish has been through the fire in a way that makes him the singular person to solve this problem. He's a capable engineer who identified a real gap. That's the profile of a thousand good YC founders. It's not the profile that makes me drop everything. I'm also noting he appears to be co-building Australis, an Australian language model, which raises questions about singular focus — though that may be inactive.

The strongest bull case for OpenSpec is the "Switzerland" argument: as GitHub and Amazon lock developers into their respective ecosystems, there's real value in an agent-agnostic, IDE-agnostic standard that works across all 28-plus coding assistants. The 27,800 GitHub stars in roughly seven months of existence is genuinely impressive community traction — that's organic developer love, and you can't fake it. If spec documents become embedded in codebases across thousands of teams, those switching costs compound. And if Tabish can build Fission AI into a compelling team collaboration layer on top of the open-source framework, the open-core monetization path is well-trodden by companies like GitLab and HashiCorp. For this to be a great investment, you'd have to believe that developer loyalty to an open standard will outweigh the gravitational pull of platform incumbents — that OpenSpec becomes the neutral spec layer the way Kubernetes became the neutral orchestration layer despite every cloud provider wanting their own. It's possible. But Kubernetes had Google's backing and emerged before the cloud providers had competing solutions entrenched. OpenSpec is arriving after the incumbents.

What I don't see — and this matters deeply to me — is any angle on economic access or making invisible systems visible. This is a productivity tool for software engineers, who are already among the best-compensated workers in the economy. It doesn't create income for people who lack it, doesn't bring transparency to an opaque system, doesn't open doors that were closed. Uber was a logistics company, but what I saw was drivers creating income on their own terms. Affirm was a fintech company, but what I saw was transparent credit replacing hidden fees. Carta was a cap table tool, but what I saw was employees finally understanding what their equity was worth. OpenSpec makes developers more efficient at specifying features for AI agents. That's useful. It's not the kind of impact that gives me a jolt. There's also no regulatory moat here, no stigmatized category that deters other investors — it's MIT-licensed open source in the hottest sector in tech. The space that scares people away is usually where I lean in. Nothing about developer tooling for AI agents scares anyone away right now.

Tabish is a solo founder, which avoids the co-founder conflict that destroyed HQ Trivia, but it also means he's carrying the full weight of what will need to become a commercial enterprise — sales, marketing, enterprise GTM — on top of maintaining a thriving open-source community. The dossier says the company is not currently hiring. That's a lot of surface area for one person, especially when your competitors have the engineering and distribution resources of Microsoft and Amazon behind them.
