# shortkit -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most striking thing about ShortKit is the gap between the founder's domain expertise and the market's structural readiness. Michael Seleman spent six years building YouTube Shorts infrastructure at Google -- that's genuinely rare. The number of engineers who've built short-form video delivery systems at that scale is probably double digits globally. When I funded Color Genomics, I was building on deep domain knowledge from my PhD work. Seleman is doing the same thing: productizing infrastructure he built inside a platform company. The question is whether the market outside that platform company actually wants to buy it as a service -- and on that question, I'm skeptical.

Start with the structural moment. What has changed in the last 12-24 months that makes embeddable short-form video infrastructure a market now? The behavioral shift to short-form video happened between 2019 and 2022 -- TikTok hit a billion MAU in 2021, YouTube Shorts and Reels launched in 2020-2021. That's not a current inflection; it's a mature trend diffusing into adjacent categories. The dossier's "why now" is mostly inferred: codec advances like AV1 hardware decoding are incremental improvements, not capability thresholds that unlock new categories the way GPT-3-to-GPT-4 unlocked legal AI for Harvey. When I map technology adoption curves, I'm looking for a specific capability jump that makes something impossible become possible. Short-form video delivery was already possible -- Seleman himself built it at YouTube. The question is whether enough non-social apps will want to embed TikTok-style feeds to create a standalone market, and I don't see evidence that inflection is happening now versus two years ago or two years from now.

The competitive landscape reinforces this concern. BytePlus is the elephant: ByteDance literally created the most successful short-form video platform in history and is selling that stack through a commercial subsidiary. ShortKit's positioning against BytePlus is "developer-first" and "lighter integration" -- which is the kind of UX advantage that erodes under competitive pressure from a vendor with unlimited engineering resources. Mux is the other structural threat: at $46.1M revenue and 4,900 customers, they have existing developer relationships and trust. Adding short-form-specific SDK features would be a product extension for Mux, not a new product. When I evaluate competitive positioning, I ask whether the startup's advantage is structural or circumstantial. Seleman's knowledge is real but not permanently exclusive -- Mux hires ex-YouTube engineers too.

The product-to-distribution trajectory is where this company loses me most. My clearest conviction bets -- Stripe, Figma, Gusto -- all started with a specific product wedge that naturally expanded into a platform serving adjacent needs through the same customer relationship. Stripe's payment API became the distribution channel for Atlas, Billing, and Treasury. Gusto's payroll became the channel for HR, benefits, and compliance. ShortKit's SDK includes feed mechanics, transcoding, CDN delivery, analytics, ad integration, and AI captioning -- but these are all features of a single product, not separate products distributed through a common channel. What's the second product? More video features? That's feature development, not distribution expansion. If they broaden to long-form and live streaming, they become Mux with less funding. The ceiling feels capped.

The bull case I'd construct: "every app becomes a media app" is directionally correct. Publishers, e-commerce brands, fitness apps, education platforms -- they all want video engagement, and building it in-house is genuinely expensive. ShortKit sits in the "boring infrastructure" zone I love -- video SDKs don't make headlines -- and Bhammar's BusRight experience ($7M raised, 700% revenue growth, customers in 23 states) demonstrates he can run a B2B sales motion. If the embeddable short-form video market turns out to be 10x larger than it appears because every consumer app eventually needs this capability, and if BytePlus's geopolitical risk and enterprise positioning leave a gap for a developer-first alternative, ShortKit could be the PagerDuty of video feeds -- boring but essential. For this to work, though, I'd need to see evidence that non-social apps are actively seeking this capability at scale, and the dossier shows zero named customers, zero revenue figures, and zero public traction signals beyond YC admission. The market pull hasn't materialized visibly.

The single miracle here is market adoption: can you convince enough product teams to embed a third-party short-form video SDK rather than building in-house or using a general-purpose video API? That's a market-pull miracle rather than a technical miracle, and market-pull miracles are harder for founders to control. When Airbnb needed marketplace liquidity, they could engineer it through Craigslist integration and professional photography. ShortKit's adoption miracle depends on a broader industry trend materializing at the right pace -- too slow and they burn cash waiting, too fast and Mux or BytePlus absorbs the opportunity. I don't see evidence the team has identified a wedge to force this adoption rather than waiting for it.

Solid founder backgrounds, genuine domain expertise, competent technical execution evident from the product they've built. But the market isn't at a structural inflection -- it's diffusing from a peak that happened years ago. The product-to-distribution ceiling is low. And the competitive dynamics favor incumbents who can add short-form as a feature rather than a standalone company whose entire business depends on it.
