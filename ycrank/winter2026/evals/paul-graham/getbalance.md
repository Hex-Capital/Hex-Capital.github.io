# Balance -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Mathias founded an accountancy. He was CFO at a YC company. He spent his days inside the broken machinery of small business accounting and decided to rebuild it with AI. That's organic problem discovery, and it's the thing I look for first. He didn't read a market report about the $680B accounting services industry and think "there must be a startup here." He was doing the work, saw how much of it was mindless reconciliation, and built the thing he wished existed. That origin story passes my most basic test -- the idea grew from lived experience, not a brainstorming session.

But here's what concerns me. Schlep blindness requires the schlep to be blind. The accounting problem isn't hiding in plain sight anymore -- it's standing in the middle of the road with $700 million in venture capital pointing at it. Pilot raised $325M. Digits raised $100M. Finally raised $305M. Haven has the essentially identical model -- AI does the reconciliation, humans review and sign off -- and already has $1M+ in revenue. When I talk about schlep blindness, I mean problems like payments before Stripe, where thousands of programmers knew the pain but their unconscious minds steered them away from dealing with banks and regulators. Nobody's unconscious is steering them away from AI accounting in 2026. It's a consensus category. The schlep is real -- multi-jurisdiction tax filing, professional liability, regulatory compliance across Danish GAAP, US GAAP, and IFRS -- but it's visible to every funded competitor in the space. Mathias's personal experience gives him conviction, but conviction alone doesn't create competitive advantage when well-capitalized teams share it.

The multi-jurisdiction launch also gives me pause. They're operating across Denmark, the US, and the UK with four people. Facebook launched at Harvard. Stripe launched for developers. The principle is to keep a fire contained until it gets extremely hot. Three countries with three tax regimes, three sets of accounting standards, and three regulatory frameworks is the opposite of deliberate narrowness -- it's surface area that dilutes the team's focus. At this stage, I'd rather see them own one market completely than be thinly present in three.

The team itself is genuinely well-composed for this specific problem. Gus has real AI engineering depth -- NLP research at Oxford, production ML systems at Orbital Witness, vision-language model pipelines. That's not someone calling APIs; that's someone who understands the underlying technology. Emil brings McKinsey rigor plus ground-level startup experience as employee #1 at Kapa AI. And Mathias brings the domain knowledge that makes the whole thing credible. The coverage across accounting, AI engineering, and operations is deliberate and complete. What I don't see is evidence of the extreme resourcefulness that separates great founders from good ones. No stories of creative problem-solving under duress, no evidence of doing things that don't scale in ways that reveal unusual determination. The backgrounds signal competence, not the kind of intensity that makes you say "these people are animals."

The bull case is structural and worth taking seriously. Traditional accounting firms can't adopt AI without cannibalizing their billable-hour model. Intuit and Xero can't become accountancies without destroying their channel partnerships with the accounting firms they sell to. This creates a genuine gap for an integrated player that is both the software and the service. If Balance can build a data flywheel -- where every client's books make the categorization models better, which makes the human review faster, which lowers costs, which attracts more clients -- the compounding could be significant. And the market is truly enormous. Even a tiny slice of $650B is a big company. The conversational interface via Slack and WhatsApp is a smart distribution insight too -- it meets SMB owners where they already are instead of asking them to learn another dashboard. If Mathias's domain knowledge translates into faster regulatory navigation than competitors, and if Gus's technical depth produces categorization accuracy that meaningfully reduces human review time, they could win on unit economics even against better-funded competitors.

But that's a lot of ifs, and the core problem remains: this is a crowded market where the differentiation is execution-dependent rather than structurally defensible. I don't see evidence of user pull beyond "serving clients" with no revenue figures attached. There's no signal of the kind of organic demand where users find you without being asked, tell their friends, or would be genuinely disrupted if you disappeared. The 753 YC launch upvotes are noise. At pre-seed I don't demand traction, but I want at least one story of a customer who can't live without this. I'm not getting that here.

This is a competent team with a genuine founder-problem connection building in a market that's already absorbed hundreds of millions in capital from competitors doing adjacent things. The organic discovery is real but the competitive moat isn't. I'd want to see them narrow to one jurisdiction, demonstrate unit economics that beat Haven's model, and show me a customer who'd be devastated if Balance went away. Until then, this is a pass from my own checkbook.
