# Tasklet -- Elad Gil Evaluation

$5M ARR with >1,200% growth since January 2026 is the kind of traction that makes you look twice at any company -- and Andrew Lee building it makes you look a third time. Lee co-founded Firebase (YC S11), served as CTO through the Google acquisition, and helped scale a platform now used by roughly 15% of all developers. Jonny Dimond led Google Cloud Firestore. These two have worked together for over a decade across Firebase, Google, Shortwave, and now Tasklet. When I invested in Stripe, part of the conviction was that Patrick and John Collison had the infrastructure DNA to build a platform layer, not just a product. Lee and Dimond have that same DNA. The $20M Series A from USV, Lightspeed, Jeff Dean, and the Collison brothers confirms that other serious infrastructure investors see it too. On the founder dimension alone, Tasklet scores near the ceiling of what I expect from any YC company.

But I always start with market structure timing, not founder biography, and here Tasklet has a problem. AI agents for knowledge work is the single most consensus category in technology right now. Every major foundation model provider -- Google with Gemini and Workspace, Microsoft with Copilot and M365, OpenAI with Operator -- is building autonomous agent capabilities integrated with productivity suites that already have hundreds of millions of users. Lindy has raised ~$50M, Relevance AI $37M, and Zapier ($310M revenue, ~$5B valuation) knows it needs to evolve toward agent-first. When I backed Coinbase in 2012, crypto infrastructure was a graveyard that mainstream investors actively avoided. When I wrote the Perplexity seed check, AI search was considered a failed concept. Tasklet occupies the opposite position: a market where every investor and every incumbent is already competing. The structural shift enabling this -- foundation model reliability crossing the threshold for autonomous multi-step execution, MCP standardizing tool connectivity -- is real, but it's priced into every deck I see. IDC projects AI copilots embedded in 80% of enterprise workplace apps by 2026. That's not a nonobvious window; that's a consensus forecast.

Tasklet's "agent operating system" framing invokes infrastructure-layer positioning, and there's a credible version of this story. The dossier describes a "cloud-native agent runtime, best-in-class context engineering, a universal connections layer, and multi-model support." If Tasklet becomes the default runtime that businesses build their automated workflows on -- the way Firebase became the default real-time database for mobile developers -- switching costs compound and the platform becomes progressively harder to rip out. Lee and Dimond have literally built that kind of sticky infrastructure before. But the current product manifests as an end-user chat interface, not a developer API. Tasklet's integration layer runs through Pipedream, which means Tasklet is building on someone else's integration infrastructure -- exactly the pattern I flag as a red flag ("startup building an application on a contested infrastructure layer"). HTTP API and MCP server support provide alternative paths, but the primary connectivity dependency on a third party is a real structural concern. The gap between "agent OS" positioning and current product architecture keeps this in the middle of the infrastructure-layer spectrum.

Andrew Lee's acknowledgment that Tasklet "is super expensive to run" activates my margin architecture concern directly. At $35-$250/month price points with compute-intensive execution -- Ubuntu VMs on Google Cloud, full browser automation, sandboxed code environments -- the unit economics face real pressure. This maps uncomfortably to the pattern I saw with Wish: growth velocity without margin strength. Model inference costs will decline over time, which helps, but Tasklet's compute footprint extends well beyond inference to include VM provisioning and browser orchestration. The tiered SaaS model is reasonable but doesn't yet show evidence of the outcome-based pricing or expansion revenue dynamics that characterize the strongest margin architectures. Tasklet's $5M ARR proves people will pay, but whether the business can reach 70%+ gross margins at scale remains an open question that the CEO himself has flagged.

On single-miracle viability, Tasklet's position is moderately favorable. The technology miracle -- LLM capabilities sufficient for autonomous workflow execution -- is happening exogenously and is already partially achieved (hence $5M ARR). The primary remaining risk is distribution: can Tasklet win against Google, Microsoft, and OpenAI when those incumbents can embed agent capabilities into productivity suites that are already installed on every enterprise laptop? This isn't a three-miracle company, but it's not a clean single-miracle company either. The Shortwave legacy adds a secondary concern -- running two products (Tasklet and Shortwave, which "remain distinct products with an integration between them") with an 8-person team risks the kind of focus dilution that kills companies competing against well-resourced incumbents. The sales email listed is still sales@shortwave.com, which is a small but telling detail about organizational transition.

The strongest bull case for Tasklet: a Firebase-pedigree team with $5M ARR growing >1,200%, backed by Jeff Dean and the Collisons, building the agent runtime layer during the biggest platform shift since mobile. Zapier's business model cannibalization risk (per-workflow pricing that breaks when the workflow disappears) creates a structural opening for a new entrant, just as legacy payment processors couldn't adapt when Stripe built the payments API. If Tasklet captures the agent runtime layer the way Firebase captured real-time data sync, the infrastructure compounding makes this a multi-billion dollar outcome. That's a real case, and the traction is extraordinary for any stage. But when I test it against my framework, the market timing dimension -- which I weight most heavily at 30 points because it's where I've generated the most differentiated returns -- actively works against Tasklet. This isn't a nonobvious market; it's the most obvious market in tech. My best investments have come from reading structural shifts that consensus dismisses, not from picking winners in consensus categories. The founder profile is exceptional, but I've seen exceptional founders get competed to death in obvious markets where incumbents have massive distribution advantages. The specific condition that would flip me to Invest: evidence that Tasklet is becoming developer infrastructure (an API/SDK that other companies build on) rather than an end-user application -- that would change the infrastructure-layer score materially and reframe the competitive dynamics away from a direct fight with Google and Microsoft.
