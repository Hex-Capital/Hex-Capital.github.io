# Payna -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The most striking thing about Payna is the direction of its pivot. The founders built an on-chain earned-wage-access platform at ETHGlobal Buenos Aires in November 2025 -- connecting DeFi liquidity to workers living paycheck to paycheck. That's an ambitious problem affecting hundreds of millions of people globally. Then they pivoted into B2B compliance workflow software for financial licensing. I've seen a lot of pivots in forty years. The good ones move toward bigger ambition as founders discover what's really broken. This one moved in the opposite direction -- from a problem that affects workers' daily survival to a problem that affects how quickly a fintech company fills out state licensing paperwork. That trajectory concerns me more than the pivot itself.

Let me apply my fundamental filter: if Payna succeeds completely, what changes? Crypto and fintech companies get their money transmitter licenses faster and cheaper. Compliance law firms lose some billing. State regulatory filings are auto-generated instead of manually assembled. This is real value -- I'm not dismissing the pain. But the consequence of full success is a useful software business, not a transformed industry. Nobody's life is fundamentally different. Financial regulation still works the same way; the forms just get filled out by AI instead of paralegals. I have a visceral reaction when the maximum upside of a startup is making an existing bureaucratic process 80% more efficient. These founders are too smart to spend their careers on a problem this constrained.

The competitive situation sharpens the concern. Brico has $21.6M in funding, marquee customers including Marqeta and BitGo, Forbes Top 50 FinTech recognition, and claims 600% year-over-year revenue growth -- all in the same licensing automation market. When I backed Juniper against Cisco, Cisco was a complacent incumbent with a legacy architecture that couldn't adopt TCP/IP routing without cannibalizing itself. That structural asymmetry is what gives an outsider a chance. Here, Brico is not a complacent incumbent -- it's a fast-moving, well-funded startup that proved the market twelve months before Payna existed in its current form. Payna's core technology -- AI document generation over publicly available regulatory data -- is not proprietary. There's no structural reason Brico can't build continuous compliance scoring or any other feature Payna claims as differentiation. This is an execution race against a competitor with a massive head start, and execution races are exactly the kind of bet where the consequence of winning is still just a share of a modest market.

The strongest bull case would require several things to be true simultaneously: that the GENIUS Act and expanding state-level crypto regulation create enough licensing demand to support multiple winners; that Payna's "compliance operating system" framing -- continuous scoring, cryptographic timestamps, proactive risk monitoring -- represents a genuinely different product category from Brico's workflow automation; and that Aaron Chen's crypto-native perspective lets him build compliance tooling that resonates with crypto companies in ways a non-crypto compliance vendor cannot. The RegTech market growing at 17-20% CAGR is real. If crypto regulation continues to fragment across states while simultaneously expanding at the federal level, the licensing compliance burden could grow faster than any single vendor can serve. In that scenario, Payna captures a meaningful slice of an expanding market. But even this bull case describes a $500M compliance SaaS business -- not an industry transformation, and not the kind of outcome that justifies the opportunity cost of founders who clearly have the energy and technical ability to attack bigger problems.

Aaron Chen shows real capability -- a16z CSX-backed startup experience, ETHGlobal wins, the Ritual Foundation Fellowship. He's clearly talented in the crypto ecosystem. But talent deployed against an incremental problem produces incremental outcomes. He Song's background is entirely opaque, which makes it impossible to assess complementary skills or learning rate. For a compliance software company, the absence of anyone with deep regulatory, legal, or compliance operations experience is a gap that matters -- not because I require domain experts (I usually prefer outsiders), but because the outsider advantage only works when the outsider brings a fundamentally different approach. Building the same product as the incumbent, just later, is not an outsider play. It's a follower play.
