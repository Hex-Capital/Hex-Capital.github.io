# Adialante -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

MRI machine design has remained essentially unchanged for forty years. That sentence alone should command attention, because the greatest returns in my portfolio — General Matter attacking nuclear fuel enrichment frozen since the 1970s, Substrate challenging chip fabrication locked into the ASML paradigm, Hadrian reimagining defense manufacturing — share precisely this structural feature: decades of technological stagnation creating enormous pent-up demand for a genuine discontinuity. Adialante's FREE technique — Frequency-modulated Rabi-Encoded Echoes, published in *Magnetic Resonance in Medicine* (Torres et al., 2022) — eliminates B0 gradient coils entirely, a component that the MRI establishment has treated as physically indispensable since the technology's inception. One-third less hardware, one-tenth the weight, one-tenth the power, silent operation. This is not an incremental improvement to existing MRI — it is new physics. The canonical question applies: what important truth does Adialante hold that the market disputes? That gradient coils are unnecessary for diagnostic-quality magnetic resonance imaging. Siemens, GE, and Philips have invested billions in the current paradigm. Hyperfine spent over $200 million and five years bringing a *different* portable MRI technology to FDA clearance. The establishment's implicit claim is that radical miniaturization requires sacrificing diagnostic fidelity. Adialante's counter-claim — that entirely new spatial encoding physics can preserve image quality while eliminating the most massive and expensive subsystem — is the structure of a genuine secret.

Efraín Torres invented the FREE technique during his PhD at the University of Minnesota under Dr. Michael Garwood, a professor of radiology with decades of imaging expertise. Torres then left academia to build Adialante around his invention — an irreversible commitment that distinguishes definite optimism from academic hedging. His co-founder Parker Jenkins shares deep prehistory: they met on the first day of classes at Marquette University, lived on the same dormitory floor, and both pursued biomedical engineering graduate studies at UMN. "Founders should share a prehistory before they start a company together — otherwise they're just rolling dice." Torres and Jenkins are not rolling dice. Adialante's technical bench — Michael Mullen (Physics PhD, UMN), Sai Abitha Srinivas (BME PhD, Vanderbilt, Rabi Award winner), Daniel Pizetta (Computational Physics PhD, University of São Paulo) — is extraordinary for a pre-seed company with approximately $3 million raised. Five PhD-level engineers building custom MRI hardware from new physics is not a team assembled to "iterate toward product-market fit." Adialante's plan is specific: begin with prostate cancer screening for urology clinics (pre-biopsy MRI, riding the NCCN/PI-RADS guideline tailwind), then expand concentrically to orthopedics, breast, renal, and neuroimaging. Start by monopolizing a small, specific market — urology practices that currently refer patients out for expensive hospital-based MRI — then expand. This is the Facebook pattern: 100% penetration at Harvard before expanding campus by campus. Adialante aims for 100% penetration at urology clinics that lack MRI access before expanding to broader oncology screening.

Adialante's mobile-as-a-service model deserves separate analysis because it represents a distribution insight bundled with the technology. A truck arrives at a medical practice; patients receive scans on-site; the clinic pays a flat per-scan fee with zero capital expenditure. This eliminates the core adoption barrier — multi-million-dollar equipment purchase — and transforms the sales conversation from a capital budget approval to an operating expense decision. No infrastructure build-out, no specialized rooms, no technical staff. The model is clever, but cleverness alone does not constitute proven distribution. Adialante has no reported revenue, no signed LOIs, no scan volume data. The $3 million raised (per MNSBIR, July 2025) and $3.5 million seed target (per UMN CTSI, March 2024) signal a company still in the pre-commercial phase, with the $1.18 million NSF SBIR Phase II grant validating the science rather than the business. Against competitors — Hyperfine with $13.5 million in FY2025 revenue, Promaxo with $72-81 million raised, Openwater with $100 million raised — Adialante is dramatically undercapitalized. But capital comparison is misleading here. Hyperfine is a bedside brain-only device (different application), Promaxo requires clinics to purchase hardware (different model), Openwater uses optoelectronics rather than MRI magnets (different physics), and Ezra merely applies AI software to existing third-party MRI machines (no proprietary hardware at all, acquired by Function Health for undisclosed terms). None of these competitors has built what Adialante is building: a gradient-free, mobile, diagnostic-grade MRI service for cancer screening. Multiple funded players in the broader "low-cost MRI" space is less concerning when none shares Adialante's specific technology or delivery model — they are adjacent, not competitive.

The bull case for Adialante activates my strongest invest signals simultaneously. Atoms over bits — custom MRI hardware with new physics, not a software wrapper. A sector frozen for four decades. Patent-pending technology requiring deep MR physics expertise that cannot be replicated by throwing capital at the problem. A founder who invented the core technology and assembled a PhD-heavy team to build it. A specific beachhead market (urology pre-biopsy MRI) with a zero-friction distribution model. And perhaps most compellingly: ninety percent of the global population lacks MRI access. Adialante is not competing for existing MRI market share — it is creating access where none exists, which is how you expand a $6.5 billion market by an order of magnitude. The General Matter parallel is structural: a physics-based technology originating from elite research (UMN vs. national labs), attacking a stagnant industry, with a pathway to government embedding. The VA healthcare system alone operates over 1,300 outpatient sites, many in rural areas without MRI access — if Adialante's mobile fleet becomes operationally embedded in VA healthcare, the Palantir switching-cost dynamic applies. The bear case is equally real: Adialante has no FDA clearance, no clinical validation data comparing FREE to conventional MRI, and building hardware plus operating a mobile fleet is extraordinarily capital-intensive. Torres is a first-time founder with no prior exit, no network provenance in my ecosystem, and no government contracts secured. The path from $3 million raised to a deployed, FDA-cleared mobile MRI fleet requires at least $50-100 million in additional capital and several years of regulatory work. Physics can fail at clinical scale — the Stemcentrx lesson is that secrets about the physical world are harder to verify than secrets about software.

The decisive question is whether Adialante's secret — that gradient coils are unnecessary for diagnostic MRI — can survive clinical validation. If the FREE technique produces diagnostic-quality images comparable to conventional MRI for prostate cancer screening, Adialante holds a monopoly position: the only company with gradient-free MRI physics, patent-protected, deployed as a zero-capex mobile service. If the images fall short of diagnostic standards, the entire thesis collapses — there is no "partial" version of this bet. The condition that would flip me from cautious invest to strong conviction: published clinical data demonstrating diagnostic equivalence to conventional MRI for prostate lesion detection, ideally in a multi-site comparison study that radiologists find convincing. Absent that, I am investing in the structure — genuine secret, monopoly path, atoms moat, definite founder — while acknowledging that the physical world has not yet confirmed the claim. The two dimensions most decisive for my verdict are Physical-World Technology Moat, where Adialante scores near the maximum any pre-seed company could achieve (new physics, custom hardware, patent-pending, years-to-replicate), and Monopoly Secret, where Adialante holds a genuine contrarian insight that the establishment dismisses but that, if validated, makes the existing paradigm obsolete for screening applications. Those two dimensions, combined, make Adialante the kind of bet my framework was designed to identify — even before the market agrees.
