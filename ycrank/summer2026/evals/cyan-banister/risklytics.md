# Risklytics -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Samuel Gold and Alexander Risio picked one of the most authentically unsexy, regulator-gated categories in fintech -- California wildfire underwriting after CA DOI release 052-2025 in May 2025 -- and staffed it with two Harvard Computer Science kids whose LinkedIn snippets read Harvard Undergraduate Capital Partners and "systems, intelligence, biology." Risklytics is what happens when a great sector meets founders who found it in a memo. The dimensional signature is unambiguous: the market fits my "too hard or too unsexy" lane, and the biographies do not fit the Palmer Luckey / John Hanke lane at all. That mismatch is the whole evaluation.

Palmer Luckey shipped Oculus out of his parents' garage before Anduril in 2017; John Hanke had spent a decade on Keyhole and Google Earth before I sat outside his door for Niantic ~2015. Risklytics's founder page shows Samuel Gold left Harvard after one year with involvement in the Harvard Financial Analysts Club and Alexander Risio finishing an AB/SM in CS at the same school -- no CAL FIRE ridealong, no Cal OES stint, no adjuster background, no parent who lost a policy in the 2.8 million California non-renewals between 2020 and 2025. The 131,929 CAL FIRE damage inspections are a publicly available training corpus that any competent ML engineer could ingest; the question I always ask is whether the founder was *of* the wildfire economy or *visiting* it, and the dossier reads visiting. The 91% discrimination rate on the January 2025 LA fires is real modeling craft. It is not lived contact with a burned homeowner.

ZestyAI's Z-FIRE is already trained on 1,500+ wildfires and 200B+ datapoints and is deployed with carriers writing 40% of California homeowners; CAPE Analytics was absorbed by Moody's in January 2025; Verisk, KCC, and Moody's RMS are the only three forward-looking wildfire cat models the CA DOI has approved for rate filings. This is not the Crusoe pattern of two or three credible VCs passing on aesthetic grounds -- this is the opposite, a fashionable climate-fintech fairway with the incumbents already sitting on the tarmac and a $76.97M-funded direct competitor with the exact same product-shape. Risklytics is chasing consensus, not front-running it, and my thesis is to find them before it becomes consensus.

The dossier lists Samuel Gold and Alexander Risio both as "Co-founder" with no CEO designated, no tiebreaker language, no operating-agreement disclosure -- 18 months out this is exactly the silence that produced the Yusupov/Kroll HQ Trivia collapse. Two Harvard classmates who overlapped in CS is the demographic profile most vulnerable to the 50/50 partnership pathology, because the friendship makes the tiebreaker conversation the one nobody wants to have. Compounding effect over 18 months: when ZestyAI undercuts on price and a design-partner carrier demands a bespoke retrofit-scoring endpoint, one of Risklytics's co-founders will want to build it and the other will want to hold the roadmap, and no one on the cap table can arbitrate. The absence of governance disclosure at pre-seed does not mean it will blow up -- it means I have to price the option that it will.

The regulated-unsexy dimension is the one place Risklytics genuinely earns weight in my framework. CA DOI's May 2025 approval framework created a real regulatory unlock, and wildfire underwriting sits in the same "moat compounds in domains other investors refuse to enter" bucket that produced Vigil Labs and Mindbloom -- the ceiling is not TAM-limited, it is distribution-limited. The counterfactual that would flip this from ~11/15 into the top of the range is a filed CA DOI Prop 103 review path for the Ember model that lets Risklytics's score enter rate filings, not just underwriting eligibility screens; without that clearance Risklytics is stuck at the eligibility layer where ZestyAI already lives.

Ember is an API. The pricing page is gated behind an API-key application form. I cannot install it, ride it, photograph it in the wild, or hand it to a friend at dinner -- the way I rode Uber in 2010, played Ingress before Niantic, or spotted a Flock camera on a pole in 2018. Risklytics scoring 13,685,446 California homes is impressive as an engineering artifact and invisible as a tactile one. This is the developer-tools abstraction failure mode from my pass example, softened only slightly by the fact that a homeowner-facing "score my address" widget would be trivial to build and is conspicuously absent.

Thirty-six-month forecast: Risklytics is acqui-hired by ZestyAI, Guidewire, or a mid-tier MGA platform for sub-$20M in cash-and-stock, or it dies quietly after burning through the YC round without closing a paying carrier before ZestyAI's incumbent contracts renew. The mechanism is the D1/D5 pairing driving the D3 stress: founders without lived carrier relationships try to sell an invisible API into a market where the buyer already has ZestyAI on the desktop and Moody's-CAPE in the enterprise agreement, and the two co-founders diverge on whether to pivot to consumer-facing retrofit marketplace or stay B2B.

The bull case that stings: DeepMind is my documented exception where elite pedigrees compounded via polymath range, and Hassabis had no more lived contact with reinforcement learning at kitchen-table scale than these two have with CAL FIRE inspections. Layer on the CA DOI May 2025 unlock as the exact "why now" catalyst I usually reward, and Risklytics could be the Vigil Labs of climate insurance -- regulated, unsexy, right timing. The dossier evidence that dismantles this: Hassabis compounded across chess prodigy, neuroscience PhD, and Lionhead game design before DeepMind; the Risklytics dossier surfaces one domain, computer science, for both founders, with a finance club and a systems track. That is not polymath range, that is a single vector. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict is a signed pilot with a named CA admitted carrier (Mercury, CSAA, Farmers) disclosed in the next two YC batches, plus a designated CEO on record -- absent both, Risklytics is a pass I want to be wrong about.
