# Cumulus Labs -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that jumps out to me about Cumulus Labs isn't the product or the founders — it's the market they've chosen. GPU infrastructure is the single hottest investment category in technology right now. Modal just raised at over a billion-dollar valuation. Baseten is at five billion. Together AI has raised over half a billion dollars and is running at $300 million in annualized revenue. RunPod has $120 million in ARR and half a million developers. This is consensus investing in its purest form — every fund in the Valley is scrambling to write checks into GPU cloud companies. And that's exactly the signal that makes me pull back. The most promising companies are not popular. They are difficult to assess. Cumulus Labs is operating in a space where every investor already gets it, every competitor is already funded, and the category has been thoroughly validated. By the time a market looks this obvious, the weirdness has been fully arbitraged out, and I'm competing on price and execution against teams with 50x more capital. That's not where I find my best opportunities.

Now, I want to know these founders' stories — not the pitch, not the benchmarks. How did Suryaa and Veer arrive at this problem? What I can see is genuinely impressive technical pedigree: Suryaa built a distributed GPU marketplace at TensorDock, ran infrastructure at Palantir, and Veer led a Space Force SBIR contract and contributed to NASA programs that are literally being flight-tested in orbit. These are not dilettantes. They have real domain expertise from both sides of the GPU equation — supply and demand. But I'm not hearing a survival narrative. I'm not finding the biographical thread that tells me this is the problem they were born to solve because of something that happened to them, something they lived through. "I worked at a GPU company" is professional experience, not biographical necessity. When I backed the Crusoe team, the story of how they independently arrived at turning waste gas into computation was inseparable from who they were. Here, I see two talented engineers who identified a market opportunity. That's different from founders who can't stop working on something because their life led them here.

The one signal that genuinely excites me is the co-founder relationship. These two met in third grade. They've been building together since childhood, through adolescence, through attending different universities, and now into building a company together in San Francisco. That kind of bond has been stress-tested in ways that a "we met at a hackathon" co-founding story never has been. After what I saw with HQ Trivia — where perfect timing and a brilliant product were destroyed by co-founder conflict — I pay very close attention to this dimension. Suryaa and Veer have a relationship that resembles the family-business dynamic I've come to prefer. Their complementary backgrounds reinforce this: one built GPU infrastructure, the other consumed it for mission-critical applications. They can argue from different vantage points because they've lived different sides of the same problem.

Let me steelman the bull case, because I owe that to any founders who've built something real. If Ion — their proprietary inference engine — genuinely delivers sustained performance advantages over vLLM and SGLang, that's a legitimate technical moat. The 12.5-second cold start versus Modal's 60 seconds is a meaningful number if it holds up at scale. And the workload migration and predictive packing IP could compound with usage data over time. There's a version of this where Cumulus becomes the Cloudflare of GPU inference — the lean, developer-obsessed alternative to bloated, expensive incumbents. The TAM is real and growing at 35% CAGR. If these founders can execute with the discipline Suryaa learned at Palantir and the mission-criticality Veer learned from Space Force, they could carve out a meaningful wedge even in a crowded market. The childhood bond means they won't fracture under pressure. That's the upside case, and it's not nothing.

But when I ask myself whether I'd drop everything to work for these founders, whether I feel that jolt of intuitive energy I felt with Niantic or Crusoe — I don't. Not because they aren't talented, but because the opportunity itself doesn't have the quality of being overlooked and misunderstood that I've learned to trust as my primary signal. Cheaper GPU inference is a good business. It's not a misfit idea. Nobody's telling these founders they're crazy — they're getting LinkedIn posts with 491 likes and building in YC. The infrastructure playbook here requires competing on engineering execution against teams with hundreds of millions in the bank, which means this becomes a capital war, not a conviction bet. And I'm not a capital-war investor. I'm looking for the pocket of dreamers and weirdos building something that most people haven't figured out yet. This isn't that.
