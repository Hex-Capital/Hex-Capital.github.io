# Harbor -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Albert Cai spent a month living on-site at a clinical research facility during Biolinq's pivotal trial. Not visiting — living there. Watching coordinators spend hours per subject per day manually transcribing source documents into a system that hasn't fundamentally changed since the 1990s. That detail is the most important thing in Harbor's dossier, because it's the clearest instance of organic problem discovery I've seen in this batch. Drew Houston built Dropbox because he kept forgetting his USB drive. Albert Cai built Harbor because he sat next to coordinators and watched them do the digital equivalent of retyping a book by hand, one clinical form at a time, into Medidata Rave. He didn't identify this through market analysis or TAM calculations. He lived inside the problem for four years at Biolinq — taking their wearable biosensor from prototype through first-in-human studies to FDA De Novo authorization — and the inefficiency of the data capture layer was so obvious and so painful that he couldn't unsee it. That's the "live in the future, then build what's missing" archetype operating at full strength.

Harbor's structural position reminds me of Stripe more than anything else in my portfolio. The Collison brothers saw that integrating payments was a miserable schlep — PCI compliance, bank integrations, regulatory paperwork — and that the incumbents' business models were built around that complexity persisting. Harbor is doing the same thing to clinical trial data capture. Medidata, acquired for $5.8 billion, charges per-subject and per-site fees on workflows that require manual data entry. Harbor charges a flat $2,000/month for commercial studies with no per-subject fees and eliminates the manual entry entirely through AI document extraction. Medidata can't match that pricing model without cannibalizing the revenue structure that justifies their valuation. They launched Rave Lite in October 2024 to simplify study builds, but bolting document extraction onto a legacy form-based architecture is the kind of retrofit that rarely works — it's the payments industry trying to match Stripe by adding an API to their existing gateway. Harbor's regulatory compliance stack (21 CFR Part 11, HIPAA, GDPR, validation packages shipped with every license) is the moat that most founders would never wade into. Clinical trial EDC software is a schlep that repels virtually everyone, and that repulsion is precisely what makes it defensible.

Nathan Leung's profile completes what Harbor needs. Employee number one at Jupiter, a YC S19 company, so he's built a startup from zero before. Product roles at Google and Ramp, so he's seen how larger organizations ship software. Seventy-one public repositories on GitHub with a top project at 301 stars — this is someone who builds compulsively, not someone who codes when asked to. He and Albert met at the University of Michigan, which means this isn't a co-founder pairing assembled through a matching service. Harbor has already configured a 1,600-subject randomized trial database in one week versus multi-week timelines from legacy vendors, and they're live in five clinical studies with six named customers including BioDynamik, NOVA Genomics, and University Hospitals. At pre-seed, with a two-person team, having paying customers and live clinical studies is the kind of traction that implies a level of resourcefulness the dossier understates. You don't get testimonials like "Harbor delivered a fully validated EDC in nine days at a fraction of the cost" from Cynthia Hudson, CEO of NOVA Genomics, without doing things that don't scale — personally configuring studies, hand-holding sites through onboarding, probably answering support emails at 2 AM.

The bear case deserves honest examination. Harbor faces a genuine commoditization risk: LLM-based document extraction is becoming broadly accessible, and a $5.8 billion incumbent with 1,600+ customers has more resources to integrate AI than Harbor has to build enterprise sales relationships. Clinical data liability is structurally scarier than most SaaS liability — a document extraction error in a pivotal trial could harm patients and trigger FDA enforcement. And selling to pharma companies with six-to-twelve-month procurement cycles is brutal for a two-person team. But every one of these concerns is also a barrier to entry. The regulatory validation burden that makes Harbor's product hard to build makes it equally hard for a fast-follower to replicate. The compliance requirements that slow Harbor's iteration cycle also slow Medidata's AI retrofit. And Albert has already navigated FDA De Novo authorization at Biolinq — he's not guessing about regulatory strategy, he's applying direct experience. Harbor's human-in-the-loop review for AI-extracted data is the right architectural choice for managing liability while the regulatory framework for AI clinical data matures.

Harbor's idea doesn't fully satisfy my "repels before it attracts" criterion. Clinical trial EDC is genuinely unsexy — no one at a YC demo day gets excited about electronic case report forms — and the regulatory burden repels most founders at the level of schlep blindness. But "AI for healthcare" is broadly trendy, and document extraction is a hot application category. Harbor benefits from the schlep factor at the domain level while riding a consensus wave at the technology level. That's not the worst position — Stripe was also riding the consensus wave of "software eating the world" while tackling the schlep of payment compliance — but it means Harbor's competitive insulation comes more from regulatory complexity than from the idea itself seeming wrong. The learning rate evidence is moderate: Albert's trajectory from consulting (Close Concerns) to product/regulatory (Biolinq) to founder (Harbor) shows increasing ambition and skill acquisition, and Nathan's progression from YC employee to Google to Ramp to co-founder shows a builder gaining context at each stage. But the dossier lacks the week-over-week growth metrics or specific pivot stories that would let me calibrate the slope more precisely.

Harbor's free academic tier and Albert's DiaTribe contributions signal genuine obsession with clinical research infrastructure rather than a financial optimization play. Nathan's 71 public repos signal someone who writes code the way a painter paints — because not doing it feels wrong. Harbor earns an invest recommendation because the organic problem discovery is exceptional, the schlep blindness dynamic creates real defensibility, and the founder pairing of deep domain expertise plus proven technical building ability is the combination that makes regulated infrastructure companies work. The condition that would flip me to Pass: if Medidata's Rave Lite achieves comparable document extraction accuracy within the next twelve months while maintaining their existing customer base, Harbor's wedge collapses. The condition that would elevate Harbor higher: evidence of 5-7% weekly growth in studies onboarded, or a signed enterprise MSA with a top-20 pharma company, would demonstrate that the product's pull is strong enough to overcome the sales cycle friction that kills most health tech startups at this stage.
