# Travo -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I look for is what the founder knows that cannot be taught. Four Stanford CS undergrads building "the most accurate and comprehensive real estate data set" for RV parks — and not one of them has ever worked a day in commercial real estate, property data, or outdoor hospitality. The CEO's prior exit was in education. The COO worked at a law firm and the Manhattan DA's office. The CPO wrote code for satellites. The CTO built backend at an edtech startup and Roblox. These are smart, technically capable people applying generic AI skills to a domain that demands deeply non-obvious knowledge: which county assessor databases are reliable, how RV park valuations actually differ from standard CRE, what data a PE firm in this niche actually trusts enough to wire money on. That knowledge lives in the heads of people who've spent fifteen years in the industry, not in a Stanford CS curriculum. When I invested in companies like Stack Overflow, the founders were developers building for developers — the specific knowledge was baked into their identity. Here, the founders could have pointed their AI pipeline at any vertical. Why RV parks? The dossier offers no answer rooted in personal obsession or lived experience.

The leverage architecture here is a data subscription — "CoStar but cheaper for niches." That's not a new form of leverage. It's a price repositioning of an existing form. CoStar already provides data leverage for commercial real estate professionals. Travo is narrowing the scope and lowering the price. Compare this to what made AngelList interesting: it wasn't "VC but cheaper," it was a fundamentally new mechanism for capital formation that didn't exist before. Or Replit — not "IDE but cheaper," but code leverage for people who would never have programmed otherwise. Travo doesn't enable a new type of person to do a new type of work. It enables the same PE firms and brokers to access data for a segment CoStar underserves. That's a real business — it's just not an asymmetric one.

The positioning is also consensus, not contrarian. "AI to collect and analyze data in an underserved vertical" describes dozens of companies in every recent YC batch. The "why now" — LLMs make scraping better — is the same "why now" every AI data company claims. Smart people aren't dismissing this idea; they're funding ten versions of it simultaneously. The niche focus on RV parks is differentiated, but it's differentiated in a way that might mean the market is too small to matter, not that the founders see something others don't. When I backed Uber at a $4M valuation, ride-sharing was illegal — that was a contrarian bet with massive asymmetric upside. "Better data for RV park investors" is neither contrarian nor asymmetric.

The strongest bull case: this is a classic innovator's dilemma wedge. CoStar, generating $3.2B annually, has no economic incentive to build comprehensive data for a niche asset class. If Travo nails RV parks, they expand to mobile home parks, self-storage, marinas — a constellation of niche CRE segments that collectively represent a real market, each too small for CoStar to prioritize individually. The AI-driven collection methodology, if it's genuinely more cost-efficient than manual approaches, creates a structural advantage in assembling datasets for fragmented, low-data-density markets. And the dataset itself compounds — first-mover coverage attracts users, user feedback improves accuracy, better accuracy attracts more users. For this to work, though, the team needs to acquire domain knowledge fast enough to know what data to collect and how to validate it, and the niche-to-adjacent expansion needs to work before incumbents notice. Both of those require either a domain advisor or a founder who has internalized the industry's information architecture — and I see no evidence of either.

No public GitHub repos for any founder on a team whose entire value proposition is AI-driven data collection. No traction signals. No pricing model disclosed. No confirmed social media presence. At pre-seed, I don't penalize the absence of scale, but I do need to see the builder's fingerprints — code, a prototype, early user reactions, something that proves the technology works. The dossier provides only credentials and intentions. The credentials are strong for a generic technical team, but weak for this specific problem. That's the distinction my framework insists on: specific knowledge, not general intelligence.
