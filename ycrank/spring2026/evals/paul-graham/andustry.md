# Andustry -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I look at with any company is how the founders found the problem. Johann Sturken grew up in a family that traded industrial goods. That's not nothing. It means he watched the painful, slow process of sourcing machinery firsthand -- not from a consultant's spreadsheet but from a dinner table. Family exposure to a problem is one of the better forms of organic discovery, because you absorb the dysfunction before you have the vocabulary to describe it as a "market opportunity." But there's a gap between growing up around a problem and being consumed by it. Johann spent the last four years at McKinsey's aerospace practice and Airbus strategy, researching hydrogen aircraft and winning NASA design challenges. Het Dave was transforming SaaS products at Aries Global. These are impressive careers, but they're the careers of people who chose this problem, not people who couldn't escape it. The organic backstory is present but thin -- more like "I noticed my family's industry was stuck in the past" than "I spent three years manually emailing suppliers and wanted to scream."

The schlep here is real, and it's the kind most AI founders avoid. Industrial procurement across 100+ countries means dealing with verification nightmares, language barriers, certification standards that vary by jurisdiction, and buyers who've relied on personal relationships for decades. Most teams building AI agents are going after knowledge work -- summarizing documents, writing emails, scheduling meetings. Going after industrial machinery sourcing means navigating a world where a wrong specification can mean a $200K machine sits unusable in a warehouse. That schlep should, in theory, deter competitors. But the evidence suggests it hasn't. Matchory raised EUR 6M in January 2025 doing supplier discovery. LightSource raised $33M doing enterprise sourcing with AI. MESH Works has $884M in RFQs on their platform with in-person audits in 40+ countries. The dossier explicitly notes that "LLM-based RFQ generation and supplier matching are reproducible." When the software layer is commoditizable and multiple well-funded competitors are already operating in adjacent segments, the schlep itself isn't enough -- you need something structural that compounds over time.

The EUR 1M+ in beta transaction volume is the strongest signal in the entire dossier. At pre-seed, with two people, real money moving through the platform means real buyers trusted this system enough to transact. That's not a waitlist or a sign-up count -- it's actual economic activity. But I want to be careful not to over-index on this. Transaction volume is not revenue. If Andustry is facilitating introductions without capturing meaningful economics, the number tells you something about the market's willingness to try new channels but little about the business. And the claim of 500K+ verified suppliers across 100+ countries from a 2-person team strains credibility. That's almost certainly aggregated directory data, not proprietary verification. Calling it "verified" without third-party evidence is the kind of puffery that makes me pay closer attention to everything else the company claims.

What concerns me most is the absence of a technical hacker on the team. Neither founder has a public GitHub profile. Johann is a mechanical engineer by training -- which is engineering, but not the kind that builds AI agent platforms. Het Dave has technology management credentials and SaaS experience, but the signal I look for is someone who stays up building the product because they can't stop. The core product here is an AI agent system that generates RFQs, searches supplier databases, and compares quotes. That's a software product. Who built it? If the answer is contractors or a dev shop, the company is a PowerPoint with a landing page. If it's Het Dave, the evidence doesn't surface that. At this stage, I need at least one founder who I'd describe as "an animal" at building software, and I don't see that here.

The bull case is worth articulating clearly: if Johann's family connection gives them insider access to the first 50 industrial buyers, and if those buyers pull the product forward because the time savings are dramatic (weeks to days for first quotes), then the transaction volume compounds into a genuine two-sided marketplace where supplier density becomes the moat. Industrial procurement is one of the last major B2B categories to be digitized, and a vertical marketplace that owns the full workflow -- discovery through quote comparison through transaction -- could be enormously valuable. The Xometry comparison is instructive: they built a $2.1B public company in custom manufacturing. Off-the-shelf industrial goods is a different and arguably larger segment. If Andustry captures even 0.1% of the $810B market, that's meaningful. The timing argument is plausible too -- LLMs crossed the capability threshold for reliable specification extraction and multi-supplier negotiation only in the last 18 months. But "plausible timing + large market" describes a hundred companies in every YC batch. What I need is evidence that these specific founders have an unfair advantage in execution, and the dossier doesn't provide it convincingly.

This is a competent team in a real market with a genuine schlep, but the pattern I see is closer to "smart people recognized an AI opportunity in a traditional industry" than "founders who can't stop working on this because the problem is in their bones." The family connection lifts it above generic, but the consultant-to-startup pipeline, the absence of visible technical craftsmanship, and the crowded competitive landscape bring it back down. I'd want to see who actually writes the code, hear the specific story of how their first five buyers found them, and understand what data they have that Matchory or LightSource can't replicate. Without those answers, I can't write a check.
