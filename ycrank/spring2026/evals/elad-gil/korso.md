# Korso -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The competitive landscape here is the first thing that jumps out, and it's the most important signal for my framework. Paperless Parts raised $30M at Series B. Uptool pulled in $6M from Khosla, Eclipse, Bessemer, and Kleiner Perkins. Gendra, Mercura AI, Endeavor AI -- at least five funded startups are building AI quoting and RFQ automation for manufacturers right now. When I backed Coinbase in 2012, no serious investors were touching crypto infrastructure. When I wrote the seed check for Harvey, legal AI was a decade-long graveyard. AI for manufacturing quoting is neither dismissed nor overlooked -- it's a recognized category with serious capital already deployed. The structural shift is real: frontier LLMs genuinely crossed a capability threshold in 2024-2025 for extracting structured data from messy manufacturing documents -- drawings, RFQ emails, WhatsApp messages. But the market has noticed. Only ~35% of procurement teams use AI for RFQs today, so the adoption window is early, but the investment window is not. This distinction matters enormously for returns. When everyone can see the opportunity, the alpha disappears and execution becomes a dogfight where the best-funded team usually wins. A 3-person pre-seed team competing against Uptool's Khosla-backed roster and Paperless Parts' established customer base faces long odds.

The infrastructure-layer test doesn't pass either. Korso's "intelligence layer for manufacturing" framing gestures at platform ambitions, but the actual product description is a vertical workflow application: ingest RFQs, generate quotes, track purchase orders. That's valuable automation, but it's not an API layer that other businesses build on top of. Stripe became the payments infrastructure for an entire generation of internet companies -- each new customer on the platform increased the value of the platform for every other customer. Korso's data moat potential is real in theory (accumulated pricing history and customer relationships create switching costs over time), but at launch this doesn't exist, and the underlying LLM/RAG stack is commoditized. Any of the five competitors can build the same RAG pipeline over the same document types. Differentiation will come from depth of manufacturing-domain training and quality of workflow integration, not from proprietary technology architecture. That's an execution race, not an infrastructure moat.

The single-miracle test is where I get most concerned. The technology miracle -- LLMs capable of reliably parsing manufacturing documents -- is happening exogenously, which is good. But Korso still needs a distribution miracle: selling to SMB manufacturers with enterprise-length sales cycles using a 3-person team, against competitors with 10-50x their funding. Manufacturing operations purchasing decisions typically involve IT, operations, and finance stakeholders. I've seen this pattern repeatedly -- the sales cycle in manufacturing is brutal for small teams, and the better-funded competitor that can hire a real sales org will outrun you regardless of product quality. That's at least two independent breakthroughs required (distribution AND competitive differentiation), which starts compounding small odds in exactly the way I try to avoid.

The strongest bull case for Korso would require believing that end-to-end workflow coverage -- from multi-channel RFQ ingestion through PO tracking and production -- creates meaningfully higher switching costs than point solutions like Uptool or Paperless Parts, and that this broader surface area lets a small team win SMB manufacturers who want a single tool rather than stitching together multiple vendors. If the founders can land even a handful of manufacturers quickly and the data flywheel starts compounding, the accumulated pricing history and customer relationship data becomes genuinely hard to replicate. Manufacturing is a $17B+ MOM software market growing at 19%, which is large enough to support multiple winners. And Martin Pan's direct experience at GM as an automations engineer, combined with Daichi's ML research background, gives the team more manufacturing-domain grounding than many AI startups targeting this vertical. If the WhatsApp-native distribution channel proves to be a backdoor into markets where email-first competitors can't reach (particularly in emerging-market manufacturing), that could be a genuine wedge. But "could be" is doing a lot of work in that sentence. I'd need to see evidence that this wedge is converting -- a signed LOI, a manufacturing facility using the product daily, anything showing the end-to-end workflow creates stickiness that point solutions can't match.

On the founder profile: all three appear to be current or recent UPenn undergraduates. No prior startup exits, no senior industry roles, no evidence of operating at a company during a scaling phase. I weight market structure more heavily than founder biography -- I've seen mediocre operators do well in strong markets. But this isn't a strong market by my definition (it's consensus, crowded), so the founders need to compensate with exceptional execution speed or domain depth, and the evidence for either is thin. The Penn Electric Racing and GM connections give manufacturing relevance, but internship-level exposure is different from the kind of deep domain expertise that let the Color Health team navigate genomics or let Harvey's founders navigate legal workflows. At pre-seed I don't penalize young founders heavily, but I do need some signal that compensates for the competitive headwinds -- and I don't see it here.
