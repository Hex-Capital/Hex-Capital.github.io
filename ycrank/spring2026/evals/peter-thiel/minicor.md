# Minicor -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The competitive landscape slide writes itself, and that is the problem. UiPath — a $13 billion public company — has already shipped an AI Healing Agent. Automation Anywhere is building cognitive automation. Automat, a fellow YC company, raised $19.25 million for general computer-operating agents. Microsoft Power Automate exists. Anthropic itself released its Computer Use API as a public beta in October 2024, handing the underlying capability to anyone with an API key. When I ask "what important truth does Minicor know that few agree with?" the honest answer is: nothing that the market disputes. Everyone in enterprise software knows that legacy desktop applications lack APIs. Everyone who has used UiPath knows that traditional RPA breaks when UIs change. Everyone who has seen Anthropic's Computer Use demo knows that AI agents can interact with screens. There is no secret here — there is a correct observation shared by a dozen well-funded competitors. And all failed companies are the same: they failed to escape competition.

The deeper structural issue is commoditization from below. Minicor's self-healing capability — the claimed 93-96% click accuracy — rests on frontier AI models that Minicor does not own or control. As Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google improve their computer-use capabilities, the floor rises for every player. An accuracy advantage measured in single-digit percentage points over "competing approaches" is not proprietary technology; it is a temporal lead in prompt engineering and orchestration that model updates will erode. The deterministic-code-with-AI-recovery architecture is sensible engineering but not a ten-times improvement that would constitute a genuine technology moat. Any competent team with Anthropic's API documentation can build a comparable system. This is the pattern I warn against most explicitly: wrapping someone else's model and calling it a product. The model provider captures the value; the wrapper captures the competition.

I will grant the bull case its due, because there is one. The strongest argument for Minicor is that it is not selling AI — it is selling managed infrastructure. SOC 2 Type II certification and HIPAA compliance at the pre-seed stage represent real, irreversible commitments that most YC companies never make. Healthcare and financial services customers do not evaluate AI accuracy in isolation; they evaluate compliance posture, deployment options (on-premise, Citrix), observability, and audit trails. If Minicor embeds deeply enough in production healthcare workflows — the "25,000 patients/day" claim suggests meaningful scale — then switching costs could compound as they do for Palantir in government intelligence systems. The co-founders' shared history is also a legitimate positive: Chishtie and Akinbile have worked together since 2019, built a nonprofit through the pandemic, and both carry enterprise-legacy experience from Phoenix, Ford, and Morgan Stanley. This is the kind of shared prehistory that survives the stress of company-building. If the managed-infrastructure thesis is correct — if compliance certifications plus deployment flexibility plus production reliability constitute a durable moat rather than a temporary lead — then Minicor could become the default plumbing layer for AI-to-legacy integration. But that "if" requires believing that UiPath's enterprise sales force, existing customer relationships, and billion-dollar R&D budget cannot replicate SOC 2 compliance and VM orchestration. I find that belief difficult to sustain.

The power-law calculation seals the verdict. Even in the optimistic scenario where Minicor dominates the "AI companies needing legacy desktop access" segment, that segment is bounded on both sides: bounded from below by improving foundation model capabilities that reduce the need for specialized intermediaries, and bounded from above by the eventual migration of legacy systems to cloud-native architectures with proper APIs. The RPA market's $28 billion TAM is real, but Minicor's addressable slice — serving AI companies rather than enterprises directly — is a niche within a niche. This could become a solid infrastructure business generating tens of millions in revenue. It will not return the fund. The category lacks the winner-take-all dynamics that produce power-law outcomes, and the competitive structure ensures that value will be distributed across multiple adequate players rather than concentrated in a single monopolist. Capitalism and competition are opposites, and this market is thoroughly competitive.

The founders deserve credit for definite commitments — compliance certifications, production deployments, a specific vertical focus in healthcare — that distinguish them from the median YC team iterating toward product-market fit. But definite execution in a competitive market produces a well-run commodity, not a monopoly. I have seen this pattern before, and the lesson of Wish applies in spirit if not in specifics: competent teams executing well in markets where structural dominance is impossible produce outcomes that are irrelevant to the power law.
