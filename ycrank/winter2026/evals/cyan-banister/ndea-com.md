# Ndea -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Ndea: this is the most consensus contrarian bet I've ever seen. François Chollet has been publicly arguing that large language models won't lead to AGI — a genuinely provocative position when you consider that hundreds of billions of dollars are pouring into exactly that paradigm. Program synthesis as an alternative path to general intelligence is architecturally weird and intellectually bold. But then I look at the $43M raise with Coatue, the wall of press coverage from every major tech outlet in January 2025, and the fact that this landed in the hottest investment category in human history. When I backed SpaceX, I was the only person in the room who thought it was a good idea. When I looked at Crusoe, people thought we were out of our minds — mining Bitcoin on gas flares in North Dakota. Those deals were unpopular and difficult to assess. Ndea's approach is unusual, but the deal itself? This is a highly competed, well-validated bet. Every VC in town knows Chollet's name and reputation. That's not where I typically find my edge.

If I could sit across from Chollet, my first question would be the same one I always ask — tell me your story, not the business. And what I'd hear, based on everything in front of me, is a brilliant trajectory: elite French engineering program, nine years as a Senior Staff Engineer at Google, creator of Keras with 63,000 GitHub stars, TIME 100 recognition. That is extraordinary domain mastery. He literally defined the problem he's now trying to solve, which is a form of conviction I deeply respect. But I'm listening for something different — I'm listening for the hardship that made this problem personal, the thing that means you can't walk away even when the money runs out and everyone tells you you're crazy. Chollet leaving Google to pursue what he believes is true has real meaning, and Knoop walking away from a company he built from nothing at Zapier shows genuine commitment. But these are choices made from positions of enormous career capital, not from the kind of desperate necessity that forges the survival instinct I've seen in my most successful founders.

The bull case here is genuinely powerful, and I want to give it its full weight. If Chollet is right that LLMs are a dead end for AGI — and his track record of identifying what current AI systems can't do is unmatched by anyone alive — then Ndea is the most important company in artificial intelligence. Period. The "circa 2012 deep learning" framing is the kind of historical parallel that, if accurate, represents a generational entry point. Chollet didn't just study the limitations of current approaches; he built the benchmark that proved them. The ARC-AGI competition showing progress from 33% to 55.5% through hybrid program synthesis methods is the first empirical signal that this paradigm works. And the co-founder pairing is genuinely excellent — a world-class researcher with a proven startup operator who already scaled Zapier to millions of users. If this were an investment where the founder-market fit alone could convince me, Chollet's fit is as tight as any I've ever evaluated.

What I can't find anywhere in this dossier is the dimension I weight most heavily after founder story: who does this help in the real world? My best investments share a common thread — they create economic access or make invisible systems visible for people who need it. Uber created income for drivers. Affirm gave people transparent credit. Carta made equity ownership comprehensible. Flock Safety brought public safety data to communities. Ndea's stated mission is to build "a factory for rapid scientific advancement" across drugs, energy, robotics, and space. Those are magnificent ambitions, but they're abstractions aimed at institutions and researchers, not at the people I think about when I imagine a company's full impact. There's no economic access story here that lights me up.

The co-founder dynamics are actually the strongest conventional signal in this evaluation. Chollet and Knoop built the ARC Prize Foundation together before starting the company — they've already navigated shared decision-making around something meaningful. The researcher-plus-operator pairing is complementary in the way I like to see, with clear domain separation. Knoop staying on Zapier's board while committing to Ndea suggests a measured, honest approach to transition rather than the impulsive bridge-burning that often precedes founder conflicts. I don't have enough data to know how they argue, but the pre-existing collaboration is a positive signal that this partnership was tested before the real pressure begins.

I respect what Ndea is building. Chollet may be the single most qualified person on Earth to pursue this specific problem, and the technical contrarianism is genuine even if the deal dynamics are not. But my framework asks me to evaluate biographical grit over credentials, unpopularity over validation, and economic access over grand ambition. On those dimensions, Ndea doesn't activate the signals that have guided my best investments. This is a company I'd watch with enormous intellectual curiosity — and if program synthesis begins demonstrating real-world applications that create access for people currently locked out, I'd revisit with fresh eyes.
