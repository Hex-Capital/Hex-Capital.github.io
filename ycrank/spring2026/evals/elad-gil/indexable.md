# Indexable -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most striking thing about Indexable is the gap between the technical artifact and the market structure it's entering. Building a custom VMM on KVM with 26-millisecond full-state forking -- files, processes, memory, databases -- is genuinely hard systems engineering. This isn't a wrapper. The content-addressable storage engine, the customized Linux 6.19 kernel, the custom Nix fork in Rust -- this is the kind of deep infrastructure work that takes years to replicate. Andrew Gazelka's Hyperion project, a Minecraft engine processing 1,000 players in 455 microseconds while targeting 170,000 concurrent players, is exactly the pedigree you'd want for building this. The technical miracle may already be built. But the market this artifact is entering is one of the most consensus-hot categories in venture capital right now, and that's a problem for my framework.

AI agent sandbox infrastructure is not a non-obvious market. E2B has 88% Fortune 100 penetration, $12M+ in implied ARR, and 500M+ sandbox sessions. Daytona just raised $24M in February 2026 pursuing fork-and-snapshot capabilities -- the same core value proposition. Modal Labs is at $1.1B+ valuation. Every AI infrastructure investor is staring at this space. When I evaluate markets, I'm looking for the gap between how the market looks today and where the adoption curve is heading -- specifically markets that most investors would dismiss as too boring, too niche, or too weird. Sandbox infrastructure for AI agents is none of these things. It's consensus. The structural moment for agent sandboxes already happened -- E2B captured it. The question is whether there's a second structural moment around branching and stateful execution that hasn't been captured yet, and whether that sub-segment constitutes a distinct market rather than a feature.

The bull case is a Stripe-like architectural bet: Stripe argued that developers needed a fundamentally different integration model for payments, not just a better PayPal. Indexable argues that agentic workflows need a fundamentally different execution model -- not ephemeral sandboxes, but version-controlled state machines where agents can branch, backtrack, and diff against prior states. If autonomous agents evolve from linear execution to tree-structured exploration (which is plausible as agents tackle more complex, multi-step tasks), then E2B's Firecracker-based architecture genuinely cannot add this capability incrementally -- they'd have to rebuild core infrastructure. That's a real structural barrier, not a feature gap. And Gazelka has already shipped the hard part: the custom VMM that makes sub-30ms forking possible. If this is a Harvey-style bet -- building ahead of the capability curve before the market recognizes branching execution as a required primitive -- then you'd want exactly this technical founder positioned exactly here. The technology cycle argument is that today's single-path agent execution is like pre-GPT-4 legal AI: everyone knows agents are important, but nobody yet realizes that the next generation of agents will need to explore parallel execution paths at speed.

But I see two obstacles that make this closer to a two-miracle problem than a one-miracle problem. The first miracle -- build the technology -- appears solved. The second miracle is market creation: convincing developers and enterprises that branching, forkable sandboxes are a required primitive, not a performance optimization on top of existing ephemeral sandboxes. That second miracle is harder because E2B's 500M+ sessions suggest the market is being served adequately by ephemeral execution for most current use cases. Daytona is already attacking the fork/snapshot space with $31M in funding and a 12-month head start in market development. If 90ms forking at the container level is "good enough" for 90% of branching use cases, Indexable's 26ms advantage at the VM level may be technically impressive but commercially irrelevant -- the way faster-than-Stripe payment processing doesn't matter when Stripe's distribution moat is the actual barrier.

The distribution gap is what concerns me most. There's no evidence Gazelka has thought about how this product becomes a distribution channel for additional products. The website has a "schedule a call" link and a Discord server. No pricing published, no Product Hunt launch, no press coverage, ~86 Twitter followers. Multi-language website support at pre-seed is unusual but doesn't constitute a go-to-market strategy. For an infrastructure product competing against E2B's 2M+ monthly SDK downloads, developer distribution has to be an obsession from day one -- it can't be an afterthought that follows the technical build. The founding team is three people with one engineering hire posted, suggesting the near-term focus is purely technical. Strong technical execution velocity, notably weak distribution velocity. This is a company that can build the product but hasn't yet demonstrated it can reach the market -- and in infrastructure, the companies that win distribution usually win the category regardless of who has the better architecture.

The technology cycle positioning has real merit. If I squint, this resembles the pattern where a correct thesis failed at the wrong time -- early sandbox solutions didn't offer full-state forking because agent workflows didn't need it yet. As agents become more autonomous and complex, the demand profile shifts. But I can't ignore that this is a consensus-hot market with hundreds of millions in competitive funding, a clear incumbent, and a direct competitor (Daytona) already shipping overlapping capabilities with 100x the capital. My best investments have been in markets other investors dismissed. This is a market every investor is chasing.
