# Terminal Use -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The most striking thing about Terminal Use isn't the team or the product -- it's the competitive landscape they're walking into. E2B has $32 million and is used by 88% of Fortune 100. Daytona has $24 million and hit $1M+ ARR in under three months. Modal Labs is at $111 million raised, ~$50 million ARR, and reportedly in talks at a $2.5 billion valuation. OpenAI has already launched Frontier with bundled hosted runtimes for agents. And Terminal Use has four GitHub repos with zero stars. This isn't a frontier nobody sees -- this is a crowded saloon where the other players showed up early and brought significantly more firepower. When I look at the competitive table here, I don't see an opportunity for a contrarian bet. I see a market where the consensus already arrived, capitalized, and is actively executing.

The founders are credibly sane. Three Palantir engineers who went to university together, became close friends at Palantir, and bring relevant infrastructure experience -- Vivek led one of the largest agent deployment use cases across US hospitals, Stavros worked on coding infrastructure and dev tooling. The pre-existing relationships are real, and that matters to me. I've seen enough speed-dating co-founder matches implode under pressure to know that genuine relational depth is load-bearing. But "sane founder" is only half my equation. The idea -- "Vercel for background agents" -- is not insane. It's the most obvious infrastructure play in the current AI wave. Every developer-tools VC I talk to is looking at this category. When the pitch deck analogy is itself a well-understood playbook ("we're X for Y" where both X and Y are legible to every investor in the room), the non-consensus premium has been fully arbitraged away. This is a sane team with a sane idea, which is the configuration my framework values least.

My deeper concern is structural. Terminal Use is pure software infrastructure -- their moat candidates are filesystem forking, task-pinned versioning, manifest-based sync. These are well-understood engineering patterns, not proprietary primitives. Any team with $20 million and competent infrastructure engineers can build these features. More importantly, the company doesn't own any framework. They're a third-party hosting layer for Claude Agent SDK and Codex SDK -- someone else's protocols, someone else's developer relationships. The Vercel analogy breaks down precisely here: Vercel's moat was deep vertical integration with Next.js, a framework Vercel created and controlled. Terminal Use is building a deployment layer for frameworks controlled by Anthropic and OpenAI, both of whom have every incentive to bundle hosting into their own offerings. OpenAI is already doing this. When I run my "does this business get stronger or weaker as software gets cheaper?" test, the answer is unambiguous: cheaper and better AI infrastructure tools make Terminal Use's specific implementation less defensible, not more. There are no physical-world operations here, no regulatory barriers, no protocol-layer lock-in, no network effects that compound with adoption. This is software infrastructure competing on developer experience in a market where well-funded competitors and the platform providers themselves are converging on the same surface area.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement. If background agents that manipulate filesystems become the dominant paradigm -- the way containerized web apps became the dominant paradigm that Vercel serves -- then whoever builds the best developer experience for deploying these agents captures a real market. The Palantir background gives this team credibility in enterprise agent deployment that E2B and Daytona may lack. And the "Woz" product -- deploying agents from Slack with MCP integration -- is a clever distribution wedge that could create workflow-level switching costs once embedded in daily team operations. If Terminal Use executes faster than its competitors on developer experience and captures the emerging "production gap" before the market consolidates, the $183 billion TAM projections make the ceiling enormous. What would have to be true: (1) filesystem-interacting background agents persist as the dominant paradigm rather than being replaced by a different architecture, (2) neither Anthropic nor OpenAI successfully bundles deployment infrastructure with their SDKs, (3) Terminal Use out-executes competitors with 5-15x more capital on developer adoption, and (4) the "Vercel for agents" analogy holds despite Terminal Use not controlling any framework. That's four independent conditions that all need to resolve favorably. I don't bet on conjunctions.

The narrative has decent magnitude -- "infrastructure layer for the agentic AI economy" can theoretically expand to encompass an enormous category. But the infinity story is capped by the application-layer position. Terminal Use will always be downstream of the model providers and framework builders. That's not where compounding happens. Compare this to Helium, which created a decentralized wireless protocol that other applications built ON TOP OF. Or Venmo, which established a new payment primitive. Terminal Use is building on top of other people's primitives, which means its ceiling is bounded by the decisions of companies it doesn't control. The absence of any public traction signal -- no revenue figures, no customer logos, zero GitHub stars, no community, no press coverage -- compounds my concern. I accept that pre-seed companies lack metrics, but the total absence of any organic adoption signal in a category where competitors are posting explosive growth numbers (Daytona doubling ARR in six weeks) suggests the product-market fit question remains wide open.
