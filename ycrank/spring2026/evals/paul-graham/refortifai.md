# RefortifAI -- Paul Graham Evaluation

DRM for AI model weights. That phrase alone tells you most of what you need to know about the shape of this bet. Software-based DRM has been losing the arms race against determined adversaries for thirty years — from DeCSS cracking DVD protection to Denuvo getting bypassed within days of each game release. The founders themselves frame this as "anti-cheat for AI models," which is honest framing but also a tell: anti-cheat systems are a treadmill, not a product. You ship protection, someone cracks it, you patch, they crack again. That can be a business, but it's a business that scales linearly with engineering effort, not one where how well you've done determines how well you do next. When I look for superlinear returns, I look for compounding dynamics. A security arms race is the opposite — it's a tax you pay forever to stay in the same place.

The team's technical composition is the most interesting signal here. Atman Kar writes operating systems in Assembly and 6502 emulators in C for fun — the kind of low-level hacking that tells me someone genuinely thinks in terms of registers and memory layouts, not abstractions. His RTL design work at Texas Instruments and his internship at Tenstorrent mean he understands the silicon layer where GPU inference actually happens. Rithik Jain did security research under Somesh Jha at Wisconsin on ROP gadgets and attack vectors — that's real offensive security knowledge, the kind you need to understand how adversaries would try to extract weights from a running process. And Sayan Mitra built AI systems at KLA's semiconductor group. On paper, this is exactly the triad you'd assemble if you wanted to build a hardened inference runtime: hardware architecture, security research, ML engineering. But that's the problem. It looks assembled. The dossier describes them as "friends who launched together," and their backgrounds don't visibly overlap at any institution or company. When I see a team whose skills map perfectly to a market gap, I worry the idea came from the gap, not from lived pain. The way to get startup ideas is not to identify complementary expertise and point it at an opportunity.

There's no origin story here that starts with frustration. Nobody on this team was trying to deploy a proprietary model to edge devices and discovered they had no way to protect the weights. Nobody was running an AI company and lost revenue because a customer extracted and redistributed their model. The problem is real — I don't dispute that model providers worry about IP protection — but the founders' relationship to it appears to be analytical rather than experiential. Compare this to Dropbox: Drew Houston didn't identify a gap in the file synchronization market. He kept forgetting his USB drive. The difference matters because organic discovery produces founders who understand the problem at a resolution that market research can't reach. When you've personally felt the pain, you know which aspects of the solution are load-bearing and which are cosmetic. When you've identified the pain from outside, you're guessing.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. If model providers move aggressively toward on-premise and edge deployment — which the proliferation of open-weight models like Llama and Mistral suggests they will — IP protection becomes infrastructure, not a nice-to-have. RefortifAI's claim of drop-in compatibility with vLLM and SGLang is the right integration strategy: it means model providers don't have to change their inference stack, just swap in a hardened runtime. If this actually works without meaningful latency overhead — a big if, and no benchmarks exist — the switching costs could be real. Once you've distributed obfuscated weights to hundreds of customer deployments, migrating away means re-distributing everything. And Palo Alto Networks paying $500M+ for Protect AI proves enterprises will write large checks for AI security. The question is whether "DRM for weights" is the right slice of AI security or whether the incumbents will subsume it. My concern is that Palo Alto, having just acquired a team of ML security engineers, is better positioned to build this as a feature than RefortifAI is to build it as a company.

What I can't see in this dossier is any evidence of resourcefulness, iteration, or user love. No traction signals. No revenue. The website returned a 403. No public community, no press, no product launches. For a pre-seed YC company, the absence of traction isn't itself damning — but the absence of stories is. When founders are truly resourceful, the stories leak out. They've built something in a weekend, or convinced a skeptical customer to pilot their prototype, or figured out a creative workaround to a technical blocker. The dossier contains none of this. I'm left evaluating credentials and market logic, which is exactly the evaluation mode that produces my worst investment decisions. My best investments — Airbnb, Stripe, Replit — all came from seeing something in the founders that transcended the dossier-level analysis. Here, I see competent engineers who identified a real technical challenge. That's necessary but not sufficient.

I'd want to meet these founders before writing them off entirely. The CTO's hobby projects suggest a genuine hacker temperament, and the security research background is legitimately relevant. But from what I can see, this is a well-reasoned market-gap play by smart engineers, not a mission born from obsessive personal frustration. The idea is technically hard but conceptually obvious — everyone can see why model providers would want this; the skepticism is about feasibility, not desirability. That's a weaker version of the "repels before it attracts" pattern I look for. I'm passing.
