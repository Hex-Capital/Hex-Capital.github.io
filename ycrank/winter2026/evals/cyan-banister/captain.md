# Captain -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Captain is that everyone else notices it too. RAG infrastructure is one of the most crowded, well-capitalized categories in AI right now. Vectara has raised $73.5 million. Unstructured has $68 million. Glean is sitting at a $7.2 billion valuation with $208 million in ARR. LangChain hit a billion-dollar valuation. Open-source retrieval frameworks are improving every quarter. When I look at a company, I'm asking myself: is this unpopular? Is it difficult to assess? Does it make people uncomfortable? Captain answers "no" to all three. This is the kind of investment where consensus has already formed — enterprise RAG is important, everyone agrees, and capital is flooding in from every direction. That's precisely the dynamic where early-stage investors get squeezed, because you're competing not against the problem's difficulty but against the wallet size of incumbents who've already decided this market matters.

When I try to hear the founders' stories — not the business, the human story — I come up mostly empty. Lewis Polansky is an Eagle Scout who founded a packaging startup and worked across several organizations including Sony and Rocketbook. Edgar Babajanyan studied infrastructure engineering at Purdue and spent three years building production RAG pipelines. Both are clearly capable builders. But what I'm listening for is the biographical thread — the hardship, the obsession that predates the market opportunity, the reason this specific person must solve this specific problem. Instead, what I see is a pattern that concerns me: Edgar's prior projects track the AI hype cycle almost perfectly — GPT4You (ChatGPT wrapper), CodeSweep.ai (AI documentation), Zenmigo (productivity platform), and now RAG infrastructure. Lewis's prior startup "solved hallucinations for code generation." These feel like smart people following where the energy is rather than people who arrived at an insight no one else could see. That's a meaningful distinction in how I evaluate founders, because when the market gets brutal — and in a space this crowded, it will — the founders who persist are the ones solving something personal, not something popular.

The strongest bull case here is Edgar's three years of hands-on RAG pipeline experience. He's actually lived inside the pain he's trying to solve. The Map-Reduce parallel LLM architecture is a genuinely interesting technical approach — distributing retrieval across many models and synthesizing results is not how most people think about this problem. SOC 2 Type II certification at pre-seed is impressive execution, showing these founders can deliver enterprise-grade infrastructure early. And the proprietary Odyssey dataset — covering companies, people, deals, investors, patents, credit analysis — is the kind of data asset that could compound into real defensibility if it grows with usage. If I imagine the world where Captain's accuracy claims hold up under independent benchmarking, where the developer-first API positioning carves out a distinct niche from Glean's end-user product, and where the Odyssey dataset becomes indispensable to a specific vertical — there's a path to a meaningful company. Garry Tan's personal endorsement isn't nothing, either.

But I keep coming back to the question I ask myself with every deal: would I drop everything to work for these founders? And the honest answer is that nothing in their story creates that pull. I don't see the adversity that forges resilience. I don't see the biographical commitment that survives when the market turns. I don't see a company that creates economic access for people who lack it — this is enterprise search infrastructure, useful but not transformative for underserved populations. It doesn't make an invisible system visible the way Carta did with cap tables or Affirm did with interest rates. It makes an already-visible system incrementally better. And in a space where well-resourced competitors can replicate a technical approach — the Map-Reduce architecture is conceptually straightforward even if execution is hard — the two-person team faces a brutal resource asymmetry selling into enterprise accounts that demand custom SLAs, security reviews, and sustained support. The naming choice tells me something too: picking "Captain" in a crowded SEO landscape suggests either boldness or insufficient attention to the practical realities of building brand awareness against well-funded incumbents.

This is a competent team building in a real market, but it's the opposite of my thesis. The best investments I've made were the ones where everyone else thought I was crazy for writing the check. Nobody thinks you're crazy for investing in RAG infrastructure in 2026.
