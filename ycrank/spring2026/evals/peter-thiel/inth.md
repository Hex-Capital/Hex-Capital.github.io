# Inth -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

$732 million to $1.82 billion — that is the total addressable market for consent management platforms, according to the dossier's own cited sources. Even at the most optimistic projection of $11.59 billion by 2034, Inth is competing for a category where the dominant incumbent, OneTrust, has achieved $500 million in revenue by selling to compliance officers at $10,000+ per contract. The entire consent management market, fully captured by a single monopolist, would not return a growth fund. This is the structural problem that no amount of clever engineering can overcome. The power law is merciless in its arithmetic: a company that succeeds completely in a small market is irrelevant to fund returns. Inth could execute flawlessly — monopolize its segment, expand concentrically, become the undisputed developer-facing privacy layer — and still produce an outcome that does not matter at the scale where capital allocation decisions are made.

Inth's implicit secret — that consent management belongs in application code rather than injected as an external script, and that developers rather than compliance officers should own this infrastructure — is not a secret at all. It is the Stripe playbook applied to a new domain, and everyone in the developer tools ecosystem recognizes the pattern instantly. Guillermo Rauch's endorsement, calling c15t "@shadcn for Consent Management," reveals rather than conceals the problem: shadcn is a component library pattern, not a monopoly architecture. When your most prominent advocate describes you using an analogy to a UI library, the market is telling you that this is a developer convenience, not a structural discontinuity. OneTrust, Usercentrics, Cookiebot, Osano, CookieYes, and Ketch already populate the competitive landscape — six named competitors with combined funding exceeding $1.2 billion. The existence of this crowded field is itself the clearest signal that no genuine secret is at work. If the insight were non-obvious, there would not be half a dozen funded companies already present.

Christopher Burns brings relevant execution credentials — a second-time founder who built Everfund to $2.15 million in funding, processed payments across 33+ countries, and has a decade of TypeScript experience. His framework-level contributions to RedwoodJS and established relationships in the Vercel ecosystem demonstrate genuine domain fluency. But Inth's dossier reveals no stated business model — the pricing page does not exist as of April 2026, the monetization path is "[Inferred]" by the researcher rather than articulated by Burns. An open-source project with 1.8 million npm downloads and no visible revenue architecture is not a definite plan; it is a bet that distribution will eventually convert to dollars through a mechanism yet to be designed. This is precisely the indefinite thinking I find uninvestable — building in the hope that customers will eventually reveal how they want to pay, rather than engineering the business model with the same conviction applied to the technology.

The strongest bull case for Inth centers on distribution, and I take it seriously before dismissing it. 1.8 million npm downloads, 388,300 monthly downloads in April 2026, 26.9% growth rate, customers including Vercel, Expo, and Zed — these are genuine organic pull signals at pre-seed, stronger than most companies at this stage. The BigCommerce Catalyst integration and framework-native SDKs for React, Next.js, and Svelte create real switching costs once embedded. Burns has solved the distribution problem more convincingly than the product problem. If Inth were attacking a market with power-law characteristics — if consent management were a wedge into a $100 billion privacy infrastructure category — this distribution engine would be a compelling invest signal. One could construct the Stripe analogy: Stripe started with a seven-line JavaScript snippet for payments and expanded into an entire financial infrastructure platform. Could Inth start with a consent SDK and expand into DSARs, data mapping, vendor monitoring, and the full privacy stack? The DSAR SDK in alpha hints at this ambition. But Stripe's wedge opened into a $100 trillion payments market. Inth's wedge opens into a sub-$2 billion consent market adjacent to a perhaps $10 billion privacy market — two orders of magnitude smaller. The distribution mechanics are impressive; the destination they distribute toward is not.

Inth operates as a pure software abstraction layer with zero physical-world technology, zero government customer pathway, and zero structural barrier that a well-resourced competitor could not replicate in months. The dossier itself acknowledges this: "A well-resourced developer tools company (e.g., Vercel, Netlify) could build a competing solution." When your best customer (Vercel) could become your most dangerous competitor by building the feature natively, the moat is made of sand. CookieBench, Inth's self-created benchmarking tool, is a marketing instrument masquerading as a technical standard — useful for sales collateral, irrelevant as defensibility. Compare this to Palantir, which embedded itself so deeply in intelligence operations that displacement required physical-world migration of classified workflows, or to General Matter, where nuclear fuel enrichment creates multi-year regulatory and physical barriers. Inth's 30ms FCP versus OneTrust's 514ms is an optimization, not a paradigm shift — it makes an existing thing faster, not a previously impossible thing possible.

The condition that would flip me to Invest: Inth demonstrates a signed government contract or regulatory mandate that requires its specific architecture — creating the Palantir-style embedding where switching costs compound annually through operational integration. Absent that, or absent evidence that the consent wedge opens into a market ten times larger than current projections, Inth remains a competent execution of a consensus thesis in a category that is too small, too competitive, and too easily replicated to produce the kind of outcome the power law demands.
