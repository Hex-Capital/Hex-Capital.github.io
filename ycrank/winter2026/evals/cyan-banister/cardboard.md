# Cardboard -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that strikes me about Cardboard is that everyone else is already here. Descript has raised $101 million. OpusClip has $50 million and ten million users. Kapwing has been at this for years. Adobe is bolting AI into Premiere Pro. Multiple Hacker News commenters on their own launch thread said "lots of players in the market doing this." And when every investor in town is already excited about a category, I've learned that the price is wrong and the opportunity has been picked over. The most promising companies I've backed — SpaceX, Uber, Crusoe — were ones where I had to explain to people why the idea wasn't crazy. Nobody needs to explain why AI video editing is interesting. That's the problem. When a category is this legible to everyone, the returns accrue to whoever has the most capital and the biggest existing user base, not to the two-person team that just launched.

If I could sit down with Saksham and Ishan and ask them my usual opening — tell me your story, not the business — what I'd hear is technically impressive but it doesn't give me that biographical thread I'm always listening for. Ishan built an app with five million downloads at age twelve, then spent five years at HackerRank building performance-critical browser applications. That's genuinely strong technical credibility for the browser NLE piece. Saksham co-founded an AI company at Entrepreneur First that shut down, was first engineer at a fintech startup, and creates content that gets 250,000 views a month. These are smart, capable builders with relevant skills. But I'm not hearing the survival story — the hardship that makes this problem feel like destiny rather than opportunity. Saksham's content creation gives him firsthand frustration with video editing tools, and that's real, but "I found this workflow annoying" is different from "this problem shaped who I am." I funded Contraline because its founder had spent years in academic research that everyone else ignored — the problem and the person were fused together. I don't feel that fusion here.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. The technical founder-market fit is genuinely uncommon: Ishan's browser performance expertise maps almost perfectly to the WebGPU/WebCodecs rendering challenge, and Saksham's AI agent experience at Iterate maps to the agentic editing layer. Thirteen releases shipped since November 2025 with only two people is remarkable execution velocity. A thousand early users and named customers including PostHog show that the product works and people want it. And the "two hard things at once" framing — building both a professional browser NLE and a reliable agentic editor simultaneously — could become a real moat if they pull it off, precisely because most competitors are only doing one or the other. If I squint, I can see a version of this where the browser-native architecture becomes the new standard the way Figma replaced desktop design tools, and the agentic layer compounds with every editing session. That's a real vision. But for it to work, they'd need to outrun competitors with fifty to a hundred times their capital, in a market where the core AI capability sits on commercially available foundation models that anyone can access. The custom renderer is accumulated engineering effort, but it's not a regulatory moat or a network effect — it's a head start that well-funded incumbents can close.

What I keep coming back to is that Cardboard doesn't create economic access or make an invisible system visible. It makes video production faster for marketing teams at companies like PostHog — teams that already have resources and budgets. When I backed Uber, it created income for hundreds of thousands of drivers. Postmates did the same for delivery workers. Affirm gave transparent credit to people who'd been exploited by hidden fees. Carta made equity ownership legible to employees who had no idea what their shares were worth. I'm always asking myself: who gets economic opportunity from this company existing? The answer here is that growth teams save time. That's a real pain point and a real business, but it's not the kind of impact that gets me out of bed.

The one dimension where Cardboard genuinely excels by my framework is co-founder alignment. Saksham and Ishan met in school and have known each other for fifteen years. After watching HQ Trivia implode because of irreconcilable co-founder dynamics — a company that had the right product at the right time and still failed entirely because of founder conflict — I take the co-founder relationship more seriously than almost anything else. Fifteen years of friendship with complementary technical skills is exactly what I want to see. These two will survive hard moments together. But a strong co-founder relationship in a consensus market without a biographical mission or an economic access thesis just isn't enough to pull me in. I'd be betting on execution in a race where the other runners have ten-year head starts and hundred-million-dollar war chests, and where the underlying AI capabilities are commoditizing by the quarter.

I'm passing on Cardboard. The founders are clearly talented, and I wouldn't be surprised if they build a meaningful business. But this isn't my kind of bet. I'm looking for the misfit founders solving problems nobody else sees, in spaces nobody else wants to enter, creating access for people who currently have none. Cardboard is a well-executed product in a crowded, popular market — and in my experience, the best investments never look obvious at the start.
