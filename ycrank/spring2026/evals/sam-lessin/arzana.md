# Arzana -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Arzana is the canonical 2026 YC AI-agent-for-vertical pitch wearing an Iowa childhood as its only non-consensus asset — William Alexander grew up inside a manufacturing sourcing firm in Okoboji, and that's genuinely the most interesting line in the dossier, but the company built around it is shaped like every other P26 SAFE on Tom Blomfield's docket. The signature here is high-on-problem-provenance, low-on-everything-else-that-matters-to-me: an authentic founder-market insight wrapped in a structure I can't price, in a sleeve I publicly de-rate, against three YC batchmates doing the same thing.

The highest rung on my round-pricing ladder is **Solana at $0.05/coin** — a category the consensus crowd had quit, so the entry price was a gift. Arzana sits at the opposite pole: Korso, Smartbase, and Tiny are all in the same P26 cohort attacking adjacent manufacturer back-office workflows (YC P26 listings), Tom Blomfield is the partner, and NetSuite and SAP S/4HANA are already shipping embedded agentic features in 2026 (top10erp.org, gurussolutions.com). When a YC batch contains four versions of the same idea and the incumbents are publicly building it, the round is consensus by definition — and the dossier discloses no price, which under my absent-evidence default I read as standard P26 pricing dressed in standard P26 dilution. The Arzana entry price isn't where I make money.

The lowest rung on my category-sleeve ladder is the canonical pitch from my own Pass Example — "AI agent for [enterprise vertical], $25K MRR, $30M post." Arzana is that pitch with the vertical filled in as "manufacturing back-office." My operating frame is that "AI is just more internet" (Joe Lonsdale podcast #113, 2024), which means an Arzana-shaped AI agent overlay on Epicor/JobBoss/NetSuite is commodity exposure to an LLM-accuracy threshold any of the ten well-funded peers will also clear. The "99.9% accuracy" claim on the arzana.com site is the tell — unverified, marketing-shaped, and identical to what Korso and Smartbase will be saying in their decks. Arzana is not the rare vertical where the category is the moat; it's the vertical where the category is the saturation.

Capital structure compounds the wrong way over 18 months for Arzana. The dossier reports founders paying themselves $10K/year (NYT, Feb 2026), which I read as scrappy but cosmetic — the underlying instrument is still a generic enterprise SaaS cap table that will need five tacks of the regatta to clear default-alive against multi-ERP integration burn and "<4 months to go-live" professional-services drag. Nothing in the Arzana dossier suggests a non-standard instrument — no revenue-share, no holdco, no employee-equity rollup, nothing that Teamshares-style underwriting would even register on. This is the bootstrappable-being-force-fit-to-VC failure mode I screen for, but without the bootstrappable economics yet visible.

Counterfactually, the founder-conviction score flips a tier for Arzana only if William Alexander has published opinionated long-form writing on manufacturing back-office dysfunction that I can find — a Substack, a Twitter thread series, a Reddit account on r/manufacturing with three years of receipts. The dossier explicitly notes "no public account found" for both founders on Twitter, no GitHub, no essays. The Iowa-sourcing-firm origin story is real, and Marshall Kools being a D1 wrestler reads as the missionary-temperament-for-non-linear-time signal I look for, but Arzana hasn't externalized any of that into the artifact I trust most. "Writing is thinking" (Antoine Buteau, ~2023), and Arzana has no writing I can read before the meeting.

The single-anchor parallel for founder-as-brand is **John Fish / Bookshelved** — a creator with owned distribution before the round, which is exactly the asymmetric input that makes a non-standard check make sense. Arzana has the opposite: no Twitter, no GitHub, LinkedIn counts unretrievable, MD&M trade-show booths, and a Zeiss logo earned via direct CEO outreach. The NYT feature is real distribution but it's earned media, not owned media — Arzana doesn't keep it after the news cycle and can't compound it into the next sales motion. This is paid-acquisition shape, not Slow Creator Fund shape.

My 36-month forecast for Arzana: $2M–$6M ARR with a Series A that either prices below the seed mark or doesn't clear at all, followed by an acqui-hire into Epicor, QAD, or a private-equity ERP rollup at sub-$50M — the integration team gets absorbed, the brand disappears. The mechanism is straightforward: NetSuite/SAP/QAD embedded AI eats the easy half of the workflow, Korso and Smartbase split the YC-channel half, and Arzana's "<4 months to go-live" professional-services drag prevents the ARR velocity needed to outrun either threat. No venture moment, no regatta tack worth underwriting from the P26 entry.

The strongest bull case for Arzana — and it does sting — is the **Ro** Known Exception: capital intensity itself becomes the moat when the integration schleps are real, and 10+ ERP integrations across Epicor/JobBoss/NetSuite/SAP/Salesforce/HubSpot is a genuine schlep that Korso (RFQ-only) and Smartbase (PO-only) haven't done. If Alexander's Iowa childhood gave Arzana a Wizard-of-Oz parts-matching insight nobody else has, the 10+ integrations become a 24-month head start no batchmate can replicate. I dismantle this with the dossier specific that wins: NYT notes neither Arzana founder had a tech background prior to founding, which means the integration platform is being built by a five-person team without the systems-architecture depth that made Ro's regulatory rails defensible. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict on Arzana: William Alexander publishes a long-form essay by Q3 2026 articulating a non-obvious mechanical insight about quote-to-cash that none of Korso/Smartbase/Tiny can copy — then I take a meeting, and I ask about price.
