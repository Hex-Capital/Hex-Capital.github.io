# RefortifAI -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I notice about RefortifAI is the "anti-cheat for AI" framing, and it tells me more than the founders probably intend. Game anti-cheat is a perpetual arms race -- EasyAntiCheat, BattlEye, Riot's Vanguard all require massive ongoing R&D investment because every protection scheme eventually gets circumvented by motivated adversaries with physical hardware access. Software-only DRM has been broken in every adjacent domain throughout computing history: DVD CSS lasted about three years, Denuvo game protection gets cracked within weeks of major releases, and media DRM remains a cat-and-mouse game decades in. The dossier itself flags that "weight obfuscation that preserves inference accuracy while preventing extraction is an unsolved problem in the academic literature." That's not just a technology risk -- it's a structural characteristic of the business. This isn't infrastructure you build once and compound; it's infrastructure you build and continuously defend. That distinction matters enormously for how I evaluate the opportunity.

On market structure timing, there IS a real structural shift happening. The proliferation of models being deployed outside cloud environments -- on-prem, edge, partner deployments -- is creating genuine demand for IP protection that didn't exist two years ago. The maturation of open-source inference engines like vLLM and SGLang creates a standardized integration surface that a drop-in replacement can target. And the M&A activity -- Palo Alto acquiring Protect AI for $500M+, F5 acquiring CalypsoAI -- validates that enterprises are writing checks for AI security tooling. But the specific sub-segment RefortifAI targets -- companies that want to distribute model weights but protect them from extraction -- is currently narrow. Most model providers either keep weights fully proprietary (OpenAI, Anthropic) or release them openly (Meta, Mistral). The middle ground exists but it's a thin wedge today. The nonobvious angle would be that this wedge is about to expand dramatically as enterprise AI deployment matures and more companies license fine-tuned models for on-prem inference. I can see the argument, but I'd want to see at least a few signed LOIs from model providers who are actively losing IP through distribution to believe the timing is right now versus eighteen months from now.

The infrastructure-layer positioning is actually the strongest element here, and it's what keeps me from a clean pass. A drop-in replacement for vLLM and SGLang that embeds in the inference stack is exactly the kind of picks-and-shovels play I gravitate toward during platform shifts. Once a model provider integrates the hardened runtime and distributes obfuscated weights to downstream customers, ripping it out means re-distributing unprotected weights everywhere -- that's a real switching cost that compounds with deployment footprint. The comparison I'd draw is to Thales Sentinel but purpose-built for AI inference rather than general software licensing. That specificity matters. Thales won't build a vLLM-compatible inference engine; the open-source inference projects don't have a DRM revenue model. There's a genuine gap in the stack. But the arms race dynamic I identified above means the infrastructure can't simply compound value the way Stripe's payment rails do -- it requires continuous security investment to stay ahead of circumvention, which looks more like a services cost structure embedded inside an infrastructure wrapper.

The bull case I have to take seriously: if the market for licensed AI model deployment grows the way I think enterprise AI will grow, the company sitting at the inference layer controlling model execution has extraordinary leverage. Every model provider who needs to ship weights externally becomes a customer, and the switching costs are structural. The team has genuinely relevant backgrounds -- RTL design at TI and Tenstorrent for GPU-level understanding, security research under Somesh Jha at UW-Madison for the adversarial side, AI engineering at KLA for the ML pipeline. That's real founder-market fit for this specific technical problem. If weight obfuscation turns out to be "good enough" rather than "perfectly secure" -- the way Denuvo protection delays piracy long enough for launch-window revenue even if it's eventually cracked -- then the business model could work on practical security rather than theoretical perfection. I'd revise my view significantly if the founders demonstrated that their target customers don't need unbreakable DRM, just "good enough" protection with a reasonable window. That reframes the miracle.

On the single-miracle test: I count at least one and a half miracles. The core technology miracle -- weight obfuscation that preserves inference performance while meaningfully resisting extraction -- is substantial and, critically, ongoing rather than one-time. An academic paper or benchmark showing their approach works at production scale with acceptable overhead would resolve the first part, but the adversarial robustness question never fully resolves. Then there's the distribution question, though the drop-in compatibility with existing inference engines partially addresses that. A three-person team maintaining parity with rapidly evolving open-source inference engines across NVIDIA, AMD, and future GPU architectures while simultaneously advancing the obfuscation research and handling the security arms race -- that's ambitious resource allocation for the problem scope.

The founders are technically well-matched to this problem but show no prior scaling experience, which is fine at pre-seed -- I've seen plenty of strong technical founders figure out the scaling challenges. The IIT Madras and BITS Pilani backgrounds suggest solid engineering foundations. What I'd want to understand in a conversation is whether they've talked to enough potential customers to know whether the demand is "we'd definitely pay for this" versus "interesting concept, maybe someday." With no traction signals, no revenue, no LOIs, and a website returning 403 errors at research time, I have no signal on customer pull. That's normal at pre-seed but combined with the unsolved core technology challenge and the narrow current addressable market, it leaves me without enough conviction to write a check with my own money. This is a company I'd want to track -- if they demonstrate the technology works at production scale with acceptable overhead and sign two or three model providers as early customers, the infrastructure positioning could make this very interesting at the next round.
