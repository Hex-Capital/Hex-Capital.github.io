# o11 -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most important thing about o11 is also the most dangerous: their distribution strategy is renting shelf space inside the store of their primary competitor. Building as a Microsoft 365 add-in and positioning yourself as "Microsoft Copilot, But It Actually Works" is a bold distribution play -- you inherit 400M+ paid Office seats as your addressable install base overnight. But you're building your house on land Microsoft owns. Microsoft controls the add-in APIs, the AppSource marketplace ranking, and the Copilot roadmap. They could restrict third-party AI capabilities, improve Copilot for finance workflows, or simply change the economics of add-in distribution. When I invested in companies like Stripe or Coinbase, they were building their own distribution rails. o11 is dependent on a platform that is actively trying to solve the same problem they're solving. That's not a distribution moat -- it's a distribution lease with a landlord who wants your customers.

The market structure here concerns me most. AI productivity tools inside Office apps is not a non-obvious market. It's one of the most consensus categories in tech right now. Microsoft is spending billions on Copilot. Gamma just hit $100M ARR and raised at a $2.1B valuation. Every major AI lab is building document generation capabilities. When I look at my best investments -- PagerDuty, Gusto, Checkr -- they were in markets that triggered dismissive reactions from other investors. "Operations alerting? Boring." "Payroll? Already solved." Nobody is dismissing AI-powered document automation. The TAM slides in this space practically write themselves, which is exactly the kind of static market sizing I distrust. The finance vertical specialization adds some specificity, but "AI for bankers and consultants" is also well-understood territory. I need to see a structural inflection that other investors aren't pricing in, and I don't see one here -- the LLM capability threshold, the Copilot pricing umbrella, the add-in marketplace rails are all visible to anyone paying attention.

The strongest bull case -- and I want to be honest about this -- rests on execution velocity and the Ajay Misra credential. Leading AI at Cal AI to $40M ARR and an acquisition by MyFitnessPal is a genuinely unusual signal for a pre-seed CTO. That's not a research project; that's a shipped product at scale. Combined with "hundreds of companies" using the product and enterprise logos like Wells Fargo and Bank of America at this stage with a two-person team, the execution evidence is materially above most YC pre-seed companies I evaluate. If you told me this team could build the definitive AI layer for finance professionals inside Office -- the next-generation Macabacus but AI-native and conversational -- I'd believe they have the shipping velocity to attempt it. Aryah at 19 with multiple prior ventures shows hustle, even if the ventures were small. The question isn't whether this team can build fast. They clearly can.

But fast execution in a market where you need multiple things to go right simultaneously is my concern. The path to a big outcome here requires: (1) Microsoft doesn't restrict or degrade add-in API access for competing AI products, (2) the two-person team achieves enterprise compliance standards (SOC 2, data residency) that Wells Fargo and Bank of America actually require for production deployment rather than pilot usage, (3) output quality for finance-specific artifacts stays meaningfully ahead of Copilot's improving baseline, and (4) Gamma doesn't pivot to in-app integrations with its $91M war chest and 70M user base. That's four conditions that all need to hold. When I apply the single-miracle test, the team's one miracle should be achievable through their own effort -- but here, the most critical variable (Microsoft's platform behavior) is entirely outside their control. Harvey's single miracle was model fidelity reaching legal-grade accuracy, and that miracle was on a clear trajectory delivered by foundation model labs. o11's most critical dependency is a strategic decision by Microsoft that could go either way.

The product-to-distribution trajectory has a ceiling I can map clearly. Going from PowerPoint to Excel to Word to Docs is product expansion, not distribution compounding. Stripe's payment integration became a distribution channel for Atlas, Billing, Treasury -- each new product leveraged the same developer relationship. o11's chart-generation feature doesn't create a relationship that makes financial model auditing easier to sell. They're adjacent features inside someone else's platform, not a distribution flywheel the company controls. The data integrations (PitchBook, Box, OneDrive) have some compounding potential if enterprise deployments create institutional lock-in, but this is speculative at this stage and requires the enterprise compliance infrastructure that a two-person team hasn't demonstrated.

I respect the execution. Cal AI is a real credential, the traction signals are strong for pre-seed, and the team is clearly building at speed. But I keep coming back to the structural position: this is a consensus market, the distribution is rented, the platform risk is existential, and the path forward requires multiple external conditions to hold simultaneously. The technology cycle timing is neither early nor late -- it's exactly when everyone else is building too, which means competition intensity will be brutal. If Microsoft Copilot remains mediocre for finance workflows for the next three years and the team can sprint to deep enterprise lock-in during that window, this could be a meaningful outcome. That's a plausible scenario. But it's not the kind of structural conviction I need to write a check.
