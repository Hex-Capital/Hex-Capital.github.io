# RamAIn -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The most striking thing about RamAIn is the competitive geometry. They're building a middleware layer in the single hottest category in AI -- computer-use agents -- where Simular has $27M and an ex-DeepMind team with a Microsoft partnership, Browser Use has $17M and 89% benchmark accuracy, and the foundation model providers themselves (Anthropic's Computer Use API, OpenAI's CUA) are building the capability natively. When I invested in Alchemy, it was infrastructure for a decentralized ecosystem where no single provider could own the stack. Here, two companies -- Anthropic and OpenAI -- could add pre-training optimization to their own computer-use APIs with a product update. The middleware squeeze is the defining risk, and no amount of execution speed resolves a structural positioning problem.

The specific knowledge question is where I get stuck. Vansh Ramani has genuine ML research credentials -- an ICLR 2025 publication, work at CMU's ML department, and a nearest-neighbor algorithm that made it into Meta's FAISS library. That's real. But it's specific knowledge about ML methodology, not about enterprise automation workflows. The pre-training approach for CUAs is a clever engineering insight, and Vansh likely has the technical depth to build it well. But could another strong ML researcher arrive at the same architectural choice? Almost certainly. Compare this to what I look for: knowledge that takes years to accumulate through obsessive curiosity about a particular domain. These founders are undergraduates. Shourya bootstrapped Genoshi to six-figure revenue, which shows energy and resourcefulness, but a McKinsey internship and a bootstrapped AI studio don't constitute deep, non-transferable understanding of enterprise procurement or healthcare workflows. They're building automation for domains they've observed, not domains they've lived inside.

The leverage architecture has potential but remains unproven. If pre-training on enterprise interfaces generalizes -- if each new interface the system learns makes it faster at learning the next one -- then this becomes a compounding data flywheel with real leverage. One pre-trained agent replacing dozens of human process steps is genuine multiplication of output per unit of input. But if each new enterprise customer requires bespoke pre-training on their specific interface configurations, this scales linearly with implementation effort. That's a services business wearing an ML costume. The dossier provides no evidence either way, and at pre-seed with no customers, there's no empirical answer. The pricing is listed as free, which tells me they haven't yet tested whether the value proposition survives contact with paying customers.

The bull case I have to take seriously: the RPA market is $35B and growing at 24% CAGR, dominated by incumbents (UiPath, Automation Anywhere) whose script-based architectures are structurally unable to make the leap to vision-model-based automation without cannibalizing their professional services revenue. If pre-trained CUAs genuinely deliver 10x faster execution than runtime inference approaches, and if RamAIn can prove this with enterprise customers before the better-funded competitors or foundation model providers catch up, the company could become the picks-and-shovels layer for agentic enterprise automation. Vansh's technical caliber is real -- contributing to FAISS and publishing at ICLR as an undergrad puts him in a small percentile of ML researchers globally. Young, technically exceptional founders in a massive market with structural tailwinds -- that combination has produced outlier outcomes before. The question is whether this particular architecture and this particular team have enough head start in a race where everyone is running.

But I keep returning to the consensus problem. "Everyone agrees computer-use agents are the future" is not a sentence that precedes asymmetric returns. The contrarian bet here would be that pre-training on interfaces is architecturally superior to runtime inference, and that this specific two-person team can establish that advantage before $87M+ in competing capital overwhelms them. I don't see the non-consensus insight. I see a competent team entering a crowded race with a plausible but unvalidated architectural choice, no customers, no revenue, and foundation model providers building the same capability as a feature rather than a company. The technology doesn't compound in a way that's structurally defensible -- it's an optimization layer on top of someone else's models.

I'd pass. Not because the founders lack intelligence or energy -- they clearly have both. But because specific knowledge about ML methodology is not the same as specific knowledge about the problem domain, the leverage architecture is unproven, and the timing is consensus rather than contrarian. The best version of this company gets acquired by an RPA incumbent or a foundation model provider. That's a fine outcome, but it's not the compounding, decade-long bet I'm looking for.
