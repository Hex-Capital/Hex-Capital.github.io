# Grove Tax -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Grove Tax is the archetype of the bet I decline. An AI tool that makes tax preparers 40% faster is a fine business for someone else to fund — it is not a bet where the consequence of success reshapes how millions of people live. When I evaluate a company, I start with one question: if this fully works, does the world visibly change? If Grove Tax automates the intake-to-delivery lifecycle for every tax firm in America, the result is that CPAs process more returns per season with fewer admin hours. That's a productivity gain inside an existing profession. Nobody's life fundamentally changes. The 70% time reduction Grove Tax claims on its website is an efficiency metric, not a consequence metric. Compare that to what happens if Commonwealth Fusion Systems works — energy becomes limitless — or if Impossible Foods works — protein production decouples from animal agriculture. Those are consequences. Grove Tax's consequence ceiling is a moderately profitable vertical SaaS company, which is exactly the outcome profile I avoid.

The technical approach confirms the pattern. The dossier itself states that "LLM-based document extraction is increasingly commoditized" and that Grove Tax's "core technical barrier is tax-form-specific accuracy at production scale rather than novel AI architecture." The two products currently shipping — Collect ($25/return for intake and document management) and Deliver ($10/return for e-signatures and branded presentations) — are workflow features, not AI breakthroughs. The genuinely hard capability, Prepare & Review, which would auto-generate draft tax returns, is listed as "Coming Soon." Grove Tax is selling the scaffolding before the building exists. When I compare this to Rocket Lab, which built novel electric turbopump engines and 3D-printed combustion chambers — a non-linear technical approach to cost reduction in physical hardware — the gap in technical ambition is stark. Grove Tax is applying existing LLM APIs to a known document processing workflow. That is a wrapper, not a wedge.

Uday Nandam spent a decade in product management at Intuit across ProConnect, Lacerte, and Intuit Link, and claims Head of Product at Thomson Reuters UltraTax. Gaurav Mathur led engineering at Airbnb and was early at Indeed. These are competent résumés — and they are exactly the domain-expert profile I discount. When I backed Peter Beck at Rocket Lab, he was a self-taught engineer from New Zealand with no aerospace degree. When I backed the 19-year-old MIT dropout at Mach Industries, he had no defense industry experience. I pick for best athlete, not most established wide receiver. Nandam knows the tax software landscape intimately, which means he also knows what the industry considers possible — and that knowledge constrains ambition. No evidence in the dossier suggests either founder has pivoted, been surprised, or demonstrated the rapid learning velocity I weight over credentials. The domain expertise is relevant to execution, but execution without a discontinuous insight produces incremental companies.

The competitive landscape reinforces my concern. Canopy has raised $292M and generated $23.9M in 2024 revenue. TaxGPT raised $5.22M. Taxnova emerged from the same a16z Speedrun cohort with $1M pre-seed. Corvee raised $10M and is launching its own tax prep brand. When four or five companies are converging on the same opportunity with the same tools, the probability of a 50x outcome for any single entrant drops sharply. Grove Tax's per-return pricing model ($30/return for the bundle) is clever — it removes per-seat friction and aligns with firm revenue — but clever pricing is not a moat. The absence of any public traction data beyond website claims (no user counts, no firm counts, no revenue) at a stage where even a handful of paying firms would be a meaningful signal leaves the option value uncertain.

The strongest bull case: the CPA workforce crisis is real and accelerating — 300,000+ professionals exited since 2020, CPA exam candidates dropped 37% from 2016 to 2023. If the accountant shortage becomes acute enough, Grove Tax's automation shifts from "nice to have" to "existential necessity" for surviving firms. And the incumbents — Thomson Reuters and Intuit — face a genuine innovator's dilemma: their revenue scales with per-seat licenses, which means AI that eliminates seats cannibalizes their business model. If Nandam's domain knowledge translates into faster iteration on the Prepare & Review module, and if that module achieves production-grade accuracy across thousands of form variants and state-specific rules, Grove Tax could capture meaningful share of a $17.6B market before incumbents respond. That is a legitimate path to a $500M+ business. But a $500M outcome on a pre-seed check is a 3-5x return at best given dilution — not the asymmetric payoff I require. I want to either lose my money or make 50 times my money. Grove Tax is designed for the middle.

The two dimensions that lock this verdict: consequence magnitude and non-linear technical approach. If Grove Tax succeeds, tax firms get more efficient — that is a $30/return improvement inside a profession, not a transformation of how humans interact with taxation. And the technical approach applies commodity LLM capabilities to a well-defined workflow without a proprietary science breakthrough or 10x discontinuity. Pass.
