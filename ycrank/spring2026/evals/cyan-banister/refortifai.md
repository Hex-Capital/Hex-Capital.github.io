# Refortifai -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Microsoft released a free, open-source Agent Governance Toolkit under MIT license on April 2, 2026 — covering all ten OWASP agentic AI risks with sub-0.1ms latency across Python, TypeScript, Rust, Go, and .NET, with integrations into LangChain, CrewAI, and Google ADK. That's the single fact I can't get past when I look at Refortifai. When the largest enterprise software company on earth gives away exactly what you're building, for free, three weeks before your YC batch starts, you need either a dramatically different product or a dramatically deeper understanding of the problem than Microsoft has. I don't see evidence of either here. Refortifai's YC page describes intercepting tool calls and enforcing governance policies — the same architectural pattern Microsoft just commoditized. And Noma Security has $132M in funding with 1,300% ARR growth. Capsule Security raised $7M in the same month. This is not a space where I'm the first check in — this is a space where every institutional investor in Silicon Valley is writing checks as fast as they can. That's the opposite of my thesis.

Sayan Mitra, Rithik Jain, and Atman Kar founded Refortifai together as friends — and that's essentially all I know about the biographical story. When I sit down with founders, my first question is always: what happened to you that made this problem find you? The dossier gives me career paths, not crucibles. Sayan Mitra's background is so thin I can't even confirm his LinkedIn profile. Rithik Jain shifted from economics to computer science at UW-Madison — admirable intellectual breadth, but not a forge. Atman Kar designed automotive RADAR hardware accelerators at Texas Instruments and interned at Tenstorrent on CPU RTL — genuine technical depth, but in hardware, not security. Nobody on this team has a confirmed background in cybersecurity, application security, or threat research. When I backed investments that connect to my IronPort security roots, I looked for founders who had been in the trenches — who had seen breaches happen, who understood attacker psychology from lived experience. Refortifai's founders assembled around a market opportunity. I can feel the difference.

Refortifai's product direction itself raises a flag I can't ignore. The YC page describes "a security agent that monitors your agent" — runtime governance for AI agents making tool calls. But the company website describes something entirely different: "anti-cheat for AI models" that obfuscates model weights before distribution, with a secured runtime that keeps original weight values unexposed. These are fundamentally different products for fundamentally different buyers solving fundamentally different threat models. The model-weight obfuscation product is actually the more interesting one to me — it's weirder, less crowded, and Atman Kar's hardware engineering background at TI and Tenstorrent gives him genuine technical credibility to build it. But Refortifai appears to be pivoting toward the consensus play, the agent runtime security product, where their team's strongest technical depth doesn't live. A pre-seed company that hasn't resolved which product it's building is a company still searching for its problem — and that search should happen before my money arrives, not after.

The bull case would go like this: AI agent security is a genuine inflection point — Gartner predicted 40% of enterprise apps will embed agents by 2026, up from under 5% in early 2025. The attack surface is real and new. Nicolas Dessaigne, who co-founded Datadog, is Refortifai's YC partner — he knows infrastructure security deeply. CalypsoAI's $180M acquisition by F5 proves acquirers are hungry for AI security assets. And if Refortifai can accumulate proprietary threat intelligence data from monitoring agent tool calls across customers, that data network effect could become a real moat over time. Maybe the product ambiguity is actually a strength — they're exploring two genuinely different wedges and YC will help them focus. That's a reasonable argument. But it falls apart on the structural question: can a three-person team with no visible security domain expertise outrun Microsoft's open-source toolkit, Noma's $132M war chest, and Capsule's head start, all in a category where the core architecture — intercept, evaluate, allow/deny — is straightforward enough that Microsoft built it as a side project? I've been burned by exactly this pattern before — products that depend on timing without structural moats underneath. Refortifai's tool-call interception layer is closer to HQ Trivia's attention capture than to Flexport's logistics network effects.

When I run the invisible system test, Refortifai partially passes — there is genuine opacity in what AI agents do with their tool calls, and enterprises genuinely don't know what's happening beneath the surface. But this isn't the kind of invisible system that moves me most deeply. Refortifai isn't making a hidden extractive structure transparent to the people being exploited by it — it's selling visibility to the enterprises that deployed the agents in the first place. That's a real business, but it's not Affirm exposing credit card opacity to consumers, or Ownwell attacking the property tax system on behalf of homeowners. The power asymmetry Refortifai addresses runs enterprise-to-enterprise, not gatekeeper-to-vulnerable-person. On cascading impact, Refortifai's success would make enterprise AI deployment safer, which has diffuse positive effects — but no direct mechanism for expanding economic access to underserved populations.

The one condition that would flip me to invest: if Sayan Mitra or another founder has a deep, verifiable security background that the dossier missed — years at a SOC, a track record of vulnerability research, a personal encounter with an AI agent breach that made this problem impossible to walk away from — and if they committed fully to the model-weight obfuscation product where Atman Kar's hardware expertise creates genuine technical defensibility. That would change the forge score and the defensibility score simultaneously. Without that, Refortifai is a team without security DNA building a consensus product in a crowded market where Microsoft just gave away the blueprint. I wish them well — the problem is real — but this isn't a Long Journey bet. The decisive dimensions are founder biographical forge (no personal stakes, no security background, score driven by absence) and infrastructure-grade defensibility (Microsoft open-source substitution, architecturally straightforward pattern, no visible moat).
