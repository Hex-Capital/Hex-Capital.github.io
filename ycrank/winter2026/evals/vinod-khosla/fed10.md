# Fed10 -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Let me state what I see immediately: Fed10 is building a cheaper policy consultant. If they execute perfectly -- flawless AI analysis, every bill in every state legislature tracked in real time, enterprise adoption across Fortune 500 government affairs teams -- the consequence is that policy monitoring costs drop from $50,000 a year to maybe $5,000. That is a useful product. It is not a transformed world. Nobody's life changes. No industry becomes obsolete. The global market they're competing in is $1.4 billion. Even if you believe AI-native pricing expands the buyer base tenfold -- every small business suddenly monitoring legislation -- you're looking at a mid-sized vertical SaaS outcome in a niche that will be crowded by the time it matures. I backed OpenAI because the consequence of general intelligence succeeding was civilizational. I backed Impossible Foods because eliminating animal agriculture would reclaim half the planet's arable land. The consequence of Fed10 succeeding is that trade associations save money on their Beltway consultants. That calculus stops me before I evaluate anything else.

What concerns me further is the competitive structure. The underlying data -- every bill, every legislative text -- is public. The technology -- LLM-based document analysis and summarization -- is available to anyone with an API key and a weekend. FiscalNote already launched PolicyNote as an AI product in January 2025. Quorum, with $61 million in revenue and 1,800 customers including half the Fortune 100, is integrating AI features. Plural Policy raised $14 million. USLege is backed by Avalanche.VC pursuing essentially the same thesis. When I invested in Juniper, Cisco's CTO told me they would never build a TCP/IP router -- the incumbent was structurally incapable of cannibalizing its own architecture. Here, the incumbents are already building the AI layer. FiscalNote's stock decline signals distress, not inability to compete -- a wounded incumbent with 12,000 government relationships and an AI product in market is more dangerous than a healthy one, not less. The barrier to a basic version of this product is low. The barrier to matching domain-specific accuracy may be higher, but that advantage accrues to whoever has the most customer interaction data, and the incumbents start with 1,800 enterprise customers generating that data today.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. These founders are remarkably young and have already operated in the policy world with real results -- passing legislation before voting age, gubernatorial appointments, the kind of precocity that signals raw capability. If I squint, I can see a version where the market expands dramatically: millions of small businesses and nonprofits that are affected by legislation but have never been able to afford monitoring. If Fed10 nails the self-serve motion at a price point ten times lower than incumbents, they could own a new category of buyer that FiscalNote and Quorum never built for. The team's combination of direct lobbying experience and technical ability is genuinely well-matched to this specific problem. And the timing tailwind is real -- LLM quality crossed a threshold for legal language parsing that didn't exist two years ago. But even this optimistic scenario produces a solid vertical SaaS business, not a category-defining company that changes how the world operates.

Here is what I notice that a generic analyst might miss: these founders are insiders, not outsiders. They are lobbyists who built a tool for lobbyists. They know what the industry considers standard, which means they also know what the industry considers the boundary of ambition. The dossier frames their domain expertise as a strength, and for most investors it is. For me, it is a yellow flag. The greatest innovations I have funded came from people who did not know what the incumbents considered impossible -- Pat Brown had never worked in food, Peter Beck had no aerospace credentials, Pradeep Sindhu came from Xerox PARC not from Cisco. Industry insiders aim for 5% year-to-year improvement and build what the market already understands. These founders have chosen a problem whose scope is exactly calibrated to their existing experience, not to the magnitude of what is possible. They are building a tool that makes consulting cheaper. They are not reimagining how democratic governance interfaces with the private sector, or how AI could fundamentally restructure the relationship between citizens and legislation. The ambition is bounded by what they already know.

I pass. The founders are capable, the timing is favorable, and the product solves a real pain point. But I invest my own money in problems whose consequences of success justify the magnitude of effort required. Making policy consulting cheaper is not that problem. Some investor will fund this and may do well -- it could be a $200 million outcome. But the gap between a $200 million outcome and what I am looking for is exactly the gap between reducing failure probability and maximizing success consequence. These founders are too talented to spend their careers making lobbyists more efficient. I would rather see them attack something that terrifies them.
