# RASPIRE -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

RASPIRE is a competent domain-practitioner team — Kareem Selim and Hassan Mostafa, years securing bank and fintech apps out of Cairo with Hassan ex-Cyshield — wrapped around the most permission-gated distribution architecture in software: enterprise security sales into regulated Saudi and Egyptian banks via a Sadq partnership unveiled at Black Hat MEA Riyadh. The signature pairing is a top-half specific-knowledge gate sitting on a bottom-quartile leverage architecture, and that pairing tells me what kind of company RASPIRE is before I look at anything else.

Stack Overflow is the load-bearing analogy on the leverage axis I run first. Atwood and Spolsky wrote code that developers pulled, indexed by Google, with the marginal cost of the next reader being zero — the company distributed through permissionless code-and-media (Naval on X, May 2018) and the unit economics never required a single SDR. RASPIRE distributes through the literal opposite: post-compilation binary fusion shipped via a strategic partnership with a Saudi vendor, exhibitor booth at Black Hat MEA 2025, into procurement cycles attached to Vision 2030. There is no organic developer pull in this dossier — no GitHub from either founder, no public technical writing, no Product Hunt, no developer community using RASPIRE because they discovered it. "Labor and capital leverage require permission" (nav.al/product-media); RASPIRE is asking the Saudi Central Bank's procurement office for permission, and that scores down before anything else loads.

A "dental SaaS for orthodontists" is the canonical low-rung pattern on market shape — a single vertical, no adjacent pivot vector, the "first idea never works" axiom (Venture Hacks 2007-10) leaves you nowhere to maneuver. RASPIRE is not that narrow — mobile RASP at $2.59B in 2026 growing 27.96% CAGR (Mordor Intelligence) is real — but the mobile-specific RASP segment at $140.1M in 2025 (Data Insights Market) is the actual SAM, and the pivot vectors from "no-code runtime protection for mobile binaries sold to MENA banks" are narrow: adjacent into API attestation (Approov's territory), or general mobile app shielding (Appdome's territory). Either pivot lands directly on top of a better-capitalized incumbent. The market has room to grow but not much room for the *first idea not to work*.

Mechanism analysis on the network dimension: RASPIRE is single-sided B2B SaaS where Bank A's purchase does not make Bank B's deployment more valuable. The dossier hints at AI-driven threat-telemetry as a future data moat, which is the only plausible compounding-user-value mechanism here — attack signatures aggregating across protected apps could in principle make the (N+1)th deployment better-defended because of the prior N. Over 18 months this either materializes as published detection-accuracy deltas vs. Appdome and Zimperium, or it doesn't, and RASPIRE collapses into a feature-parity vendor in G2's already-25-vendor RASP category. The dossier discloses zero telemetry volume or accuracy figures, so the data-moat thesis is currently a marketing slide, not a mechanism.

Counterfactual on specific knowledge: Selim and Mostafa pass the practitioner gate — years of bank/fintech app security, Hassan at Cyshield, the kind of lived problem ownership that distinguishes them from an ex-FAANG PM identifying a TAM. To flip into the top quartile alongside Aravind Srinivas at Perplexity, I would need to see one of them as a named contributor on Frida, a published reverse-engineering toolchain, a CVE history, or a multi-year open-source RASP project that predated the company. The dossier shows none of that, and "No GitHub repos found" for both founders is the silence that pins this score below where Replit's Amjad Masad sat in April 2023.

Single-anchor parallel on the founder trio: Atwood and Spolsky read as 10-year partners because their writing — Coding Horror, Joel on Software — let me triangulate intelligence and integrity in public for years before the check. RASPIRE's founders have a clean LinkedIn footprint, CyberTalents speaker recognition for Selim, and no contested exits or partner litigation in the search results. The dossier shows nothing negative and nothing distinctive; the absent-evidence default holds.

RASPIRE in 36 months reaches $3-8M ARR concentrated in MENA banking, fintech, and government, and gets acquired by Appdome or Zimperium for $40-80M as a no-code onboarding flow bolted onto a 400+ defense library — or it stalls under $3M ARR and dies into Appdome's marketing copy. The mechanism is unavoidable: a permission-gated distribution model selling commoditized RASP primitives into a procurement-cycle market means revenue grows linearly with sales headcount, never exponentially with code, and the better-capitalized incumbent ($39.6M raised at Appdome, $60.1M at Zimperium) wins the durable enterprise relationships unless RASPIRE gets bought into one.

The strongest objection is the Perplexity exception (~2022) — I funded a single-sided foundation-model-app that violated my network-effects criterion because three other gates cleared emphatically: permissionless API distribution, Berkeley ML PhD specific knowledge, pre-consensus AI timing. The bull on RASPIRE would argue Selim and Mostafa's MENA banking domain depth is the equivalent specific-knowledge gate, that "vibe-coded" apps flooding production is the pre-consensus timing, and that the 20M+ end-user deployments are early proof of pull. That case fails on the leverage architecture: Perplexity distributed through a free web app anyone in the world could hit; RASPIRE distributes through a Sadq channel partnership into Saudi regulated banks. The Anchorage Digital permission-as-moat override doesn't rescue this either — that exception required my decade-long crypto category accumulation since MetaStable (2014), and I have no parallel mobile-security category bet that earns RASPIRE the same exemption. The verdict flips to invest only if Selim ships a public open-source binary-instrumentation tool that gets 5,000+ GitHub stars from developers integrating it without ever talking to a salesperson — the Stack Overflow pattern in code form. Until then, this is the kind of company a Khosla or Gil should price; it is not the kind of architecture I write checks against.
