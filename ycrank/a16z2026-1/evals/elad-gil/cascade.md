# Cascade -- Elad Gil Evaluation

$183K in contracted ARR from zero in three months, selling into architecture and construction firms -- an industry that typically takes six months just to schedule a software demo. That traction velocity is the most analytically informative data point in this dossier, and it tells me something the competitive landscape alone wouldn't: Cascade is solving a pain point so acute that even one of the slowest-adopting industries in tech is buying fast. The first three clients doubling their spend adds a second signal -- this isn't a novelty trial, it's a tool that embeds deeper over time. When I see expansion revenue at pre-seed in a conservative vertical, I pay attention.

The market structure question -- what changed to make this possible NOW -- has a real but not deeply nonobvious answer. LLM capability crossed a quality threshold for generating construction proposals from technical specifications, and post-COVID cloud ERP adoption by AEC firms (Deltek Vantagepoint, Ajera, Costpoint) created integration surfaces that didn't exist at scale five years ago. Both shifts are genuine. But "AI applied to vertical X" is the most popular investment thesis of 2024-2026, and multiple competitors have already recognized AEC as underserved: Joist AI ($4M seed), Workorb AI ($2.6M raised), and AutogenAI ($65.3M total) sitting one vertical expansion away. Cascade's timing advantage is real but measured in months, not years. Where the nonobviousness comes in is something I've seen repeatedly in my portfolio -- the Gusto pattern. Payroll was unglamorous; construction proposal automation is unglamorous. Prestige-seeking founders chase legal AI and healthcare AI. The absence of founder competition in "boring but critical" operational infrastructure is itself a structural advantage, and it compounds because the best technical talent keeps flowing to flashier categories. Cascade benefits from the same competitive vacuum that made Gusto and Samsara possible.

The infrastructure-layer test is where Cascade gets genuinely interesting. The "data layer for all construction projects" positioning is aspirational at 15 customers, but the product architecture suggests a path: start with project discovery and proposal writing, then expand into spec analysis, compliance (Davis-Bacon certified payroll), bid leveling, and joint-venture matching. Each feature deepens the switching cost. The ERP integrations with Deltek, Ajera, Costpoint, and OpenAsset are the key mechanism here -- once a firm's project history, capability profiles, and proposal templates are flowing through Cascade, ripping it out means disconnecting from your own operational data. This mirrors how Stripe went from payment processing to the payments API that entire business workflows depended on. Cascade isn't there yet, but the product roadmap shows they're building toward a platform, not a point solution.

The single-miracle test is favorable. The AI capability miracle is happening exogenously -- foundation models are improving on their own curve, and the $183K cARR suggests the current capability level is already sufficient for AEC proposal quality. Government RFP portals are publicly accessible. AEC firms are proven software buyers (Procore built a $10B+ business selling to these same firms). The one remaining miracle is standard execution: can Cascade build the integrations, maintain data pipeline reliability across thousands of government portal formats, and scale the sales motion beyond the Head of Growth's Procore network? That's execution risk, not a breakthrough requirement.

Hannia Zia's profile activates a pattern I weight heavily. Scaling Google Pay India from 0 to 100 million users means she operated inside Google during a hypergrowth product launch -- the kind of scaling experience that teaches you how organizations break and how to rebuild them. Joana Ferreira brings ML depth from Google Search and Amazon, and both founders stress-tested their working relationship at UnlikelyAI before starting Cascade. Clear CEO/CTO split, no ambiguous authority structure. The Head of Growth -- ranked #2 sales rep at Procore, driving $3M ARR in 2025 -- is the kind of hire that signals founder judgment. Procore IS the construction management platform; having someone who knows every buyer in the industry changes the distribution calculus entirely for a vertical SaaS company.

The most thesis-specific risk: AutogenAI's $65.3M in funding and existing horizontal proposal infrastructure means adding AEC-specific features (Davis-Bacon compliance modules, Deltek integrations, construction spec parsing) is an engineering investment, not a fundamental product rebuild. If AutogenAI reads the same traction signals I'm reading and decides AEC is worth pursuing, Cascade gets outgunned on capital while its data moat is still thin at 15 customers. What would flip me: if Cascade's next two quarters show the same growth trajectory and client expansion pattern, the data flywheel starts compounding fast enough that horizontal entrants face a cold-start problem on AEC-specific training data. Evidence I'd watch for: net revenue retention above 150%, win rates against AutogenAI in head-to-head evaluations, and the first client referral chains forming organically.

Cascade earns a low Invest from me, driven primarily by two signals: the infrastructure-layer trajectory (ERP integrations creating compounding switching costs in a Gusto-like "boring but critical" category) and the single-miracle viability (AI quality already sufficient based on $183K cARR and expansion revenue, with no regulatory or distribution breakthroughs required). The market timing is real but not deeply nonobvious -- that's the gap that keeps this from scoring higher.
