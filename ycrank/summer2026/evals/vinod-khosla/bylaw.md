# Bylaw -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

When I read the **Bylaw** dossier -- three University of Waterloo first-year CS classmates (Mazin Al-Ani, Farhan Ur Rehman, Gurshabd Singh Varaich) building a "Runtime Evidence Gate" SDK for AI agent writes -- the signature lands immediately: lowest on Consequence Magnitude, marginally less-low on Founder Learning Rate, and the pairing tells me exactly what kind of company **Bylaw** is. This is a governance-and-compliance middleware play dressed in AI-agent vocabulary, sold into the seam between LangSmith, Langfuse, OpenAI, and Braintrust -- which is to say, sold into the integration partners most likely to absorb it. The Outlook Business test -- "make a great company and the money takes care of itself" -- inverts here: **Bylaw**'s own description leads with "guarantee your agents use the right evidence before they act," and that is the language of a feature, not a company.

On Consequence Magnitude, the **OpenAI** (2019) check sits at the top of my ladder because the success-state was civilizational -- I wrote $50M into a no-revenue, unconventional-structure entity because if it worked, the labor economy bent. **Bylaw**'s maximum-ambition success-state is that every write-enabled agent in an enterprise stack runs an evidence manifest before calling a refund API or a CRM update. That is a useful audit log. It is not the collapse of an incumbent regime; it is a SOC-2 line item with telemetry. The dossier itself flags the framing -- "signed audit logs," "allow/restrict/review/block," EU AI Act high-risk obligations applying Aug 2, 2026 -- which is the language of compliance arbitrage, and I told TechCrunch in 2013 I am not a big fan of governance. Where **OpenAI** asked "what happens if machines can do 80% of economically valuable work," **Bylaw** asks "what happens if every agent write is logged correctly." Those are different questions at different orders of magnitude.

The lowest rung on my Technical-Risk ladder is the GPT-4-wrapper sales-call summarizer I pass on in thirty seconds -- a software-only execution risk with standard burn-to-ARR economics that any Tier-1 generalist will fund. The failure mechanism is over-resourcing: I am the wrong investor because the company does not need the risk tolerance I price for. **Bylaw** sits inside that mechanism. The dossier states explicitly that the LLM "assists compilation only, not runtime decisions" -- meaning the runtime is a deterministic policy engine, which is a 1990s rule-engine architecture wrapped in a 2026 SDK. There is no catalyst-yield miracle here, no battery-chemistry miracle, no foundation-model training run, no fleet, no plant. Three Waterloo undergrads can ship this on a YC standard-deal $500K, and the entire competitive set -- Guardrails AI at $7.5M seed, Prompt Security at $23M total, Galileo at $68M -- has already proved the category does not require Khosla-shaped capital.

Founder defies expert consensus -- mechanism analysis. The pattern that compounds over 18 months is whether a founder has staked a public, falsifiable position their field rejected; **Physical Intelligence** with Levine and Finn earned the right because they had publicly argued for end-to-end robot learning when the field was still hand-coding manipulation. **Bylaw**'s three founders have LinkedIn headlines like "cs @ uw, swe @ optiver" and intern stints at Meta, Optiver, BitGo, theScore, and Ford. No public writing, no thesis paper, no controversial position, no Twitter presence the dossier could even find. Eighteen months from now this dimension does not compound -- it stays flat -- because there is no instigator posture to build a research-PI-shaped reputation around. The Berkeley 28,000-prediction study I cite at TED applies in reverse here: absence of expert-defiance is not damning by itself, but absence of any public intellectual surface area at all is a soft negative I price in.

Founder learning rate -- counterfactual. To flip this dimension from a default-low into a **Slash**-tier signal, the dossier would need to show a documented pivot inside **Bylaw**'s short YC history: a prior product the three founders shipped that failed and taught them why facts-not-tool-eligibility was the right wedge, or a public retraction of an earlier thesis. The dossier shows none of that -- it shows a clean linear narrative ("met in first year at Waterloo, interned at companies where unverified writes have financial consequences, built the obvious thing"). That is the inverse of the Kanye-pivot story that justified my $100M Series C into Slash. The counterfactual is not implausible -- they are early enough that a Q4 2026 pivot away from the evidence-gate framing into, say, an agent-side-effect simulator would re-rate this dimension overnight -- but on present dossier evidence the score sits at default.

Required-capital profile -- single-anchor parallel. Compare **Bylaw** to **Commonwealth Fusion** (2019), where I led $115M Series A because the SPARC tokamak required deployment-scale capital no consensus VC would touch and the roadmap to first plasma demanded it. **Bylaw**'s roadmap requires an SDK, integration shims into four observability platforms, and a deterministic policy compiler -- a three-engineer effort with a 12-month runway off a YC check. I am structurally over-resourced for this deal. Even if **Bylaw** wins, the path to the win runs through a $5M seed, a $20M Series A from a developer-tools specialist like Bessemer or Index, and an enterprise GTM motion. None of those rounds need me, and inserting Khosla capital does not buy the company anything it cannot buy more cheaply elsewhere.

My 36-month forecast for **Bylaw**: acquired sub-$60M by Galileo, Braintrust, or Langfuse as a feature absorption, or quietly shut after OpenAI ships native evidence-verification primitives in its Agents SDK -- the mechanism being platform-vendor absorption by the same integration partners listed on usebylaw.com. The Galileo Agent Control open-source release in March 2026 with AWS, CrewAI, and Glean as launch partners is the leading indicator; when an Apache 2.0 control plane with Fortune 50 distribution exists in the same week **Bylaw** is incorporating, the runtime-gate SKU compresses to zero on a 24-month clock.

The strongest objection to that forecast runs through the EU AI Act -- the bull case is that high-risk obligations applying Aug 2, 2026 create a regulatory forcing function strong enough that every enterprise deploying write-enabled agents needs an auditable evidence layer by mid-2027, and **Bylaw**'s signed audit logs become the de facto compliance artifact, the way Auth0 became the de facto identity artifact for a previous regulatory cycle. The bear-side dismantling: regulatory forcing functions do not create $10B companies, they create $200M acquisition targets for the platforms regulators actually audit, which is precisely the OpenAI/Anthropic/AWS layer **Bylaw** integrates with rather than displaces. Glean is my Known Exception for backing a domain-expert founder in enterprise infrastructure, but Glean's override signal was capital scale and Fortune 500 information-asymmetry magnitude -- **Bylaw** has neither. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: if by Q2 2027 **Bylaw** has shipped with three named Fortune 500 design partners and a published latency benchmark under 50ms per gate-check at p99, the regulatory bull case becomes load-bearing and I would re-engage at Series A.
