# Synthetic Sciences -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the thing about "AI co-scientists that run research end-to-end" -- this is not an insane idea. This might be the single most consensus idea in AI right now. Google announced its own AI Co-Scientist in 2025. Edison Scientific raised $70M from Jeff Dean to build autonomous AI scientists. Elicit already has 400,000 researchers using their platform monthly. Sakana AI is sitting at a $2.65B valuation with their AI Scientist project. When I say I look for a sane person with an insane idea, I mean the idea should provoke genuine skepticism or confusion from most investors. "We're going to use AI to automate scientific research" provokes neither -- it provokes nodding heads and oversubscribed rounds. This is a sane person with a sane idea, which in my framework means the returns are already priced into the market before anyone writes a check.

The founders deserve credit where it's due. Aayam Bansal at 18 with 15+ publications at ICML and NeurIPS, research stints across CMU, Harvard, and MIT CSAIL, and a prior exit -- that's a genuinely unusual signal. Ishaan Gangwani's USACO Platinum and IOAI credentials add technical depth. These are builders, not pitch-deck optimizers, and they're building a product they themselves would use. But founder credibility without a non-consensus idea just means you've found a talented person who will execute well in a category where execution alone can't win. You need structural advantage when Google, OpenAI, and a $70M-funded competitor are all converging on the same thesis.

Now let me run my real test -- the one that matters more than anything else in my framework right now. Would this business be good if AI didn't exist? The answer is obviously no. There IS no business without AI. The AI is not the "cherry on top" -- the AI is the whole sundae, the bowl, and the table it sits on. This is exactly the category I avoid. Their moat claim is a "data flywheel" where process-level research traces improve their agents over time, but every AI company in existence claims this same flywheel, and I have yet to see one that actually compounds into defensibility before a better foundation model simply leapfrogs the accumulated process data. Their pricing -- $50/month, $200/month SaaS tiers -- is literally the business model I've spent two years arguing is dying. Software is a business tool, not a business model. And when your software is a wrapper around someone else's LLMs running on someone else's GPUs, you don't even own the tool.

The bull case I'd want to believe: these founders are genuine domain experts building for their own tribe, and the integration complexity of connecting frontier models to research-specific execution environments -- GPU orchestration, biology toolchains, LaTeX pipelines, credential syncing across GitHub and Weights & Biases -- creates a product surface area that horizontal AI providers won't prioritize as a standalone product. If the RL training infrastructure generates genuinely proprietary process data, and if that data creates meaningfully better research agents, you could imagine a world where Synthetic Sciences becomes the researcher's operating system with compounding advantages. The BixBench Verified claim of 92% (even if on a subset) suggests real technical capability. And the market is enormous -- $34B by 2035. If these founders can move faster than incumbents in a specific vertical wedge like computational biology, they might establish enough researcher lock-in to survive. But "might" is doing a lot of work in that sentence, and the Birchbox lesson haunts me here: narrative magnitude without structural moat produces the worst venture outcome. A painting company with AI scheduling has a moat Google can't touch. A research workflow platform built on Google's models has a moat Google can dissolve with a product announcement.

The primitive-versus-application distinction seals my assessment. Venmo created a new payment primitive. Aleo created a new privacy protocol. Helium created a new wireless infrastructure layer. Synthetic Sciences is building an application -- a well-crafted one, potentially -- on top of existing primitives owned by others. They're not establishing a new standard or layer that other companies build on. They're assembling other people's components into a product. That's a feature, not a platform, and features get absorbed. The recent pivot from InkVell (document formatting) to full-stack AI research platform during YC shows ambition, but it also means they're months into building a product that competes against teams with years of head start and orders of magnitude more capital. Two eighteen-year-olds with $1.5M against Edison Scientific's $70M is not a fair fight, and I don't see the structural asymmetry that would let them win it.
