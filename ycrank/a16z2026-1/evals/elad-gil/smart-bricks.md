# Smart Bricks -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Smart Bricks claims a ~$12M revenue run rate on a $5M pre-seed raise, which -- if real -- would make it one of the most capital-efficient companies I've seen at this stage. But no independent source verifies this figure, and the metric definition is unspecified. Is that gross transaction value flowing through the platform? Platform fees? ARR? The $5M raise is inconsistent with $12M in genuine recurring revenue -- a company with that kind of traction at eighteen months post-founding would command a significantly larger round and wouldn't need to describe itself through an a16z Speedrun portal. I've seen enough company descriptions conflate transaction volume with revenue to flag this as a verification problem, not a traction signal.

The market structure question kills my enthusiasm before I get to anything else. "AI + real estate investing" is not nonobvious -- it's one of the most obvious application categories of the current AI wave. EliseAI has $392M raised and $100M+ ARR. Roofstock has $373M and a $1.94B valuation. Cherre has $130M for CRE data management. The proptech market is saturated with well-funded players, and every one of them is integrating AI capabilities. When I backed Harvey, legal AI had been a graveyard for a decade because the technology literally couldn't meet the precision bar -- there was a specific, measurable capability threshold being crossed. Smart Bricks doesn't have an equivalent structural catalyst. LLMs analyzing property listings and underwriting documents is valuable, but it's not a threshold event that unlocks a previously impossible market. The "why now" reduces to "AI got better," which is true but consensus. Every proptech founder pitching today says the same thing.

Smart Bricks describes itself as "intelligence infrastructure" -- the company claims it "has built the stack itself" rather than building on top of existing tech. But the actual product is a consumer-facing investment platform with property listings, golden visa programs, and end-to-end transaction support. That's an application, not infrastructure. Compare to Stripe, which built a payments API that thousands of businesses integrated into their core workflows, creating compounding switching costs. Smart Bricks doesn't have an API layer. Other businesses don't build on top of it. Investors use it as a tool, and when a better tool appears -- or when a well-funded competitor like EliseAI expands into investor-facing workflows -- there's no structural lock-in preventing migration. The "1M+ proprietary and public data feeds" is a developing asset, but data aggregation is a feature, not a moat, until the proprietary transaction data creates a learning loop that competitors can't replicate. At pre-seed, that loop hasn't started compounding.

The single-miracle test is where Smart Bricks fails most clearly. The company operates across Dubai, London, New York, and Miami -- four distinct regulatory regimes for real estate transactions, each requiring separate licensing, disclosure rules, and consumer protection compliance. That's not one miracle, it's four regulatory miracles running in parallel, plus the technology miracle of building models accurate enough for six-figure investment decisions across heterogeneous markets, plus the distribution miracle of getting HNW investors to trust an AI platform with their capital allocation. I count at least three to four independent breakthroughs required simultaneously. Mohamed Mohamed's background at Blackstone and Goldman gives him the network to approach institutional investors, but network-based distribution doesn't scale the way product-led growth does, and it doesn't solve the multi-jurisdiction compliance burden.

Mohamed's credentials deserve a fair reading. Blackstone, Goldman Sachs, McKinsey's AI practice, BCG, and angel investments in 30+ startups -- this is a founder who understands both institutional capital allocation and AI deployment. The advisory role to ADQ ($200B sovereign wealth fund) is a genuine distribution asset. Forbes 30 Under 30 Middle East. These are real. But they're advisory and investing credentials, not operating credentials. Mohamed hasn't scaled a company through the 10-to-1,000 transition. He's a sole founder -- no technical co-founder identified for a company positioning as an "applied AI lab." The 20-person team is substantial for pre-seed but raises questions about burn rate relative to the $5M raise, and no named technical leaders appear in any public source. For an AI-native platform processing 100,000+ listings daily, the absence of a visible technical co-founder is a gap I can't ignore.

The bull case requires believing three things simultaneously: the $12M revenue figure is real and represents platform fees (not transaction volume), the multi-geography strategy creates a cross-border investing moat rather than a compliance burden, and Mohamed's institutional finance network provides a distribution channel that compounds faster than competitors can replicate. If all three are true, Smart Bricks could become the Bloomberg terminal for individual real estate investors -- a genuine infrastructure layer for a $47B proptech market growing at 16% CAGR. The angel investors from OpenAI, Anthropic, DeepMind, Airbnb, and Blackstone suggest that sophisticated people have conviction. But this bull case requires multiple things to be simultaneously true with no independent verification of any of them, which is exactly the compounding-small-odds problem my framework is designed to filter against.

Smart Bricks reminds me less of Harvey (a company attacking a genuinely stuck market with a specific capability threshold) and more of the pattern I see in obvious-market applications: talented founder, real domain knowledge, but building at the application layer in a crowded category without a structural timing advantage. The Wish parallel is instructive -- consumer-facing marketplace riding a platform wave without infrastructure positioning or margin architecture to provide structural protection when growth slows. I pass. The two dimensions most decisive: Nonobvious Market Timing (this is a consensus category with well-funded incumbents, scoring near the bottom of my range) and Single-Miracle Viability (four geographies, unproven AI accuracy for high-stakes decisions, and trust-based distribution each requiring independent breakthroughs).
