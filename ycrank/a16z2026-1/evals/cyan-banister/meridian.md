# Meridian -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

"Not some B.S SaaS company or AI wrapper." That's my partner Lee Jacobs's line, and it's the first thing that surfaced when I read Meridian's dossier. The core technology stack — RAG over documents, LLM-based generation, template-based output — is acknowledged in the dossier itself as "reproducible with off-the-shelf components." Meridian's founders frame the differentiation as a behavioral data layer that accumulates over time, and that's a real thesis, but it's aspirational at four months in. Glean is sitting at $200M ARR with $610M in funding. Kenley graduated YC S24 targeting the exact same buyer. Microsoft Copilot is embedded in the Office suite every consulting firm already uses. When I run the ten-year question — where does this go if everything works? — I see a well-executed vertical SaaS company for consulting, not infrastructure that transforms what's possible. The surface isn't hiding something infrastructural underneath. The surface is the product.

The biographical forge is where I always start, and Meridian's founders give me pause. Kashyap Nathan dropped out of Georgia Tech, worked as an FDE at Palantir, and did something called "ChatGPT for Gov" at OpenAI. Christopher Farrington is a third-year Georgia Tech student whose LinkedIn still reads "Incoming SWE Intern - Deloitte." Shanay Champaneri is a Cornell undergrad who interned at Palantir and Robinhood. These are credentialed, technically sharp young people — but when I ask my opening question, "tell me your story, not the business," what I hear is "I worked at Palantir and OpenAI and saw a market opportunity in consulting knowledge management." That's not a biographical forge. That's career optimization. Chris's Deloitte internship gives a window into consulting, but spending a summer interning is categorically different from spending a decade watching institutional knowledge hemorrhage from a firm you helped build. Nobody on this team was forged by the problem they're solving. The problem didn't find them — they identified it from a list.

Consulting knowledge management is a real pain point, but it is not an invisible system. Every managing partner at every consulting firm in the world knows they lose expertise when people leave. McKinsey, Bain, and BCG have invested hundreds of millions over decades into knowledge management platforms. The $23.2B market exists because this problem is well-understood, well-funded, and well-served — not because it's hidden. There is no information asymmetry being disrupted here, no opaque structure extracting value from people who don't understand it. When I backed Affirm, credit card terms were genuinely invisible to consumers — the system was designed to obscure the cost. When I backed Ownwell, property tax assessments were opaque by institutional design. Meridian is selling productivity software to sophisticated enterprise buyers who already know exactly what their problem is. That's a fine business. It's not my kind of bet.

The $700K in contracted revenue at four months — if real — would be remarkable execution from founders this young. Managing partners referring Meridian "unprompted" and competitive bake-off wins against Databricks and Palantir are strong signals of product quality. But every one of these data points is self-reported from the a16z Speedrun portal with zero independent verification. No press coverage. No named customers. No third-party validation. And then there's the YC departure — accepted to F25, then left the batch with no public explanation. That's not automatically disqualifying, but combined with the unverified traction claims, it creates a pattern where the fundraising narrative may be outpacing verifiable substance. I've been burned by this pattern before. IRL fabricated user metrics. The story was better than the building.

The strongest bull case for Meridian: Palantir's Forward Deployed Engineering model is genuinely the closest thing in tech to consulting work, and Kashyap Nathan lived inside that model. If the $700K is real and the data moat compounds, Meridian could become the Carta of consulting — the switching costs from organized knowledge, configured templates, and tracked client relationships would make departure painful after enough engagements. The consulting vertical is large enough to sustain a significant standalone company even without horizontal expansion. Young founders with this level of early traction have defied age-based skepticism before. But for me to lean in, I would need to see the biographical inevitability — what happened to these founders that made this problem find *them* — and I would need to see evidence that the behavioral data layer creates genuine infrastructure rather than a feature that Glean adds in a quarter. Neither is present in this dossier.

Meridian is a competent team building a consensus product in a transparent market. The founders are smart but not forged. The space is crowded, not contrarian. The system being improved is visible, not hidden. I wish Kashyap, Chris, and Shanay well — they may build a real company here — but this isn't a Long Journey bet. The two dimensions that lock this: Founder's Biographical Forge (no personal crucible connecting any founder to this problem) and Contrarian Conviction (enterprise AI for consulting is the opposite of making investors uncomfortable — it's the pitch deck VCs are hoping to see right now).
