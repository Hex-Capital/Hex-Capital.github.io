# DroneTector -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's my problem with DroneTector: counter-drone is no longer weird. After Gatwick, after Ukraine's 1,600 drone attacks per month, after Red Sea shipping costs doubled -- every defense investor, every government procurement officer, every VC with a "hard tech" thesis has this category circled in red. DroneShield is a public company with AU$221 million in cash. Axon acquired Dedrone. Echodyne raised $195 million from Bill Gates. When I look at the competitive landscape here, I see a market where consensus has already formed. The most promising companies are not popular, and counter-drone technology is very popular right now. That doesn't mean DroneTector can't win -- it means the weirdness premium that drives my best investments has been arbitraged away by geopolitical events that made the threat obvious to everyone.

I want to hear these founders' stories -- not the business, not the radar specs. What I see in the dossier are three PhDs who built impressive technology during their academic research. Matthew Moore spent his doctorate building and testing 94 GHz radar for drone micro-Doppler detection. That's deep, specialized expertise, and I respect it. But the biographical thread I'm listening for -- the personal hardship that makes this founder's commitment to this specific problem unshakeable through years of crisis -- I don't hear it. These are brilliant researchers spinning university IP into a company, which is a perfectly valid origin story, but it's not the kind of story that gives me that jolt of intuitive energy. When I backed Contraline, the founder had spent years in academic research on male contraception that everyone else dismissed. The key difference is that Contraline was in a stigmatized, unpopular category. DroneTector is in the hottest defense category of the decade.

The technical differentiation is real and worth acknowledging. Millimetre-wave radar at 94 GHz with micro-Doppler signature classification is genuinely different from what the funded competitors are doing -- Dedrone relies on RF detection that can't see autonomous drones, Echodyne operates at lower frequencies, Robin Radar pivoted from birds using X-band. Moore is one of a small number of people globally with hands-on experience building this exact system, and the peer-reviewed publications create a credibility floor that most pre-seed hardware startups lack. If I were evaluating purely on technical moat and founder-market fit in the narrow sense of "does this person understand the physics," the answer is clearly yes. NATO DIANA and DASA backing add institutional validation that matters in defense procurement -- this team can navigate regulated, complex spaces, which reminds me of what drew me to Anduril.

The bull case I have to take seriously: this could be the precision tool that wins in a market full of blunt instruments. If existing radar systems genuinely cannot detect $50 nano drones, and if the battlefield keeps proving that nano drones are the primary threat, then DroneTector's ground-up design for this specific problem could make them the company that NATO, the UK MOD, and airport authorities standardize on. Defense markets reward purpose-built solutions, and first-mover advantage in a specific detection capability can compound through proprietary signature databases and procurement lock-in. The Anduril parallel is instructive -- Palmer Luckey built purpose-designed technology for a defense establishment using legacy systems, and the market rewarded specificity over generality. If DroneTector can survive the procurement cycles on limited capital and get deployed before Echodyne or Robin Radar extend into millimetre-wave frequencies, the data moat could become insurmountable.

But I keep coming back to what concerns me. Thomas Doherty was CEO of Quantum Fabrix -- a Paul Graham-backed quantum hardware company -- until a new CEO was appointed in October 2025, just months before DroneTector's YC batch. That timeline makes me uncomfortable. I've learned from HQ Trivia and GameCrush that I need to trust what I see when something feels off about founder dynamics. Maybe Doherty made a clean break and is fully committed. Maybe there are residual obligations. I don't know, and at pre-seed I'm betting on people above everything else. Three PhD co-founders from overlapping St Andrews circles have a shared intellectual foundation, which is good, but I'd want to understand how they fight -- not whether they can collaborate on research, but whether they can survive a cash crisis when a defense contract slips by eighteen months.

This is a pass for me, though not because the technology isn't impressive -- it clearly is. I pass because the market has become consensus, the founder stories don't give me the biographical grit signal I weight most heavily, and the competitive landscape includes well-capitalized companies that could extend into this frequency range. When I invest my own money, I'm looking for the misfits building something that makes other investors uncomfortable. DroneTector makes investors excited, and that's precisely what gives me pause.
