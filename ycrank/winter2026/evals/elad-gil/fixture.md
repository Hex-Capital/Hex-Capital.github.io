# Fixture -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most striking thing about Fixture is the mismatch between the founder team and the market they've chosen. Matt Riley and Quin Hoxie co-founded Swiftype through YC W12, raised $20M, executed an acquisition to Elastic, and spent years running search infrastructure at public-company scale. Hoxie was a Distinguished Engineer at Elastic; Riley ran the entire Search business unit as GVP. They have 10+ years of working together through a startup, an acquisition, and a large org. That co-founder relationship is battle-tested in a way that eliminates one of the three things that kill pre-PMF companies. Riley's stint as a YC Visiting Partner for two batches means he's been sitting across the table from dozens of startups struggling with their GTM stack -- he's experienced the problem from the customer side. This is a founding team that belongs in a top-decile company. The question is whether they've picked a market structure that gives them room to build one.

CRM for startups is not a non-obvious market. It's one of the most consensus categories in enterprise SaaS right now. Attio has raised $116M and explicitly brands itself as "AI-native CRM." Clay is at a $3.1B valuation with roughly $100M in projected revenue. Folk, Affinity, and a dozen others are all competing for adjacent segments. HubSpot gives away a free CRM tier that is the default entry point for most early-stage startups. When I look at my best investments -- PagerDuty, Gusto, Checkr -- they all entered markets where other investors said "too boring" or "too small." Nobody is saying that about AI-native CRM. Everybody is funding it. That's the opposite of the structural setup I look for. The $88B global CRM TAM on the slide deck is exactly the kind of static market sizing I warn about -- it doesn't tell you anything about whether a new entrant can capture share in a segment that already has well-capitalized incumbents and well-capitalized challengers.

The bull case is more interesting than the surface positioning suggests. There's a specific structural bet embedded in Fixture's "agent-native" and "use from any AI tool" framing that most investors -- including the ones funding Attio and Clay -- might be misreading. If the primary interface for GTM workflows shifts from a human clicking through a CRM UI to an AI agent consuming CRM data via API, then the architecture of the CRM changes fundamentally. Attio is building a better UI. Fixture appears to be betting that the UI becomes secondary -- that the CRM becomes an infrastructure layer consumed by agents, similar to how Stripe became an infrastructure layer consumed by developers rather than a payments dashboard consumed by finance teams. Riley and Hoxie's search infrastructure background at Elastic is directly relevant here: building the indexing, retrieval, and context-aggregation layer that AI agents need is a search problem, not a traditional CRM problem. If that thesis is correct, Fixture is building for a paradigm that hasn't arrived yet while competitors are optimizing for the current one. That's the Swiftype DNA showing -- they built search-as-a-service before most companies understood they needed it.

But the "if" in that paragraph is doing a lot of work. The agent-native CRM thesis requires that agentic workflows actually become the dominant GTM interface -- and we don't have evidence that's happening at scale yet. It's a plausible extrapolation of the LLM capability curve, but it's not the kind of specific threshold-crossing I saw with Harvey, where I could map GPT scaling laws and predict when model fidelity would reach legal-grade accuracy within 12-18 months. Agent-driven GTM is more speculative and more dependent on broader ecosystem shifts. Meanwhile, Fixture's "zero data lock-in" philosophy directly undermines the switching-cost moat that makes CRM businesses durable. If the data is portable, the entire moat rests on the AI agent layer being structurally better than what competitors build -- and that's a thin layer on top of rapidly commoditizing LLM capabilities. That starts to look like a product that could be replicated rather than a distribution channel that compounds.

The product-to-distribution trajectory has structural potential because CRM is inherently a platform category -- Salesforce proved that a customer relationship database becomes a distribution channel for adjacent products. But Fixture is pre-beta, competing against companies with hundreds of millions in funding, and its stated commitment to data portability makes the expansion path harder, not easier. The single miracle here needs to be something like "agent-native CRM becomes the obvious architecture before Attio or HubSpot retrofit agent capabilities onto their existing products" -- and that's a race against well-resourced competitors who are already shipping AI features, not a miracle being delivered externally like GPT-4 was for Harvey.

I respect the founders deeply. Riley and Hoxie have built, shipped, scaled, and exited together. They have the search/data infrastructure chops to build a genuinely differentiated context layer. Riley's YC network creates a warm distribution channel into exactly the right customer segment. In a less crowded market, this team would get my highest conviction. But I've seen great founders get taken out by market structure too many times. The startup CRM segment has multiple well-funded competitors, a free incumbent tier from HubSpot, and a positioning ("AI-native CRM") that's shared by at least three other funded companies. The agent-native thesis is the one non-consensus angle here, and it's worth watching -- but it's too speculative for me to underwrite at this stage without evidence that the paradigm is actually shifting.
