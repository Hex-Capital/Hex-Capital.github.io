# Corelayer -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The competitive dynamics in this market are what I'd examine before anything else. incident.io raised $62M in April 2025 explicitly to build AI agents that resolve incidents. PagerDuty ($441M ARR) and Datadog ($30B market cap) are both adding AI capabilities to their existing platforms. Shoreline.io was absorbed by Nvidia, BigPanda by Velocity, FireHydrant by Freshworks -- five acquisitions in eighteen months. When I write about "market-ending moves," this is what I mean: the incumbents and well-funded challengers are not sleeping. Corelayer is entering a space where multiple well-resourced players have publicly declared intent to build the same autonomous debugging capability. That's the opposite of the structural vacancy that made Anduril compelling after Google walked away from Project Maven.

The "why now" question here is straightforward but undifferentiating. LLMs crossed the threshold for reasoning about logs, code, and data simultaneously sometime in 2024-2025. That's real -- two years ago, an AI agent that could autonomously debug a production data pipeline was a research project. Today it's buildable. But this capability threshold is available to every engineering team with API access to frontier models. incident.io, Rootly, and any Datadog product team can wire up the same LLM capabilities. When the "why now" is a broadly available technology improvement rather than a specific structural shift unique to your approach, it creates a timing advantage measured in months, not years. The AIOps market at $14.6B growing to $36B is consensus -- every investor with a pulse knows AI operations tooling is large and growing. This is not a non-obvious market. Nobody dismisses AI-powered debugging as "too boring" or "too niche" in 2026.

Where Corelayer gets more interesting is the regulated-industry positioning. Confidential compute, on-prem deployment, PII masking, zero-data retention -- these aren't features, they're compliance barriers that most AI startups don't bother building. I know from building Color Health in a CLIA-regulated environment that compliance infrastructure is genuinely miserable to construct and genuinely effective as a moat once established. Financial services firms processing hundreds of billions of rows daily cannot send production data to a cloud-hosted AI agent -- their compliance teams would kill the deal in week one. If Corelayer is the only AI debugging platform that can deploy inside a bank's security perimeter with hardware-backed confidential compute, that's a real wedge. The Goldman Sachs backgrounds of both founders aren't just resume lines -- they understand procurement cycles, security review processes, and the specific pain of debugging data pipelines at that scale. Getting SOC 2 Type I certification as a two-person pre-seed company is a non-trivial execution signal that suggests they're serious about this wedge.

The problem is that this plan requires multiple miracles stacked sequentially. Miracle one: AI debugging agents need to be accurate enough that regulated enterprises trust them with production data analysis -- "good enough with human review" works in legal (Harvey's model) but debugging production systems at scale demands higher reliability thresholds. Miracle two: a two-person team needs to execute enterprise sales cycles with Fortune 100 financial institutions, which typically involve six-to-twelve month security reviews, proof-of-concept deployments, and procurement approvals. Miracle three: they need to build and maintain on-prem deployment infrastructure across diverse customer environments. Miracle four: they need to outrun incident.io's $62M war chest targeting the same AI agent capabilities. That's four low-probability events compounding. When I see a startup that needs multiple sequential breakthroughs, the math doesn't work regardless of how compelling each individual miracle sounds.

The strongest bull case is that Corelayer becomes the Gusto of production debugging for regulated industries -- a "boring but critical" infrastructure play that seems niche but actually serves every data-heavy enterprise that can't use cloud-hosted alternatives. If the data-layer debugging focus (pipeline anomalies, data quality, not just infrastructure outages) proves to be a distinct enough category that incumbents don't naturally cover it, and if the on-prem and confidential compute requirements create enough friction that incident.io and Datadog can't easily follow, then the Goldman Sachs network could land three to five design partners that validate the wedge. The ROI framing -- 100 engineers saving 14% of their time, $2.8M in annual savings -- suggests a real budget line if the product delivers. Regulated industries adopt slowly but stay forever, and the switching costs of an AI agent that has learned your specific data schemas and failure patterns are substantial. For this to work, though, every one of those miracles has to land, and the founders haven't yet demonstrated any public traction to suggest the first miracle is within reach.

The product-to-distribution trajectory is the other structural concern. The 30+ integrations are connections into existing tool ecosystems -- they don't create a distribution channel. Stripe's API wasn't just a payment tool; it was a developer relationship through which you could distribute Atlas, Billing, and Treasury. What's Corelayer's equivalent? If they own the debugging layer, can they expand into data quality monitoring, compliance automation, or incident post-mortem analytics? Possibly, but the dossier shows no evidence the founders have articulated this multi-product vision. At pre-seed, I don't penalize heavily for not having a platform roadmap, but the product as described is a feature that any observability platform could add -- and several have explicitly announced they will.
