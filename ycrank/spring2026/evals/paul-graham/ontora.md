# Ontora -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Ontora is that "AI replaces management consulting" is exactly the kind of idea that sounds good to everyone. If you said it at a dinner party in San Francisco right now, people would nod along. That's the tell. The best startup ideas generate the opposite reaction — confusion, discomfort, people explaining to you why it won't work. Nobody is going to tell you that automating McKinsey sounds like a bad idea. Which means either everyone is already working on it, or the actual hard problem is hiding somewhere the founders haven't shown me yet. When I look at the dossier, I see a company leading with a trillion-dollar TAM number for the global consulting market. In my experience, that framing correlates almost perfectly with ideas that were chosen from a menu rather than discovered through pain.

Maximilian Arnold is clearly smart. CODE University, Stanford, ETH Zürich — and more importantly, he built CoAnalyst at Porsche, an agentic RAG assistant for SAP procurement data that was projected to save millions across Volkswagen Group. That's real. He built something technically analogous to what Ontora does, inside a large enterprise, and saw firsthand how slow and painful organizational data extraction is. This puts him a cut above someone who read a Gartner report about automatable consulting tasks. But building an internal AI tool with Porsche's resources and selling enterprise software from a three-person startup are fundamentally different activities. At Porsche, he had the data, the users, and the budget already in the building. Ontora has to convince procurement departments to hand over their CRM, email, and meeting transcripts to a company they've never heard of. That's the schlep he hasn't demonstrated he can handle yet.

The Vertiv testimonial is the most interesting data point in the dossier — a strategy manager at a public company saying "what used to take our operating team months of interviews and analysis, Ontora delivered in a single afternoon." If that's real and representative, it's a genuinely powerful signal. A 100x speedup for a specific enterprise workflow is the kind of thing that makes people reach for their wallets. But one testimonial on your own website doesn't tell me whether this was a paid engagement, a demo, a favor for a friend, or a real deployment. I'd want to call Julien Kang and ask: did you pay for it? Would you buy it again? Are you telling your peers about it? The gap between "impressive demo" and "product I'd stake my budget on" is where most enterprise AI companies die.

Here's the bull case I've genuinely considered: Arnold lived inside the enterprise data problem at Porsche and built the precursor technology. The consulting industry's business model — billing human hours — structurally prevents incumbents from automating themselves, which is exactly the kind of incentive misalignment that creates openings for startups. And Celonis, the $13 billion gorilla in process mining, is built on structured event logs from ERP systems, not unstructured conversations and documents. If Ontora's approach of extracting organizational knowledge from messy, human-generated data actually works reliably, they're in genuinely different territory. That's a reasonable argument. What I can't get past is the absence of any evidence that these founders have the particular kind of scrappiness this market demands. Enterprise sales at pre-seed without a GTM team, without visible revenue, with a ghost third co-founder I can't find anywhere online — that's not stealth mode, that's not having done the hard work yet. David Korn has no confirmed LinkedIn, no Twitter, no GitHub. I need to know who's on the team before I invest in them.

The co-founder dynamics trouble me independently of David Korn's invisibility. Arnold studied in Berlin, Stanford, and Zürich; Iwanowitsch studied at St. Gallen. I can't find a shared employer, a shared university, a shared project — nothing that tells me how these people know each other or why they decided to build together. The best founding teams have a history, even a short one. They've pulled an all-nighter on a hackathon project, or built something together at a previous company, or been friends since childhood. When I can't see the connective tissue between co-founders, I worry about what happens when things get genuinely hard — and in enterprise AI sales, things will get hard fast.

What I'd need to see to change my mind: evidence that the Vertiv engagement was real and repeatable, a second or third enterprise customer showing the same reaction, and most importantly, some story from these founders that reveals the animal quality — something where they hacked around a constraint, shipped something ugly but functional, or convinced someone to use their product through sheer force of determination rather than a polished demo. Right now I see credentials and a clean website, and those are the wrong signals. The best founders at this stage are too busy acquiring users to make their website look this good.
