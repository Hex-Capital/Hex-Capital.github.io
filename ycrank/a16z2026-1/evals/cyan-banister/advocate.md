# Advocate -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

There's a real invisible system here, and I want to name it plainly: CMS has told healthcare facilities "you can bill $62 a month per patient for chronic care management under CPT 99490," and the facilities can't capture it because the documentation requirements were designed by people who've never worked a shift in a skilled nursing facility. Care coordinators — the people scheduling follow-ups, making referrals, running social determinant screenings — are doing reimbursable work every day and leaving hundreds of dollars per patient on the table because the coding system is opaque by design. That pattern lights up my entire framework. When I was young and navigating systems built to extract value from people who didn't understand them, nobody handed me a manual. The care coordinator at a behavioral health center in rural Ohio is in the same position — the money exists, the rules say she's earned it, but the system is architected so she can't reach it. Advocate's thesis is to make that invisible money visible. The structural parallel to what Affirm did with credit card opacity is real: take a financial system where the rules exist but are deliberately impenetrable, and build a layer that makes them actionable for ordinary participants.

Andrew Baran was raised by a mother who worked as a care coordinator. That's not nothing — he watched someone he loves do this work and not get compensated for it. But when I ask my first question — tell me your story, not the business — the rest of the biographical picture reads differently. Columbia class president, Oxbridge scholar, McKinsey due diligence, Radian Capital healthcare investing, part-time at Citadel during school. That's an exceptionally credentialed person who identified a market gap through professional proximity and family connection. It's not the same as someone who was broken by the system and rebuilt themselves around fixing it. The Leilo co-founding claim adds friction: public sources from Shoutout LA and BevNet identify Sol Broady and Ethan Blattman as Leilo's founders, not Baran. I don't know the full story, but unverifiable claims on a founding team's profile create exactly the kind of credibility question that makes me slow down. Justinas Banys brings genuine healthcare data engineering from Garner Health — a $1.35B care navigation company — which is directly relevant domain experience. The team has the right professional ingredients. What I don't feel is inevitability.

The space itself doesn't make anyone shift in their chairs. Healthcare billing optimization is one of the most well-funded categories in healthtech: CodaMetrix has raised $109M, Waystar is a public company doing $1.1B in revenue, Sully.ai has $35M and is building full-stack AI hospital employees. Advocate's niche — care coordination billing at post-acute facilities — is underserved precisely because it's low-ASP and fragmented, which may keep Waystar out but also limits the scale of the opportunity. The core technology stack — speech-to-text plus LLM-based code mapping against CPT databases — is built entirely on commodity components. Abridge and DeepScribe already capture clinical voice data and are extending into coding workflows. Adding care coordination codes to their pipeline is a product sprint, not a company. The dossier's own analysis identifies this commoditization risk explicitly, and I see no proprietary data, no network effect, no regulatory moat at this stage that would prevent a well-funded incumbent from replicating the workflow in a quarter. My failures — HQ Trivia, GameCrush — all lacked structural defensibility, and I've learned to weight that signal heavily even when the product thesis is sound.

The strongest bull case for Advocate is this: the timing window created by expanded CMS care coordination codes meeting 2024-2025 ASR and LLM quality thresholds is genuinely narrow, the sales channel (facility administrators at skilled nursing homes and home health agencies) is one that enterprise RCM companies don't cover, and the ROI pitch is trivially compelling — pure found money with no workflow changes. If Advocate can land ten facilities, accumulate proprietary billing-outcome training data, and build audit trail switching costs before Abridge or CodaMetrix notice this niche, the data moat could compound. The CMS expansion of reimbursable codes (CCM, PCM, behavioral health integration) is a real regulatory tailwind. And the cascading impact is genuine: more billing capture at skilled nursing facilities means more resources for care coordination serving elderly and low-income patients, which is the kind of second-order access expansion I believe constitutes real impact investing.

But I keep coming back to what's missing. No traction signals of any kind — no revenue, no LOIs, no prototype usage, no social media presence, a website that redirects to a different domain showing minimal content. The branding choice of "Advocate" in healthcare is a discoverability disaster given Advocate Health's $27B presence. The regulatory exposure is material: automated billing packet generation sits directly in False Claims Act territory, and coding errors that lead to upcoding could expose facilities to serious liability. These aren't disqualifying at pre-seed, but combined with the thin biographical forge and commodity technology risk, they add up to a bet where the invisible system thesis is strong but the founders and moat haven't earned my conviction. The dimension that most clearly separates Advocate from a Long Journey investment is the founder forge — Baran's path from Columbia to McKinsey to healthcare VC to startup, with a family connection providing the problem insight, is competent and motivated but not the biographical crucible that drives my highest-conviction checks. The dimension that almost pulls me back is the invisible system — CMS billing opacity exploiting care coordinators at understaffed facilities is exactly the kind of hidden tax I was built to recognize.
