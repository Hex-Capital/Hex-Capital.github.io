# PartyHat -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Jarret Cuisinier spent years as VP of Marketing and Operations at Avast, a company with 400 million consumer cybersecurity users that sold for $8.1 billion. That's genuine specific knowledge -- he watched from inside the machine how consumer security products acquire, convert, retain, and churn users at planetary scale. The question I keep returning to with PartyHat is whether that knowledge has been productized into something durable, or whether it's been deployed to wrap commodity APIs in a better TikTok presence. The answer, from everything I can see, is the latter. VPN infrastructure is white-label. Dark web monitoring comes from off-the-shelf APIs like SpyCloud and Have I Been Pwned. Scam call databases are commercially licensable. PartyHat's $2.99/month subscription assembles components any well-funded team could replicate in a quarter. Cuisinier knows this market -- but knowing a market is not the same as having built something only you could build.

The leverage architecture is where PartyHat breaks down most severely under my framework. A subscription software product has the shape of permissionless leverage -- code that serves users while you sleep. But when the code is largely rented, the economics change. Every VPN session, every dark web scan, every scam database query carries a per-unit cost to a third-party provider. The zero-marginal-cost promise that makes Notion or Perplexity structurally powerful does not apply here. PartyHat is closer to the AI wrapper archetype I consistently pass on: assembling someone else's capabilities behind a UI and hoping brand carries the weight. The dossier itself concludes that "defensibility depends on brand execution and distribution efficiency rather than proprietary technology." Brand is not permissionless leverage. Brand requires constant capital investment -- TikTok ads, Instagram content, influencer partnerships. Capital is permissioned leverage, dependent on fundraising, subject to diminishing returns. When I invested in Notion, the product WAS the distribution -- templates spread virally because using the product created shareable artifacts. PartyHat's product is a VPN that works. That doesn't spread.

The principal-agent signal is ambiguous. Cuisinier left a comfortable VP seat to build this, which shows risk tolerance. But the trajectory reads like an experienced operator who identified a market gap and assembled a team to fill it -- not someone who couldn't stop thinking about how cybersecurity should work differently. Myneni's background in VFX (*The Day After Tomorrow*, *Hulk*) and indie game development (*Age of Rivals*) demonstrates strong full-stack engineering, but nothing connects him to the cybersecurity problem specifically. He's a capable generalist brought in to build. Both founders' LinkedIn headlines read "Building something new?" -- the language of people excited about founding, not about the problem. I worry this is the pattern I've learned to avoid: "if you're more passionate about founding a business than the business itself, you can fall into a ten year trap."

The bull case deserves honest examination. Cuisinier's insight might be genuinely contrarian: every incumbent in consumer security -- Norton, Avast, Aura -- markets through fear and complexity because their existing customer base (older, trust-oriented) demands it. Gen Digital's $4.73 billion in revenue comes from customers who respond to "your identity is at risk" messaging. Pivoting to irreverent, youth-targeted branding ("Always Wear Protection") would cannibalize that positioning. This is a real principal-agent problem at the incumbent level, structurally similar to Google's inability to cannibalize ad-supported search. If PartyHat captures the Gen Z cybersecurity consumer before Norton figures out how to launch a sub-brand, the 2-person team at $2.99/month could reach meaningful scale. The 1,000+ users and 4.7-star rating across 106 App Store reviews show early product-market pull, even if the numbers are modest.

But the structural problem remains: brand moats in consumer software are fragile and capital-intensive to maintain. Gen Digital just acquired MoneyLion for $1 billion to bundle financial wellness with cybersecurity. Nord Security has 15 million+ VPN subscribers and launched NordProtect as a bolt-on. Aura raised $898.8 million and generates $385.3 million in revenue. PartyHat is bringing a brand strategy to a capital war. The compounding test fails -- a VPN subscription doesn't get better the more people use it. There's no data flywheel, no network effect, no knowledge accumulation. Each month PartyHat must re-earn its subscribers' attention against incumbents who can bundle more features (credit monitoring, insurance, financial tools) at similar price points. Cuisinier's specific knowledge of the Avast playbook is real but replicable -- any senior hire from Gen Digital's marketing team brings comparable understanding.

Two dimensions locked my score. Permissionless Leverage Architecture scores low because PartyHat rents its core technology stack from commodity providers -- the subscription wrapper doesn't transform rented components into zero-marginal-cost leverage. Specific Knowledge Productization scores at the lower end of its positive range because Cuisinier's Avast experience is genuine domain expertise, but it's marketing and distribution knowledge applied to assembled commodity technology, not technical knowledge productized into a defensible product. PartyHat has a real founder in a real market, which prevents a Pass verdict. But the absence of proprietary leverage -- the thing that makes a small team's output compound rather than deplete -- keeps this in Neutral territory. I'd need to see evidence of proprietary AI threat detection or a distribution mechanic where the product itself generates organic growth before writing a check.
