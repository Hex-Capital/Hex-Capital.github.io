# Samora AI -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the fundamental problem with Samora AI: Kartik Sawhney might be one of the most impressive founders I've seen in this batch -- Stanford CS, Microsoft's AI research division, Queen's Young Leaders Award, built I-Stem to serve half a million people across 80+ countries, and did all of it while blind from birth. That's not just "sane" -- that's exceptional operator credibility. But the idea? Multilingual voice AI agents? This is quite possibly the single most crowded, most consensus category in all of tech right now. Bland AI has raised $65 million. Synthflow, $30 million. Vapi, $20 million plus. Retell AI is processing ten million minutes per month. When I look for a sane person with an insane idea, the "insane" part has to provoke genuine skepticism or confusion from most investors. Nobody is confused by "AI voice agents for customer support." Every VC on Sand Hill Road is tripping over these pitches. This is a sane person with a profoundly sane idea, and that pairing doesn't produce the non-consensus returns I'm looking for.

The structural issue goes deeper than crowding. My "cherry on top" test asks: would this business exist without AI? Samora fails that test completely. The entire product IS AI -- specifically, OpenAI's AI. Their stack is Whisper for transcription, GPT-4/5 for reasoning, and OpenAI's TTS for voice output. Every component is a commercial API that any competitor can access tomorrow morning. When the foundation model providers improve multilingual support -- and they will, it's on every roadmap -- Samora's current differentiation evaporates. This isn't a painting company with a tech layer or a parking infrastructure play where software makes physical operations more efficient. This is pure software built on someone else's protocol, which is the exact category I've been arguing publicly is getting destroyed. "Software companies that had moats are learning they aren't moats anymore" -- and this company's moat is thinner than most because it doesn't even own the underlying technology.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement, though, because the founder-market fit here is unusually specific. The I-Stem team spent years building technology for underserved, multilingual populations with disabilities across 80+ countries. That experience -- handling dialects, code-switching, accessibility constraints, working with institutions like UNICEF and the Government of India -- maps directly onto Samora's hardest problem. And the institutional customer base is interesting: UNICEF, GOI, Signify aren't the kind of buyers that Western developer-first voice AI platforms are chasing. If Samora could become the trusted, managed voice infrastructure layer for governments and international development organizations globally -- organizations that need compliance, multilingual coverage across obscure dialects, and human escalation -- that's a wedge competitors won't pursue because the sales cycles are long and the margins look ugly to Silicon Valley. For this to work, Samora would need to parlay those institutional relationships into something resembling lock-in: regulatory approval processes, custom dialect models trained on proprietary call data, deep integration into government procurement systems. $1.1 million in revenue (even from an unreliable source like Getlatka) and a million calls processed suggest there's real demand. But the path from "trusted institutional vendor" to "massive business" is narrow and slow.

What kills this for me is the protocol-versus-application question. Samora is an application built on OpenAI's platform. It creates no new primitive. It establishes no layer that other businesses build on. It generates no compounding network effects. When I backed Venmo, I was backing a new payment primitive -- a protocol-layer shift in how money moves. When we seeded Solana, it was a new Layer 1 that an entire ecosystem would build on top of. Samora is a managed service wrapper around commodity APIs, and the "infinity story" is capped by the fact that every improvement in the underlying models simultaneously helps every competitor. The narrative can expand -- "voice infrastructure for every non-English organization" -- but the company's structural claim on that narrative doesn't strengthen with scale the way a protocol's does. Compare this to Birchbox: impressive founders, real early traction, massive addressable market, but the model was trivially replicable and the data never compounded into lock-in. Samora's 1M-call dataset might seem like a moat, but Retell is already at 10M+ minutes per month. Data advantages in commoditized AI don't scale the way founders hope.

I have real respect for this team and I'd want to know Kartik Sawhney -- his personal story and operator credentials are remarkable. But remarkable founders in consensus markets buying commodity AI APIs is not my bet. I'd rather fund a team with half his pedigree attacking a problem nobody else thinks is a real market.
