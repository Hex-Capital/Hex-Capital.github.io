# Carrot Labs -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the competitive landscape, and it tells me everything about the timing. Braintrust raised $80M in February 2026. Arize has $131M. LangChain/LangSmith sits at a $1.25B valuation. When I invested in Uber at a $4M valuation, ride-sharing was illegal in most cities -- the consensus was that it was impossible. When I backed Bitcoin in 2014, serious people called it a joke. The asymmetric payoff comes from being right when the crowd is wrong. Carrot Labs is entering a space where the crowd has already decided it's right -- over $500M in venture capital has flooded into AI evaluation and observability in the past eighteen months. "Anyone in this business who's chasing hot markets gets killed." The continuous optimization angle is a differentiation within a consensus category, not a contrarian bet against one. The price of being right here has already been bid up by incumbents with fifty to a hundred times more capital.

The specific knowledge question is where this breaks down for me. What do these founders know that can't be taught? Yuta Baba was a Senior Data Scientist at Snowflake working on financial forecasting algorithms -- that's relevant ML experience, but it's exactly the kind of knowledge that can be trained. Snowflake employs hundreds of people with comparable skills. His prior venture, CareVo, was in senior care in Japan -- a meaningful personal story (he became a caregiver for his mother), but it represents a significant domain shift to AI agent optimization. Christopher Acker's professional background before Carrot Labs is essentially undocumented publicly. No GitHub repositories for either founder. For a company selling technical optimization tooling to engineering teams, the absence of visible technical artifacts is a structural concern, not a gap I can fill with optimism. I backed Stack Overflow because it was built by developers who lived inside the developer knowledge problem. I backed Replit because Amjad Masad had been building coding environments for years before starting the company. Where is the equivalent depth here?

The leverage architecture is standard SaaS -- software as leverage is the baseline, not the differentiator. The company claims a data flywheel where "proprietary know-how" accumulates in fine-tuned models, making them more valuable and harder to copy over time. In principle, that's a compounding mechanism. In practice, the fine-tuning happens through foundation model APIs that Carrot Labs doesn't own or control. OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google can change pricing, capabilities, or terms at any time. The leverage is borrowed, not owned. Compare this to Stack Overflow's knowledge graph, which was genuinely proprietary -- every question and answer made the platform more valuable in ways no competitor could replicate through engineering alone. Carrot Labs' moat depends on someone else's infrastructure remaining stable and accessible, which is the opposite of defensible compounding.

The strongest bull case would require two things to be true simultaneously: first, that continuous closed-loop optimization of production AI agents is genuinely a distinct category from monitoring and evaluation -- not a feature but a platform -- and second, that the founders possess technical depth invisible from public data that enables them to build this before Braintrust adds it as a feature with their $80M war chest. If the transition from "observing agents" to "continuously improving agents" is as significant as the transition from log monitoring to APM was for Datadog, then category definition matters more than capital. And if Baba's ML systems experience at Snowflake translates into a genuinely novel optimization architecture, the 43% latency reduction and 18% quality improvement they claim could represent real customer value that compounds. But I'd need to see the technical artifact -- the actual product, the architecture, the code -- to believe that this team has built something a well-funded competitor cannot replicate in a quarter. The dossier gives me no basis for that conviction.

This is a competent team in a real and growing market, building what might be a good product. But it fails my core test. The founders don't demonstrate knowledge that exists outside the standard educational and professional pipeline. The market is consensus, not contrarian. The technology relies on borrowed leverage from foundation model providers. And the competitive moat is aspirational, not operational. I need at least one of these dimensions to be exceptional to write a check from my own money. None of them are.
