# Inth -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Christopher Burns shipped c15t to 2.6M npm downloads, planted it inside Vercel, Expo, Zed, Infisical and Unkey, and *then* applied to YC -- which makes Inth the rare pre-seed dossier where the artifact predates the deck by a factor I usually only see retrospectively. The signature here is unusual: Inth scores very high on schlep zone -- GDPR, CCPA, ePrivacy, Quebec Law 25, the kind of regulatory grind every other devtools founder in the Spring 2026 batch is unconsciously routing around -- and very low on co-founder configuration, because YC lists Burns as solo and Will De Ath is "Head of Growth," not a hacker partner. Solo schlep operator with shipped distribution. That is a specific kind of bet, not a generic one.

Brian Armstrong rebuilt his rejected Coinbase application as a working Android Bitcoin wallet, which is the canonical post-rejection animal moment in my notes. The mechanism that makes the analogy load-bearing is that Armstrong shipped a working compliance-adjacent artifact *before* the institutional check existed. Burns did the homologous thing in advance of YC: he authored c15t, watched it accumulate 2.6M downloads and embeds at Vercel and Zed, and only then walked into Andrew Miklas's office. Where Inth diverges from the Armstrong rung is direction -- Armstrong's stunt was a rescue under pressure, Burns's was patient OSS compounding from his prior life at Everfund, which raised $2M and processed payments across 33+ countries. Less dramatic, but the same underlying tell: an animal leaves tracks, and Inth's tracks are 6.5K npm downloads a day.

Solo-founder pitches are the failure pattern I wrote about explicitly in "What We Look for in Founders" -- the mechanism is that one person cannot hold both the technical surface and the customer surface as the company expands, and they fold under that load. Does Inth's dossier show this pattern? Partly yes. Burns is the sole founder on the YC page, and Inth is moving from a single OSS SDK into a repo-scanning evidence platform plus a managed DSAR product -- two new technical surfaces -- while the second named hire is growth, not engineering. De Ath was an Everfund co-founder, so the working relationship is real, but he is not the missing CTO. This is a genuine Red Flag from my own essays, and I am not going to wave it off because I like the schlep.

Idea-organicity at Inth runs through a mechanism that compounds: Burns spent seven years at Everfund as CTO of a GDPR-regulated multi-tenant donation platform, hit consent and DSAR pain in his own codebase, built c15t for himself, and only then noticed that Vercel and Expo wanted the same thing. That is the Drew Houston USB-stick shape, not the MBA market-scan shape. Over 18 months this compounds because the next features Inth ships -- repo scanning, owner mapping, regulator evidence packs -- are also features Burns wanted while running Everfund, so the roadmap is auto-correcting against his memory rather than against a customer-discovery spreadsheet.

The schlep test at Inth is where I get most interested. Privacy compliance in the codebase is exactly the kind of unpleasant regulatory ground that scared smart founders away from Stripe in 2010 -- ePrivacy, Quebec Law 25, the EU AI Act, GDPR enforcement, audit evidence generation. The counterfactual that would flip my schlep score down is if Inth turned out to be a cookie-banner SDK racing OneTrust on price; the dossier rules that out by naming "Inth DSAR" as the managed product in development and by quoting Burns explicitly framing AI-agent code changes as the new privacy attack surface. Cookie banners commoditize; repo-resident evidence generation against regulators does not.

Hacker genealogy at Inth has one strong anchor -- c15t itself. 1,691 GitHub stars and embeds at Zed and Infisical is the same kind of tribal-marker signal that Patrick Collison's Young Scientist Lisp prize was before Stripe. It is not Aaron Swartz co-authoring RSS, but it is a recognized hacker-community footprint that predates the company, and the OpenAlternative listing plus Heavybit and Scaling DevTools podcast appearances suggest Burns is operating inside the devtools tribe rather than adjacent to it. That is enough.

My 36-month forecast for Inth: Burns reaches $3-8M ARR on a c15t-funneled managed DSAR and hosted consent product, or gets acquired by Vercel or Transcend in the $30-80M range as an OSS-distribution play -- not a Stripe-scale outcome, because the solo-founder ceiling caps the company below the trio-led portfolio anchors, but a real business produced by the schlep-zone wedge plus the already-shipped distribution. The mechanism is that c15t's install base at Vercel and Expo gives Inth a developer-trust channel into engineering buyers that Transcend's $90M and Privado's $17.5M cannot replicate without an OSS rewrite.

The strongest case against Inth: Apache 2.0 on c15t means Ketch, Transcend, or Privado can fork the wedge tomorrow, and a shadcn/c15t fork already exists on GitHub -- so the "moat" is a single hacker's reputation, not a defensible asset, and a solo founder racing better-capitalized rivals on a forkable SDK is exactly the configuration my "Founder Mode" instinct says folds. The Burns-only configuration plus a commoditizable wedge is the bear mechanism, and it stings. The dossier evidence that wins is the embed list: Vercel, Expo, Zed, Infisical, Unkey didn't pick c15t because of the license, they picked it because Burns shipped the better artifact, and the fork doesn't undo production installs. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: if by Spring 2027 Inth has no publicly named paying enterprise customer for the managed DSAR product, the solo-founder-plus-commoditization bear case wins and I mark this down.
