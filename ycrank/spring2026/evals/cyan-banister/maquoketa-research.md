# Maquoketa Research -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Maquoketa Research: defense drones are no longer the uncomfortable bet they were five years ago. When I backed Anduril, saying "I invest in weapons companies" at a San Francisco dinner party would clear the room. That discomfort was signal — it meant the space was structurally underinvested relative to its importance. Today, Germany is pouring €9 billion into strike drones, Ukraine has ordered thousands of units, the U.S. Army has stood up the PBAS program, and there are at least five well-capitalized startups with active military contracts competing in exactly this category. Helsing is valued at €12 billion. Anduril has raised over $3.5 billion. Neros just won a PBAS Tranche 1 selection. The window where a first check into loitering munitions felt like writing a check into SpaceX rockets blowing up on launch pads — that window has closed. This is now a crowded, validated, consensus-attractive space where the incumbents aren't legacy primes fumbling around; they're well-run startups that already have the contracts, the clearances, and the production capacity coming online.

When I sit down with founders, the first thing I want is their story — not the business, not the TAM, not the competitive matrix. Tell me what happened to you that made this problem impossible to walk away from. With Maquoketa, I see three Berkeley-trained engineers — Yeager in EECS with a trading tech background at SpiderRock, Hernandez in EE with genuine phased array and RF expertise from Berkeley Wireless Research Center, and Williams, about whom I can find literally nothing public. The technical chops are real, particularly Hernandez's RF specialization, which is rare and directly relevant to building drones that can survive contested electromagnetic environments. But technical credential is not biographical forge. I don't see evidence that any of these founders served in the military, worked in defense procurement, witnessed the human cost of outdated munitions systems, or had any personal encounter with the problem domain that preceded the company. This reads like talented engineers who identified the hottest market in defense tech and assembled a team to enter it. That's career optimization — it's not the story of someone who would be building this thing in their garage regardless of whether a VC ever wrote them a check.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. Hernandez's phased array and RF design capability from BWRC is not something you can hire off AngelList — that's a genuinely scarce technical foundation for building autonomous drones with electronic warfare resistance, which is arguably the key differentiator between a real loitering munition and a modified DJI quadcopter with a grenade taped to it. If their autonomy stack and EW-hardened guidance represent a meaningful performance gap over what FPV-centric competitors like Neros offer, there's a real wedge. AeroVironment's new Switchblade factory won't be operational until late 2026 or early 2027 — that's a production bottleneck creating a genuine procurement window. And the fact that Stark Defence won a €268 million German contract despite failing all four test strikes tells you something revelatory about how defense procurement actually works: sometimes the relationship and positioning matter more than the product, which means a company with YC's defense network and Harj Taggar's introductions might compress timelines faster than the dossier suggests. If Maquoketa can demonstrate a working prototype with superior autonomous targeting in contested RF environments, they could become an acquisition target for a prime or a larger defense startup looking to bolt on that capability.

But here's what I keep returning to: there's no invisible system being disrupted here. The defense procurement pipeline is opaque and extractive, yes — it's one of the most opaque systems in American life. But Maquoketa isn't making it transparent; they're trying to enter it. They're building a product to sell through the same channels, to the same buyers, under the same ITAR constraints and clearance requirements. There's no structural power shift from gatekeepers to users. There's no "hidden tax" being eliminated from ordinary people's lives. When I backed Affirm, you could explain in one sentence what was being made visible: credit card companies hide the true cost of borrowing, and Affirm shows it to you. I can't construct that sentence for Maquoketa. They're building a better weapon — and there's nothing wrong with that — but it doesn't activate the part of my framework that generated my highest-conviction bets.

The competitive math is sobering even if I set aside my other concerns. Five funded competitors with $100 million to $3.5 billion in capital, active military contracts, and production facilities — and Maquoketa has no disclosed revenue, no contracts, no SBIR awards, no partnerships, and a website that returns a 403 error. Defense procurement cycles run one to three years minimum. A five-person team without disclosed security clearances faces real friction just getting into the rooms where contracts are awarded. I've seen founders survive impossible odds — Crusoe nearly died in 2020 and I stayed because the thesis was sound and the founders had proved through the valley that they were forged for it. But I need to see evidence of that forge before I commit through what will inevitably be a long, capital-intensive, relationship-dependent journey to first revenue. The dossier gives me no evidence these founders have been told "no" repeatedly and kept building, no evidence of bootstrapped traction, no evidence of personal mission beyond market opportunity.

I respect what they're attempting. The technical foundation in RF and phased array is legitimate, and the team covers the core engineering disciplines needed. In a different configuration — with a founder who served multiple tours and witnessed soldiers die because they lacked expendable precision munitions, or with a prototype already demonstrated to a program office — this could activate my framework differently. As it stands, this is a competent team chasing a validated market without the biographical inevitability, the contrarian positioning, or the structural disruption thesis that drives my strongest bets.
