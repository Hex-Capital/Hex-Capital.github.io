# Alchemy -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I notice first: this is a company that every investor in the room immediately understands. AI makes microscopy image analysis faster — 20 hours to 5 minutes, researchers describe what they need in plain English instead of writing code. It's clean, it's logical, and it's exactly the kind of pitch that would get heads nodding at a demo day. And that's my problem with it. The most promising companies I've backed have been ones that made people uncomfortable, that were hard to even explain at a dinner party. When I wrote my first angel check into SpaceX, people thought it was lunacy. When I championed Crusoe, people couldn't even parse the sentence "we mine Bitcoin on gas flares." Alchemy is the opposite — it's immediately legible, immediately reasonable, and sits squarely in the "AI makes workflows more efficient" category that has become the most crowded thesis in venture capital right now. The weirdness has been pre-arbitraged out of this opportunity before it even started.

When I look at the founders, I'm searching for that biographical thread — the story that explains why this person, solving this specific problem, right now. Phillip Baek studied biomedical engineering, did cancer research at Peking University, worked as an EP Mapping Specialist at Boston Scientific. That's life sciences adjacency, and it's real. But EP mapping and cancer-related weight loss research are not bioimage analysis. He hasn't been the grad student staying up until 2 AM manually segmenting cells in CellProfiler, cursing at ImageJ macros that keep breaking. Neither has Liam McBride, whose impressive technical pedigree — a patent at 19, UIUC CS and ECE degrees, interests in AI safety and hardware-software co-design — positions him well as an ML engineer but doesn't root him in this particular problem. I don't see the biographical wound that drives obsessive founder persistence. When I backed Contraline, the founder had spent years in academic research on male contraception that everyone else considered a dead end — his life and the company's mission were the same story. Here, I see two talented recent graduates who identified a market gap, not two people who lived inside a pain they couldn't walk away from.

The bull case deserves real engagement, because the structural gap in this market is genuine. Open-source tools like CellProfiler and QuPath are powerful but demand scripting skills most bench scientists don't have. Enterprise platforms like Visiopharm and Aivia are bundled with hardware or priced for pathology departments, not individual research labs. There's a real middle layer — the academic researcher with a microscope and a question — that's underserved by both ends. And the timing argument is legitimate: vision-language models only became capable enough for this kind of natural-language-to-pipeline assembly in the last eighteen months. If Alchemy can build workflow lock-in by embedding their pipelines into published papers and lab SOPs, they could own a sticky position that's hard to dislodge. For this to be a great investment, what would have to be true is that the founders' domain-adjacent backgrounds are close enough to iterate with real researchers, that they build the library of validated analysis nodes faster than anyone else, and that the data flywheel from pipeline interactions creates compounding accuracy that open-source tools can't match. It's plausible. But plausible isn't what gives me a jolt.

What I don't see here is economic access being created. My best investments have put money in drivers' pockets, given credit to people who were invisible to banks, let tradespeople find customers who needed them. Alchemy makes researchers slightly more productive — that's valuable, but it's efficiency optimization within an already-privileged institution, not unlocking opportunity for people who don't currently have it. The company also doesn't operate in a hard, regulated space in the way I look for. This is a research tool, not a clinical diagnostic — it doesn't need FDA clearance, doesn't face the kind of regulatory moat that deterred investors from Contraline or Anduril. That means the barrier to competition is lower: any ML-capable team with bio domain knowledge could build a similar natural-language pipeline assembler, and the incumbent microscope manufacturers have the distribution relationships and existing customer bases to bundle comparable features into their platforms, just as Leica did when they acquired DRVISION.

On co-founder dynamics, I have almost nothing to work with. Two recent graduates with no visible shared history — no common university, no common employer, no public signal about how they found each other or how they argue. After what happened with HQ Trivia, I pay close attention to this. I'm not seeing red flags, but I'm not seeing evidence of stress-tested partnership either. At this stage, that absence is a gap I'd want to fill in a conversation, not a disqualifier.

This is a competent team with relevant-adjacent backgrounds building a sensible product for a real gap in the market. But it doesn't set off any of the signals I've learned to trust — no biographical grit narrative that makes the mission feel inevitable, no weirdness that makes other investors uncomfortable, no economic access story, no regulatory complexity creating a natural moat. It's the kind of company I'd expect to see funded well by analytically-minded investors who are optimizing for market sizing and workflow efficiency. That's not how I invest. I invest with my own money based on conviction about people and ideas that don't look obvious yet, and this one already looks obvious.
