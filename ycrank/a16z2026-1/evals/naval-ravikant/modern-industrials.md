# Modern Industrials -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Modern Industrials has a competitor problem that reveals a deeper structural issue. Ventura, in YC W26, is building the identical product — AI quoting automation for building materials distributors — and Ventura's CEO has a prior exit in wholesale distribution SaaS. That's not a resume line. That's specific knowledge so deep it shapes how you design the product, which customers you call first, what edge cases you anticipate in ERP integration. When I evaluate Modern Industrials' founding team — Ankit Bhargava from xAI, Vatsal Bhargava from Google Ads ML, Austin Mao from McKinsey — I see three genuinely talented AI engineers who identified an under-digitized vertical through analysis, not through obsession. No founder has documented experience in building materials, lumber, or distribution. The dossier confirms this directly. In my framework, the question is always: if this founder disappeared, would the company's core insight survive? At Modern Industrials, the core insight is "LBM distribution is manual and AI can automate it." That insight survives the founders' departure because it's not theirs — it's available to anyone who reads a McKinsey industry report.

The leverage architecture partially redeems what the specific knowledge lacks. AI quoting automation is textbook permissionless leverage — zero marginal cost per quote processed, scales to thousands of distributors with a small team, operates 24/7 versus the industry's 9-to-5 manual workflows. Modern Industrials sits on top of existing ERPs rather than replacing them, which is the correct architectural choice. You want to be the intelligence layer, not the record-keeping system. But ERP integration dependency — DMSi Agility, ECI Spruce, Epicor BisTrack all control the API access — creates a permissioned constraint inside what should be permissionless leverage. If Epicor decides to build native AI quoting or partner exclusively with Ventura, Modern Industrials loses access to its own customers' data. That's not code leverage. That's renting someone else's platform.

The co-founder relationship is the strongest signal in the dossier and the one a generic analyst would underweight. Vatsal and Ankit Bhargava are brothers. Austin Mao is their childhood best friend of twenty-plus years. This is the opposite of my red flag about co-founders who met at a startup weekend — this is a relationship stress-tested across decades, with the kind of trust that survives the moment the company hits real adversity. Vatsal left Google. Ankit left xAI. These are real sacrifice signals. Ankit's $200K+ profit from algorithmic trading systems on Polymarket and Kalshi, and Vatsal's $10K MRR NBA injury platform, both reveal builder instinct — people who ship things because they can't stop building. The principal-agent diagnostic here is nuanced: they're authentic principals as builders, but agents to the LBM domain. They chose this industry; they weren't called to it.

Four people, already live with "national players," shipping AI quoting automation from xAI-grade infrastructure knowledge. The team-to-value ratio is strong. Ankit's experience building agentic AI systems at xAI is directly relevant to the product architecture — this isn't a wrapper around GPT-4, it's likely a purpose-built agentic system for document parsing, pricing logic, and order generation. Modern Industrials could plausibly reach meaningful revenue with under fifteen people. That's the Cursor pattern — exceptional technical density per person producing outsized output. The hiring signal ("AI native engineers in NYC") suggests they intend to stay lean rather than build a sales army.

The bull case I have to take seriously: maybe AI capability IS the bottleneck here, not domain knowledge. If the hard problem in LBM quoting is parsing faxes, handwritten spec sheets, and non-standard PDFs — and LLMs only crossed that performance threshold in 2024-2025 — then Modern Industrials' frontier AI expertise from xAI and Google is the relevant specific knowledge. Domain knowledge can be acquired through customer deployment; the ability to build reliable agentic AI systems cannot be acquired quickly. Vatsal's claim that they've "already gone live with national players" suggests rapid domain learning through deployment. If those national accounts stick and expand, the data flywheel — proprietary pricing rules, product catalogs, customer behavior — could compound into a real moat within twelve to eighteen months. The $450B+ market is large enough that even modest penetration produces venture-scale returns.

I keep coming back to the same structural comparison, though. When I backed Perplexity, Aravind Srinivas had specific knowledge in both the leverage mechanism (AI) AND the domain (search). Modern Industrials has the leverage mechanism but not the domain. Ventura has both. In a head-to-head race for the same customers in the same vertical, specific knowledge plus leverage beats leverage alone. Modern Industrials is three brilliant engineers who could build almost anything — and that's exactly the problem. The company they're building doesn't require them specifically. Another talented AI team with actual distribution experience — which is literally what Ventura is — has a structural advantage that compounds with every customer deployment. The access democratization angle is thin: Modern Industrials makes existing distributors more efficient, but it doesn't unlock access to building materials for a population previously excluded. This is optimization, not democratization. I pass, but I'd take a second look if they showed evidence of a data advantage that Ventura can't replicate, or if the "national players" turn into named logos with expansion metrics. The decisive dimensions are specific knowledge — where the evidence points to AI generalists rather than domain principals — and the direct competitive overlap with Ventura, which makes the absence of domain expertise a live risk rather than an abstract concern.
