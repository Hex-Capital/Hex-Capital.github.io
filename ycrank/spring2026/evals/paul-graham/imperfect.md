# Imperfect -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Matin Tamizi built garth, an 810-star Garmin Connect Python client, and then Garmin broke the authentication flow that made it work. He deprecated it in March 2026. That single fact contains the entire tension of Imperfect's bet: the founder is clearly a hacker who lives inside the wearable ecosystem — you don't build SSO auth libraries for Garmin recreationally — but the platform he depends on can unilaterally destroy his infrastructure. When I invested $15K in Stripe for 2%, part of what I was betting on was that Patrick Collison was building the platform, not building on someone else's. Imperfect is building on Garmin, on Apple HealthKit, on Google Health Connect. That's a fundamentally different structural position, and the garth deprecation isn't a hypothetical risk — it already happened to this founder's own code.

Matin's career arc is genuinely impressive and earns real respect. Balanced (YC W11) processed $500M+ in card volume before shutting down and transitioning 320 customers to Stripe. He then spent nearly a decade at Stripe itself across sales, operations, corporate development, product, and engineering management. Cuenca, his Mexican neobank, raised $15.9M from Stripe and a16z, got CNBV regulatory approval — which anyone who's dealt with Mexican financial regulation knows is a serious schlep — and exited. That's three real companies, real complexity, real outcomes. Imperfect benefits from a founder who has navigated regulatory minefields and managed graceful failures. But I don't have the cereal-box story for Imperfect specifically. The resourcefulness evidence is inherited from prior lives, not observed in this one. And the dossier lists no co-founder — Matin is solo on the YC page. I've said it enough times that I shouldn't have to explain why this worries me: starting a startup is too hard for one person, and a team of 5 employees is not the same as having a co-founder who will argue with you at midnight about whether you're wrong.

"AI coach that connects your wearables to get you race day ready" — Imperfect's tagline activates none of my pattern-matching for contrarian bets. When I look at the competitive landscape, I count TrainerRoad ($15.1M revenue, 250M+ training activities), TrainingPeaks ($15M revenue), Athletica AI, HumanGO (NSF-recognized, $40/month), Spoked, and Cycling Coach AI. That's six competitors I can name, in a market that every TAM analysis describes as growing at 14-19% CAGR. Garmin, Apple, and Google all have first-party biometric data and could ship native AI coaching without touching a third-party API. Imperfect's multi-sport breadth is a differentiator, but it's the kind of differentiator that sounds great in a pitch and demands enormous execution bandwidth from a five-person team covering cycling, trail running, swimming, CrossFit, and climbing simultaneously. The idea sits squarely in the zone where everyone nods and says "that makes sense" — which is exactly the zone where returns have been competed away. No one reacts to Imperfect with "that will never work." They react with "oh, several companies are doing that."

The strongest bull case for Imperfect runs through the Dropbox parallel: if Matin is an obsessive endurance athlete who got frustrated with static training plans the way Drew Houston got frustrated forgetting his USB drive, then the garth library is the artifact of that frustration, and the product grows from authentic personal need. The fintech-to-fitness pivot looks less like a domain shift and more like an experienced infrastructure builder applying data-aggregation expertise to a problem he lives inside. Garry Tan as YC partner suggests the application was compelling. And Matin's track record of navigating regulated complexity (Cuenca's CNBV approval, Balanced's payment processing) means he can handle the messy integration work that repels less experienced founders. But the bull case requires evidence the dossier doesn't provide: where are the users? Imperfect offers a free app download with no pricing page, no public user counts, no app store ratings, no press coverage, no Product Hunt launch. The Strava community clubs are a distribution signal but not a traction signal. At pre-seed I don't penalize the absence of revenue, but I do notice the absence of any evidence that anyone is using Imperfect obsessively in its crappiest form — and that's the signal I look for most.

Imperfect's origin story is missing from the dossier, and that absence is itself informative. When founders discover a problem organically — when they lived in the future and built what was missing — they have a specific, vivid narrative they can't wait to tell you. The garth library hints at personal domain engagement, but hints aren't stories. Matin's connective thread between fintech and fitness is "data aggregation across fragmented platforms" — which is a real technical pattern but an abstract one. It's the kind of thread a smart person constructs after choosing a market, not the kind that emerges from a specific moment of frustration. I could be wrong about this. But when the narrative is absent, I have to go with my default prior.

The condition that would flip me to Invest is straightforward: show me 500 athletes using Imperfect weekly with retention curves that prove the adaptive coaching is meaningfully better than what they were doing before. That would demonstrate both organic demand and the data flywheel that could become Imperfect's moat. Right now, what I see is a capable, experienced solo founder in a consensus market with strong incumbents and platform dependency risk. Matin Tamizi has the track record to build a real company — that's not the question. The question is whether Imperfect is the right company for him to build, and the dossier doesn't yet answer it. The two dimensions that lock this score are Idea That Repels Before It Attracts — Imperfect's concept generates no friction, which in my framework means no return premium — and the absence of any visible evidence that Imperfect has found the intense early users who would signal product-market fit even at this stage.
