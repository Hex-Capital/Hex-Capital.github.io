# Drip -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Drip is selling batched prepaid-balance settlement as an escape valve from Stripe Billing's ~1,000 events/sec ceiling ? and Stripe already paid roughly $1B for Metronome in January 2026 to close exactly that gap. That is the distinctive fact about Drip: the incumbent defined the problem, bought the best-in-class solution, and stapled it to the payment rails that Drip's prepaid-balance model is trying to route around. I start every evaluation with consequence magnitude, and when I ask the question I always ask ? if Drip works completely, does the world visibly change? ? the honest answer is that a few hundred AI API companies get cheaper metering and tighter dunning. That is a tool. Tools are fine. Tools are not what I write personal checks for.

Drip's pitch pattern is the exact anti-pattern I described in my Pass Example: an infrastructure layer that captures share of an existing spend category (payment processing, metering SaaS) rather than creating new demand. Metronome, Orb, Lago, Flexprice, Zenskar, Schematic ? the dossier names six direct competitors and the one with distribution just got absorbed by Stripe. Drip is not opening a new market the way Rocket Lab opened dedicated small-sat launch; Drip is arguing about how to slice the per-token invoicing pie. When my highest-conviction bets work, fusion becomes limitless, protein decouples from animal agriculture, AI primary care approaches zero marginal cost. When Drip works, an AI startup's billing stack is 30% cheaper and 100x faster. That is a 2x-to-10x engineering win on a back-office function, not a consequence explosion.

Mike Levin's background at GitHub's metered billing platform is precisely the credential most founders would lead with ? and it is the credential I most distrust. My stated line is that "most large innovations have generally not come from people who are experts in the field," and Drip's founder is the domain expert rebuilding a lighter version of the system he maintained. That can produce a solid company. It rarely produces an improbable one, because domain experts extrapolate the known constraints of the category they came from. Lucas He, listed as Co-Founder & CTO, has no verified public footprint in the dossier ? not a red flag at pre-seed, but zero additional signal either. I do not see the naive outsider energy of a Peter Beck building Rutherford engines in a garage, or a 19-year-old dropping out of MIT to compete with Lockheed. I see a competent ex-GitHub backend engineer who noticed Stripe Billing's throughput ceiling and built the obvious thing.

The prepaid-balance settlement primitive is the most interesting technical wedge Drip has, and it is not a 10x technology leap ? it is a financial workflow rearrangement. Batching thousands of micro-charges into single settlements to dodge per-charge processor fees is clever product design, not novel science. It also triggers the one unstated risk the dossier flags correctly: holding prepaid customer balances in the U.S. is a money-transmitter footgun state by state. That is exactly the kind of "requires regulatory navigation to reach viability" profile I now filter out after KiOR and Range Fuels. Oscar Health is my Known Exception where I accepted regulatory risk ? but Oscar was attacking healthcare affordability for millions, not payment-processor fees for API startups. The affordability thesis Drip can argue, "near-zero payment fees," is a B2B infrastructure cost savings, not a basic human need becoming free.

The bull case deserves to be stated at full strength: Stripe's $1B Metronome acquisition validates that standalone usage-based billing is a venture outcome; AI-agent and per-token pricing have genuinely pushed event volumes past what Stripe Billing was architected for; Mike Levin built this system at a company (GitHub) that actually operated at that scale; the prepaid-balance model, if it works, collapses payment-processor fees from ~2.9% to near zero for micro-transactions, which for a high-volume AI API is material margin. An advocate would say: this is Drip riding the exact wave that just produced a billion-dollar exit, with a founder who lived inside the problem. That is a real argument for a Sequoia or a Craft ? it is not an argument for me. Metronome sold for $1B. That is a fine outcome for a seed investor. It is below the floor of what I personally need a bet to be able to produce, because my portfolio math requires 50-100x option payoffs, not single-B exits.

The closest portfolio parallel I can reach for is instructive only by contrast: DoorDash is my Known Exception where I overrode the non-linear technology requirement because the consequence magnitude was planetary food access and the founder was a high-velocity immigrant outsider. Drip has neither override. The consequence is infrastructure efficiency for a specific developer niche, and the founder archetype is a domain expert, not a Tony Xu. The one verifiable signal that would flip me: if Drip lands, within six months, a non-trivial percentage of OpenAI- or Anthropic-scale token-billing volume running through its rails ? meaning the prepaid-balance model is absorbing a meaningful slice of the actual frontier AI settlement flow, not just serving mid-market API startups. That would turn Drip from a Metronome alternative into AI-era payment infrastructure, and the consequence calculus changes. Absent that, this is a competent team building a competent product in a category Stripe just acquired its way into.

The two dimensions that lock this in the Pass tier are Consequence Magnitude and Improbable Ambition. Drip's fully-successful end state is "a better Metronome with prepaid settlement," and Stripe+Metronome is now the house Drip is trying to live inside. My framework does not score infrastructure share-fights highly no matter how clean the API.
