# Indexable -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the thing that hits me immediately: this company is building AI agent infrastructure. That's the single hottest category in venture capital right now. Six funded startups, three major cloud providers launching competing products in the same quarter, and every demo day pitch I've heard in the last twelve months has some flavor of "infrastructure for agents." E2B has 88% of the Fortune 100 signed up. Modal's at a billion-plus valuation with $50M ARR. Daytona raised $31M and crossed a million ARR in two months. There is zero mispricing here. The upside is fully in the price -- actually, it's probably overpriced given how many well-funded teams are attacking the same wedge. This is the exact opposite of the setup I look for. When I seeded Solana, literally no one wanted to write that check. When I looked at Eaze, every serious VC was running the other direction. Indexable is swimming in a pool where everyone already jumped in.

Now, the technical approach -- building a custom VMM on KVM rather than using Firecracker or Docker -- is genuinely contrarian *within* the category. And Andrew Gazelka is clearly a systems engineering animal. Hyperion, a Minecraft engine in Rust targeting 10,000 concurrent players with a thousand GitHub stars, is the kind of project that tells you someone can actually think at the kernel level. The xAI stint adds credibility. "Do less, deeply" as a GitHub bio is the kind of thing a grounded, non-hype engineer writes. The founder profile is solid -- maybe even great. But here's my problem with the sanity-insanity ratio: the *approach* is insane (custom VMM, custom Linux kernel, custom Nix reimplementation, RDMA transport layer -- all with three people), but the *idea* is completely sane. Everyone agrees AI agents need sandboxes. That's backwards from what I want. I want a sane person building in a category that makes people uncomfortable. This is a sane person building in a category that makes every VC salivate.

Let me steelman the bull case, because there's one angle worth wrestling with. What if the market is wrong not about the *category* but about the *architecture*? What if every competitor using Firecracker microVMs or Docker containers is building on fundamentally insufficient technology, and when agents graduate from code execution to full stateful operations -- writing to databases, managing processes, mutating file systems -- the only infrastructure that actually works is something that can fork an entire running environment atomically in 26 milliseconds? That's not "faster cold starts." That's a different capability. E2B caps sessions at 24 hours with no database forking. Cloudflare Workers use V8 isolates that are stateless by design. If autonomous agents need to branch execution paths with full state fidelity -- like git for running systems -- then Indexable might be building the only thing that actually works at scale. In that world, everyone else has the wrong foundation, and technical superiority eventually wins. The Daytona comparison is the closest -- they have fork/snapshot primitives -- but they're using containers, not a custom VMM, which limits the depth of what they can fork.

The problem with that bull case is threefold. First, this is still software -- no physical-world moat, no protocol-level network effect, no asset that can't be replicated by a well-funded team. AWS could build a custom VMM if the market demanded it. Cloudflare has the kernel engineers. The technical lead is real but time-limited, not structural. Second, the maintenance burden is existential at three people. A custom VMM, a patched Linux 6.19 kernel, an RDMA transport layer, AND a Rust-based Nix reimplementation? That's the surface area of a fifty-person infrastructure team compressed into what appears to be mostly one engineer. One person gets hit by a bus and this collapses. Third -- and this is the big one -- I've said publicly and repeatedly that AI benefits incumbents. Every time you get pitched infrastructure for AI agents, the question is: who has distribution? Cloudflare has millions of developers. Vercel has the Next.js ecosystem. AWS has everyone. Indexable's sandbox might be technically superior, but "technically superior" doesn't beat "already embedded in every developer's workflow." Betamax, etc.

The capital math is ugly too. To compete with E2B ($32M+), Daytona ($31M), and Modal ($111M), Indexable needs to raise aggressively and fast. Infrastructure businesses are capital-intensive by nature -- you need to provision compute, build out regions, hire kernel engineers who cost $400K+. This means factory-farm fundraising: seed, Series A, Series B, each round bigger than the last, all to compete for market share in a crowded category where the incumbents already have customers. That's the conveyor belt I keep saying is dead. When I tell founders "think of every round as the last money you'll ever raise," I mean build something that can survive on its own economics. AI sandbox infrastructure at this funding level, in this competitive environment, cannot.

Look, Gazelka is clearly talented. The Hyperion-to-xAI-to-custom-VMM pipeline shows real founder-market fit in systems engineering. If this were a contrarian category -- if he were building this same technology for something the market had dismissed -- I'd be much more interested. But he's bringing a knife to a gunfight where six other people already have guns and three of them are arms manufacturers. The technical differentiation is real but not durable enough to overcome the distribution and capital disadvantages. Pass.
