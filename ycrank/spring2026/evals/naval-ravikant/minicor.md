# Minicor -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Minicor's most revealing feature is its origin story: the company pivoted from Laminar, a low-code integration platform, after hitting a wall trying to connect to an on-premise Windows-based medical record system with no API. The legal entity is still Laminar Run, Inc. That pivot tells me something important — the founders found the problem through genuine work, not through market research or a slide deck about RPA TAM. But discovery through work and specific knowledge are different things. Faizaan Chishtie has integration experience from Phoenix (YC W21), Typecast, and Secoda. Saheed Akinbile spent three years at Ford on Android Auto and worked at Morgan Stanley. That's enterprise software experience adjacent to the problem, not years of obsessive expertise in desktop automation and computer vision orchestration. If Faizaan disappeared tomorrow, could a strong ML engineering team rebuild Minicor's self-healing agent layer? Almost certainly yes. The core orchestration — a reflection agent verifying actions against screen content — is competent systems engineering, not non-replicable knowledge. Contrast with Aravind Srinivas at Perplexity, whose years at Google Brain and OpenAI were literally the engine of the product. Minicor's engine is clever engineering on top of foundation model capabilities that Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google are shipping to everyone.

Minicor's leverage architecture is where the structural concern sharpens. Every workflow runs on a Windows VM consuming compute and LLM inference — that's not zero marginal cost. Minicor claims 93–96% click accuracy versus 80–85% for raw foundation model computer-use, but that accuracy gap is a depreciating asset. Foundation model providers are improving rapidly; a 10-percentage-point advantage measured today could be a 3-point advantage in twelve months. Minicor is building on rented leverage — the moment Anthropic or OpenAI bundles managed desktop orchestration into their platform, the core differentiation compresses to the compliance and observability layers. Those layers are real (SOC 2 Type II and HIPAA compliance at pre-seed is genuinely expensive to replicate), but compliance is a barrier, not a moat. It buys time rather than compounding advantage. Notion, by contrast, generates more value with every template shared, every workspace created — the product compounds. Minicor's compliance certification does not compound.

The strongest bull case for Minicor runs through UiPath's principal-agent problem. UiPath (NYSE: PATH) built its $2B+ revenue business on brittle, script-based automation that breaks when vendor UIs update — and their professional services arm profits from fixing those breaks. Self-healing AI agents directly cannibalize UiPath's maintenance revenue. UiPath's engineering teams have the talent to build this, but they're agents of their own P&L, structurally unable to destroy their highest-margin revenue stream. Minicor, as a five-person principal team with no legacy revenue, can move where UiPath's agents cannot. This mirrors the Google-versus-Perplexity dynamic that anchored one of my highest-conviction bets. Minicor processing 25,000 patients per day for a single customer with a five-person team is a legitimate production signal — that's not a demo, that's load-bearing infrastructure. Six customer logos on the website and SOC 2 Type II at this stage suggest a team that ships rather than pitches.

Faizaan and Saheed co-founded Software For Love together at University of Ottawa before either started a company — that pre-existing relationship passes my co-founder filter cleanly. They did not meet at a startup weekend. Faizaan previously built Homeboard to mid-five-figure ARR and went through YC with Phoenix, so he's been through the startup cycle before. The pivot from Laminar to Minicor signals willingness to follow the problem rather than force the original idea — a principal behavior, not an agent behavior. But Minicor is their second idea, not their lifelong obsession. The principal signal is moderate, not exceptional: they committed to the pivot, but I don't see evidence they'd be building desktop automation tools without venture funding.

Minicor's team-to-value ratio is its strongest dimension relative to my framework. Five people, zero open job postings, one customer at 25,000 patients per day, and SOC 2 Type II certification. That's remarkable output density. The supported system list — Athena, Epic, Cerner, CDK Global, SAP, HighJump — suggests breadth across healthcare, automotive, and supply chain from a tiny team using AI-first development. On access democratization, Minicor unlocks data trapped in legacy Windows desktop systems that vendors are actively restricting from third-party API access. AI companies selling into healthcare, automotive, and logistics gain access to systems of record that were previously walled off. That's genuine gatekeeping removal, though it's one step removed — Minicor serves the AI companies, not the end patients or end-users directly.

The condition that would flip me to Invest: if foundation model computer-use accuracy plateaus at 90% for another 12–18 months while Minicor's orchestration layer demonstrably compounds — more supported systems, more workflow templates, more customer-specific edge cases encoded as proprietary training data that creates an actual data flywheel. Right now, Minicor's advantage is an engineering lead on a closing accuracy gap, plus compliance certifications that any well-funded competitor can obtain in six to nine months. The UiPath principal-agent dynamic is structurally real, and the 25,000 patients/day traction signal is genuine. But without evidence that the founders possess non-replicable specific knowledge — knowledge that would take a competitor years, not months, to accumulate — Minicor remains competent engineering in a real market rather than the kind of founder-knowledge-productized-as-leverage that produces asymmetric returns. The decisive dimensions are Specific Knowledge Productization (integration experience but not deep domain obsession) and Permissionless Leverage Architecture (real code leverage undermined by nonzero marginal cost and foundation model commoditization risk).
