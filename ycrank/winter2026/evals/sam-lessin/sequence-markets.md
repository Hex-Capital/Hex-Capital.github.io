# Sequence Markets -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The competitive landscape here tells the whole story before I even get to the founders. Talos just closed at a $1.5 billion valuation with $196 million raised. FalconX sits at $8 billion with $430 million in the bank. CoinRoutes has been shipping smart order routing since 2017. 1inch has raised roughly $190 million for DEX aggregation. When I see a five-person pre-seed team proposing to build the same category of product that four well-capitalized companies already occupy -- and that product is a well-understood concept borrowed from traditional equities markets -- I'm not looking at a "sane person, insane idea." I'm looking at a sane person with a very sane idea, and that's the configuration that produces the worst risk-adjusted returns in venture. The returns are already priced in by the incumbents. Peter Bai's background at TMX Group and a $12.8 billion pension fund gives him real credibility on market microstructure -- he's genuinely "sane" for this problem -- but the idea needs to provoke skepticism from other investors, and "smart order routing for crypto" provokes enthusiasm, not skepticism. That's a TAM story, not a contrarian bet.

The "AI-powered routing" positioning is actually a red flag in my framework, not a selling point. When the company's claimed differentiator is that their routing decisions use AI, I ask: will every single competitor also have AI-powered routing within twelve months? The answer is obviously yes. Talos won "Best Quantitative Trading Technology Provider" at Hedgeweek in 2025 -- they're not sleeping on algorithmic optimization. FalconX reported 30x year-over-year revenue growth when they were scaling. These are not slow incumbents waiting to be disrupted by a better algorithm. The AI angle here is the core value proposition, not the "cherry on top" that I look for. Strip the AI out and ask what's left: a multi-venue routing layer that connects to exchange APIs. That's an engineering project, not a business with structural defensibility. This is exactly the pattern where software IS the moat, and I've spent the last two years arguing publicly that those moats are dissolving.

Structurally, Sequence occupies the application layer in the worst possible way for my thesis. They're middleware -- routing orders across other people's protocols and other people's exchanges. Compare this to my portfolio: Solana created a new Layer 1 that thousands of applications build on. Aleo established zero-knowledge privacy as a cryptographic primitive. Helium built decentralized wireless infrastructure. These are new layers of the stack that generate compounding network effects. Sequence sits on top of everyone else's stack and adds routing logic. If Coinbase changes its API, Sequence breaks. If Uniswap modifies its contracts, Sequence adapts or dies. The venue integration dependency is real operational complexity, but complexity isn't a moat -- it's a cost of doing business that any funded competitor also pays. There's no protocol position here, no new primitive being established, no ecosystem forming around Sequence's infrastructure.

The bull case I have to genuinely engage with: Talos and FalconX serve institutions with enterprise sales cycles and presumably high minimums. There's a real gap for active mid-tier traders and retail participants who need execution quality but can't access these platforms. If Sequence nails this segment with a self-serve, non-custodial product, they could build a data flywheel from execution volume that improves routing quality over time. The non-custodial architecture is smart post-FTX positioning. And crypto market fragmentation is accelerating as DEX proliferation continues -- the problem Sequence solves is genuinely getting worse, which means the TAM for execution infrastructure is expanding. If I squint, I can see a world where Sequence becomes the "Stripe of crypto execution" -- the developer-friendly infrastructure layer that every fintech app plugs into for best execution. That narrative has real elasticity. But here's why I don't buy it: this is precisely the Birchbox lesson I carry. A legitimate market gap with no structural barrier preventing incumbents from moving downmarket. Talos, flush with capital at $1.5 billion valuation, could launch a self-serve tier tomorrow. The gap exists because it's not yet worth their attention, not because it's structurally defensible.

Muhammad Awan's near-total lack of public background compounds my concern. His YC tagline -- "I like building cool projects" -- tells me nothing about whether he can build low-latency trading infrastructure that handles real money under adversarial conditions. No confirmed technical background, no prior companies, no public GitHub presence. At pre-seed I don't expect celebrity founders, but when you're entering a market with billion-dollar incumbents, I need to see evidence that the technical co-founder has built something comparable before. The co-founder relationship is also opaque -- geographic proximity in Ontario is not the same as the deep relational foundation I've seen in the founding teams that survive genuine adversity.

This is a pass. The idea is consensus, the moat is software, the positioning is application-layer middleware, and the competitive landscape includes multiple companies with hundreds of millions in funding doing the same thing. Bai's domain credibility is real but insufficient to overcome the structural problems in how I evaluate businesses. I'd rather fund someone building physical execution infrastructure for digital assets -- custody hardware, settlement rails with regulatory moats -- than a software routing layer that any well-funded team can replicate.
