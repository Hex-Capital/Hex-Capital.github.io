# Rubric AI -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The competitive landscape here tells you everything before you even get to the founder. Rubric AI is a two-person pre-seed startup entering a market where Scale AI sits at $29B (with Meta acquiring 49%), Surge AI is bootstrapping $1.2 billion in revenue and seeking a $25B valuation, Mercor is at $10B, and -- this is the part that should make you wince -- Labelbox has already shipped a product literally called "rubric evaluation." The company is named after a feature that an incumbent already sells. When I backed Venmo with my dad, there was no incumbent peer-to-peer mobile payment infrastructure to compete against. We were funding a new primitive in a world that didn't know it needed one. Here, the primitive already exists. The market has already been defined, carved up, and dominated by companies with hundreds of millions in revenue. This isn't a frontier -- it's an occupied territory.

My sane-person-insane-idea filter breaks down immediately because neither component is present in the right form. Pragya Saboo shipped Time Tracking at Asana and built Virtual Care features at Oscar Health -- that's genuine product management competence, and I respect the ability to take something from inception to launch. But "sane" in my framework means credibly sane *for this specific problem*. A "post-training research and product lab" needs someone who has lived inside RLHF pipelines, published on reward modeling, or built training infrastructure at a frontier lab. The dossier shows no ML research, no AI infrastructure experience, no publications. The second founder isn't even publicly identified. When the Mysten Labs team left Meta's crypto division to build Sui, their credibility was immediate -- they'd built the thing at scale and were now building a better version. That's the "sane" I need for a research-positioned company selling to Anthropic and OpenAI. A product manager pitching evaluation methodology to the world's most sophisticated AI researchers is a credibility mismatch that no amount of hustle resolves.

More fundamentally, this company fails my most basic structural test: would this business exist if AI didn't? Obviously not. The entire value proposition is predicated on frontier AI labs needing better evaluation signals. This is the precise category I've been publicly warning against -- AI-first companies where AI *is* the product, not the cherry on top. When I backed Craftwork, the insight was that painting crews are an AI-resistant moat. Software makes dispatch better, but you still need humans on ladders. Here, there's no ladder. The "moat" is evaluation methodology -- which is software and process knowledge that any well-funded competitor can replicate or any frontier lab can build internally. Anthropic and OpenAI have some of the best researchers on the planet. The idea that they need a two-person startup to design their evaluation rubrics requires a theory of why these labs can't do this themselves, and I haven't heard one.

The bull case, if I'm being honest with myself, isn't nothing. Meta's acquisition of Scale AI genuinely fragmented the market. Google, OpenAI, and xAI reportedly pulled work from Scale over data-sharing concerns. That creates a real opening for independent, trusted evaluation partners. If Rubric AI's unidentified second founder is a world-class ML researcher who left one of these labs -- someone who brings the technical credibility and the institutional relationships -- this could be a wedge play into a suddenly disrupted vendor ecosystem. The "research lab" positioning, if backed by genuine research capability, could differentiate from the workforce-management platforms. And the market is growing at 22-29% CAGR. For this to work, the unnamed co-founder would need to be extraordinary, the methodology would need to be genuinely novel (not just annotation management with better UX), and the company would need to lock in one or two frontier lab relationships before incumbents adjust. That's a lot of "would need to be" for a pre-seed check.

But even in that bull case, I'm still looking at a services business selling to fewer than twenty customers globally. The narrative ceiling is visible from here. "Evals for frontier labs" doesn't expand into an infinity story -- it's a niche within a niche. Compare that to Helium, where "IoT hotspots" grew into "decentralized wireless infrastructure" over seven years, with each milestone making the next narrative expansion more credible. Where does Rubric AI's narrative go? "Evals for all AI companies" is the obvious expansion, but that's exactly where Scale, Surge, Mercor, and Labelbox already operate. There's no protocol-layer compounding here, no new primitive being established that creates an ecosystem. This is an application built on someone else's infrastructure, selling to someone else's platform.
