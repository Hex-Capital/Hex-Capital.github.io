# Balance -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Balance: this is one of the most crowded categories I've seen at pre-seed. Pilot has raised $325 million. Digits has nearly $30 million in revenue. Finally has taken in $305 million total. Puzzle, Truewind, Haven — everyone and their cousin is building AI accounting right now. Haven, which is also pre-seed, already has over a million in revenue using the exact same model: AI does the reconciliation, human accountants sign off. When I look at a deal, I'm asking myself whether this company is unpopular, difficult to assess, and working on something others consider too strange to touch. Balance is none of those things. It's popular. It's easy to assess. And dozens of well-funded teams are already chasing the same insight — that LLMs are finally good enough to automate bookkeeping. The weirdness has been arbitraged away before the company even got started.

Now, Mathias's background is genuinely relevant — he founded an accountancy and served as CFO at Female Invest, which went through YC. He's lived inside the problem. I respect that. But when I sit across from a founder, I'm listening for the story before the business — the specific hardship or obsession that makes them the only person who should be solving this. What I see here is strong professional domain experience. Mathias ran an accounting firm and saw the inefficiency. Emil came from McKinsey and was employee number one at Kapa AI. Gus has an impressive AI research pedigree from Oxford and Imperial. These are accomplished, well-credentialed people operating in a space they understand. But that's different from the kind of biographical fire I look for — the founder who was told no fifty times and kept building because this problem was woven into their identity. Nothing in the dossier tells me these founders couldn't have just as easily started a company in a different vertical. That doesn't make them bad founders. It means I can't distinguish their commitment from competent market selection.

The strongest case for Balance is the structural insight about market positioning. Traditional accounting firms can't cannibalize their billable-hour model with AI. Software companies like Intuit and Xero can't enter the services business without torching their partnerships with accounting firms. That gap — between software and services — is real, and Balance is positioning in it by being the accountancy rather than the tool. The conversational interface through Slack and WhatsApp is a genuine UX insight that resonates with my pattern of investing in companies that make invisible systems visible. Small business owners shouldn't need to log into a dashboard and decipher financial jargon to understand their own money. Putting your finances in a Slack channel where you can just ask "how did we do last month?" — that does create real accessibility. I can see a world where this becomes the way millions of small businesses interact with their financial health, and that vision has economic access implications I care about.

But I keep coming back to defensibility, which is one of the first things we look at in early-stage investing. The dossier itself says "no strong moat signals are identifiable from public sources," and I agree. The AI-plus-human-review model is replicable — Haven is already doing it. LLM capabilities for categorization are accessible to any well-funded team. The switching costs from embedded financial history are real but take years to compound. Meanwhile, Balance is operating across three jurisdictions at pre-seed with four people, which feels like it diffuses focus rather than concentrating it. That multi-jurisdiction complexity could eventually become a moat, but right now it's just surface area for errors in tax filings where the company bears professional liability. If an AI-generated output slips through human review and a client gets hit with penalties, that's an existential risk for a four-person company.

If I'm being honest about what would have to be true for this to be a great investment: Mathias's experience running an actual accountancy would need to give Balance a service-quality advantage that pure software founders can't replicate. The conversational interface would need to become the dominant interaction pattern for SMB finance, creating a distribution moat through habit and word-of-mouth. And the company would need to pick one market — probably the UK or Denmark, where there's less well-funded competition — and dominate it before expanding. If all three of those things happen, this could be a meaningful company. But that's a lot of "ifs" in a space where competitors have hundreds of millions of dollars and years of head start. I'm not the right investor for a bet that requires outexecuting well-capitalized competitors in a consensus category. My best investments have always been the ones nobody else wanted to fund.
