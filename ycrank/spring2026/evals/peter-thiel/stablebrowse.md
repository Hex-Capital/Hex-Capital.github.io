# StableBrowse -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

$96 million in aggregate competitor funding across five direct rivals — Browserbase at $40M, Browser Use at $17M, Steel.dev at $17M, Kernel at $22M — all targeting the identical buyer: developers building AI agents that interact with the web. StableBrowse enters this market with standard YC capital. I do not need to read further to know the structural diagnosis, but I will. When I see five funded companies converging on the same thesis in the same 18-month window, I am not looking at a market — I am looking at a stampede. The existence of a competitive landscape slide is itself the clearest possible red flag. StableBrowse's claim is that stripping the rendering engine and outputting semantic markdown represents a fundamentally different architecture. But Browser Use already has 50,000 GitHub stars pursuing semantic output. Browserbase has processed 50 million sessions. The "insight" that AI agents do not need pixels is not a secret anyone disputes — it is the operating consensus of the entire category. Every player in this market agrees on the destination; they differ only on implementation details. That is competition, and competition is for losers.

StableBrowse's claimed technical specifications — 130.6 MB per instance, 4ms cold start, 85% token reduction versus conventional browsers — describe performance benchmarks, not a monopoly mechanism. The strongest bull case an advocate would construct runs as follows: building a rendering-free browser engine from scratch, rather than wrapping Chromium, creates an architectural discontinuity analogous to reusable rockets versus expendable ones. Competitors built on Chromium would have to re-architect their entire stack to match. CDP compatibility provides a frictionless migration path. The AI agents market at $182.97 billion by 2033 offers power-law magnitude if StableBrowse captures the infrastructure layer. This argument has internal logic. It collapses on two facts. First, the reusable-rocket analogy requires that the establishment declare the approach impossible — but nobody disputes that rendering-free browsing is feasible; competitors are converging on it from different angles. Second, CDP compatibility cuts both directions: it lowers adoption friction but also lowers switching costs. A customer who migrates to StableBrowse via CDP can migrate away just as frictionlessly. StableBrowse has built a highway with no toll booths.

Somansh Shah graduated from Purdue in 2025 with a data science internship at Lenovo and a $6,600 startup expo prize. Deepit Shah holds a BS/MS from UIUC with an Amazon internship and — more troublingly — a LinkedIn indication of returning to Google full-time. Two of four founders, Sarthak Awasthi and Jay Mehta, have common names with no publicly verifiable connection to StableBrowse. Building a browser engine from scratch — or deeply forking one while preserving JavaScript execution and web compatibility — is among the most demanding systems engineering undertakings in software. StableBrowse's founding team shows no visible experience in browser engine internals, web standards implementation, or systems-level infrastructure at scale. The definite-optimism framework requires not just bold ambition but credible specificity: a founder who can describe exactly what the world looks like when they succeed and has made irreversible commitments that only make sense if the vision is correct. StableBrowse presents no published roadmap, no pricing, no public repository, and no evidence of irreversible commitment beyond YC admission.

Platform risk from OpenAI, Google, and Anthropic building native browsing into their agent infrastructure represents the most structurally lethal threat StableBrowse faces. When your potential customers' primary suppliers absorb your functionality into their core offering, you are not building infrastructure — you are building a feature that will be bundled away. Palantir survived platform risk from AWS and Azure because its software was so deeply embedded in intelligence operations that extraction would compromise national security. StableBrowse has no such embedding, no institutional anchor customer, and no government pathway. The distribution dimension shows zero evidence of organic pull: no revenue, no user counts, no GitHub presence, no social media accounts. The calendly link on the website suggests StableBrowse is pushing outward rather than being pulled inward. PayPal knew it had distribution when eBay sellers began adding its logo to auctions unprompted. StableBrowse has no equivalent signal.

StableBrowse is pure software infrastructure with no physical-world component, no regulatory moat, no hardware barrier, and no proprietary dataset. A well-funded competitor — or an LLM provider with unlimited engineering resources — could rebuild the core technology. We wanted flying cars, and StableBrowse is offering a slightly different way to parse web pages. The atoms-over-bits criterion exists precisely because software layers without physical anchors face replication timelines measured in months, not years. StableBrowse's technical complexity in building a rendering-free engine is real engineering work, but it creates a head start, not a moat.

The one verifiable condition that would flip me to Invest: StableBrowse demonstrates that its rendering-free architecture achieves reliable compatibility across 95%+ of the modern web — a claim no one has proven — AND secures an exclusive infrastructure contract with a major LLM provider or government agency that creates structural switching costs. Without that, StableBrowse is entering the cleantech pattern: entrepreneurs swept up by the AI-agent trend rather than possessing a specific, non-obvious insight about the world. The competitive market, the consensus thesis, the pure-software architecture, and the unproven founding team place this firmly outside my framework.
