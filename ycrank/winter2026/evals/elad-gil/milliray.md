# Milliray (formerly DroneTector) -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The structural moment here is real and well-evidenced. Over $1 billion in counter-drone purchases closed in December 2025 alone. Ukraine demonstrated 1,600+ drone attacks per month in 2024. The Red Sea drone campaign doubled global shipping costs. NATO DIANA launched in 2023, creating an institutional procurement pathway for European deep-tech defense startups that didn't exist before. This isn't a hypothetical market sizing exercise -- procurement dollars are flowing NOW in a way they weren't two years ago. The counter-drone market is projected to grow from $3 billion to $16 billion by 2034, and DroneShield's 276% revenue growth in FY2025 confirms the adoption curve is inflecting. What's non-obvious is the specific niche Milliray targets: millimeter-wave radar at 94 GHz for nano-drone detection. Most counter-drone investment has gone to RF signal detection (Dedrone), multi-sensor platforms (DroneShield), or kinetic defeat systems (Fortem). The detection gap for the smallest drones -- which emit no RF signal when flying autonomously -- is underappreciated. That's the non-obvious sub-market within a market that's becoming consensus.

This maps to a pattern I've invested in before. Like Anduril, there's a structural vacancy in defense where existing players can't serve a specific need -- defense primes optimize for high-value platform programs, and building a specialized sub-$100K radar for nano drones is economically unattractive relative to their cost structures and sales cycles. Like Coinbase operating under financial regulation that scared away software-focused investors, Milliray's defense procurement environment -- NATO DIANA qualification, DASA backing, potential ITAR-adjacent requirements -- creates barriers that deter most venture investors but serve as moats for early movers. A UK-based sovereign European radar provider has structural positioning as NATO allies reduce US dependency. The CEO's PhD is literally on 94 GHz Doppler radar for drone micro-Doppler detection -- he built and tested the core technology and published peer-reviewed results in IET Radar, Sonar & Navigation. The COO brings automated reasoning and ML for the classification algorithms. Three Royal Academy of Engineering Enterprise Fellowships provide institutional validation. This is genuine founder-market fit rooted in years of specialized research, not a pivot into a hot sector.

The product-to-distribution trajectory is where I have less conviction. "Detect. Track. Identify. Integrate." suggests the founders are thinking about becoming infrastructure rather than just a sensor vendor -- radar hardware as the wedge, then software analytics, multi-sensor fusion, and integration with defeat systems as the expansion products. Defense procurement switching costs would lock in the relationship once integrated. But hardware companies face harder distribution expansion paths than software companies. Stripe could ship Atlas, Billing, and Treasury through the same API integration at near-zero marginal cost. Milliray distributing additional products through installed radar hardware requires physical deployment, maintenance, and integration work. There's no evidence the founders have explicitly articulated a multi-product platform vision, and at 3 people with no disclosed manufacturing partner, the path from research prototype to production-grade units at competitive economics remains unclear.

The execution risks are what keep me from higher conviction. Three specific concerns. First, the CTO is listed as CEO of Quantum Fabrix, an ion trap quantum computing venture -- for a 3-person deep-tech hardware startup, having a co-founder with divided attention is material. Second, the competitive landscape is formidable: DroneShield has AUD 216 million in revenue with a $2.3 billion pipeline, Robin Radar has 200+ units deployed in Ukraine, and Dedrone is now backed by Axon's $2.1 billion revenue base. Any of these could add millimeter-wave capabilities through R&D or acquisition. Third, defense sales cycles of 12-24+ months with a 3-person team and pre-seed capital creates a real cash runway question. No revenue, no named customers, no LOIs, no pilot contracts are publicly disclosed. "Deployment-ready" without disclosed deployments is a claim, not traction.

The bull case that would change my mind: if Milliray's 94 GHz radar demonstrably detects nano drones that every existing system misses, and if NATO DIANA or DASA converts into an actual procurement contract within 6-9 months, this becomes a genuine breakout. The technology would be validated, the go-to-market channel proven, and the sovereign European positioning would attract follow-on defense contracts across NATO allies. At that point, it's the Anduril pattern at smaller scale -- a structural vacancy filled by a technically credible team with institutional backing. The $16 billion market by 2034 creates room for multiple winners, and a specialized detection layer that integrates with larger platforms could be extremely valuable as an acquisition target for DroneShield, Axon, or a defense prime. If the CTO commits full-time and they secure a manufacturing partner, the path narrows to one miracle: proving the radar works in operational conditions at production quality.

Today, I see a compelling structural moment with genuine founder-market fit, operating in a defense infrastructure market with regulatory moat characteristics I've profited from before. But the gap between "PhD-built research prototype" and "production-grade defense product with paying customers" requires hardware scaling, defense procurement navigation, and competitive differentiation maintenance simultaneously -- that's closer to three miracles than one, and the team's bandwidth is further stretched by the CTO's dual-venture situation. I'd take the meeting, ask hard questions about manufacturing strategy and CTO commitment, and revisit if they land a pilot contract. Not writing a check today.
