# Standout -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that jumps out at me isn't the product or the founders -- it's that Founders Fund backed Placement in 2019 with this exact thesis. A talent agent for tech professionals. Placement raised a $3M seed, pioneered the model, and was quietly absorbed into Exec rather than scaling independently. I was at Founders Fund during that era. I watched that experiment play out. And now here we are again, same pitch with an AI wrapper, and Clera is already running the identical playbook with 60,000 users. When an idea keeps getting re-attempted in the same form and the predecessors haven't broken through, that's not a signal of hidden opportunity -- that's a signal that the structural economics of candidate-side representation in tech hiring may be genuinely broken. The question I'd want answered is: what did Placement's failure teach you, and why does AI change the unit economics enough to make this work? I don't see that answer in anything public.

When I sit down with founders, I don't want to hear the business first. I want to know how they got here -- what personal experience made this problem theirs to solve. Alexis Aftalion is clearly a capable operator: COO at Zealy, scaling to 1.5 million monthly users and $3M ARR, prior time as a VC at Alven. Witold de La Chapelle is a strong engineer -- Dropbox, Samsara, Chime, Carnegie Mellon -- and he built a job board that reached 100K monthly users. These are solid professional credentials. But I'm not hearing the survival story. I'm not hearing about the time they were invisible in a hiring process that chewed them up, or the moment they realized the system was rigged against candidates because they lived it. The biographical thread connecting these founders to this specific problem feels professional, not personal. When I backed Travis at Uber, it wasn't because he had logistics experience -- it was the gravitas, the years my husband and I had spent talking about what a racket the medallion system was. That kind of personal fire is what sustains founders through the valley of death, and I'm not sensing it here.

The structural insight is genuinely interesting, and I want to be honest about that. Recruiters work for employers. LinkedIn makes its money from companies, not candidates. That creates an invisible system where job seekers don't realize nobody is actually advocating for them. This pattern -- making an opaque system transparent -- is something I've been drawn to repeatedly: Affirm exposing hidden credit card fees, Carta replacing opaque cap table spreadsheets, Flock Safety bringing surveillance data to communities. If Standout could genuinely become the candidate's advocate at scale, that would matter. But here's what gives me pause: the "8 Best Operators" roster features engineers from OpenAI, Anthropic, and Whop. These are the most sought-after people in the entire technology ecosystem. They don't lack representation -- they have recruiters banging down their doors. A service that helps elite AI engineers find their next role at a Sequoia-backed startup isn't creating economic access for underserved populations. It's a concierge for people who already have options. If Standout were matching overlooked talent -- bootcamp graduates, career changers, people from non-traditional backgrounds -- with startup roles, I'd feel that jolt of energy I look for. I don't feel it here.

The "weird factor" test is where this falls flat for me. The most promising companies I've backed were the ones nobody else wanted to fund because they sounded absurd. Mining Bitcoin on gas flares. Private rockets. Male contraceptive gel. An AI recruiting agent on WhatsApp is... sensible. It's a reasonable application of current technology to a known market problem. It's the kind of idea that makes VCs nod along in a meeting, not the kind that makes them uncomfortable. And when I see that 43% of organizations were already using AI for recruiting in 2025, and that Clera operates a near-identical model, I know the consensus has already formed. This isn't a contrarian bet. It's a crowded lane.

The bull case would require a few things to be true: that AI fundamentally changes the unit economics Placement couldn't crack, that WhatsApp creates a sticky relationship moat Clera can't replicate, and that the team's consumer-growth experience from Zealy translates into a winner-take-all dynamic in candidate-side recruiting. If Aftalion can execute at the same pace he did at Zealy -- 1.5M MAU is no joke -- and if the candidate data from discovery calls creates genuinely proprietary matching intelligence, this could work. The 35% of users reaching a first interview within four days is a real signal of product-market resonance. But I'd need to see the founders demonstrate a deeper, almost irrational commitment to this specific problem -- the kind of commitment that survives when Clera raises a big round, when WhatsApp changes its API terms, and when the market corrects and startup hiring freezes.

This is a pass for me. The founders are competent and the market insight is real, but nothing about this company makes me uncomfortable in the way my best investments always do. It's too legible, too consensus, and aimed at the wrong end of the economic spectrum. I invest my own money in misfits solving problems that are inseparable from their own story, and I'm not finding that here.
