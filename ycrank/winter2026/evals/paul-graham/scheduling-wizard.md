# Scheduling Wizard -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that catches my attention first is the managed-service model. Every other company in physician scheduling sells software and says "here, figure it out." Scheduling Wizard says "give us your constraints and we'll hand you back a finished schedule." This is interesting because it's exactly the kind of strategic choice that looks wrong to most investors — managed services don't scale, everyone knows that — but it's the same pattern as the Collison installation. Patrick and John didn't say "here's our API docs, good luck." They said "right then, give me your laptop" and did the integration for the developer. Scheduling Wizard is doing the Collison installation for hospital departments. The question is whether the manual work is building something — whether each engagement encodes domain knowledge into their Scheduling Programming Language in a way that progressively automates the process — or whether it's just three people wrangling spreadsheets forever.

The origin of the idea passes my organic test, though not at the highest level. Sam Oberly was already a scheduling person before he became a healthcare scheduling person. He did Minor League Baseball scheduling. He did war games analysis at the Department of Defense, which is constraint-optimization work. Then he was at Johns Hopkins — one of the top academic medical centers in the country — and encountered a harder, uglier version of the same problem. This isn't Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive, but it's not a brainstorming session either. It's the natural path of someone deepening expertise in a specific type of problem, and the proximity to Johns Hopkins gave him direct access to the pain. The schlep factor is genuinely high: ACGME duty-hour regulations, contractual obligations, HIPAA compliance, Epic and Cerner integrations, department politics, fairness constraints across dozens of physicians with competing preferences. This is precisely the kind of regulatory-logistical mess that makes smart people unconsciously look the other way. The fact that they chose to absorb the schlep themselves rather than pushing it onto users via self-serve software makes the schlep willingness even more credible.

Fourteen hospitals — Mass General, Johns Hopkins, UT Southwestern, HCA Healthcare, UCSF — with a three-person team of recent graduates. That's a real signal. You don't get major academic medical centers to outsource a critical operational function by sending cold emails. Someone at these institutions trusted these three enough to hand over something important. The customer testimonial from a Johns Hopkins clinical fellow describing the service as "seamless" is thin but real. What I can't determine from the dossier is whether these departments are paying. If 14 hospitals are paying, this is a strong invest. If these are free pilots, it's a different story. The absence of any revenue signal is the single biggest gap in my evaluation. At pre-seed I don't expect much revenue, but I expect founders to mention it if it exists.

The bull case is genuinely compelling: the managed-service approach could be a brilliant wedge. Hospitals are drowning in software tools that add to the administrative burden rather than reducing it. A service that makes the problem disappear entirely — where the output is a finished schedule, not another dashboard to learn — has a fundamentally different value proposition. If the Scheduling Programming Language is accumulating real domain knowledge with each engagement, then every new department makes the service faster and cheaper to deliver, and at some point the automation is so good that it becomes software with a services wrapper. The QGenda exit at $2.5B validates that physician scheduling is a real, acquirable market. And the land-and-expand motion within hospital systems — 19 departments across 14 hospitals — maps well to how enterprise healthcare sales actually works.

What holds me back is straightforward. These are class-of-2024 founders — smart, clearly hustling, but with almost no professional experience. The CTO's LinkedIn still lists his job as GEICO product analyst. There are no public GitHub repos from two of the three founders, and Oberly's repos are mostly academic data analysis in R and MATLAB. I can't tell whether the Scheduling Programming Language is a genuine domain-specific language with real technical depth or a marketing term for some Python scripts with constraint solvers. YouShift, one batch ahead in YC and backed by Bain Capital Ventures, is attacking the same market with a self-serve approach that inherently scales better. And QGenda, now inside Hearst with $2.5B of validation and 694 employees, could decide to build a managed-service tier for academic medicine next quarter. Three recent graduates running a managed service against that competitive landscape need to be building automation very fast.

I'd want to sit with these founders for thirty minutes and ask one question: show me what the Scheduling Programming Language actually does. If it's a real DSL that encodes scheduling constraints in a composable, reusable way — if each department they onboard makes the next department 20% faster — then the managed service is a trojan horse for a genuinely defensible platform, and I'd move toward investing. If it's ad hoc code that requires manual customization each time, they're building a consulting firm in a market where incumbents have 694 employees and $2.5B acquisitions. The answer to that question determines whether this is a 70 or a 55.
