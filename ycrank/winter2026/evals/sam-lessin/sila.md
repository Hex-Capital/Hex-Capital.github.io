# Sila -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the thing that jumps out before anything else: Sila is building in the single hottest consensus category in enterprise software right now. "Agentic team chat" is not an insane idea -- it's the idea that literally every well-funded player in the productivity space is simultaneously pursuing. Glue raised $20M in October 2025 with David Sacks as co-founder, positioning with near-identical language. Dust.tt already has $7.3M ARR and $21.5M raised. Slack launched Agentforce. Microsoft Teams has Copilot with agent capabilities serving hundreds of millions of users. Notion is adding agentic features to a platform with $500M in annual revenue. When I run my primary filter on Sila -- is this a sane person with an insane idea? -- the answer is definitively no. This is a sane idea. Maybe the sanest idea in enterprise software in 2026. And when the idea is this consensus, the returns have already been priced out by competition before you even write a check.

The founders are technically capable Cornell CS graduates who shipped a real AI product -- a chatbot deployed across 16 classes -- and co-founded together before Sila, which I generally like seeing. Pre-existing co-founder relationships matter. But their specific credibility doesn't map to the problem they're attacking. Enterprise messaging is a deeply distribution-driven business. Slack won because Stewart Butterfield understood product virality from gaming. Teams won because Microsoft bundled it with 365. What in the founders' background -- building an AI course marketplace and a university chatbot -- gives them a structural edge in enterprise communication distribution? I don't see it. And when you're competing against David Sacks (who literally built and sold Yammer, the enterprise social network) at Glue, the credibility gap is not trivial.

This company fails my "cherry on top" test in the most direct way possible. If AI didn't exist, Sila doesn't exist. The AI capability IS the product. I've said this publicly and I'll say it again: the winner of the AI race depends on consumer distribution, and incumbents with distribution -- Apple, Google, Salesforce, Microsoft -- will capture the value. Sila's entire moat proposition is software-based: network effects from accumulated context, workflow lock-in from configured AI teammates. But those are exactly the kind of moats that are dissolving. If Slack ships a better Agentforce integration next quarter, what prevents every potential Sila customer from just staying on Slack? Nothing structural. There's no physical infrastructure, no regulatory position, no proprietary data that compounds independent of the product. The switching cost argument that works for established messaging platforms (conversation history, integrations, organizational habits) actually works *against* Sila -- it's the reason enterprises won't switch away from Slack and Teams *to* Sila, not the reason they'd stay.

Let me steelman the bull case, because it exists. If you squint, there's an Airtable-style exception argument: maybe "human-AI team communication" isn't a feature bolted onto Slack but a genuinely new primitive -- the way Airtable wasn't just a spreadsheet but a new no-code database primitive. If Sila becomes the default workspace where AI agents and humans collaborate, it could become infrastructure that other tools integrate with. The narrative -- "the operating system for the AI-augmented workplace" -- has theoretical infinity optionality. And the incumbents genuinely do face an architectural dilemma: rebuilding Slack from scratch around AI-native primitives risks breaking their existing integrations and contracts. The gap is real. But the problem is that this argument applies equally to Glue, which has twenty times the funding and a co-founder with direct enterprise social experience. A new primitive needs to be *yours* -- it needs to be something only you can establish. When six companies are racing to create the same primitive, it's not a primitive play. It's an application land-grab, and in application land-grabs, distribution and capital win.

The absence of any traction signal -- no revenue, no user counts, no waitlist size, nothing -- at a stage where Dust.tt already has $7.3M ARR and Glue has a shipped product, compounds every other concern. The founders' LinkedIn profiles still list saiera as their primary affiliation. The brand "Sila" competes for search visibility with at least four other companies including Sila Nanotechnologies, which has raised $375M+. These are execution details, not thesis-level concerns, but they accumulate in a category where speed and distribution are everything. I'm passing.
