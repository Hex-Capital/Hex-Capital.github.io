# Foaster -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The competitive landscape table in this dossier is the evaluation. Five funded companies — Distyl AI at $202 million, PromptQL at $136 million, Aily Labs at $101 million, Skan.ai at $57 million, Dialogue AI at $6 million — are building variations of the same thesis: AI can automate the work that management consultants do. When I can populate a comparison matrix with half a dozen players pursuing identical territory, there is no secret. There is a trend. And entrepreneurs swept up by trends rather than driven by hard analysis produce the competitive dynamics that destroy value for everyone involved. "AI will transform consulting" is the consensus of every venture firm, every McKinsey partner running an internal AI task force, and every LinkedIn thought leader posting about the future of professional services. The canonical screening question — what important truth do very few people agree with you on? — finds no answer here. Everyone agrees. That is the problem.

The innovator's dilemma framing deserves serious engagement, because it is the strongest structural argument in Foaster's favor. McKinsey genuinely cannot offer a service that is "3–5x more cost-effective" without cannibalizing the leverage model that generates its profits — partners billing out junior analysts at multiples of their cost. A new entrant that rebuilds consulting delivery around AI agents rather than human pyramids could, in theory, capture the market that incumbents are structurally unable to serve at the new price point. If Foaster's AI-conducted interviews genuinely produce superior organizational insight — not just cheaper approximations but actually better maps because they capture 100% of employees rather than a biased sample — then the incumbent response is paralyzed. This is real. But the innovator's dilemma is a description of market structure, not a monopoly strategy. It explains why McKinsey will not compete effectively, but it does not explain why Foaster will beat Distyl AI, or Aily Labs, or PromptQL, or the next dozen YC companies that will pitch the same thesis next batch. The dilemma creates an opportunity. It does not create a monopoly for any particular entrant.

Consulting is structurally hostile to power-law returns. It is a services business that scales linearly with headcount and engagement volume. The aspiration to make operational maps a "persistent asset" — enabling follow-on analyses "without starting from scratch" — gestures toward a product transition, but this is speculative and the dossier contains no evidence it has been attempted, let alone validated. The global consulting market exceeds $300 billion, which sounds impressive until you recognize that no firm has ever captured more than a low single-digit percentage of it. There are no network effects in consulting. There is no winner-take-all dynamic. A successful AI consulting firm might reach a few hundred million in revenue and remain perpetually in competition with a dozen others. That is a fine business. It is not a fund-returner. It is not zero to one — it is the same thing, done slightly differently.

The founders present an interesting contradiction. Alexandre Combes claims to have built Repondia, where AI agents handled 60,000+ customer calls for restaurant groups — directly relevant experience in deploying conversational AI at scale. The Werewolf benchmark on LLM social intelligence attracted 300,000+ views and was shared by OpenAI's president, suggesting genuine technical credibility in designing agent interactions. These are real credentials for building the product. But the dossier reveals no independent verification of Repondia — no web presence, no press, no Crunchbase profile — which makes the most relevant prior experience unassessable. And more fundamentally, technical capability in a competitive market is not investable in my framework. Brilliant engineers building another entry in a crowded category are still competing, and competition is not a problem you solve with better engineering. It is a structural condition you escape by finding a different market entirely.

The technology moat is nonexistent. The dossier states explicitly that "the core technology stack uses generally available foundation models." This is a software wrapper around commodity infrastructure. Every competitor has access to the same foundation models, the same API capabilities, the same ability to build interviewing agents. The per-client operational maps create modest switching costs within an existing account, but this is account-level stickiness, not a market-wide moat. No patents, no regulatory barriers, no physical-world component. A well-capitalized competitor could replicate the core product in months. We wanted flying cars — instead we got another API integration layer.
