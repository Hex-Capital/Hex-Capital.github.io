# Sherpa -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

In my *Zero to One* lectures and in every conversation I have with founders, I pose the same question: "What important truth do very few people agree with you on?" The answer Sherpa offers — that AI can automate A/B testing for websites — is not a truth that few people agree with. It is a truth that *everyone* agrees with. Every CRO platform vendor, every marketing technology analyst, every growth team at every Series B company is already building or buying exactly this capability. When I wrote that entrepreneurs who got swept up in cleantech were following social trends rather than hard analysis, I was describing a pattern, not just an era. The AI-applied-to-existing-SaaS-category wave is this decade's cleantech: dozens of teams wrapping the same foundation models around the same workflows, each claiming to be the autonomous version of something that already exists. Sherpa is an AI CRO tool in a market where Optimizely runs $400M in ARR, VWO generates $64M in revenue, and Mutiny has raised $72.6M specifically to build AI-powered website optimization. Where is the secret that the market disputes? I cannot find one.

The competitive landscape here is damning on its own terms. The dossier lists four direct competitors, and three of them are well-funded incumbents with established customer bases and distribution. Mutiny is the most lethal: it already serves B2B marketers with AI-generated content and personalization, it has $20.7M in revenue, and adding automated A/B test cycles to its existing platform is a feature sprint, not a company. The business-model-conflict argument — that Optimizely and VWO cannot automate because it would cannibalize their professional services revenue — is a real structural observation, but it does not apply to Mutiny, and it does not apply to any new entrant who decides to build the same JavaScript-snippet-plus-LLM architecture that Sherpa uses. The core technology is explicitly reproducible: generate marketing copy variants with a language model, inject them via JavaScript, measure conversion. This is a feature that will ship inside a dozen platforms within eighteen months, not a monopoly that compounds over decades.

The founders worked together at Lindy, which satisfies a minimal shared-prehistory criterion, but nothing in the dossier suggests the kind of extreme prior execution that triggers my highest conviction. Kinnan graduated in 2024 with a finance degree; Sonam built features at Microsoft and Lindy. These are competent early-career builders, not founders who have demonstrated the capacity to bend an industry through sheer force of definite will. More revealing is the absence of any articulated long-term vision in the dossier. "Turn more website visitors into customers" is a product tagline, not a thesis about the future. I see no evidence of a bold, specific multi-year plan — no irreversible commitment that would only make sense if a particular view of the world is correct. The company reads as a lean-startup iteration: ship a tool, find customers, optimize. That is the opposite of definite optimism.

The strongest argument for Sherpa would require believing that the data flywheel — learning from 1,047,213+ website visits — constitutes an early moat that compounds into structural dominance. If Sherpa accumulates enough cross-customer conversion data to predict winning variants before testing them, that predictive engine could become genuinely superior to manual hypothesis creation. This is the path from tool to monopoly: not the A/B testing itself, but the accumulated knowledge of what converts across industries, page types, and visitor segments. If that dataset reaches sufficient scale before incumbents match it, Sherpa could become the conversion intelligence layer that every website depends on. But one million visits is a rounding error at the scale of Optimizely's customer base, Lindy is the only named customer, and the claimed 15-20% conversion lift in 30 days is exactly what every CRO vendor promises. The data flywheel is theoretically real but empirically unproven, and the head start is measured in weeks, not years.

This is a pure software company built on a JavaScript snippet and API calls to foundation models — the most commoditizable architecture in the current technology landscape. There is no physical-world barrier, no proprietary hardware, no regulatory moat, no government customer creating irreversible switching costs. The technology is a thin layer atop someone else's model. I wanted flying cars, and what I see here is another SaaS dashboard. The power-law math does not work: even in the optimistic case, CRO tooling is a fragmented, competitive market where no single player has achieved winner-take-all dynamics in decades of trying. This company could become a reasonable small business. It will not return the fund.
