# EigenPal -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about EigenPal is that every customer logo on their website is a former employer of one of the founders. McKinsey -- Novak interned there. Elastic -- Blaszyk worked there. Accenture -- Novak interned there. MIT -- Novak studied there. This could be read two ways. The generous reading: they leveraged genuine relationships to land their first enterprise customers, which is exactly the kind of unscalable, door-to-door hustle I look for. The skeptical reading: these aren't real customers at all, but former colleagues lending their names. The dossier found no case studies, press releases, or independent verification of any of these relationships. At pre-seed I don't demand much traction evidence, but when a company prominently displays logos and claims "$1B+ revenue enterprises," the absence of any corroboration makes me uneasy.

The origin of this idea concerns me. Blaszyk came from Elastic, a search and data company. Novak came from McKinsey and Accenture, where you see a lot of enterprise document chaos. So there's professional adjacency to the problem. But professional adjacency isn't the same as organic discovery. I don't see the moment where a founder was personally drowning in broken OCR output and couldn't take it anymore. I see two smart people who observed document processing pain in corporate environments and decided it was a good market to enter. That's not disqualifying, but it puts the idea closer to "manufactured from professional exposure" than "grew organically from a problem they couldn't stop thinking about." The schlep is real -- banking compliance, on-prem deployment, SOC II certification -- and I respect that. Most AI startups avoid the enterprise compliance grind. But the schlep alone isn't enough without a compelling origin story.

The competitive landscape is the elephant in the room. Rossum has raised $114.5M and is doing nearly $45M in annual revenue. Hyperscience has raised roughly $300M. Google, AWS, and Azure all offer document extraction APIs bundled with cloud contracts enterprises already pay for. This is emphatically not a market hiding in plain sight due to schlep blindness -- it's a market that dozens of well-capitalized companies are already attacking. When I see a pre-seed company entering a space with this many funded competitors, I need an exceptionally clear answer to "why will you win?" EigenPal's differentiation -- evaluation-first workflows, on-prem deployment, natural language configuration -- these are features, not structural advantages. Features get copied. The underlying LLM-based extraction capability is commoditizing rapidly. This pattern -- entering a well-funded, well-understood market with feature differentiation -- is exactly what worries me about consensus categories.

The strongest bull case: LLMs genuinely changed what's possible in document processing in the last two years, and the incumbents (ABBYY, Kofax) are burdened with legacy template-based architectures they can't easily abandon without cannibalizing existing revenue. If EigenPal can move fast enough to establish enterprise relationships -- particularly the European bank deals, where compliance requirements create real switching costs -- they could build a moat before incumbents fully transition. Blaszyk's technical credentials are legitimate: Imperial CS, meaningful open-source contributions, deep experience at Elastic with search infrastructure. The docx-js-editor project (443 stars) demonstrates genuine document engineering capability, not just API wrappers. If Novak's claim of being a "3-time founder" means he's learned from prior attempts, and if the European bank pilots convert to real revenue, this could work. Enterprise document processing has enormous switching costs once you're embedded in a workflow.

But I keep coming back to two concerns. First, Novak's prior company Assetario appears to still be active. When a co-founder has an ongoing venture, I want clarity on commitment. Second, the technical moat question. Building an AI wrapper around document extraction is something any competent engineering team can do today. The real moat in enterprise IDP comes from accumulated training data, deep vertical expertise, and compliance infrastructure built over years -- which is exactly what the incumbents and well-funded competitors already have. EigenPal is betting they can outrun both the old guard and the new well-funded players on product velocity. That's a hard bet to make at pre-seed with no disclosed revenue.

Blaszyk is a genuine hacker-builder, and that counts for something significant with me. His GitHub profile shows real contributions to real infrastructure -- not weekend projects. The combination of Blaszyk's technical depth and Novak's enterprise sales exposure is a reasonable founding team for this problem. But a reasonable team in a crowded market with feature-level differentiation and unverified customer claims doesn't clear my bar for writing a personal check. This feels like a competent team that identified a real market through professional observation rather than organic necessity, entering a space where the competitive dynamics require either massive capital or a genuine technical breakthrough -- and I see neither.
