# Totalis -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's the thing that jumped out at me before anything else: Kalshi is sitting at a $22 billion valuation with over a billion dollars in recent funding, and they've already built the proprietary RFQ parlay system that Totalis wants to abstract across platforms. Polymarket acquired a derivatives platform. Robinhood is licensing Kalshi's technology directly. This isn't a David and Goliath story where David has a sling nobody expected — this is a pre-seed team trying to build a neutral infrastructure layer on top of companies that are structurally incentivized to never let that layer exist. When I backed SpaceX, there was no well-funded private rocket company that could replicate what Elon was building. The incumbents were bureaucratic government contractors who couldn't move. Here, the incumbents are fast-moving, well-capitalized startups who are already building the exact features Totalis proposes to offer. That's not a contrarian bet — that's running headlong into entrenched, motivated competition.

When I sit down with a founder, the first thing I want to hear is their story — not the TAM slide, not the competitive landscape, but what happened in their life that made this problem theirs. What I see with Eric and Pravesh is two deeply talented Waterloo students who are prolific hackathon builders — eleven wins, fifty-five thousand dollars in prizes, co-op rotations at blockchain companies, prediction market projects. That's impressive execution ability. But the story is "we were in the Waterloo Blockchain Club and saw an opportunity in prediction market derivatives." That's a market thesis, not a biographical forge. Nobody was burned by the absence of cross-platform parlays. Nobody's life was shaped by prediction market fragmentation. When I ask "what happened to you that made this idea find you?" I hear silence where I need to hear a story. Some of my best investments came from founders who couldn't NOT work on their problem — the Crusoe founders who watched energy being literally thrown away in fire, the Flock Safety founder whose community was suffering from property crime. The prediction market parlay problem is intellectually stimulating, but it doesn't have that gravitational pull from lived experience.

The "invisible system" test is where this falls apart for me most clearly. Prediction markets are, by their nature, transparency instruments — they make hidden beliefs about future events visible through price discovery. Building derivatives on top of that isn't exposing an opaque system; it's adding financial sophistication to an already-visible one. When I backed Affirm, the thesis was that credit card companies were hiding the true cost of borrowing from ordinary people. When I backed Ownwell, property tax assessments were a black box that extracted value from homeowners who didn't know they could fight back. Totalis isn't revealing a hidden tax or breaking an information asymmetry that hurts vulnerable people. It's building a better trading instrument for people who are already sophisticated enough to trade on prediction markets. The world doesn't become more transparent if Totalis succeeds — it becomes more complex for an already-sophisticated user base.

The strongest bull case would require believing that prediction markets are at the same inflection point private space launch was when I wrote my SpaceX check — that we're watching a $44 billion market that will become a multi-trillion-dollar financial infrastructure category, and that a neutral derivatives layer will be as essential to that ecosystem as Bloomberg Terminal is to traditional finance. The seventeen-times volume growth in a single year is genuinely extraordinary. If the CFTC's regulatory formalization creates a landscape with many competing exchanges, the neutral aggregation layer becomes more valuable, not less. And if these founders' deep DeFi engineering backgrounds and rapid prototyping ability let them ship a working cross-exchange parlay engine before incumbents lock the door, they could accumulate the kind of pricing data and market maker relationships that become a compounding moat. Pravesh building EigenBets before Totalis shows he's been circling this problem. For this bull case to work, though, you need Kalshi and Polymarket to keep their APIs open, the regulatory landscape to fragment rather than consolidate, and market makers to trust a pre-seed startup with their liquidity — each of those is a bet in itself, and needing all three to hit is a lot of simultaneous breakthroughs for one check.

This is a consensus space attracting massive capital, with no biographical inevitability from the founders, no invisible system being disrupted, and severe competitive exposure to well-funded incumbents who are building this internally. The founders are clearly talented engineers and relentless builders — the hackathon record proves that. But when I ask myself whether I can see this becoming something that changes who has access to what in the world, I come up empty. I wish them well, but this isn't a Long Journey bet.
