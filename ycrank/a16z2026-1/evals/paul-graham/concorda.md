# Concorda -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Concorda's most striking feature is one that should make any investor pause: legal AI is the hottest consensus space in enterprise software right now. Harvey is at $11B valuation with $190M ARR. EvenUp cleared $2B. Legaltech funding hit $4.3B in 2025, up 54% from the prior year. When I see a startup entering a market where everyone already agrees the opportunity is massive, my instinct is to check the exits — not because the market is wrong, but because the returns have already been competed away. The best startup ideas initially seem like bad ideas. "AI for lawyers" has not seemed like a bad idea to anyone since approximately mid-2023. YC has already backed Crimson, a London-based startup with near-identical positioning as "the AI associate for litigation lawyers." Concorda is arriving at a party that's already crowded, and the biggest guests brought billions in funding with them.

Samuel Oh's origin story is the best thing in this dossier. "I spent years drafting motions knowing there had to be a better way" — that's the sentence structure of authentic problem discovery. He practiced at Avantech Law, he drafted the motions himself, he felt the pain. This is the Dropbox pattern: Drew Houston kept forgetting his USB drive, Oh kept wasting hours on boilerplate motions. Both founders hold law degrees and have courtroom experience — Oh at Avantech, Ke Ma as an Assistant State's Attorney in Cook County. They are their own first users, which is exactly what I mean by "live in the future, then build what's missing." But Ma's McKinsey stint introduces a wrinkle I can't ignore. When one co-founder's most recent pre-startup experience is management consulting, the question becomes whether the company was born from personal frustration or from a strategy framework. The a16z Speedrun description — "AI operating system for trial lawyers" — has the polish of something that emerged from a positioning exercise, not a weekend hack. I've seen too many ex-consultants "identify opportunities" through competitive analysis and customer interviews to dismiss this pattern.

The $204K in annualized revenue within three months is genuinely notable for a two-person pre-seed company. That number does real work — it suggests someone is paying for what Concorda delivers, which is more than most companies at this stage can claim. But the dual services-plus-SaaS model is what makes me hesitate. Concorda isn't just building software; it's practicing law. The "AI-native litigation services" component means the company carries professional liability, malpractice exposure, and state bar regulatory requirements that pure software companies avoid. More fundamentally, services revenue scales linearly. Two people delivering legal services while simultaneously building a SaaS platform is either extraordinarily resourceful or a recipe for doing neither thing well. When I think about Stripe, the Collisons built pure infrastructure — they weren't also running a payments processing consultancy on the side. The services wedge might be tactically smart for landing early clients, but it creates a gravity that pulls founders toward billable hours and away from product iteration. The vision scales superlinearly only if the software eventually replaces the services. With two people, I'd want to see evidence that they're building toward that transition, not just generating revenue from both simultaneously.

What I don't see in this dossier is the thing I trust most: stories. Not credentials, not revenue numbers, not market sizing — stories of founders hacking their way through obstacles, building things at absurd speed, refusing to accept constraints that would stop normal people. Oh and Ma have strong resumes. Harvard CS, UChicago Law, Cornell, Northwestern Law, McKinsey, Cook County prosecution. But resumes tell me what institutions thought of them, not how they behave when everything breaks. The Airbnb founders didn't have impressive resumes — they had cereal boxes. I'd want to sit across from Oh and Ma for ten minutes and hear them describe what happened when their first client's case went sideways, or how they built their prototype, or what they did when Harvey announced a competing feature. The dossier gives me none of that. The absence isn't disqualifying at pre-seed — many founders simply haven't generated those stories yet — but it means I'm scoring on priors rather than evidence, and my priors for two credentialed professionals entering a consensus-hot market are moderate, not high.

The bull case for Concorda is that litigation-specific depth defeats horizontal breadth. Harvey serves eight of the top ten US law firms across contracts, compliance, due diligence, and litigation — but a trial lawyer preparing for a patent case might need the kind of end-to-end execution workflow that a general-purpose tool doesn't provide. Concorda's four modules (Case Command, Motions & Briefs, Discovery, Claim Charts) represent genuine domain architecture that would require Harvey significant investment to replicate at the same depth. If Oh and Ma can lock in five or ten mid-size litigation firms before Harvey prioritizes this vertical, they could build switching costs through firm-specific voice models and case data. The $204K revenue in three months suggests they've already started this process. For this bull case to hold, Concorda needs to convert services clients into software clients faster than Harvey builds downward into litigation — and faster than Crimson, EvenUp, Supio, and Thomson Reuters converge on the same niche. That's a lot of simultaneously favorable conditions.

Concorda lands in the territory of competent founders tackling a real problem in a market that no longer offers contrarian returns. Oh's personal litigation experience and the early revenue signal are genuine positives. But the consensus-hot market, the direct YC-backed competitor, the McKinsey co-founder pattern, and the absence of resourcefulness stories beyond credentials prevent me from getting excited. The two dimensions that most determine this verdict are Idea That Repels Before It Attracts — which scores low because legal AI repels no one — and Organic Problem Discovery, which scores reasonably well on Oh's direct experience but is diluted by Ma's consulting-to-startup trajectory and the overall polish of the positioning.
