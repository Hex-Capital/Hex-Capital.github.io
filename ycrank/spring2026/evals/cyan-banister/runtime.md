# Runtime -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Gus Trigos shipped four full-stack products in three months at Nuvocargo after his previous company Mentum was acquired, and in the process discovered that non-engineers could ship software too — if someone built the guardrails and control plane around coding agents. That's the origin story of Runtime, and I want to be honest: it's a real story. He lived inside the workflow before he decided to productize it. He's a repeat YC founder (S21 Mentum, $4.2M raised from Gradient Ventures, acquired in 2025), and he's back in the current batch building on something he directly experienced. But when I ask my first question — "tell me your story, not the business" — what I hear is professional pattern recognition, not biographical inevitability. Trigos didn't grow up navigating broken systems or arrive at Runtime through a crucible that left him no choice. He saw an operational gap at his day job and built a product for it. That's good entrepreneurship. It's not the kind of forging that makes me write a check I'm willing to lose.

Runtime's competitive positioning is the most analytically interesting — and concerning — feature of this company. E2B has raised $21M and signed 88% of Fortune 100 companies. Daytona raised $31M total and crossed $1M ARR within two months of relaunch. Runloop raised $7M from Scale AI and Google veterans. Runtime has 105 GitHub stars. The dossier notes that E2B has already announced "orchestration tools for managing fleets of AI agents," which is precisely Runtime's value proposition. Meanwhile, Anthropic's Claude Code already ships with built-in cost tracking and permission controls. Runtime sits in a layer between agent vendors who have every incentive to build team management into their own products and infrastructure providers who are already expanding upstream. When I backed Uber, the taxi medallion system couldn't adapt — it was structurally calcified. When I backed Carta, incumbents had no economic incentive to make cap tables transparent. Runtime's neighbors, by contrast, are agile venture-backed companies and well-resourced AI labs with direct incentive to absorb this functionality.

The space Runtime operates in — AI developer tools, coding agent management — is the opposite of the deals that have defined my career. My biggest wins came from spaces where other investors physically recoiled: private rockets blowing up on launchpads, surveillance cameras in neighborhoods, male birth control. Runtime is building in a category where VCs are competing to deploy capital. The AI Code Assistants market is projected at $127B by 2032 with a 48% CAGR according to MarketsandMarkets. Every partner at every firm in San Francisco can articulate why this is a good market. That's exactly the signal that tells me this isn't my kind of bet. Runtime's thesis requires zero courage to state at a dinner party. Nobody shifts in their chair when you say "we need better observability for coding agents." There's no stigma, no regulatory frontier, no weirdness beneath the surface.

I want to give the bull case its full weight because I've been wrong before. The Carta parallel is the strongest argument: Runtime could become the system of record for agent sessions the way Carta became the system of record for cap tables. If every coding agent session routes through Runtime's control plane — with guardrails, audit trails, spend tracking, role-based access — the switching costs compound. Runtime supports five different agents (Claude Code, Codex, Gemini CLI, Copilot, OpenCode), and no single agent vendor has incentive to build multi-vendor management. The agent-agnostic positioning is genuinely smart. And the timing is real: coding agents crossed from individual developer toys to organizational infrastructure in 2024-2025, creating the management gap Runtime addresses. Trigos's open-core licensing (AGPLv3 server, Apache 2.0 CLI) creates a developer adoption funnel. If Runtime captures the "Datadog for coding agents" position, this is a massive outcome. But here's where the parallel breaks: Carta's cap table data was structural and permanent — you couldn't rip it out without losing your equity records. Runtime's session data is ephemeral. A company can switch control planes between sprints. The moat that made Carta defensible doesn't exist here, and the 105 GitHub stars versus E2B's Fortune 100 adoption tells me the network effects haven't started compounding.

Runtime solves a legible problem — engineering leaders need visibility into what agents are doing — but legibility is the issue. There's no invisible system being made visible here. When I backed Affirm, credit card companies were actively obscuring lending terms from consumers who didn't understand compound interest. When I backed Ownwell, the property tax assessment system was deliberately opaque. Runtime's customers are sophisticated engineering leaders who already know they need agent management. The problem is operational, not structural. Nobody is being exploited by information asymmetry in the coding agent space. Runtime makes a chaotic new workflow more organized, which is valuable, but it doesn't reveal a hidden tax or shift power from gatekeepers to users. The access expansion angle — enabling PMs and designers to ship code without Git knowledge — has real impact, but the beneficiaries are already-employed knowledge workers at tech companies, not underserved communities gaining access to something previously gatekept.

One condition would flip me: if Runtime showed me proprietary data revealing that enterprises are losing 40-60% of their coding agent spend to unmonitored, duplicative, or hallucination-driven agent sessions — a genuine "invisible tax" that engineering leaders don't realize they're paying — and that Runtime's observability layer captures and compounds this data into a defensible benchmarking advantage that no sandbox provider or agent vendor can replicate, I'd revisit. That would transform Runtime from an operational dashboard into an intelligence layer exposing a hidden cost structure. But I don't see that evidence today. What I see is a competent repeat founder building consensus tooling in a crowded, well-funded space with moderate technical barriers and weak structural defensibility. Trigos earned my respect with the Mentum exit and the genuine Nuvocargo origin story, but Runtime doesn't activate my framework. The biographical forge is professional, not personal. The space is hot, not hard. The system being addressed is visible, not hidden. This isn't a Long Journey bet.
