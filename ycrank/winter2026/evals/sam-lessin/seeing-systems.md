# Seeing Systems -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The first thing that strikes me about Seeing Systems is how deeply consensus this market is right now. Defense tech funding hit $49.1 billion in 2025, a near-doubling from the prior year. Every pitch deck in the Valley leads with Ukraine as the proof point for autonomous drones. NATO procurement offices are practically begging for this category. When I see a company building exactly what every defense-focused VC is hunting for, my filter immediately trips -- because the returns on consensus-validated opportunities in hot markets get competed away by capital before the product ships. Building military FPV strike drones in 2026 is not an insane idea. It is possibly the most sane idea in defense technology right now. That is, for my framework, a problem.

The Le Maitre brothers do clear the "sane" bar convincingly. Matthew's trajectory -- Cambridge Physics and Computer Science, the Mobile Robotics Lab, then Jane Street, which selects for a very specific kind of rigorous quantitative mind -- gives me confidence he can build the software stack. Alexander's self-taught embedded electronics background with prior EOD hardware for military users demonstrates he can ship atoms into contested environments, not just demos into VC meetings. Brothers as co-founders is one of the strongest relationship signals I know -- deeper than any co-founder matching service can replicate, and the durability under stress matters enormously in defense, where procurement cycles will test your patience in ways consumer startups never do. The founder quality is real. But a sane person pursuing a sane idea is my least interesting category. I need the idea to provoke genuine confusion or dismissal from sophisticated investors, and this one provokes the opposite -- enthusiasm.

Where my framework gets genuinely engaged is the physical-world moat architecture. Forward Deployed Engineers embedded with each military unit is the Palantir playbook, and Palantir's moat turned out to be one of the deepest in enterprise software precisely because relationships, operational context, and switching costs compound when you're physically present with your customer. The modularity thesis -- swap payloads, sensors, compute, and comms rather than replace entire platforms -- creates potential for a platform standard that military units train around and build logistics chains for. Once a Royal Marine Commando squad has standardized on your modular architecture and your training programs, ripping that out to install a competitor's system involves retraining, requalifying, and rebuilding supply chains. Those switching costs are real, physical, and AI cannot replicate them. The fact that they already have UK Royal Marines, multiple UK units, and four NATO forces using their prototypes at pre-seed is extraordinary traction for a two-person team. For reference, Stark had $100 million in funding and failed all four strikes in UK military trials last October. Seeing Systems is inside those same UK units with working hardware on a fraction of the capital.

The bull case I have to genuinely wrestle with: if Aerie AI becomes the coordination layer that NATO forces standardize on for swarm operations across drone types, this stops being a product company and starts becoming infrastructure. That's the primitive-layer play hiding inside what looks like an application-layer drone company -- the same structural observation that made Airtable interesting to me even though it looked like SaaS on the surface. If Aerie AI evolves from coordinating their own drones to coordinating any drone platform in a NATO theater, they've created a new primitive for multi-agent military coordination. The fibre-optic guided variant for jamming immunity addresses a problem that is getting worse every month as electronic warfare advances, and most RF-dependent competitors have no answer yet. If you squint right, there's an Airtable-shaped exception here -- what looks like a product is actually a coordination primitive waiting to emerge.

But I can't invest on squinting. Right now, Aerie AI is "in development," not deployed. The FPV drone hardware itself is deeply commoditized -- Ukraine produces four million drones per year, and basic FPV designs are widely available. The core hardware moat is thin. The software moat is hypothetical. And the capital mismatch is severe: Shield AI at $5.6 billion, Anduril at $30.5 billion, and Stark at $100 million are operating in overlapping segments with resources that dwarf a $500K YC check by orders of magnitude. Defense procurement rewards incumbency and scale in ways that consumer markets do not -- there is no viral growth hack for getting approved as a defense vendor across multiple NATO countries with their respective export control regimes. A two-person team navigating ITAR, the UK Export Control Act, and EU dual-use regulations simultaneously is not operationally realistic without significant scaling.

The missing signal that would flip me is evidence that the modularity architecture or Aerie AI represents something structurally different from what well-funded competitors can build -- a genuine new primitive rather than a smarter product architecture. If Aerie AI had demonstrated multi-platform coordination with third-party drones and was being adopted as a standard by military units independent of Seeing Systems hardware, I would reassess this entirely. That would make this a protocol play, not a product play, and I invest very differently in protocols. But the dossier shows a hardware company with an AI roadmap, not a coordination protocol with a hardware demonstration vehicle. In the current configuration, this is a well-executed application-layer defense company in the hottest, most competitive segment of the defense market, built by credible founders whose idea provokes excitement rather than skepticism. I need skepticism.
