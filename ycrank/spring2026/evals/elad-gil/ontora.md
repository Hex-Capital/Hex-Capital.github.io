# Ontora -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Max Arnold spent a year at Porsche AG building agentic SAP process mining for procurement, and that system is now deployed across four Volkswagen subsidiaries. That's the single most interesting fact in Ontora's dossier — not because it proves the product works, but because it demonstrates something I weight heavily: the founder built and deployed the core concept at production scale inside a Fortune Global 500 organization before starting the company. When I backed Harvey, I wasn't evaluating Winston's biography for its own sake — I was confirming that the founder understood the buyer's workflow at a molecular level. Arnold's Porsche deployment, combined with Leon Iwanowitsch's experience building AI automations inside consultancies, means Ontora's team has seen both the technology build and the buyer persona from the inside. That said, neither Arnold nor Iwanowitsch has scaled a company. Arnold led seven engineers at sterna.studio and was a project lead at Porsche — strong execution credentials, but there's a difference between deploying a system inside an existing enterprise and building an organization from three people to three hundred. Ontora has a clear CEO designation (Iwanowitsch), which avoids my co-founder ambiguity red flag, but the scaling test remains unproven.

"AI will disrupt management consulting" is consensus — possibly the single most discussed enterprise AI application in business media right now. McKinsey's layoffs are front-page news. Everyone from Y Combinator founders to LinkedIn influencers is predicting the end of $500K diagnostic engagements. That makes Ontora's market timing, on the surface, obvious rather than nonobvious. But Ontora's specific angle deserves sharper scrutiny. Celonis ($2.4B raised, ~$771M revenue) mines system event logs. Skan AI ($57.6M raised, $22.2M revenue) captures screen activity. Glean (~$800M raised) searches existing documents. None of them capture the 80% of operational knowledge that Ontora claims is tacit — knowledge that was never written down and never generated a system log. The specific insight that conversational extraction of undocumented processes is the missing layer for enterprise AI is more differentiated than the generic "AI replaces consultants" thesis. Ontora's claim that 95% of enterprise GenAI pilots deliver zero business impact because agents lack operational context, if directionally true, positions the company not just as consulting replacement but as a prerequisite for enterprise AI to work at all. That reframing is more interesting than the surface-level pitch. Still, the structural shift here — LLMs crossing a quality threshold for nuanced interviews — is visible to every process mining company and every consulting firm. Celonis could add conversational discovery to its platform. The window exists, but I wouldn't call it nonobvious.

Ontora's infrastructure ambitions are visible in the product architecture — a Unified Context Layer, an API/MCP Server, and the stated goal of feeding operational knowledge into other AI tools — but the go-to-market tells a different story. The website positions Ontora as a consulting replacement at ~$50K per engagement versus $500K+ for McKinsey. That's an application-layer value proposition: run the process, deliver the report, collect the check. The infrastructure play — becoming the default operational context layer that every enterprise AI deployment depends on — would look like Stripe's trajectory, where a simple payment API became the payments infrastructure an entire generation of companies built on. Ontora's context layer could follow that path: start with consulting replacement as the wedge, accumulate proprietary process data, then become the API that enterprise AI agents call to understand how a company actually works. But wedge-to-platform transitions are aspirational until they're not, and right now Ontora has one testimonial from a strategy manager at Vertiv and no evidence of API adoption or platform-layer usage. The infrastructure potential elevates Ontora above a pure services play, but I'd need to see customers integrating the context layer into their AI stack before scoring this as a genuine infrastructure company.

McKinsey's inability to cannibalize their own $500K engagements is the structural incumbent paralysis that draws my attention. BCG derived 20% of 2024 revenue from AI consulting — they're investing in AI for clients, not automating their own diagnostic methodology. That's the classic innovator's dilemma: the incumbent's business model prevents them from deploying the disruption, even when they see it coming. Ontora occupies the kind of boring, operationally essential category — process discovery and knowledge mapping — that I've seen outperform glamorous categories repeatedly. Nobody at a demo day gets excited about interviewing employees to map procurement workflows. That's actually a competitive advantage: fewer prestige-seeking founders enter the market, and the companies that do build sticky, high-margin businesses. Gusto did this in payroll, Checkr did it in background checks. Ontora's process discovery category has similar characteristics: universal demand, operational necessity, and incumbents structurally unable to compete on the new paradigm.

The single-miracle test for Ontora is moderately favorable. The primary breakthrough required is that LLM-driven interviews must extract accurate, actionable tacit knowledge at a quality level C-suite executives will trust for strategic decisions. That miracle is partially exogenous — foundation model capability improves on its own curve, and Ontora benefits from each quality increment without having to build the models themselves. The dossier flags LLM hallucination risk at the C-suite decision level, which is real but progressively mitigating. Enterprise sales cycles (6-12 months) and data sensitivity compliance (SOC 2, HIPAA, GDPR) are genuine execution challenges for a three-person team, but I categorize these as standard operational hurdles rather than independent miracles — they're solvable with competent execution and don't require structural breakthroughs. Ontora's miracle count is closer to one than three, which keeps the company within my acceptable risk envelope.

The strongest bull case for Ontora centers on three facts: Arnold's production deployment at Porsche/VW proves the core concept works at enterprise scale, the consulting incumbent paralysis creates a structural moat against the most likely competitors, and the context layer positioning could evolve into genuine enterprise AI infrastructure. If I saw evidence that Ontora's API/MCP Server was being integrated into customer AI workflows — not just delivering reports but serving as a persistent operational knowledge layer — I'd reassess aggressively upward. That would transform Ontora from a consulting tool into infrastructure, and infrastructure during a platform shift is where I write my largest checks. The bear case is equally concrete: Ontora's ~$50K engagement pricing suggests project-based revenue without clear recurring dynamics, the technical moat is moderate (any team with frontier LLM access could build structured interview capabilities), and Celonis with $2.4B in funding could add conversational process discovery as a feature rather than a competitor. Ontora's margin architecture is unproven — project-based consulting replacement doesn't naturally produce the negative net revenue churn and pricing power I look for. If six months from now Ontora is still selling one-off diagnostic engagements without evidence of platform adoption or recurring revenue, the business model concern becomes disqualifying.

Ontora lands in my neutral range. The market timing is real but closer to consensus than nonobvious — "AI disrupts consulting" is the most discussed enterprise AI thesis in the market today, even if Ontora's tacit knowledge angle adds genuine differentiation. The infrastructure aspiration exists in the product architecture but remains unproven in the go-to-market. Arnold's Porsche deployment and Iwanowitsch's consulting domain knowledge create strong founder-market fit without demonstrated scaling capability. The one condition that would flip me to invest: evidence that Ontora's context layer is being consumed as infrastructure by customer AI systems — API calls, MCP integrations, persistent operational knowledge serving other tools — rather than delivered as project-based reports. That would signal the wedge-to-platform transition that separates applications from infrastructure companies.
