# Parasma -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Sean Cole built a viral demo and now has a company; Parasma is what happens when shipping rhythm is the strongest signal in the dossier and index position is the weakest. The high/low signature is unambiguous: Parasma scores like a founder I'd want to meet attached to a structural position I'd never underwrite -- an algorithm layer sitting on top of Cortical Labs' CL1 (a single-vendor $35K wetware box from an $11.6M-raised Melbourne startup) with one shipped artifact, no revenue, and a platform owner that already ships the Python interface Sean used.

**Perplexity (2022)** is the load-bearing analogy on founder shipping, and the parallel is real, not generous. Aravind pinged me on LinkedIn and showed up a week later with the thing built; Sean Cole, with "little experience in biological computing" per ZME Science, paired 200,000 human neurons to a learning algorithm and got them stumbling through Doom in roughly one week, then put the repo on GitHub as `doom-neuron` and earned Scientific American, Tom's Hardware, and Popular Science coverage between February and April 2026. That is the Perplexity-pattern of "built between meetings" rather than the McKinsey-MBA-deck pattern. Where Parasma diverges from the Perplexity rung: Aravind shipped a *standalone product* with its own distribution; Sean Cole shipped a *demo on Cortical Labs' Cortical Cloud*, which means the shipping artifact and the vendor-dependency liability are the same artifact.

**Lyft Series C** is the cautionary rung on index position, but the failure mechanism for Parasma is sharper than #2-in-a-network-market -- it's *#1 in a layer the platform owner can absorb in a sprint*. Cortical Labs already ships the Cortical Cloud Python interface that Sean used; FinalSpark already exposes its Neuroplatform's 16 organoids over a Python API. The dossier states plainly that "the hardware vendor can extend upstream into the algorithm layer," and with Cortical Labs at $11.6M total raised and pre-revenue themselves, they have every incentive to. Parasma is not in the workflow path of a compounding category the way Stripe sits in e-commerce; Parasma is in the *upgrade path* of two specific vendors who outweigh it on capital, wet-lab access, and customer relationships.

Parasma's market catalyst is real but supply-side, and that's the wrong shape. Cortical Labs' March 2025 CL1 commercial launch and the Cortical Cloud Python interface are a genuine 24-month catalyst -- programmable wetware was lab-only before that, full stop. The mechanism that fails over 18 months: an AI-energy thesis ("one million times less energy" per FinalSpark) only compounds into revenue if buyers are *actively shopping* for substrate alternatives, and the dossier shows zero buyer pull -- DARPA O-CIRCUIT funds R&D directly, hyperscalers aren't named, and no LOI counterparties exist. Supply-side catalysts without buyer pull produce demos, not curves; Parasma has demos.

Revenue velocity is where the AI-cycle test gets brutal. The counterfactual for Parasma to flip a tier on this dimension: a named research-lab or hyperscaler pilot with disclosed dollar value, signed in the last 90 days. The dossier gives me press from six publications and a University of Sussex Facebook post -- attention, not buying behavior. "If you're an AI company and you're not seeing explosive growth quickly, something's fundamentally broken" -- Parasma is six months into public visibility with no MRR disclosed, no Product Hunt, no pilot, no LOI. That is the *false signal* pattern from my TechCrunch piece, where press inflates founder-narrative ahead of curve.

**Color Genomics (2015)** is the only single-anchor parallel that matters on the single-miracle test, and it cuts against Parasma. Color worked because commodity sequencing was a *multi-vendor* hard layer -- Illumina, BGI, others -- and I *operated* the company rather than passively angel-checked it. Parasma requires four independent miracles: neuron throughput reaches commercially useful levels (currently "stumbling through Doom, badly"), Cortical Labs and FinalSpark don't absorb the algorithm layer, enterprise AI buyers actually adopt biological substrate over GPUs at scale, and the regulatory/ethics posture on human-cortical-tissue compute holds. That's not a single miracle isolated to GTM execution; that's the science-project compound-probability stack I've spent fifteen years staying out of.

**36-month forecast:** Parasma is acqui-hired by Cortical Labs for under $10M as their in-house algorithms team within 24 months, or pivots to research-services contracts with university labs and DARPA-adjacent grants by month 36. Independent path to $10M ARR: I don't see it. The mechanism is the index-position failure compounding with the platform owner's incentive to absorb the layer that makes their hardware useful.

The strongest bull case rhymes with my **Color Genomics** Known Exception: workflow infrastructure on top of a hard-tech layer, founder with direct demonstrated execution on that exact stack, category with a credible energy-economics tailwind, and a viral demo functioning as cold-inbound distribution the way Aravind's pings did for Perplexity. Sean Cole on paper is a Perplexity-pattern solo founder in a Color-pattern category. The bear case wins on two facts the dossier surfaces directly: Color sat on *commodity multi-vendor* sequencing and I operated it, while Parasma sits on *single-vendor proprietary* wetware and Sean is solo with no co-founder, no advisors disclosed, no wet lab. The flip condition I'd actually pay attention to: Sean signs non-exclusive algorithm-layer deals with both Cortical Labs and FinalSpark within 12 months, and discloses >$500K in research-lab revenue. Absent that, this is a talented founder in the wrong structural position, and the right move is to watch Sean's next company rather than write into this one.
