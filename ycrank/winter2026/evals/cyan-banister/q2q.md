# Q2Q -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice here isn't the product or the founders — it's that the company is already dead. Q2Q wound down during its own YC batch, and Brianna pivoted to Copperlane with a different co-founder. Claire Wu's whereabouts after the wind-down aren't documented. When I see a co-founding pair dissolve that quickly, I think about HQ Trivia — though this is a quieter version of that lesson. The fact that the partnership didn't survive even to pivot together tells me something about the foundation, even if I don't know the specifics. I'd want to understand what happened between them, because the story of how a team breaks apart often reveals more than the story of how they came together.

If I could sit down with Brianna and Claire and ask them to tell me their stories — not the business, just how they got here — I'd hear about two accomplished Penn M&T students who worked at exactly the firms you'd expect: Jefferies, Palantir, Qualtrics, a PE buyout shop. These are impressive credentials, and Brianna clearly understood the deal sourcing workflow from the inside. But that's professional proximity to a problem, not the kind of biographical commitment I look for. When I backed Contraline, it was because the founder had spent years in academic research that everyone else ignored — the problem was woven into his life's work. Here, the connection between founder and problem feels more like "I noticed an inefficiency at my last job" than "I have to solve this because nobody else will." And the speed of the pivot to mortgage lending reinforces that — if the PE deal sourcing problem were truly personal, you don't walk away from it in a few months.

The idea itself sits squarely in consensus territory. "AI agent automates outbound for PE firms" is the kind of pitch that every accelerator saw fifty variations of in 2025. There were at least fifteen competitors in the space, and Datasite was already consolidating — acquiring both Grata and Blueflame AI. When I look for companies to back, I'm drawn to the ones that other investors find uncomfortable or confusing. Nobody found this confusing. It was a straightforward LLM application layered on top of a sales workflow, with no meaningful technology risk and no structural barrier to replication. The commoditization risk was high from day one, and the market proved it.

To be fair to the bull case: if Q2Q had survived and executed well, you could imagine a world where accumulating proprietary data on owner contact patterns and outreach response rates created a compounding advantage over time. Brianna's PE background was genuine domain knowledge, and the insight that PE firms need end-to-end outbound automation — not just another database — was directionally correct. If they'd found product-market fit with a handful of PE firms willing to let an AI phone agent call business owners on their behalf, that could have been a wedge. But "could have been a wedge" is different from the jolt of intuitive energy I feel when something is genuinely special. I never felt that here.

What I can't get past is that Q2Q doesn't create economic access for anyone who needs it. It makes wealthy PE firms slightly more efficient at acquiring companies. There's nothing wrong with that as a business, but it doesn't activate the part of my investing lens that cares about whether a company alleviates poverty or makes invisible systems visible. When I think about Uber creating income for drivers, or Affirm making credit transparent, or Flock Safety bringing safety data to communities — those companies changed the economic reality for people who had been excluded. Q2Q served people who already had every advantage. That's a fundamental misalignment with how I think about where my money should go.
