# snag -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Selin Sonmez and Niko Georgantas have been building together for a long time — Knock Knock City, LuggageHero, OneRoof, now snag. Three ventures, same co-founders, same city, same domain orbit. That pattern tells me something real about their relationship and their stubbornness, and the Knock Knock City exit to LuggageHero proves they can ship a product that someone else values enough to acquire. But when I run my opening question — tell me their story, not the business — I get designers from Pratt Institute who moved through Red Antler and IBM and eventually identified a market gap in student housing. That's a career arc, not a crucible. I don't see the biographical moment where housing insecurity or the sublet system's opacity became something Selin or Niko experienced in a way that made them incapable of working on anything else. Forbes 30 Under 30 and Seth Godin's altMBA are impressive credentials. They are not forging events. The pivot from OneRoof ($4.6M raised from General Catalyst for a neighbor community app) to snag in late 2025 reads like smart founders iterating toward product-market fit — but iterating toward a problem is different from being shaped by one.

The traction numbers deserve attention: $6.5M in booking requests in 30 days with 40% month-over-month growth, 30,000 student users, 3,000 listings, and a 4.7-star iOS rating across 184 reviews — all for a product released November 2025. Five months to that kind of marketplace density in NYC is genuinely impressive execution. But I need to flag the metric ambiguity that the dossier identifies: "booking requests" is not completed bookings, and no revenue signal exists anywhere in the record. I don't know if snag takes a transaction fee, a listing fee, or nothing at all. The subletter earnings displayed on the website (Maku: +$55,781; Anastasia: +$41,400) are customer earnings, not company revenue. For a company backed by a16z through Speedrun, I'd want to understand whether those booking requests are converting and whether there's a monetization mechanism beyond hope.

The sublet market IS opaque — I know that world. Young people navigating housing through Facebook groups and Instagram DMs with zero verification, inconsistent quality, and all the information asymmetry favoring whoever already holds the lease. That resonates with me personally. But when I apply my invisible system test rigorously, snag is solving a coordination problem more than it's dismantling an extractive structure. The taxi medallion cartel extracted billions from riders through regulatory capture. Credit card companies extract through deliberate opacity in interest terms. The sublet market's problem is fragmentation, not extraction — Facebook groups aren't running a racket, they're just terrible at organizing housing information. Snag makes the disorganized visible and verified, and that has genuine value. It's just not the structural power shift I look for in my highest-conviction bets — compare this to what Affirm did to credit card opacity or what Ownwell does to property tax systems, where the extractive mechanism was deliberate and the transparency is revolutionary.

Nothing about snag makes a sophisticated investor shift in their chair. A GenZ housing marketplace is consensus-friendly — everyone agrees young people need flexible housing, everyone understands marketplace mechanics, and the competitive landscape confirms broad investor interest with Blueground at $352M raised and June Homes at $50M before its acquisition. NYC Local Law 18 pushing demand from sub-30-day stays into the 1-3 month sublet window is a smart regulatory tailwind, but it's a visible, well-understood catalyst, not a contrarian insight that others are missing. The AI social-post-to-listing conversion is clever product work, but the dossier correctly notes any team with ML capability can replicate it — this isn't proprietary infrastructure, it's a feature. Snag's real defensibility is geographic network density: 30K users and 3K listings in NYC creates genuine two-sided liquidity that a new entrant can't easily match. But that density must be rebuilt city by city, and the seven university partnerships (NYU, New School, Pratt, Baruch, NYIT, UAlbany, Pace) are replicable by any competitor willing to do campus-by-campus outreach.

The strongest bull case: Selin is a 3x consumer founder with a prior exit, and the Depop parallel is real — Depop built a GenZ marketplace that aggregated a fragmented peer-to-peer market (fashion resale) and sold to Etsy for $1.6B. If snag can achieve similar cultural identity among GenZ renters, the path to a large outcome exists. The NYC regulatory environment genuinely advantages 1-3 month sublets over Airbnb-style short-term rentals, creating structural demand. And the co-founder relationship across three ventures suggests the kind of resilience I value — they've survived a pivot and kept building. For this to become a Long Journey bet, I would need to see that snag's monetization works (conversion from request to booking to revenue), that the playbook transfers beyond NYC, and that the founders have a deeper personal stake in this problem than serial iteration. The cap table complexity from OneRoof's $4.6M raise and the ambiguity around remaining runway from the prior product are diligence items I'd want resolved before any check.

Snag is a competently built consumer marketplace with real early traction, founded by experienced operators who know NYC housing. The product solves a genuine coordination problem for an underserved population. But through my lens — the lens of someone who bets on founders forged by the problem itself, operating in spaces that provoke discomfort, attacking extractive systems that exploit the powerless — snag doesn't activate my conviction. It's a good company in a recognizable category. It's not magically weird, and the founders' story, while admirable, is one of persistent iteration toward opportunity rather than biographical inevitability. I wish Selin and Niko well — their persistence across three ventures is real — but the dimensions that drive my highest-conviction checks (biographical forge, contrarian discomfort, structural extraction) are where snag scores lowest against my framework.
