# Return Signals -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Return Signals is the textbook example of what I mean when I say AI is a bad startup investment. Strip away the LLM and ask: what is this business? It's an SMS service that texts customers after delivery. The AI isn't the cherry on top -- it IS the sundae. My fundamental test is whether a company would be a good business if AI didn't exist, and here the answer is unambiguously no. Without the LLM generating conversational messages, you have a template-based outreach tool indistinguishable from what Attentive and Postscript have offered for years. That structural reality dominates everything else in my analysis.

Ilya Valmianski is credibly sane -- a Physics PhD who built SmartTriage at Kaiser Permanente, which he describes as one of the largest patient-facing ML applications deployed across KP Southern California. That's genuine infrastructure-scale conversational AI experience. His claim of a prior company reaching $40M ARR (unverified but stated on the YC page) adds to the credibility. But a sane person with a sane idea is my least favorite configuration. "Use AI to proactively text customers about potential returns" is not an idea that makes VCs uncomfortable or confused -- it's an idea that makes every e-commerce operator nod and say "obviously." The insight is correct but consensus, which means the market has already priced it in. Every incumbent in the SMS and returns ecosystem is looking at this exact play. The co-founder pairing also gives me pause -- Alejandro's BD background at Blueground and Nestpick is relevant for sales, but there's no visible shared history between the founders, and the jump from healthcare AI to e-commerce returns feels more like opportunity-seeking than domain obsession.

The competitive dynamics here trigger every alarm in my framework. Attentive is at $500M ARR. Klaviyo is public at $1.2B ARR. Loop Returns has raised $176M. These are incumbents with massive e-commerce distribution, existing SMS infrastructure, and engineering teams that could build a post-delivery conversational AI feature in a quarter. When I say "winner of the AI race depends on consumer distribution," this is exactly what I mean -- the company with the existing brand relationships and SMS pipes will capture this value, not the startup that needs to convince brands to add another vendor. The Emotive acquisition by Privy in July 2025 is instructive: a conversational SMS company with $74M in funding couldn't survive as a standalone business and got absorbed. That's not a cleared competitive landscape -- it's a warning about the category's viability as an independent company.

The strongest bull case requires believing that returns-specific conversational data creates a compounding moat over time -- that as Return Signals processes millions of return conversations across hundreds of brands, the system accumulates sizing curves, quality-issue patterns, and exchange-preference data that no competitor can replicate without equivalent deployment. If the 70% return-to-exchange conversion rate and 5-10% margin uplift numbers hold at scale, the ROI story to brands is compelling. And if they can lock in enough mid-market apparel brands before Attentive or Loop ships their version, the switching costs from brand-specific trained models could be real. I take this seriously. But this moat is entirely theoretical at pre-seed -- there are no named customers, no disclosed revenue, and no evidence the data flywheel has begun spinning. The Birchbox lesson haunts me here: a genuine insight about customer behavior that's trivially replicable by better-capitalized players. Birchbox understood subscription discovery economics better than anyone, and it didn't matter because the model had no structural defense.

This company also fails my primitive test completely. Return Signals is an application built on three layers of someone else's infrastructure: SMS carriers (deliverability and cost controlled by telcos), LLM APIs (model quality controlled by foundation model companies), and Shopify's platform (distribution controlled by Shopify's ecosystem decisions). That's triple platform dependency with no path to becoming infrastructure that others build on. The narrative ceiling is correspondingly visible -- "post-purchase concierge for apparel e-commerce" is a feature description, not an infinity story. Could it expand to all e-commerce categories? Sure, but then you're competing with every CRM and customer success platform. The story narrows rather than expands under pressure.

I respect the execution signals -- a 61% response rate on unsolicited SMS is genuinely impressive if it holds, and the blog-driven SEO play shows commercial instinct. But I cannot write a check into a company that embodies the three things I most actively avoid: an AI-first value proposition where the AI is the product, a SaaS business model in a category where incumbents have overwhelming distribution advantages, and an application-layer position with no path to becoming a new primitive. This is a feature that Attentive will ship, not a company that will compound.
