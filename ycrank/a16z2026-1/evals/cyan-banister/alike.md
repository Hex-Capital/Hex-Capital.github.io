# Alike -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Enterprise AI coordination is the hottest space in venture right now — Gartner reports a 1,445% surge in multi-agent system inquiries from Q1 2024 to Q2 2025, and every major platform is racing to own this layer. Google launched its open-source A2A protocol with 50+ partners including Salesforce, SAP, and Workday. Slack is positioning itself as an "agentic OS" with 30 new AI features and auto-provisioning for every Salesforce customer. Dust raised $21.5M from Sequoia and already has $7.3M ARR. CrewAI raised $18M from Insight Partners. Moveworks was acquired by ServiceNow for $2.85B. When I look at Alike entering this landscape, I don't see a room where VCs are shifting uncomfortably in their chairs — I see a room where everyone is reaching for their checkbooks at the same time. That's the opposite of the signal I look for. My best investments have been the ones where 300 investors said no, where the founder's pitch provoked confusion or discomfort. Alike's pitch provokes nodding heads, and that worries me more than it excites me.

When I ask "tell me their story, not the business," here is what I hear from Alike's founders: Addi Haran Diman started university at 13 and finished a PhD in social data science at Oxford by 21. Danial Hussain was the first person from a foundation-year background and Pakistani heritage to become Oxford SU President. Max Van Kleek has 9,012 Google Scholar citations and 25 years of HCI research at MIT, PARC, Nokia, IBM, and Oxford. These are genuinely impressive credentials — Addi's intellectual precocity and Danial's barrier-breaking at Oxford show real drive. But the biographical question I need answered is different: what happened to these founders that made enterprise coordination an obsession they couldn't walk away from? Addi's PhD studied social networks and group behavior, which is relevant domain training, but studying coordination academically is not the same as being broken by a coordination failure that you had to fix to survive. The founding narrative reads as "we were brilliant Oxford academics who spotted a massive market" — and I've seen that story before. It's the pattern I find hardest to invest in, because the founders who discovered their problem through credential rather than crucible are the ones most likely to pivot when the market gets hard.

Max Van Kleek's depth is the strongest signal in this dossier, and I want to be honest about that. Twenty-five years of privacy-preserving systems research, an MIT PhD from CSAIL, and nearly ten thousand citations in the exact intersection of human-AI interaction and privacy — that's not an AI wrapper team. Alike's granular per-person, per-channel privacy controls and full traceability of agent reasoning are grounded in real academic IP, not marketing language. If I were building an enterprise product that touches sensitive workplace communications, Max is the CTO I'd want. But he's maintaining a full-time Associate Professor position at Oxford while serving as CTO. When I backed SpaceX, Elon was sleeping on the factory floor. When Crusoe Energy nearly died in 2020, the founders were in the field, not splitting time with tenured positions. The dual commitment raises a question about whether this team has fully burned the boats — and at pre-seed, in a market where Dust and CrewAI are already executing full-speed, execution velocity is existential.

The invisible system test is where Alike loses me most clearly. My strongest portfolio companies — Affirm attacking credit card opacity, Ownwell exposing property tax exploitation, Carta revealing the cap table mystery — all identified a hidden structure that was extracting value from people who didn't understand it. Alike targets coordination overhead: meetings, check-ins, status updates, cross-functional alignment threads. That's a real pain point. But it's not hidden. Every enterprise worker in America knows they have too many meetings. There's no information asymmetry being exploited, no gatekeeper extracting a hidden tax, no opaque system being cracked open. Alike is an optimization layer on a visible inefficiency, and those are important businesses, but they don't activate the deepest part of my investment conviction — the part that comes from having navigated systems designed to keep people like me in the dark.

The bull case deserves serious examination. The timing is genuinely powerful: Gartner predicts 40% of enterprise apps will embed task-specific AI agents by 2026, up from under 5% in 2025. If that prediction holds, the coordination layer between those agents becomes critical infrastructure — and Google's A2A protocol validates the need without necessarily commoditizing the product layer on top. Alike's 20 signed design partners, including Exus Renewables ($1B backing from Partners Group), K-ID ($51M from Lightspeed and a16z), and NewtonX ($47M, trusted by Microsoft and TikTok), show that real companies see value in the coordination-specific approach. The Carta comparison is instructive: Carta's switching costs came from accumulated organizational data, and Alike's claim that the system "learns team coordination patterns over time" follows the same structural logic. If Alike can embed deeply enough before Slack's native AI features mature, there is a path to a real data moat. What would have to be true for this to work: Max would need to go full-time, the team would need to convert design partners to paying contracts faster than Slack and Google commoditize the layer, and the coordination intelligence would need to be demonstrably superior to what platforms build natively. Those are three simultaneous requirements, and the probability of all three materializing before the incumbents close the window is what keeps my hand on the table rather than reaching for my checkbook.

Alike has a capable team in a massive market at the right moment. If I were scoring on credentials and timing alone, this would look different. But I invest based on a framework forged by my own experience — I need to feel the biographical inevitability in the founders, I need to see an opaque system being made transparent, and I need the space to be one that makes other investors uncomfortable. Alike scores low on all three of my highest-weighted criteria. The consensus enthusiasm around enterprise AI agents is the strongest negative signal: when everyone wants to fund a category, the founders who win are the ones who would be building regardless of investor appetite, and I don't see evidence that this team's connection to the problem runs that deep.
