# Adialante -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Efraín Torres invented the physics. That sentence contains the entire specific knowledge argument for Adialante. Torres didn't identify "affordable MRI" as a market gap and then hire engineers to solve it — he spent years inside a PhD program at the University of Minnesota under Michael Garwood, one of the world's leading MRI researchers, and built a fundamentally new MRI encoding scheme called FREE (Frequency-modulated Rabi-Encoded Echoes) that eliminates gradient coils entirely. Published in *Magnetic Resonance in Medicine* in 2022. Patent-pending. If you removed Torres from Adialante, you wouldn't have a company missing its CEO — you'd have a company missing its core technology. The gap between "inventor of the physics" and "identified the opportunity through market research" is the gap between specific knowledge and generic competence. Torres is on the Evan Williams side of that line — Williams at Blogger had years of specific knowledge about public communication that became Twitter. Torres has years of specific knowledge about MRI encoding that became Adialante. The team he's assembled — Parker Jenkins (co-founder, hardware focus, same BME pipeline from Marquette through UMN), a Rabi Award winner in Sai Abitha Srinivas, three additional PhDs in MR physics and computational physics — compounds the knowledge density. Hyperfine spent over $200M and five-plus years to bring a portable MRI (different technology, brain-only) to FDA clearance. That's the time-to-replicate for anyone chasing Adialante's physics, and they'd be chasing a moving target.

Siemens Healthineers, GE HealthCare, and Philips collectively dominate the $6.5B MRI systems market by selling multi-million-dollar machines to hospitals. Adialante's mobile-as-a-service model — a truck shows up at your urology clinic, patients get scanned, you pay per scan, zero capital expenditure — directly cannibalizes the installed base economics of every incumbent. The principal-agent problem here is structural and familiar: Siemens' sales organization is an agent of the capital-equipment P&L. They will not build a mobile fleet that turns their $3M sale into a $300 per-scan commodity until it's far too late. Torres, as a principal with no legacy revenue to protect, can move where Siemens' agents cannot. This is the same architecture I recognized in Perplexity versus Google — Google's search team had every technical capability to build AI search, but the $175B ad revenue model made them agents of their own balance sheet. Adialante occupies the same structural position against MRI incumbents, and ninety percent of the global population currently lacks MRI access (MNSBIR, Jul 2025), which means the addressable whitespace is not Siemens' existing customers but the billions of people who've never had a scan.

Adialante's access democratization is the clearest in this batch. When I built AngelList, the insight was simple: startup investing was restricted to connected insiders, and software could remove that gatekeeper. Adialante removes a gatekeeper made of concrete and superconducting magnets — the $2-5M machine, the shielded room, the cryogenic infrastructure, the specialized technical staff. A flat per-scan fee from a mobile truck is structural access expansion, not incremental improvement. Rural urology clinics in northern Minnesota (Jenkins' stated motivation, per Fogarty Innovation, Aug 2024) cannot install a conventional MRI under any economic model. Adialante makes the scan possible where it was previously impossible. That's democratization at the physics layer, and it's the pattern that produces my highest-conviction investments.

The leverage architecture is where Adialante breaks from my typical portfolio — and I won't pretend otherwise. Every mobile MRI unit requires a truck, a driver, a technician, and physical presence at the clinic. Revenue scales linearly with fleet size, not exponentially with users. Adialante's reconstruction algorithms have some code leverage, but the business model is fundamentally capital-intensive and operations-heavy. You cannot run this company with five people at any meaningful scale. Promaxo has raised $72-81M and Openwater $100M — Adialante's $3M-plus puts them at a significant capital disadvantage in a hardware race. The Varda Space Industries parallel is instructive but imperfect: Varda creates compounds that are physically impossible to manufacture on Earth, making the leverage entirely unique. Adialante creates scans that are currently possible with conventional MRI, just inaccessible to most people. The moat is economic and logistical, not physically impossible to replicate. That's a weaker form of physics leverage than microgravity. The FDA regulatory pathway adds further capital and time risk — FREE has no predicate device, likely requiring De Novo authorization rather than a simpler 510(k), and no submission has been publicly announced.

Torres and Jenkins met on the first day of classes at Marquette University, pursued the same BME program through undergrad, then both moved to the University of Minnesota for graduate work in the same field (Fogarty Innovation, Aug 2024). That's not a "find a co-founder" event — that's a decade-long relationship built inside the exact domain they're commercializing. Torres is a first-generation college graduate from Chicago's south side who spent his PhD inventing this technology. He was working on FREE before Adialante existed, before venture funding was a consideration. Jenkins cites rural healthcare gaps in northern Minnesota as personal motivation. These are principals, not agents. If Adialante fails to raise its next round, I expect Torres to be in a lab somewhere still working on gradient-free MRI. That's the signal that matters — not the pitch, not the TAM slide, but what the founder does when nobody's watching.

The bull case for Adialante is one of the strongest specific-knowledge-plus-democratization combinations I've encountered at pre-seed. The bear case is equally real: this is hardware, this requires fleets and staff and FDA clearance and nine figures of capital to scale, and revenue grows with trucks deployed, not users acquired. If this were pure software — if FREE were an algorithm that ran on existing MRI hardware — this would be an 85-plus without hesitation. It's not. The capital intensity and operational scaling requirements are genuine structural constraints. The condition that would flip me to Pass: if the FDA pathway forces a full PMA rather than De Novo, adding three-plus years and tens of millions in clinical trial costs. The condition that confirms the Invest: a successful FDA De Novo classification or 510(k) clearance, plus one signed clinic contract with published per-scan economics. Adialante's score is anchored by exceptional specific knowledge (Torres invented the core physics, 26/30) and access democratization (removing the MRI gatekeeper for ninety percent of the world, 9/10), tempered by a leverage architecture that is physical rather than permissionless (10/25) and a team-to-value ratio constrained by fleet operations (6/15). The specific knowledge alone would justify a check. The question is whether the Varda exception — physics moats override lean-team preferences — extends to medical devices with linear scaling. I believe it does, narrowly.
