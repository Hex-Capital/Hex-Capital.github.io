# Dispatch -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Varda Space Industries has raised $329M and flown six reentry missions. That's the number Dispatch needs to stare at every morning. Because the question I always ask -- "what if they're right and the market is wrong?" -- has a problem here: the market already agrees. In-space manufacturing is not a contrarian bet in 2026. Space Forge pulled in $38M led by the NATO Innovation Fund. Reditus Space, one YC batch ahead of Dispatch, raised $7.1M and plans to launch its reusable ENOS vehicle this summer -- potentially before Dispatch's Free Flyer 1 even gets off the ground in mid-2027. When I seeded Solana at four cents, the entire crypto establishment had written off alternative L1s. Nobody is writing off space manufacturing right now. The risk is not in the price. The upside is substantially priced in by the existence of Varda alone, and there are four-plus funded competitors all converging on the same reentry vehicle segment.

Payton Case and Andrew Mello spent roughly four years together at Astranis building geostationary satellites -- a company that's raised $350M+ and actually put hardware in orbit. Mello's prior tour through Apple AirPods, Amazon warehouse automation, and Zoox autonomous vehicles gives him cross-domain hardware chops that matter for a business building heat shields and avionics from scratch. The co-founder relationship is real, forged over years of shared work, not a founder-matching event. That pattern -- backing people I've observed across sustained collaboration -- is one of my strongest signals. But "two senior Astranis engineers start a space company" is not an insane idea. It's the obvious career move. The sanity-to-insanity ratio is inverted from what gets me excited: sane people doing a sane thing in a validated market. Compare this to when I backed Teamshares -- employee-owned small business rollups as a venture bet sounded genuinely crazy. Dispatch building reentry vehicles after Varda proved it works six times? That's rational, not radical.

Dispatch's most interesting structural move is the platform-versus-product positioning. Varda manufactures its own pharmaceuticals on orbit; Dispatch hosts third-party payloads and returns them. This maps onto my protocol-over-application thesis -- the same logic that made me back Solana instead of dapps. If Dispatch's Phase 2 vision materializes -- permanent uncrewed "lights-out" stations with 100kW power by late 2029 -- they become the infrastructure layer for everyone else's manufacturing, not a competitor to any single manufacturer. That's a genuine primitive: autonomous orbital manufacturing infrastructure as a service. But the primitive is three-plus years away, contingent on raising enormous amounts of capital, and Dispatch has $500K today. The cargo return vehicle (Phase 1) is a logistics service, not a category-creating primitive. Dispatch won't become a verb.

Dispatch absolutely demolishes the AI kill test, and this is where my Metropolis parallel kicks in. I seeded Metropolis in parking infrastructure and it's now valued at roughly $5B because you cannot replicate parking garages with an LLM. Similarly, no amount of AI can build you a heat shield rated for Mach 20+ reentry, operate an autonomous space station, or recover 30kg of manufactured semiconductors from orbit. Dispatch's full-scale heat shield test in the Mojave -- claimed at 100x cheaper than competitors' approaches -- represents physical-world engineering that software cannot commoditize. This is the category of investment I love: physical operations that software amplifies but can never replace. If Dispatch were operating in a mispriced market, this durability score would make me very excited.

The capital discipline dimension is where Dispatch's thesis breaks against my framework. Hardware spacecraft companies with multi-year pre-revenue timelines are the definition of the factory farm system I've declared dead. Dispatch needs to go from $500K to orbital demonstrations to permanent space stations -- that's three-plus major capital raises over four-plus years, each contingent on technical milestones in an environment where launch delays are the norm, not the exception. "Every time you raise capital, you should think of it as the last capital you will ever raise" -- and there is no universe in which Dispatch survives on $500K. Their business model structurally requires the conveyor belt of escalating venture rounds.

The bull case an advocate would make: Dispatch is the AWS of space manufacturing -- the horizontal infrastructure play in a market where everyone else is vertically integrated. Case and Mello have direct spacecraft ops experience from Astranis, they've already tested a full-scale heat shield, and the single-use approach at 100x cost reduction could create a structural unit economics advantage over reusable competitors like Space Forge. The Phase 2 autonomous station is genuinely visionary, and if launch costs continue dropping with Starship, the TAM projections of $23-39B by 2035 could make Dispatch's infrastructure position enormously valuable. The Slack exception in my framework is relevant: I paid consensus prices for Slack because the primitive was real. Autonomous orbital manufacturing stations might be a real primitive too. But Slack had Stewart Butterfield -- a proven founder with a prior exit who'd already demonstrated the behavior change. Dispatch has two first-time founders with an unproven heat shield and zero customers or LOIs. The condition that would flip me: if Dispatch secured a binding payload contract with a named pharmaceutical or semiconductor company before their first flight, proving that customers want the infrastructure model over Varda's vertical integration, I'd revisit aggressively.

Dispatch is a credentialed team building physically durable infrastructure in a market that has already been validated by better-funded competitors. The AI kill test score is exceptional; the consensus mispricing score is not. The two dimensions that lock Dispatch into a Neutral verdict are mispricing (the market already loves space manufacturing -- four funded competitors, $329M+ to the category leader) and capital discipline (hardware spacecraft requiring years of pre-revenue capital raises). If this were a mispriced category, the physical moat and founder credentials would push me to write a check. But I'm not in the business of paying fair prices for good companies. I'm in the business of finding extreme risk/reward asymmetry, and Dispatch doesn't have it.
