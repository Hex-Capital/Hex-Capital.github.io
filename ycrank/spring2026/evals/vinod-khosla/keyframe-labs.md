# Keyframe Labs -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Keyframe Labs is a competent foundation-model team building an avatar layer for AI agents, and the analytical signature is the exact inverse of the shape I underwrite: the technical-risk dimension is the highest rung the dossier reaches (Parth Radia and Kaahan Radia shipped Cosmo 1.5 in October 2025 and Persona-1 in January 2026 as in-house models, not API wrappers), while the consequence-magnitude dimension is the lowest -- "$0.06 a minute for lifelike video calls" does not name an incumbent regime that disappears if Keyframe Labs wins. That pairing -- real model work in service of a feature-scale consequence -- is what makes Keyframe Labs a pass, not the absence of traction.

On consequence, the high rung on my ladder is **Impossible Foods** (2011), where the success-state was the displacement of animal protein production -- a named civilizational variable. Keyframe Labs's success-state, read straight from the dossier, is that AI agents talk to humans with a face attached at $0.06/minute instead of being text-only. That is the **DoorDash** (2013) shape minus the consumer-network compounding and minus the labor-market displacement frame I anchored to my Fortune 2026-03-06 "$15 trillion of GDP is labor" thesis -- Keyframe Labs sits adjacent to my pass-example ("GPT-4 summarizes sales calls"), separated only by the fact that it ships its own model weights instead of OpenAI's. The dossier lists corporate training, interview practice, telehealth, customer support, digital twins -- five vertical slices of an existing $12.9B avatar TAM (Precedence Research, 2026). No incumbent system collapses. I stop here in my normal entry-point flow.

The low rung on the founder-defiance ladder is the "20 years at Salesforce now applying the same playbook with AI" pattern my TED Talk warned against, and Keyframe Labs sits one notch above it -- not because Parth Radia or Kaahan Radia are unimpressive, but because their dossier reads as domain-expert continuity rather than expert defiance. Five years at Ericsson on voice/video foundation models leading a team of 30, prior AT&T Labs work on "world's first 3D call over production networks," plus five years at Zipline on perception for autonomous drones -- this is exactly the resume any Tier-1 generalist underwrites. There is no public moment where Parth Radia contradicted his own field, no "too important not to do" framing, no instigator posture from my "World Needs 12 Good Instigators" TED talk. Competent is not the same as defiant.

On capital risk that repels consensus VCs, the mechanism analysis matters more than the ladder: Keyframe Labs trains its own talking-head foundation models, which is real GPU spend, but the 18-month compounding curve for sub-second avatar generation in 2026 is a falling-cost engineering problem, not a **Commonwealth Fusion** (2019) physics problem. Tavus already raised $24.1M (TechCrunch March 2024) doing the same thing with Scale Venture Partners and Sequoia capital -- the existence of a Sequoia-backed direct competitor is the cleanest possible signal that this thesis does not repel consensus VCs. It attracts them.

For learning rate, the counterfactual is the cleaner test than a ladder: what would have to be true in the Keyframe Labs dossier to flip this dimension a tier? A documented prior pivot from Parth Radia -- "we shipped Cosmo 1.5 expecting telehealth, the buyers were recruiting platforms, we rebuilt Persona-1 for interview practice" -- would do it. The dossier shows two model releases three months apart but offers no public thesis-update narrative, no "we were wrong about X" moment. Absent that, I score to my default; this is not a penalty so much as a recognition that the Keyframe Labs founders have a short company history and have not yet had the chance to reveal what their learning rate actually is.

On required-capital profile, the **OpenAI** (2019) parallel is instructive only as an inversion: I wrote $50M because the roadmap demanded capital depth no consensus VC would touch. Keyframe Labs's roadmap demands maybe $20-30M to reach meaningful ARR -- developer self-serve via embed code, Cal.com booking for enterprise, two-person team with zero open jobs. I am over-resourced for this deal. Any seed fund with a $3M check writes this. My presence on the cap table adds dilution, not signal.

My 36-month forecast on Keyframe Labs: acquired by Tavus, HeyGen, or a CRM/contact-center incumbent (Salesforce, Zendesk, Five9) for $50-150M as a real-time avatar feature layer, with ARR in the $3-8M range at acquisition -- not dead, because Parth Radia and Kaahan Radia have shipped real models and the customer base is non-zero, but never a standalone outcome at the scale my portfolio is calibrated to. The mechanism: HeyGen at $95-100M ARR and Synthesia at $146M ARR (Sacra / d-id.com, 2025-2026) have the cash to either build the real-time tier themselves or buy it, and the $0.06/min consumption pricing is a feature not a moat once Tavus matches it.

The strongest bear-of-my-bear -- the bull case I have to dismantle -- is the **Glean** exception: a domain-expert founder profile that I overrode because the AI-agent layer was about to become a Fortune-500 infrastructure category. If Khosla's own "80% of jobs by AI by 2030" thesis (Fortune 2025-07-01) is right, every agent gets a face, the visual layer becomes the Twilio of AI agents, and Keyframe Labs's $0.06/min consumption pricing -- which structurally cannot be matched by HeyGen's $29/mo subscription tiers without ARR cannibalization -- compounds into the default API. The dossier evidence that defeats this case: Glean had Arvind Jain plus ex-Google distribution into the Fortune 500 from day one; Keyframe Labs has eight unattributed testimonials on a landing page and zero named enterprise logos. Without distribution, the consumption-pricing thesis is theoretical. The condition that flips my verdict is concrete: three named Fortune 500 logos on multi-year contracts disclosed publicly, each above $500K ACV, within the next nine months. Short of that, Keyframe Labs is a competent acqui-hire trajectory, not a Khosla deal.
