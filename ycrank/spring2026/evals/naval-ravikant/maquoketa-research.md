# Maquoketa Research -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The most honest thing I can say about this company is that it structurally contradicts nearly every filter I use. Hardware attack drones are the definitional opposite of permissionless leverage. Every unit has a bill of materials, a manufacturing process, a supply chain, and an ITAR compliance burden. Revenue scales with production capacity, not with code. The competitors -- AeroVironment with $990M+ in Army contracts, Anduril at $3.5B+ raised, Helsing at a €12B valuation -- have already locked up procurement relationships and factory capacity. A five-person pre-seed team is not structurally advantaged here the way a five-person software team would be. This is labor and capital leverage dressed up in a YC demo day pitch.

The specific knowledge question centers entirely on Hernandez. His Berkeley Wireless Research Center affiliation and phased array/RF design specialization is genuine domain expertise -- phased array systems require years of deep engagement with electromagnetic theory, antenna design, and signal processing that you cannot teach someone in a bootcamp or extract from a textbook. That knowledge is real and non-trivial. But I need to distinguish between specific knowledge that is *relevant to* the company and specific knowledge that *is* the company. Ev Williams at Twitter didn't just know about blogging -- his years at Blogger had crystallized an insight about public communication that became the product itself. Here, I see a talented RF engineer whose expertise maps to one component of a complex weapons system. The dossier provides no evidence that Hernandez has been obsessively building toward autonomous munitions specifically, versus applying general RF expertise to a hot market. Yeager's background is EECS plus trading technology at SpiderRock -- useful engineering capability, but nothing defense-specific. Williams is a ghost -- zero public data. The team covers the right three disciplines, but depth of founder-specific-knowledge remains unproven.

I tested this against my Varda exception: does Maquoketa create a genuinely new form of leverage through physics that competitors cannot replicate? Varda manufactures pharmaceutical compounds in microgravity that are literally impossible to produce on Earth -- that's a moat enforced by orbital mechanics. Maquoketa's in-house phased array capability is sophisticated, but it's not physically impossible for Anduril or AeroVironment to hire RF engineers and build competing systems. They have 1000x the resources. The fact that Stark Defence won a €268M German contract *despite failing all four test strikes* tells you something uncomfortable about this market: procurement relationships and government trust matter more than technical superiority. That's a regulatory-adjacency moat, and I'm structurally skeptical of businesses whose advantage depends on government favor rather than technological leverage that compounds.

The bull case deserves serious engagement. The timing window is genuinely interesting -- AeroVironment's new Switchblade factory won't be operational until late 2026/early 2027, and Ukraine alone ordered 6,000 HX-2 units from Helsing. Germany redirected €9B+ to startups over incumbents. The principal-agent problem at defense primes is real: Lockheed and Raytheon's profit models are optimized for high-margin, low-volume platforms, making it structurally painful to pivot to expendable $10K drones that cannibalize their $2M missile revenue. If Maquoketa's RF/EW-resistance layer creates autonomous drones that actually work in GPS-denied, electronically contested environments -- where modified FPV drones fail -- that's a genuine capability gap. And OTA procurement pathways can compress timelines from 36 months to under 12. If Hernandez's phased array knowledge turns out to be as deep as his BWRC affiliation suggests, and if the team can deliver a working prototype that outperforms in contested environments, there's a path to meaningful early contracts. The market is expanding at a pace that creates oxygen for new entrants.

But even the bull case fails my compound interest test. Each drone is a one-time-use munition -- there's no data flywheel, no network effect, no trust accumulation that compounds with each deployment. Manufacturing knowledge compounds slowly through iteration, but that's industrial learning, not the exponential compounding I look for. And the business fundamentally requires proportional headcount to scale -- you need factory workers, compliance officers, cleared personnel, government BD. This is a company that might succeed, but it will succeed through capital leverage and labor leverage, not through the permissionless leverage architecture that generates the convex returns I'm structured to capture. I'm the wrong investor for this bet.
