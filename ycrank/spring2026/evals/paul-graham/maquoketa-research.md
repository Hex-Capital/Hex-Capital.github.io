# Maquoketa Research -- Paul Graham Primary Source Research: Structured Quote Record Evaluation

Defense hardware is the domain where schlep blindness is most powerful. "Your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps" — and building autonomous attack drones involves every schlep imaginable: ITAR compliance, security clearances, explosive handling, test ranges, 12-36 month procurement cycles, facility requirements. Most YC founders wouldn't touch this with a ten-foot pole, which is precisely why it sat as a problem for incumbents to solve badly. The fact that a team of Berkeley engineers is willing to wade into this muck is, in itself, a signal worth paying attention to. It's the same structural dynamic that made Stripe possible — "It's a lot more intimidating to start a startup like this than a recipe site," and payments sat broken for a decade because of that intimidation. The question is whether the Maquoketa founders are the ones to push through the schlep, or whether they've simply underestimated it.

The market dynamics here are genuinely unusual and worth noting clearly: this is a market that is actively choosing startups over incumbents. Germany handed €9B+ in strike drone contracts to Helsing and Stark Defence, not to Rheinmetall. Stark got €268M despite failing all four test strikes — which tells you the procurement apparatus has decided it needs startup-speed iteration, not incumbent reliability. AeroVironment can't get its Switchblade factory running until late 2026. The big defense primes face what I'd call business-model cannibalization — their profit models are built on high-margin, low-volume platforms like cruise missiles and manned aircraft, and pivoting to cheap expendable drones undermines their entire economic structure. This is the kind of structural opening where new entrants don't just have a chance — they have an advantage the incumbents structurally cannot replicate. As a market, this scores high.

The CTO hire is the strongest concrete signal in this dossier. Alejandro Hernandez, out of the Berkeley Wireless Research Center, specializing in phased array systems and RF design — that's not a credential you slap on a LinkedIn profile to sound impressive. BWRC is a serious research lab, and phased array work maps directly to the core hard problem in autonomous munitions: guidance, comms in contested electromagnetic environments, and EW resistance. "The component of entrepreneurship that really matters is domain expertise," and Hernandez has it on the technical side. The team covers the three essential engineering disciplines — software/systems (Yeager), RF/electronics (Hernandez), mechanical (Williams) — which is the right architecture for building a complete drone system in-house. These are not software founders playing at hardware.

But here's what troubles me, and I want to be honest about it: I have no evidence these founders are relentlessly resourceful. None. The Airbnb founders sold cereal boxes. The Collisons grabbed your laptop and installed Stripe on the spot. Those were observable acts of determination I could evaluate. With Maquoketa, I have Berkeley credentials, a 403 error on the website, no social media presence, no GitHub, no press, no disclosed conversations with military customers, and one co-founder — Williams — with literally zero findable background. "You can recognize genuinely smart people by their ability to say things like 'I don't know'" — and what I have to say here is: I don't know if these founders have the determination to survive a 24-month procurement cycle with pre-seed capital. Defense stealth is real, and I won't penalize them for not tweeting, but the complete absence of any observable behavior makes it impossible to apply what I actually care about most. "If we've learned one thing from funding so many startups, it's that they succeed or fail based on the qualities of the founders." I can see their credentials. I cannot see their qualities.

The bull case is real and I want to lay it out fairly. If Hernandez's phased array work produces a guidance system that gives these drones genuine EW resistance — the ability to operate in GPS-denied, jammed environments — that's not something FPV assemblers or software-overlay startups can replicate. It would be a genuine technical moat, not a feature. Combined with the production bottleneck at AeroVironment and the demonstrated willingness of governments to hand billion-dollar contracts to startups, the right team building the right product here could hit a market nerve in the way that produces exponential returns. "When a startup grows fast, it's usually because the product hits a nerve." The nerve is there. The question is whether this team can build the product that touches it, and then survive the procurement gauntlet long enough to get paid. If I could meet these founders — if I could spend seven minutes with them and see whether they're earnest, whether they have that "full of energy" quality that overrode my doubts about Airbnb — I might feel very differently.

What keeps this in neutral territory is the competitive gap. Neros has $121M and a US Army PBAS selection. Anduril has $3.5B and a broad autonomy stack. Helsing has €1.37B and a €12B valuation. Maquoketa has a YC check and three Berkeley engineers. "Good people can fix bad ideas" — but this isn't a bad idea, it's a good idea that well-funded, competent companies are already executing on. The window exists because incumbents are slow, but the startups eating this market are not slow. They're fast, well-funded, and already have contracts. For Maquoketa to win from here, they'd need either a genuinely superior technical approach (possible but unverified) or a willingness to find and serve a niche that the bigger players are overlooking — some specific military customer with a specific need that a 5-person team can serve better than anyone. That's the "do things that don't scale" path, and it's the only one I can see working.
