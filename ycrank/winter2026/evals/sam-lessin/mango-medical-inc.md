# Mango Medical -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The first thing that jumps out is the $10M ARR LOI from an unnamed "top orthopedic company." At pre-seed, before FDA clearance, before revenue, before a website that even loads properly -- that number is either a remarkable signal or a meaningless one. And in medtech, I know the answer: orthopedic device companies hand out LOIs like business cards at trade shows. These are non-binding expressions of interest contingent on regulatory clearance, clinical validation, integration milestones, and half a dozen other gates the company hasn't passed through. The fact that the company is "pursuing" 510(k) clearance -- not that it has filed, not that it has a timeline -- means this LOI is contingent on a regulatory event that took Formus Labs seven years from founding to achieve. I don't score an LOI as revenue. I don't even score it as strong demand signal when the commitment costs the signer nothing.

Now, my framework starts with whether this is a sane person pursuing an insane idea. Adrian Kilian has a background in dentistry -- which is adjacent to surgical planning but is not orthopedic surgery -- plus Stanford Ignite, which is a part-time executive education certificate, not an MBA or a deep technical credential. Jorge Padilla Pérez brings AI and robotics expertise from TU Munich. The TU Munich spinoff origin is the strongest credibility signal here: it suggests real research underpinning, not a weekend project. But the "sane" register is moderate, not exceptional. There's no one on this team who built the planning infrastructure at a Stryker or a Zimmer Biomet, no orthopedic surgeon co-founder who's done a thousand fracture reconstructions. And on the "insane" side of the equation? AI-powered surgical planning from CT scans is about as consensus as healthcare AI gets. Formus Labs, Enhatch, Pixee Medical, CustoMED, and Brainlab are all building here. When five funded competitors are pursuing the same thesis and the pitch deck leads with TAM projections for "AI in orthopedic surgery" -- that's the opposite of non-consensus. This is a competent team chasing a visible opportunity, which in my framework is the least interesting combination.

Here's what genuinely concerns me, and where my lens sees something most healthcare-focused investors miss: AI IS the product. Strip the AI out of Mango Medical and there is nothing left. No physical infrastructure, no installed hardware base, no proprietary data flywheel with years of clinical outcomes, no protocol that other systems integrate with. The entire value proposition is "our AI converts CT scans to 3D plans faster." My question is always "does this business get stronger or weaker as software gets cheaper?" -- and the answer here is unambiguously weaker. CT scan segmentation and 3D reconstruction are well-documented computer vision tasks. As foundation models for medical imaging improve -- and they will, because this is where enormous research dollars flow -- the technical barrier to building a competitive pipeline drops toward zero. The FDA clearance pathway provides a temporal moat, a speed bump that slows competitors by 12-24 months. But it's not a structural moat. Formus Labs already has clearance. Enhatch already has clearance. The speed bump has already been crossed by others.

The market structure tells a damning story for the "infinity optionality" test. The U.S. orthopedic surgical planning software market is $24.63 million. The global preoperative planning software market is $113.92 million. These are not infinity-story numbers. The company invokes "Invisalign for bone fractures," which is a revealing analogy -- but it actually cuts against them. Align Technology's moat wasn't the planning software. It was the vertically integrated manufacturing of custom aligners plus the clinical workflow lock-in plus the training network for orthodontists. The planning software was the cherry on top of a physical-world business. Mango Medical is proposing to be just the cherry, without the sundae underneath it. And historically, when orthopedic device companies want planning software, they acquire it -- TraumaCad and OrthoView were both absorbed by incumbents for what were almost certainly sub-$50M prices. The ceiling for a standalone planning software company in this space is a modest acquisition, not a generational outcome.

The strongest bull case would require Mango Medical's fracture-specific focus to constitute a genuinely different technical problem from joint replacement planning -- one where most competitors can't easily extend their existing models. If fracture reconstruction is sufficiently harder, sufficiently different from the hip and knee replacement planning that Formus Labs and Enhatch have built for, then there's a defensible niche. And if the unnamed LOI partner is a top-three device company that wants to own the fracture planning category the way Zimmer Biomet owns hip planning through Formus Labs, the commercial path could materialize quickly post-clearance. But even in this optimistic scenario, I'm looking at a company whose best outcome is acquisition by an orthopedic device incumbent at a price that doesn't move the needle for my portfolio. The narrative doesn't expand -- it narrows into a feature of someone else's implant ecosystem. That's the Birchbox lesson applied to medtech: narrative magnitude without structural moat, in a market where the acquirers set the price.
