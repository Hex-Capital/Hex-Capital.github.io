# Panorama -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Panorama's pitch — AI that watches your team work and recommends automations — is the kind of idea that sounds good to absolutely everyone. That's the problem. When I hear "TikTok-like recommendation engine for workflows," I hear a founder borrowing someone else's metaphor rather than describing a problem they felt in their bones. Zapier is worth $5 billion. n8n just raised at $2.5 billion. Workato raised $421 million. ServiceNow paid $2.85 billion for Moveworks. The collective market is screaming that workflow automation is a great category. Which means the returns have already been competed away for anyone entering without a genuinely non-obvious angle. Panorama's differentiation — observing patterns rather than requiring users to configure triggers — is a product design philosophy, not a contrarian insight. No knowledgeable person hears this pitch and says "that will never work." They say "makes sense." And "makes sense" is exactly what I don't want to hear.

The founders are technically accomplished. Jingwei Hao progressed from Senior Engineer to Staff to Tech Lead Manager at Lyft, ran key infrastructure at Cash App, and contributed to Envoy Mobile. Jaclyn Lunger earned an MIT PhD in ML, then as the first employee at Lila Sciences helped build systems that contributed to a $1.3 billion valuation. These are real credentials. But credentials from large organizations are not the same as the animal quality — that slightly-too-intense obsessiveness I look for. Jingwei's GitHub shows zero public repositories. The co-founders share no visible prior relationship: no common employer, no shared university, no evidence of having worked together before starting Panorama. The dossier is strikingly devoid of stories. No "I was leading a migration across 20 Lyft teams and realized how much time we wasted on X." No scrappy early customer acquisition tales. Truly resourceful founders generate anecdotes the way stars generate light — they can't help it. The absence here is itself data.

The organic discovery signal is weak. Panorama's problem statement — "knowledge workers spend significant time on repetitive, structured tasks" — reads like the opening slide of a McKinsey engagement, not a founder's frustration narrative. Compare this to Dropbox: Drew Houston built it because he kept forgetting his USB drive. One sentence, and you understand the entire company's reason for existing. Panorama's origin story is invisible. Jingwei's infrastructure background gives him plausible exposure to cross-team coordination overhead, and I could squint and imagine he experienced the problem at Lyft or Cash App. But if that were the case, he'd be telling that story. Founders who discover problems organically can't stop talking about the moment of frustration. The dossier's silence suggests this idea was selected from the menu of attractive AI application categories in 2025-2026, not extracted from lived experience.

There's also a founder-market fit gap I find concerning. Jaclyn Lunger's deep expertise is in ML for materials science — perovskite surfaces, adsorption energies, computational chemistry. This is genuinely impressive work, and it's almost entirely orthogonal to understanding how marketing teams use Notion or how engineering managers track burnout in Slack. Panorama isn't hackers building for hackers; it's infrastructure engineers and materials scientists building for knowledge workers in domains they haven't inhabited. My framework says founders should be building for people exactly like themselves. The strongest version of this company would be built by someone who spent five years as a program manager drowning in recurring documents and cross-tool coordination — someone who IS the user.

The bull case for Panorama requires three things to be true simultaneously. First, that Jaclyn's ML rigor translates into a recommendation engine so superior that teams feel magic during the observation period, overcoming the 1-2 week cold-start delay. Second, that Microsoft Copilot Studio, Google AgentSpace, and Slack's native AI don't simply replicate the "observe and suggest" pattern using their own first-party data access — data Panorama must reach through APIs these platforms control. Third, that the team can acquire enterprise customers fast enough to build a proprietary data moat before incumbents move. The SOC2 Type I certification at pre-seed shows some execution discipline, and the Product Hunt #2 launch with 363 upvotes demonstrates competent go-to-market mechanics. But execution discipline and launch execution are table stakes, not the compounding advantage that separates startups from small businesses.

Panorama is a Pass. The two dimensions most decisive are Organic Problem Discovery and Idea That Repels Before It Attracts. On discovery: the absence of any founder origin narrative, combined with Jaclyn Lunger's domain expertise being orthogonal to the product's target users, places Panorama squarely in the "chose an idea from a menu" category I'm most skeptical of. On contrarianism: enterprise AI workflow automation in 2026 is the definition of a consensus-attractive pitch, sitting in a market validated by $2.85 billion acquisitions and $5 billion valuations — exactly the kind of space where the return premium has already been arbitraged away by incumbents with native data access advantages.
