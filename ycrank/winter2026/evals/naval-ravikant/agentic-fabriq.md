# Agentic Fabriq -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the competitive landscape, and it tells me everything about where this bet sits. Arcade.dev -- founded by a former Okta executive -- raised $12M to build essentially the same "Okta for Agents" thesis. Composio has $29M and 500+ integrations already live. OASIS Security has $75M from Sequoia and Accel. Opti raised $20M. When five funded companies are pursuing the same category with more capital, more domain credibility, and more distribution, you're not making a contrarian bet -- you're entering a consensus market late and undergunned. "You get paid for being right first" -- but being right at the same time as everyone else, with less capital, is just being right and poor.

The specific knowledge question is where the deal falls apart for me. Paulina and Matthew are clearly intelligent -- MIT AI research at Kavli, Haystack, CSAIL, FutureTech. But their research is in astrophysics ML and general AI systems. Neither has built identity management systems. Neither has worked in enterprise security. Neither has operated inside an IT organization struggling with agent permissioning. The knowledge they possess -- how to build ML models, how to do academic research -- is teachable knowledge. It's the kind of background any strong MIT undergrad could acquire. Compare this directly to Alex Salazar at Arcade.dev, who spent years inside Okta understanding how enterprises think about identity lifecycles. That's the non-transferable domain insight I'm looking for. When I backed Stack Overflow, Joel Spolsky wasn't just a smart engineer -- he'd spent a decade as a developer frustrated by how programming knowledge was organized. The founder's obsession has to be the problem itself, not the technology adjacent to it.

The leverage architecture is the one dimension where the thesis has genuine structural merit. A control plane between agents and their tool access is a platform-layer play -- one integration serves every organization, policies codify and compound, and the switching cost deepens with each connected tool. This is the same structural logic that made Okta valuable: identity infrastructure becomes load-bearing for the organization. If Agentic Fabriq built a dominant position here, the leverage ratio would be real -- a small engineering team maintaining connectors and policies that govern agent behavior across thousands of enterprises. The problem is that this leverage architecture is identical to what every competitor in the space is building. The structural advantage of a control plane doesn't differentiate you when five other companies are building the same control plane.

The strongest bull case requires believing two things simultaneously: that the agent identity market will be large enough to support multiple winners (plausible -- IAM is a $25B+ market), and that these specific founders will out-execute teams with more capital, more domain expertise, and more enterprise credibility. For that second condition to hold, Paulina and Matthew would need to have discovered something architectural -- a fundamentally different approach to agent identity that the ex-Okta founder and the Sequoia-backed team haven't seen. Nothing in the dossier suggests they have. Their differentiation is described as "admin-facing control plane" versus "developer-facing auth SDK" -- a product positioning choice, not a technical insight. Product positioning can be copied in a quarter. I need to see knowledge that takes years to accumulate, and I don't see it here.

The integrity and commitment signals are fine -- dropping out of MIT together shows conviction, and the friendship-based founding relationship is the kind of long-term alignment I value. But conviction about the wrong bet still loses. These are young, smart builders who noticed a real market trend and moved fast. That's admirable. But when I evaluate whether this founder's specific knowledge uniquely qualifies them for this particular problem, the answer is clearly no. The domain expertise advantage belongs to the competitors. What remains is a race on execution speed and product taste, with a significant capital disadvantage, in a market where incumbents like SailPoint are already shipping agent identity features. This is the pattern I describe when I talk about passing on vertical SaaS -- anyone who spent six months studying the agent security space could have arrived at the same product concept.
