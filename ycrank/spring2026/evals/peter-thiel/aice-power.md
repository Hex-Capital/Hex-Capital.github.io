# AICE Power -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

AICE Power is a sector-correct company with a thesis-incorrect founder posture: subsea defense hardware is exactly the atoms-heavy regulated category I have been overweight for two decades, and yet on May 16, 2026 — fewer than four weeks before this dossier was assembled — Grégoire Chomette and Rémi Bouteiller executed a "120° pivot" from building-energy sensors into submarine drones. That single fact is the entire analysis. Anduril (2017) did not pivot into defense; Palmer Luckey was building toward it from the Oculus exit onward. AICE Power is doing the opposite: arriving at the right sector by indefinite-optimism iteration, which is the exact failure mode Zero to One (2014) was written against.

On the contrarian-secret dimension, the load-bearing analogue is Anduril (2017), where Luckey could name the Lockheed/Raytheon cost-plus failure mode in one sentence and explain why software-defined defense at startup velocity was structurally impossible for the primes. The dossier on AICE Power offers nothing of that shape. "Swarms of modular subsea drones" versus Anduril's Dive-LD/Dive-XL or Saronic's surface vessels is a product-architecture positioning claim, not a secret. There is no named architectural reason Anduril — which just closed $4B at a $60B valuation in March 2026 with a $1.7B Royal Australian Navy Ghost Shark contract already running — cannot ship swarm subsea once it decides to. AICE Power's differentiation as written in the dossier is inferred by the analyst, not articulated by the founders; the absence of a founder-named incumbent blind spot is itself the signal.

The Anduril comparison cuts the other way on monopoly trajectory. Anduril (2017) had a credible path to integration-depth monopoly with DoD because Luckey arrived with cleared relationships and a billion-dollar exit; AICE Power arrives with two engineers, no disclosed contracts, France-to-SF manufacturing that triggers ITAR friction, and a competitive field where Anduril ($1B 2024 revenue), Saronic ($1.75B Series D at $9.25B in March 2026), and Saildrone ($43M revenue, +231% YoY) are already eating the maritime-autonomy budget. The dossier notes UUV hardware "can be assembled from commercially available subsea components" — that is the commoditization signal. There is no pricing-power hypothesis here; the implicit moat is "we will be the swarm one," and any of the eight named incumbents can copy it inside their existing program offices.

On definite plan versus indefinite iteration, the May 16, 2026 pivot from energy sensors to submarine drones is not a neutral data point — it is a revealed-preference statement that AICE Power is operating in iteration mode. SpaceX (2008) committed to reusable launch on day one and did not deviate for fifteen years. The six-figure ARR Bouteiller's portfolio cites was on the prior product; it does not transfer, and a team that swings from building-energy sensors to defense UUVs inside a YC batch is signaling that the next external shock will produce another swing. Definite optimism in defense looks like a 5-year procurement roadmap with named DIU or Navy program offices; this dossier surfaces neither.

Founder lineage is where AICE Power earns its only real partial credit, and the parallel is Asana (2010), my known exception where Moskovitz's Facebook shipping pedigree overrode the missing structural moat. Chomette has MIT AeroAstro with Radovitzky and prior NASA asteroid-risk research; Bouteiller was founding engineer at Amira Health and built the prior AICE sensor stack to six-figure ARR. That is real technical pedigree. But the Asana override worked because Moskovitz had already shipped a generation-defining product. Neither Chomette nor Bouteiller has a naval, subsea, or DoD-procurement line on their record — defense buyers transact on cleared-personnel trust, and the founders are arriving without it. The lineage is competent, not load-bearing.

The atoms-heavy and regulated-sector dimension is the only place AICE Power scores genuinely well. Subsea defense hardware with ITAR exposure, French manufacturing, and naval customer set is the right atoms-heavy posture by definition — the Founders Fund (2011) manifesto pushes capital here precisely because two decades of VCs refused the category. If AICE Power had Anduril's founder profile in this category I would be writing a check. The sector is correct; the thesis around it is not.

My 36-month forecast on AICE Power: dead or pivoted again, sub-$500K revenue, no DIU or Navy program-of-record award, with the most charitable outcome an acqui-hire by a Tier 2 European defense integrator wanting cleared French engineering talent. The mechanism is simple — Anduril and Saronic absorb the swarm category through capital and incumbency before AICE Power closes its first contract, and a 2-person team on YC-standard funding cannot survive multi-year DoD procurement cycles in a category where Anduril's $1.7B Australian contract is the comparable.

The strongest objection is that Anduril (2017) was also small and contrarian-coded at founding, and Replicator-era procurement explicitly favors distributed small-vendor swarms — the 41.24% naval/coastal UUV share forecast in 2026 is a real tailwind, not a slide. If AICE Power lands a single DIU phase contract inside twelve months, the bear thesis breaks. The dismantling: Luckey arrived with the Oculus exit, the DoD relationships, and a contrarian secret he could name in one sentence; AICE Power arrives with a four-week-old pivot, no disclosed defense contacts, and a competitive set that just absorbed $5.75B in capital. The condition that flips my verdict: a named DIU, SBIR Phase II, or allied-navy contract disclosed within nine months — until then, AICE Power is sector tourism wearing a Founders Fund-shaped costume, and I pass.
