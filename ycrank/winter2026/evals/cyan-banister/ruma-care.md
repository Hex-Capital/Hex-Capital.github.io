# Ruma Care -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Christina Huang has been taking biologics for seven years. She's been denied, fought insurance companies, and lived inside the system that her company is trying to fix. When I hear that, I sit up. That's the biographical thread I'm always listening for -- the founder whose life and the company's mission are woven from the same cloth. And Meng Fei didn't just interview doctors about the problem from a conference room. He went and got hired as a medical assistant at a rheumatology clinic in San Mateo, sat in the chair, filled out the fax forms, navigated the portals. That kind of deliberate immersion is not common. Most founders talk to customers. These two lived the problem from both sides -- patient and provider. That's real.

What I notice about Ruma Care -- and what a typical analyst would frame as "prior authorization automation" -- is that this is actually a transparency play. Seventy-plus online portals, paper faxes, phone trees, denial letters with no explanation. It's an opaque system designed to exhaust people into giving up, and the people who suffer most are patients with chronic autoimmune conditions who need $100K+ medications to function. Ruma is doing for biologics access what Carta did for cap tables and what Affirm did for consumer credit -- taking an invisible, deliberately confusing power structure and making it legible. The $60 billion in unaccessed care isn't a market inefficiency. It's a system working as designed to deny access. That resonates with me deeply. When you reduce denial rates by 50% and cut staff time by 20x, you're not just saving clinics money -- you're getting patients their medication months sooner, which means months less suffering, months more ability to work and live.

Here's where I struggle, though. Prior authorization is not an unpopular problem to solve. Nobody in healthcare is going to look at this and say "that's crazy, why would you do that?" Cohere Health has raised $200 million. Waystar is a public company doing a billion in revenue. Develop Health just closed a $14 million Series A. Everyone knows PA is broken. This is the opposite of the signal I've learned to trust -- the best investments look bad to most investors, and Ruma Care looks good to most investors. It's sensible. It's logical. It's the kind of company that gets consensus nods in a room full of VCs, and consensus is exactly where I've learned the worst risk-adjusted returns live. When I backed Crusoe Energy, people thought mining Bitcoin on gas flares was absurd. When I backed Contraline, male contraception made investors visibly uncomfortable. Those reactions were the signal. Ruma Care doesn't generate that reaction, and the absence of discomfort concerns me.

The bull case that would change my mind: if the biologics niche proves to be a genuine wedge that's structurally difficult for the well-funded competitors to serve. Cohere sits on the payer side, which is a business model conflict with serving providers. Waystar optimizes for breadth across the entire revenue cycle, not depth in one drug category. Develop Health is broader in medication access generally. If Ruma's denial-pattern learning creates a compounding data moat specific to biologics -- every processed PA making the next submission smarter -- and if the buy-and-bill economics ($80-150K per patient) create enough provider willingness-to-pay that Ruma can build an extraordinarily capital-efficient business within a narrow but deep niche, then this could be the kind of company that looks obvious in retrospect. The CMS mandate forcing FHIR APIs by 2027 could cut both ways -- it lowers barriers for competitors but also creates a regulatory tailwind that accelerates adoption. And the Biologics Access Symposium with Stanford Medicine suggests these founders can convene the right community. If I squint, I can see a version of this where the niche specificity IS the moat, and the Yale-to-clinic pipeline demonstrates exactly the kind of founders who can navigate healthcare's maze of payers, providers, and regulators.

But I keep coming back to the competitive landscape. The founders are strong, the problem is real, and the economic access dimension is exactly what I look for. Christina and Meng Fei have complementary skills and a pre-existing Yale relationship, which gives me some comfort on co-founder alignment, though I'd want to hear how they argue and who decides when they disagree. What I can't get past is that this is a well-understood market with well-capitalized incumbents, and the core workflow -- extracting payer rules, auto-filling forms, submitting to insurers -- doesn't have the kind of permanently defensible moat I need to see. The biologics-specific data asset is a time-based advantage, but time-based advantages erode when a $200 million competitor decides your niche is interesting. I'd watch this one. I'd root for them. But it's not the kind of bet where I feel that jolt -- that intuitive flash I had with Niantic where I could see the whole future unfolding. This feels like a good company solving a known problem, and my best investments have never been described that way.
