# Proximitty -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The competitive landscape tells the entire story before I even get to the product. Prodigal Technologies has $14.2 million and a multi-year head start building AI for loan servicing. Skit.ai has $47.6 million and $22.4 million in revenue doing voice AI for collections. TrueAccord has $78 million for AI-driven digital collections. LoanPro raised a $100 million Series A for its loan management platform. Peach Finance has $35.8 million for API-first servicing. This is not a market with a secret hiding in plain sight -- this is a market where the thesis "AI will transform loan servicing and collections" has been funded to the tune of a quarter billion dollars across multiple well-capitalized players. When five companies and three incumbents are all converging on the same insight, the insight is not an insight. It is a consensus, and consensus markets are where returns go to die. The mimetic dynamics here are textbook: each player watches the others, copies the features that seem to work, and burns capital matching subsidies and sales efforts. No one achieves structural dominance because everyone is playing the same game.

The claimed differentiator -- computer vision-based browser automation that requires no API integration -- is the strongest element in the pitch, but it fails the durability test in a way that should concern any serious investor. Anthropic shipped computer use in October 2024. OpenAI has Operator. Google is building equivalent capabilities. The entire foundation model industry is converging on the thesis that AI agents should be able to operate browser interfaces autonomously. Proximitty is building its moat on top of a capability that the largest technology companies on Earth are actively commoditizing and distributing for free or near-free. This is not proprietary technology that compounds over time -- it is an application layer sitting above an infrastructure that is being pulled out from underneath it. When the platform eats the feature, the company built on that feature disappears. I have seen this pattern repeatedly: a startup identifies a genuine capability gap, builds a product around it, and then watches as the underlying platform incorporates that capability natively. The window between "this is differentiated" and "this is a feature of the operating system" has been compressing with every generation of foundation models.

The founders deserve separate examination because they are more capable than the company's structural position suggests. Ho scaled Taptap Send from $75 million to $200 million ARR -- that is not trivial execution, and his McKinsey credit risk advisory work gives him genuine domain fluency. Zhang built security infrastructure serving 300,000 Bloomberg terminals and led ACI.dev to 4.7 thousand GitHub stars, demonstrating both enterprise engineering depth and open-source AI agent expertise. These are credentials that suggest they could build something meaningful. But competence in execution is not the same as possessing a secret about the world. Nothing in the dossier suggests that either founder holds a contrarian belief about lending, collections, or financial technology that the market has rejected. "Collections workflows are manual and should be automated by AI" is a statement that every venture-backed competitor in this space would enthusiastically endorse. The founders appear to be talented operators entering a crowded arena, which is precisely the profile that my framework predicts will produce mediocre outcomes regardless of individual capability.

The strongest bull case would require believing three things simultaneously: that the no-API deployment model creates a genuine 10x reduction in time-to-value for bank IT departments compared to every API-integration competitor; that Proximitty can lock in enough institutional workflows before browser automation becomes commodity infrastructure; and that the accumulating borrower-level data creates a compounding advantage in collection rates that no competitor can replicate. If all three were true, there is a version of this company that becomes the default overlay for legacy loan servicing systems -- a wedge that bypasses the entire integration ecosystem and creates switching costs through workflow lock-in rather than data migration. The compliance monitoring layer adds real friction for would-be competitors given the regulatory density of FDCPA, TCPA, and state-level rules. This is not an impossible outcome. But it requires winning a speed race against both well-funded direct competitors and the foundation model providers themselves, in a market where no structural mechanism guarantees that early deployment compounds into unassailable advantage. The 20 percentage point improvement in first-contact rates is meaningful but not the kind of order-of-magnitude shift that makes the old approach economically unviable.

I am passing. The absence of a genuine secret is disqualifying in my framework. What Proximitty has is a clever deployment mechanism -- browser automation instead of API integration -- applied to a consensus thesis in a heavily funded market. Clever mechanisms are not secrets. A secret would be something like "collections is fundamentally misunderstood as a contact-rate problem when it is actually a behavioral prediction problem, and everyone solving for contact rate is solving the wrong equation." That would be contrarian. "We can automate what agents do manually, faster and without integration projects" is an efficiency thesis that every competitor shares. The market will resolve this through competition, and competition produces exactly the margin compression that makes venture returns impossible.
