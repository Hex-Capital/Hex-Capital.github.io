# Smartbase -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Sam Goldman has founded three companies across three entirely different domains: Sphinx Labs in web3 DevOps ($2M raised), Anytrace in AI support engineering (YC S25), and now Smartbase in manufacturing order entry (YC Spring 2026). The dossier says Goldman and Taira Fujioka "worked on factory floors" and "slept at a factory" to understand their new problem. Here's the tension I keep turning over: is this the running back quality — persistent on the goal of building something valuable, flexible on which field to play on — or is it something closer to what I'd call domain tourism? When I backed Patrick Collison at Auctomatic and then again at Stripe, the thread connecting those companies was obvious. Collison was a programmer building infrastructure for programmers, and each venture deepened his understanding of the same fundamental problem. Smartbase's founders went from smart contracts to support tickets to faxed purchase orders. I can't find the thread. That doesn't automatically disqualify them — the Reddit founders built something completely unrelated to their original pitch — but in that case I gave them the idea and I'd already evaluated them face-to-face. With Smartbase, I'm reading a dossier, not sitting across a table, and the pivot pattern is doing real work against them.

Taira Fujioka's credential — youngest SDE 2 at AWS SageMaker Inference, building scalable inference infrastructure for Salesforce, JP Morgan, and Qualtrics — is the strongest single founder signal in Smartbase's dossier. That's not a padded resume line; SageMaker Inference at scale is genuinely hard distributed systems work, and it's directly relevant to building the AI extraction pipeline Smartbase needs. Goldman's track record of shipping products (39 GitHub repos, Sphinx reaching $2M in funding, two YC acceptances) shows he can build and sell. Getting accepted to YC twice in consecutive batches means Harj Taggar or someone on the team saw enough quality to bet again after Anytrace didn't work out. Smartbase's founders can clearly build the product themselves — that box is checked. But I'm looking for stories of creative workarounds under constraint, the Airbnb cereal-box moments, and the dossier gives me none. "Slept at a factory" is immersion, which I respect, but immersion is research, not resourcefulness. Smartbase is onboarding first customers onto five-figure annual contracts — that's real money from real manufacturers — which is a better signal than a pitch deck, but I want to know how they got those customers. Did they cold-call 500 factories? Did they physically walk into a machine shop and refuse to leave until someone showed them a stack of unprocessed POs? The dossier is silent on acquisition stories, and truly resourceful founders almost always generate notable anecdotes.

Smartbase's problem — manufacturers hand-keying purchase orders into ERP systems, 15 minutes per PO, from faxes and handwritten forms — is a legitimate schlep. This is the kind of work that makes every developer I know grimace. Nine ERP integrations (Acumatica, Epicor, Infor, Dynamics 365, NetSuite, QuickBooks, SAP, SYSPRO, Sage X3) maintained by two people is either heroic or reckless, and I genuinely don't know which. Stripe succeeded with the schlep of payment infrastructure because the Collison brothers were building for people exactly like themselves — developers who hated integrating payment systems. Smartbase's founders are developers building for manufacturing floor workers they met during deliberate research, not people they already were. The schlep is real but the discovery isn't organic. Goldman and Fujioka didn't encounter this problem in their own work; they went looking for a problem to solve after Anytrace, found manufacturing PO entry through what appears to be market scanning, and then immersed themselves. That's diligent and it's admirable, but it's not "live in the future, then build what's missing." It's "visit someone else's present, then build what they're missing."

Conexiom at $170M raised and ~$75M annual revenue serving 16 of the top 20 industrial distributors creates a genuine structural gap that Smartbase could exploit — enterprise incumbents almost never move downmarket successfully because their sales teams, pricing, and implementation models are all calibrated for large accounts. Smartbase targeting SMB manufacturers with five-figure contracts (versus what I'd estimate are Conexiom's six-figure-plus enterprise deals) is a reasonable wedge. But the idea itself doesn't repel — it generates boredom, which is a weaker form of the "seems like a bad idea" quality I look for. Nobody hears "automated PO entry for manufacturers" and says "that's insane, it'll never work." They say "oh, sure, that makes sense" and then lose interest because the work of building it sounds tedious. Smartbase sits in the rare category where the schlep creates a moat but the idea doesn't create controversy. The "why now" is also fairly clean: LLMs can now extract structured data from heterogeneous documents at SMB-viable costs, and ERP APIs have matured. That's a genuine timing window, but it's visible to everyone, which means Workist ($11.6M raised), Buddy, and Conexiom's own January 2025 "AI-Powered Ideal Order Platform" launch are all converging on the same opportunity.

The strongest bull case for Smartbase is the ClassDojo parallel: founders entering a schlep-heavy domain outside the typical YC comfort zone, where the sheer unpleasantness of the work (ERP integrations, factory floor sales, heterogeneous document formats) creates natural barriers to competition from polished founders who'd rather build another AI wrapper. Goldman and Fujioka have demonstrated they can ship products, get into YC, and — critically — they voluntarily left the developer-tools world for manufacturing floors. That takes a kind of courage or restlessness. If Smartbase's first customers are genuinely retained on five-figure annual contracts six months from now, and if the team can demonstrate 5-7% weekly growth in POs processed or revenue, I'd revise upward significantly. The verifiable condition that would flip me to Invest: evidence that their first 3-5 customers are using Smartbase as their primary PO entry workflow (not a trial, not a pilot — the actual production path) and that retention is holding. That would prove the product works in the messy reality of factory operations, not just in demo environments.

But I keep returning to the pivot pattern. Three domains in roughly three years — web3, AI support, manufacturing — is a lot of searching. Smartbase's founders are technically strong and clearly hardworking, but I don't see the deep problem-obsession that distinguishes the best founders I've backed. The factory floor immersion feels like due diligence, not destiny. The core extraction technology is increasingly commoditized via LLM APIs, which means Smartbase's moat depends entirely on the depth and reliability of those nine ERP integrations — a defensibility argument that's also an engineering risk argument at two-person scale. I'd score Smartbase in the Neutral range: real schlep, real technical competence, real early revenue signals, but no organic discovery story, no contrarian insight that makes smart people say "that's crazy," and a founder trajectory that reads more like adaptive searching than obsessive conviction about a specific problem.
