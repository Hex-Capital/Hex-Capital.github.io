# Alt-X -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The competitive landscape tells me everything I need to know about the timing problem here. Endex has $14M from the OpenAI Startup Fund. Shortcut has $78M+ from Sequoia at a $300M+ valuation. Both are building general-purpose AI agents that live inside Excel. Alt-X is a two-person team with YC funding trying to carve out a vertical niche -- alternative-asset underwriting -- while these horizontal players accumulate capital, talent, and training data at 50-to-150x the resource level. The vertical-beats-horizontal thesis is real in some markets, but it requires the vertical to have genuine domain complexity that horizontals can't absorb as a feature sprint. When Shortcut decides to add a PE underwriting template, what stops them? When Microsoft ships Copilot with financial document extraction, what's left? This is not a contrarian bet. This is a consensus category -- "AI automates tedious spreadsheet work" -- with a niche positioning that feels more like a marketing choice than a structural moat.

Ryan Samadi's background is legitimate: Citadel, Bank of America, a PE shop, a student-managed fund, and Stanford CS with an AI concentration. He has genuinely sat in the analyst seat, manually copying numbers from CIMs into Excel at 2 AM. That's real pain recognition. But here's the specific knowledge test: what does Samadi know about this problem that couldn't be learned by any smart engineer who spent three months embedded in a PE fund's deal team? The insight that private-market underwriting runs on brittle, manual Excel work is not a secret -- it's a complaint I hear at every LP dinner. Thousands of former analysts carry this same frustration. The knowledge that would be non-transferable is either a technical breakthrough in document extraction accuracy for financial data, or a deep structural insight about why previous automation attempts failed in institutional contexts. Neither is visible in the dossier. The CTO's background is largely unconfirmed, with no public repos or clear technical signal. I can't evaluate a founding team's technical depth when the technical co-founder is opaque.

The leverage architecture concerns me most. Alt-X is an application-layer tool built on top of foundation models, inside someone else's platform, for a specific vertical. Every term in that sentence works against leverage. Each asset class -- real estate, private equity, venture -- requires its own KPI taxonomies, extraction logic, and model templates. Scaling from RE to PE is not a marginal cost near zero; it's substantial new domain work. The $30/month price point confirms this: if the tool were creating transformative leverage for analysts processing multi-hundred-million-dollar deals, you could charge $300/month without hesitation. Pricing at $30 suggests the founders themselves see this as a lightweight productivity enhancement, not a category-defining capability. Compare this to what I look for: Stack Overflow created a knowledge graph that became more valuable with every question asked. Replit built a coding platform where one person's improvement serves millions. Alt-X builds deal-specific models one at a time, getting slightly better at extraction with volume. The compounding is real but modest -- it's incremental ML improvement, not network effects.

The strongest bull case would require believing that alternative-asset document understanding is a genuinely hard domain where generalist AI agents will persistently underperform. If PE and RE documents are so idiosyncratic -- each fund structures deals differently, each CIM has unique presentation of financials, each asset class has non-obvious KPI relationships -- that Endex and Shortcut's general-purpose approaches produce unreliable outputs in this vertical, then deep vertical specialization becomes a real moat. If Samadi's combined finance-and-AI background lets him build extraction models that achieve 98% accuracy where generalists hit 80%, that gap matters enormously when you're underwriting a $200M deal. And if family offices and mid-market PE firms develop workflow dependency on Alt-X's templates, switching costs become real. The data flywheel from processing hundreds of deals across asset classes could compound into something defensible. For this bull case to hold, you'd need the domain complexity to be persistent -- not just a temporary gap that foundation models close in 18 months.

I don't find that bull case convincing enough to invest my own money. The core technology -- extracting structured data from PDFs and generating Excel models -- sits squarely in the capability zone of foundation models that are improving quarterly. What's domain-specific today becomes commoditized capability tomorrow. The founders are building a feature, not a company. Smart, hardworking people in a real market, but without the specific knowledge depth, leverage architecture, or contrarian positioning that would make this an asymmetric bet.
