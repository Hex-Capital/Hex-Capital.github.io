# IncidentFox -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that jumps out immediately is that there are at least ten funded startups doing approximately the same thing. AI SRE agents are not a problem hiding in plain sight -- they're a problem everyone is racing toward. When I wrote about schlep blindness, the key insight was that the best opportunities are ones smart people unconsciously avoid because the work is ugly. Payments before Stripe. Hospitality logistics before Airbnb. Nobody was unconsciously avoiding "use AI to investigate production incidents." If anything, the opposite happened: every engineer who's ever been paged at 3am and who has access to GPT-4 had this idea simultaneously. That's a category signal I pay close attention to, and it's usually negative. When a dozen startups with similar pitches are all raising money in the same 12-month window, the idea isn't contrarian -- it's consensus. And consensus ideas have already been priced.

That said, I don't want to dismiss the organic element entirely. Long Yi was on the Stateful Infra team at Roblox, doing SRE work for systems serving 100 million daily active users. He didn't read about incident fatigue in a blog post -- he lived it. Jimmy Wei built conversational AI at Meta FAIR and social communication features at Roblox. The idea grew from genuine operational pain, not a brainstorming session about hot AI categories. If I'm being honest, the founder-problem fit here is real in a way that many of their ten competitors probably can't match. But organic discovery alone doesn't make the investment. The question is whether the organic discovery led to an insight that others can't easily replicate, and I'm not convinced it did.

The technical signal is the strongest thing in their favor. Two engineers from Roblox, 1,003 commits on the repo, 40+ integrations already built, a working product deployed in Slack. They're building it themselves, which is what I always want to see. The auto-discovery of integrations -- where the agent analyzes your codebase and past incidents to generate its own connectors without manual setup -- is the kind of engineering work that's genuinely hard to do well. If that actually works reliably, it could be the wedge that separates them from competitors who require weeks of manual configuration. The open-source strategy (Apache 2.0 core) is also a thoughtful distribution choice that signals they understand how developer tools spread. These are builders, not presenters.

The bull case would go something like this: the incumbents -- PagerDuty, incident.io, Rootly -- have architectures designed around coordinating humans, and bolting AI investigation onto a human-coordination platform is fundamentally harder than building investigation-first. The auto-integration discovery is a real technical moat if it works. The OSS approach creates bottom-up adoption in exactly the way that made GitLab and PostHog work. And the founders' Roblox SRE experience gives them pattern-matching about real incident workflows that pure AI researchers lack. If all of that comes together, you'd have a company that wins the AI SRE category through superior developer experience and community adoption. It's a plausible story.

But I keep coming back to the competitive landscape. Neubird has $22.5M and Microsoft's backing. incident.io just raised $62M specifically to build AI agents for incident resolution. PagerDuty is a public company with $467 million in revenue adding AI features. When I look at IncidentFox's current position -- still seeking their first two pilot partners, 413 GitHub stars, no revenue -- they're bringing a knife to a gunfight in terms of resources. The question becomes: is the founding team so exceptionally strong that they can outrun well-funded competitors the way Stripe outran established payment processors? Maybe. But the Collisons had a fundamental architectural insight that the entire industry had been wrong about, plus they were going door-to-door installing their product on individual laptops. I don't yet see the equivalent level of either insight or intensity here. These are capable young engineers who found a real problem in a hot category. That's a starting position shared by hundreds of YC applicants, not a distinguishing one.

The founders are early in their careers -- Long Yi graduated in 2023 -- with no prior startup experience and no evidence yet of the kind of adaptive resourcefulness that separates founders who succeed from founders who merely execute a plan. I don't hold youth against anyone; we funded Reddit when Steve Huffman was 22. But the Reddit team wasn't entering a market with ten funded competitors and a $400M-valuation incumbent building the same thing. In a less crowded space, I'd weight the organic discovery and technical ability more heavily. In this space, I need to see something extraordinary, and what I see is competent.
