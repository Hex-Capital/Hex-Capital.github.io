# Prototyping.io -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's my problem with Prototyping.io in one sentence: the market is hot. When I see that RMFG raised $4.5 million, Jiga raised $19.1 million, Toolpath raised $10 million, Korso is in YC, and Factorem is funded -- all in 2024-2025, all doing AI-native manufacturing -- I know the "insane idea" premium has been completely arbitraged away. This is the opposite of Helium sitting in the wilderness for seven years before anyone understood decentralized wireless. Every VC with a manufacturing thesis has already spotted "AI + custom parts" as an opportunity. Xometry is at $687 million in revenue, Protolabs at $533 million, and Fictiv just got acquired for $350 million. The thesis that AI can improve manufacturing workflows is the most obvious application of current AI capabilities to the physical world. When I funded Solana at five cents, nobody else wanted to touch a new Layer 1 blockchain during crypto winter. Here, everybody wants to touch AI manufacturing. That's a fundamentally different risk profile for my money.

The founders are clearly competent engineers -- Revanth's IIT Bhubaneswar silver medal and Schlumberger manufacturing experience give him legitimate domain knowledge of what makes custom parts hard to produce, and Prerit's Microsoft AI work and published distributed systems research at ACM CoNEXT mean the technical execution isn't in question. These are sane people. But sane people with a sane idea is just a normal company. The "sane person, insane idea" calibration requires the second half to be genuinely non-consensus, and "let's use AI to analyze CAD files for manufacturability" is approximately the first thing every hardware engineer thinks when they see what LLMs can do with 3D geometry. The co-founder pairing also gives me pause -- Prerit's LinkedIn describes "recently joining forces" with Revanth, and I see no shared employer, university, or visible prior relationship. That speed-dating co-founder dynamic worries me when the business will require grinding through supply chain operations for years.

The structural moat question is where this falls apart for me. Strip away the AI, and what do you have? An RFQ marketplace connecting engineers to manufacturers. That's Xometry, which has a decade of supplier relationships, millions of processed parts worth of quoting data, and $300 million worth of Thomasnet acquisition for distribution. The DFM analysis layer -- the thing that makes Prototyping.io "AI-native" -- is precisely the capability that will be commoditized first. Autodesk could ship DFM checks into Fusion 360 as a feature. SolidWorks could integrate it natively. Every CAD platform has the geometry data already. When I evaluate whether AI makes a business stronger or weaker, this company lands squarely in the "weaker" camp -- the AI capability that IS the product will get cheaper and more widely available, not more defensible. Compare this to Craftwork, where the moat is painting crews and operational logistics -- you can't download a house painter. Here, you can absolutely download a better DFM model.

The strongest bull case would require believing that the DFM-to-order integration creates a new buying primitive for hardware engineers -- that the tight loop of "analyze my part, tell me what's wrong, fix it, order it" becomes a workflow that engineers refuse to abandon. There's a version of this story where Xometry's marketplace incentives structurally prevent them from offering honest DFM feedback (because telling an engineer their part is over-designed reduces order revenue), and Prototyping.io captures the buyer relationship through radical transparency on manufacturability. If they built genuine CAD plugin integrations and accumulated proprietary data on failure modes across thousands of geometries, the switching costs could compound. Revanth's domain expertise is the right background to build that feedback loop. But this bull case requires them to outrun five funded competitors, two public companies, and every CAD platform's product roadmap -- all from a standing start with zero revenue, zero customers, and a two-person team. I'd need to see signed LOIs from hardware teams, a functioning supplier network with quality data, or evidence of any engineer actually using this product before I could take that bull case seriously.

This is an application on someone else's infrastructure -- manufacturers' machines, cloud AI models, existing CAD file formats. There's no new primitive being created here, no protocol that others build on, no compounding network effect beyond a theoretical data flywheel that requires scale they don't have. The narrative can stretch to "operating system for hardware development," but that same narrative is available to every competitor in this space, and the ones with $19 million or $687 million in revenue are telling it more credibly. I pass.
