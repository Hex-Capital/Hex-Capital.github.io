# Maquoketa Research -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's what jumps out at me immediately: Stark Defence won a €268 million German Bundeswehr contract after failing all four test strikes. Let that sink in. The market is so supply-starved for loitering munitions that you can literally fail your product demonstration and still get a quarter-billion-euro check. That's not a market — that's a feeding frenzy. And when there's a feeding frenzy, every VC in the world sees the same opportunity at the same time. Defense tech in 2026 is what AI copilots were in 2024 — a category where the consensus has fully priced in the upside. Anduril at $14 billion. Helsing at €12 billion. Neros at $121 million raised. When I look at Maquoketa, my first question isn't "is this a good company?" — it's "is there any asymmetry left in the price?" And the honest answer is: I don't think so. Every sophisticated LP and every smart fund manager already knows that loitering munitions are the future of warfare. That's not a secret. It's a PowerPoint slide at every defense conference on earth.

The primitive test kills this for me. Loitering munitions have existed since IAI built the Harpy in the 1980s. Switchblade has been in theater for years. What Maquoketa is building is a better version of something that already exists — maybe with fancier RF, maybe with more autonomy, maybe cheaper. But "better attack drone" is not "Venmo." It's not creating a new verb or a new behavior. It's competing on execution within an established and increasingly crowded category. Five competitors with active military contracts and a combined several billion dollars in funding are already doing this. The team is building a widget, not a primitive. And I don't invest in widgets.

Now, the bull case — and I want to be honest about it because the structural dynamics here are genuinely interesting. The large defense primes face a real cannibalization problem. Lockheed and Raytheon make their money on $100 million platforms, not $10,000 expendable drones. Their business models can't pivot to high-volume, low-margin munitions without destroying their profit structure. That's a legitimate structural opening for startups. And the CTO's background at Berkeley Wireless Research Center doing phased array and RF design is not something you can fake — that's real, hard physics knowledge. If the team can build autonomous guidance and EW-resistant navigation that actually works, and if they can get it into a form factor that's cheaper than Switchblade but more capable than an FPV conversion, there's a real product. The physical-world moat is there in theory — hardware, ITAR, clearances, actual engineering — this passes my AI kill test. Nobody's replicating phased array antenna design with a language model. But passing the AI kill test is necessary, not sufficient.

What concerns me most is the regulatory dependency combined with no observable founder track record. Defense procurement is the ultimate "cities won't let the scooter companies be profitable" problem — your entire business model depends on government procurement officers saying yes, on timelines you don't control, through processes that can take years. The team is young Berkeley graduates with no prior exits, no demonstrated defense procurement relationships, and no evidence of existing security clearances. I haven't watched these people through multiple ventures. I can't calibrate the sane-to-insane ratio because I have no data points. The idea itself isn't even insane — "build attack drones for the military" is the most rational pitch in defense tech right now. That's actually the problem. When the idea is rational and the category is hot, you need the execution to be extraordinary, and I have zero evidence of that at pre-seed with no traction, no contracts, no SBIR awards, nothing.

The capital math is brutal too. Their competitors have raised $100 million to $3.5 billion. Defense hardware requires real capital — prototyping, testing, compliance, clearances, manufacturing. A 5-person team on YC money competing against Anduril isn't scrappy and disciplined, it's potentially just underfunded. Unless they have a plan to survive and iterate without raising a massive Series A in this red-hot category at a premium valuation, they're signing up for exactly the factory-farm fundraising conveyor belt I think is broken. And if they do raise a big round, they'll raise it at a price that already reflects the category hype — leaving no asymmetry for early investors like me.

I respect the technical DNA here. Phased array RF design from BWRC is the kind of genuine hard-tech capability that most YC defense companies don't have. If I'd watched Yeager and Hernandez through two or three prior ventures and seen them execute under pressure, this might be a different conversation. But I haven't. And the category is priced for success, not for discovery. When the whole market agrees that something is the future, you're not finding value — you're paying for it.
