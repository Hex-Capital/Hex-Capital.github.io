# AICE Power -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Verdigris Technologies has been clipping CT sensors onto electrical panels and running AI disaggregation since 2011 — fifteen years and $41.8M in funding doing essentially the same thing AICE Power is pitching me today. The differentiation? AICE Power targets smaller buildings. That's a go-to-market choice, not a new primitive. When someone tells me they're building "the same technology for a different segment," I hear "we're competing on price in someone else's category." The BEMS market is valued at $7.6B growing at 13.2% CAGR, Infogrid has raised $148M, Facilio has raised $45.9M — this is not a space the market has dismissed. Every climate-focused fund in Europe is writing checks here, especially post-EPBD. There is no mispricing. The risk is not in the price — the consensus premium is.

Grégoire Chomette has an MIT MS in Aero/Astro, published ML work at NASA on asteroid risk assessment, and did energy research at EPFL. Rémi Bouteiller designed multi-chip PCBs, built BLE firmware with two-week battery life at Amira Health, and managed manufacturing in Shenzhen. These are clearly competent, technical people. But competence in service of "cheaper BMS for supermarkets" doesn't trigger my sanity-insanity filter. Nobody hears "we monitor electricity in E.Leclerc stores" and thinks "that's fucking crazy." AICE Power is a rational idea pursued by rational founders — and that combination, in my framework, is the most boring possible configuration. I want the engineer who calmly explains why something impossible is inevitable. What Chomette and Bouteiller are explaining is sensible and achievable, which means the upside is capped by execution, not by category creation.

AICE Power's claim of "5% the cost of traditional BMS" with 30-minute installation is genuinely compelling as a product pitch — but it's a pricing innovation, not a primitive. Nobody will say "AICE me." Compare this to Venmo: before Venmo, peer-to-peer mobile payments didn't exist as a behavior. After Venmo, people used the company name as a verb describing a new action. AICE Power is making an existing behavior — monitoring building energy consumption — cheaper and faster to deploy. That's valuable! It's just not my kind of bet. When I backed Metropolis at seed, the insight wasn't "cheaper parking management software." It was that controlling physical parking infrastructure creates a moat that no app can replicate — and that AI amplifies a physical asset, it doesn't replace it. AICE Power's clip-on CT sensors are commodity hardware, openly acknowledged in the dossier as "a known technology." The physical component doesn't create a Metropolis-style moat; it creates a logistics headache.

The bull case deserves honest construction. AICE Power could be doing for small commercial buildings what Gusto did for SMB payroll — democratizing a capability that only enterprises could previously afford, with multi-site chains providing massive deployment leverage from single procurement decisions. E.Leclerc has 700+ stores; Accor has 5,000+ hotels. One deal scales to hundreds of sites. EU EPBD regulations will mandate monitoring, creating forced demand. The 10+ live sites tracking 5+ MW show AICE Power can actually deploy, and Bouteiller's Shenzhen manufacturing background means the hardware scaling risk is partially mitigated. If the data from thousands of deployed sites eventually enables something new — guaranteed savings contracts, energy performance insurance, real-time demand response markets for small commercial — AICE Power could evolve from "cheaper monitoring" into a genuine financial primitive. That's the path I'd need to see evidence of to get excited.

But here's why I pass: the AI kill test cuts deep on AICE Power. "AI disaggregation of electrical loads" is precisely the kind of feature that Honeywell, Siemens, and Johnson Controls can add to their existing building management platforms. These incumbents already have relationships with commercial building operators, established integrator channels, and brand trust in the energy space. If AICE Power proves that small-format commercial is a real segment, every major BMS vendor will clip on their own sensors and ship their own dashboards — and they'll have 10,000x the distribution. The dossier itself notes that AICE Power's "data moat is unrealized at current scale (10+ sites)." Ten sites is not a moat. It's a proof of concept. The one condition that flips me: show me that AICE Power's deployed data creates a genuinely new financial instrument or behavioral primitive — energy-backed lending, real-time energy arbitrage for SMBs, something that transforms monitoring data into a new asset class. Cheaper monitoring alone is a feature, not a company.

AICE Power scores lowest on Consensus Mispricing — climate energy tech with multiple well-funded competitors and regulatory tailwinds is the definition of consensus — and on Primitive Creation, where "cheaper BMS" fails to create any new category or behavior. Those two dimensions, which together represent 55 of my 100 points, are what lock this into Pass territory despite solid founder credentials and a real, growing market.
