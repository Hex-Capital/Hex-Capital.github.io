# Oddpool -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Oddpool is two credentialed infra engineers — Avi Arora out of Microsoft's prompt-injection model and Ritesh Malpani out of Bloomberg buy-side — building a venue-neutral data API for prediction markets four months after Polymarket acquired Dome for an estimated $10-20M. The dimensional signature here is a company that scores reasonably on technical lived-adjacency and catastrophically on governance: roommates-since-freshman-year with no named CEO, and Ritesh listed publicly as co-founder of *Benchspan* (YC P26) at the same time. I am never playing the game I seem to be playing, and the game I am being shown — "institutional data layer for prediction markets" — is the game Dome just exited. The game underneath is whether these two engineers, splitting attention across two YC Spring 2026 companies, can outlast a Polymarket follow-on acquisition or a FinFeedAPI feature ship. I do not think they can.

Anduril's Palmer Luckey shipped Oculus from his parents' garage *before* he pitched defense, and that prior-product trajectory is what made the 2017 check obvious — the founder had already proved he could ship hardware to scale in an adjacent domain. Oddpool's founders sit one rung below: Avi trained the ML model that secured 20B Microsoft requests/year and Ritesh made Bloomberg's buy-side trading systems "40% faster," which is genuine infra credibility and the closest analog in the dossier to lived-adjacency for institutional market data. But neither founder has shipped a comparable *product* — no prior data-API exit, no quant-fund operator background, no story about losing weeks normalizing prediction-market ticks themselves. The Microsoft and Bloomberg lines read as well-credentialed engineers who *could* build this, not founders who *had* to build this. That distinction is the difference between an Anduril check and a polite pass.

HQ Trivia is the failure mechanism I keep returning to: Yusupov and Kroll were co-founders without a clean tiebreaker, and when the product friction surfaced in 2019-20, the partnership collapsed in feud. Oddpool's dossier presents a *structurally worse* version of that anti-pattern. Two co-founders, no named CEO in the YC page or the launch post, and — this is the part that stops me — Ritesh Malpani's LinkedIn lists him as Co-founder of Benchspan, a separate active YC P26 company in AI-agent monitoring. That is not a governance ambiguity I project might emerge at Series A; it is divided attention *at the pre-seed*, surfaced before I have even taken a meeting. The HQ Trivia exception was earned by tactile viral pull I could feel through my phone. Oddpool offers no such override; the product is a developer API, not a thing I can play.

Polymarket's $10-20M Dome acquisition on February 19, 2026 — only four months before Oddpool's launch — is the dispositive market-structure fact, and it cuts against magical weirdness rather than for it. The slot Oddpool is filling is the slot the YC ecosystem *just watched* a YC-backed predecessor exit. That is the opposite of "find them before it becomes consensus." Eighteen months from now, the compounding pressure is one-directional: Kalshi will either license exclusively to a new aggregator or buy one; FinFeedAPI (CoinAPI's team, already shipping normalized Polymarket/Kalshi/Myriad/Manifold) will close the orderbook-depth gap as a feature ship, not a rebuild; and Oddpool's "historical orderbooks back to March 2026" head start gets shorter every quarter a competitor records ticks in parallel. The captured-history moat is real but it is a *decaying* moat against well-capitalized incumbents.

For the capital-intensive/unsexy-regulated dimension, the counterfactual is what would flip Oddpool a tier. If the dossier said Oddpool had inked a CFTC-registered execution partnership, or that one of the founders had operated inside a CFTC venue's compliance stack, this becomes a *Vigil Labs*-class regulated-finance bet and I lean in. Instead Oddpool is a tiered SaaS — Free / $30 Pro / $100 Premium / Enterprise — sitting on top of regulated venues without itself touching regulation. The "several thousand dollars in MRR" from the prosumer tier is honest pre-seed traction but it is *consumer-degen* MRR, not institutional ARR, and the gap between $30/mo whale-tracking subscribers and a Citadel-tier data contract is precisely the gap that killed every "Bloomberg for X" attempt I have watched.

Compare Oddpool's physical-verifiability profile to Niantic in 2015. I sourced Niantic by playing Ingress obsessively since 2012 — the artifact was in my hand, on my phone, walking around my neighborhood. Oddpool's artifact is a free tier with 1K requests/month and a "762 arb opportunities last week" landing-page claim; I can query it, but I cannot *feel* it the way I felt Ingress. The 700K+ indexed markets across Kalshi and Polymarket is a respectable engineering output, not a tactile artifact a normal human observes in the wild. This is developer-tools abstraction, evaluated through founder testimony and a pricing page, which my framework defaults below midpoint for a reason.

My 36-month forecast: Oddpool gets acqui-hired by Kalshi, a CFTC-registered new entrant, or a data incumbent (Bloomberg, ICE, Polygon.io) for $5-15M between months 12 and 24 — the Dome template, slightly cheaper because the neutral-aggregator slot is now contested rather than novel — or dies when Ritesh chooses Benchspan and Avi runs out of runway. Either way, the outcome is not venture-scale at my entry price. The mechanism producing this is the governance signature: split-focus co-founders cannot out-execute a venue-funded acquisition motion and cannot out-fundraise a CoinAPI-scale incumbent.

The strongest objection is that Dome itself proved the acqui-hire happens *fast* — Gabriel Jarrosson's note has it at four-to-five months post-Demo Day — which means a pre-seed check into Oddpool could mark up inside a single fiscal year on the same playbook. That is a real bull case and it stings, because liquidity-in-a-year is exactly the kind of "judicious magic bullet" my Full Ratchet conversation flagged. I dismantle it on two facts: first, Dome had to be neutral to be acquired, and Polymarket has already burned its acquisition shot on neutrality, which raises the bar for what Oddpool must build to be the *second* acqui-target; second, Ritesh's Benchspan co-founder listing on a public LinkedIn page is the kind of environmental signal I read when no one knows I am reading it — the Wi-Fi-name-at-the-Four-Seasons tell — and what it tells me is that this is not the founder's primary bet. One verifiable condition flips me: Ritesh publicly winds down Benchspan and Oddpool names a single CEO with documented operating authority before the next round. Until then, I pass with warmth — these are good engineers I would happily see again on their next, undivided thing.
