# Gojiberry AI -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

$1.4M ARR in nine months, bootstrapped, 1,000+ paying customers — those numbers from Gojiberry AI's YC page would make any investor lean forward. Then I look closer and see Gojiberry AI's own blog referencing "$500K in annual recurring revenue in 9 months" while a LinkedIn post claims $1.5M. Those figures can't all describe the same moment truthfully. I learned from IRL — where fabricated metrics masked a hollow core — that when the narrative drifts ahead of the math, I need to slow down. The traction might be real and growing fast enough that snapshots taken weeks apart create genuine confusion. Or it might be the kind of storytelling that outstrips building substance. Either way, the inconsistency is the first thing I'd want resolved in a founder conversation, because once trust in the numbers wobbles, I start questioning everything downstream.

Pierre-Eliott Lallemant bootstrapped to €500K at 22 through eBay dropshipping, built a French YouTube channel on internet business, co-founded CoCo AI (WhatsApp marketing, ~€500K ARR, sold in 2025), and now leads Gojiberry AI. Román Czerny co-founded CoCo AI alongside him. Dylan Teixeira built Edusign to 600K+ users across 1,200 schools, raised €1M, and sold it in 2025. Three repeat founders, three exits — that's genuine competence and I don't dismiss it. But when I ask my question — "tell me your story, not the business" — what I'd hear is: "We were sending 5,000 cold emails a day at CoCo and the process was broken." That's professional frustration informing a product decision. It's not a biographical crucible. Nobody here was forged by an experience that left them unable to walk away from this specific problem. The path from dropshipping to YouTube to WhatsApp marketing to AI sales outreach reads as serial optimization across adjacent opportunities — founders who are very good at finding the next thing, not founders for whom this thing found them. And Pierre-Eliott is simultaneously co-founding Standout, also in YC Spring 2026. A founder splitting himself across two startups in the same batch is the structural twin of the divided leadership that destroyed HQ Trivia — a company that had the right product at the right cultural moment and still failed entirely because of split attention and unclear commitment.

Here's the bull case, stated honestly, because the advocate has real ammunition: Gojiberry AI pivoted from a dead product (2,000-person waitlist, zero conversions on an AI sales assistant) to intent-based LinkedIn outreach and found explosive product-market fit — 100 customers in 60 days, 44% month-over-month growth, #1 Product of the Day on Product Hunt with 411 upvotes. The founders didn't just identify a market; they failed, pivoted fast, and built something 1,000+ customers pay $99/month for before taking institutional capital. That pivot velocity is the resourcefulness I look for — it's what I saw in founders who proved the market wanted them before proving they wanted VC money. Could any of my Known Exceptions apply? The RTFKT exception requires a genuinely novel category intersection at a singular cultural inflection — but Gojiberry AI occupies one of the most crowded categories in software, competing directly against Artisan ($46.1M raised, $5M ARR), Amplemarket ($12M raised), Salesforge (254 G2 reviews), Valley, and Persana AI. There is no narrow timing window. There is a gold rush, and Gojiberry AI is selling better shovels alongside a dozen other shovel companies. The Superplastic exception requires something "magically weird" and genre-defying — an AI-powered LinkedIn outreach tool is neither. The bull case is strong on execution, genuinely strong, but execution in a consensus category without structural differentiation is a pattern I've watched produce quick growth followed by commoditization.

Gojiberry AI fails my invisible system test as cleanly as any company I've evaluated. Cold outreach is not an opaque, extractive structure exploiting information asymmetry against people who don't understand it. When I backed Affirm, Max Levchin was exposing how credit card companies buried the true cost of borrowing inside fine print that trapped consumers. When I backed Ownwell, the founders were revealing that property tax assessments were systematically wrong and homeowners didn't know they could fight back. Gojiberry AI makes salespeople's LinkedIn outreach more efficient by surfacing intent signals — competitor engagement, job changes, hiring spikes. That's genuinely useful. But salespeople are not a population being exploited by an opaque system; they're professionals navigating a well-understood workflow with abundant tooling. There's no hidden tax being exposed, no cartel being dismantled, no power being redistributed from gatekeepers to ordinary people. Gojiberry AI improves a process. It doesn't reveal a structure.

The defensibility picture is where my concern sharpens from skepticism to conviction that this isn't my bet. Gojiberry AI's core capability — monitoring 30+ LinkedIn signals and auto-generating personalized outreach messages — is technically reproducible by any team with API access and competent ML engineering. The dossier explicitly states "no patents or unique data sources" and that "multiple competitors offer overlapping functionality." The "continuously learning" optimization claim appears on the YC page, but every competitor with a machine learning model makes identical claims. More fundamentally, the entire product sits on LinkedIn's platform — API restrictions, policy changes against automated outreach, or account suspensions could disable core functionality overnight, and the Salesforge review already flags Reddit complaints about spam behavior. When I backed Crusoe Energy, the founders were building physical data centers at remote gas flare sites — infrastructure that compounded with each deployment and couldn't be replicated by a competitor from a laptop. Gojiberry AI is a software layer on someone else's rails, with a Pro tier capped at two LinkedIn senders, in a market where well-resourced incumbents like ZoomInfo have broader multi-channel architectures they could easily extend. The dossier even notes that LinkedIn itself could build native intent-based outreach features, which would directly threaten Gojiberry AI's entire value proposition.

The condition that would flip me: if Pierre-Eliott chose Gojiberry AI exclusively — dropped Standout, committed fully — and the team demonstrated a proprietary data layer or infrastructure asset beneath the LinkedIn surface, something that compounds across their 1,000+ customers in a way competitors can't replicate even with equivalent engineering talent, I'd come back to this. The execution velocity is real and it deserves respect. But my portfolio was built on founders who couldn't walk away from their problem and companies that were secretly building something infrastructural beneath a surface that looked strange. Gojiberry AI has talented operators building a reproducible product in a consensus market, splitting founder attention across ventures, against well-capitalized competitors doing nearly identical work. The two dimensions that lock this verdict are contrarian conviction — AI sales tools are the most consensus category I can imagine, the exact opposite of rockets blowing up on launch pads or surveillance cameras making civil liberties advocates nervous — and invisible system disruption, where Gojiberry AI simply has no structural thesis. Competent founders building in a crowded space without a biographical mandate or a hidden system to expose — I wish them well, but this isn't where I put my own money.
