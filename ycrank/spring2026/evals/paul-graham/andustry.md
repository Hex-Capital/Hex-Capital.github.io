# Andustry -- Paul Graham Primary Source Research: Structured Quote Record Evaluation

Industrial procurement is one of those problems I've written about without naming it directly. It's a textbook case of schlep blindness: "Your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps." Sourcing mid-size machinery across borders involves weeks of back-and-forth emails with suppliers who may not speak your language, comparing quotes that arrive in incompatible formats, verifying certifications you barely understand. It's exactly the kind of tedious, unglamorous problem that most YC applicants would never think to tackle -- they'd rather build another developer tool or a consumer social app. That's what makes Andustry interesting to me. The fact that it sounds boring is evidence in its favor. The biggest startup ideas are terrifying, but the biggest startup *opportunities* are often just exhausting.

Johann Sturken is the founder signal I care about most here. Family background in industrial goods trading, mechanical engineering degrees from RWTH Aachen and TUM (the MIT equivalents in Germany for manufacturing), McKinsey's aerospace and defense practice, Airbus strategy. This isn't someone who read a TechCrunch article about "AI in procurement" and decided it sounded hot. He grew up inside this problem. "The component of entrepreneurship that really matters is domain expertise." When I see a founder who can walk into a factory in Shenzhen or Stuttgart and speak the language -- both literally and technically -- of the people buying and selling machinery, that's a real advantage. Het Dave complements with AI/SaaS transformation experience, and they share the CDTM program, which is selective enough that I trust the filtering function. But I'd want to understand how deep the relationship runs. Startups do to the co-founder relationship what a dog does to a sock.

The EUR 1M+ in beta transaction volume is the number that catches my eye. At pre-seed, with two people, that's actual money flowing through the system. It's not revenue -- we don't know their take rate, and it could represent a handful of large machinery transactions -- but it's evidence that someone trusted this platform enough to place real orders. That's qualitatively different from a waitlist or a deck of LOIs. The question I'd be asking in the interview is: how did those transactions happen? Were the founders personally shepherding each one, calling suppliers, basically acting as human middleware with an AI veneer? If so, that's actually *good* at this stage. "Startups take off because the founders make them take off." The Collison installation for industrial procurement would look like this: a buyer mentions they need a CNC machine, and Johann says "give me your specs, I'll have three quotes for you by Friday" -- and then the AI handles 80% of the work while he personally closes the gaps.

The bull case here is genuinely compelling. This is a narrow-and-deep well, not a broad-and-shallow hole. Industrial machinery is specific enough that general procurement platforms (Zip, ORO Labs, Coupa) won't optimize for it, but large enough ($810B, growing) that the ceiling is enormous. If Andustry can build real liquidity on both sides -- enough buyers generating RFQs that suppliers check the platform daily, enough responsive suppliers that buyers get fast quotes -- the marketplace flywheel creates defensibility that no amount of LLM capability can replicate. Network effects in two-sided marketplaces are the best moat in startups. And the timing argument is sound: LLMs crossed the threshold for reliable specification extraction and multilingual supplier communication in 2024-2025, making this automation feasible for the first time.

But I keep bumping into the defensibility question, and I can't resolve it from the dossier alone. The 500K+ supplier database could be the seed of a real moat -- or it could be scraped from Thomasnet, Alibaba, and European trade directories. If it's the latter, every one of the well-funded competitors (Matchory at $8.86M, LightSource at $33M) can replicate it. The AI layer -- RFQ generation, quote comparison, supplier matching -- is reproducible by any team with decent engineers and API access. "Starting a startup is where gaming the system stops working." Users are like sharks: meat or no meat. If Andustry's AI agent produces better supplier matches and faster quotes than a procurement manager could achieve manually, that's meat. If it's roughly equivalent to what someone could do with ChatGPT and a supplier spreadsheet, they're dead. The answer depends entirely on proprietary data they're accumulating through transactions -- pricing intelligence, supplier response rates, quality scores derived from actual outcomes. EUR 1M+ in beta could be the beginning of that data flywheel, or it could be too thin to matter yet.

What gives me pause is the absence of a cockroach signal. When I funded Airbnb, the cereal boxes told me everything: these founders would do literally anything to survive. I don't see that kind of desperate resourcefulness in this dossier. Johann leaving McKinsey is meaningful -- it's a real sacrifice -- but it's also a well-trodden path for German engineering graduates who want to do startups. The YC acceptance from Munich shows initiative. But where's the moment where they did something unreasonable to make this work? I'd want to see that in the interview. "After minute number one or two, the probability of changing your mind is pretty high." If Johann walks in and talks about industrial procurement the way Brian Chesky talked about hospitality -- with an intensity that borders on obsessive -- my score goes up significantly. If he talks like a McKinsey consultant presenting a market analysis, it stays where it is.

This is a company I'd take the interview on. The schlep is real, the domain fit through Johann's family background is genuine, the market is massive and under-digitized, and EUR 1M+ in beta says somebody wants this. But the defensibility question is unresolved, the competitive landscape is crowding up fast, and I haven't seen enough evidence of the kind of determination that separates founders who build something interesting from founders who build something that defines a category.
