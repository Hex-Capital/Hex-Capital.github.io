# Overshoot -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that jumps out at me about Overshoot is that it's competing in what might be the single most popular investment category in Silicon Valley right now. AI infrastructure. Every fund I know is tripping over itself to write checks into inference platforms. Together AI -- which is in our portfolio -- has raised over half a billion dollars. Fireworks has $331 million. And here's a two-person pre-seed team saying they'll win by being more focused on video. I don't doubt the technical insight is real -- that video codecs and streaming protocols require specialized optimization that text-first platforms haven't built. But this is the opposite of what I look for. The most promising companies are not popular. They are difficult to assess. Overshoot is easy to assess: it's a well-understood infrastructure play in the hottest market on the planet. That's not where I find my best investments.

Now, if I'd been sitting across from Zakaria and Younes, my first question wouldn't have been about their inference latency. It would have been: tell me how you got here. And what I can piece together is impressive on paper -- Zakaria spent seven years at Uber building the surge pricing system, then wrote GPU kernels for inference at Meta. Younes was the founding engineer at Cosmonio, built a CV platform from scratch, and rode it through an Intel acquisition. These are strong credentials. But credentials aren't what I'm listening for. I'm listening for the biographical thread -- the hardship that forged the commitment, the personal stake that means this founder will keep going when the company is in crisis at 2 AM and nothing makes sense. I don't have that story here. What I have is two talented engineers who saw a gap in the market and decided to fill it. That's a fine reason to start a company. It's just not the reason that makes me write a check from my own money.

The one signal that genuinely resonates with my framework is the cousin relationship. After HQ Trivia taught me that co-founder conflict can destroy even the best product at the best time, I started paying close attention to how founders handle disagreements. I've said publicly that I have a soft spot for family-run businesses because they come with built-in trust and tested conflict resolution. Zakaria and Younes being cousins, with complementary skills that map cleanly onto the product -- one bringing low-latency systems experience, the other bringing CV platform engineering -- that's a real positive. They've probably been arguing and resolving things since childhood. That matters more than most investors think.

For the strongest bull case: if VLMs truly require a fundamentally different serving infrastructure than text LLMs -- if the codec and streaming optimization layer is deep enough to sustain a durable technical moat -- then being the specialized platform that owns this modality could be enormously valuable as video AI applications proliferate across security, robotics, and consumer products. The timing argument is genuine: open-source VLMs only became capable enough for real-time video understanding in the last year or so, and the general-purpose inference platforms haven't yet invested in video-specific optimization because it's still a small fraction of their volume. A team with exactly these credentials, moving fast in this window, could establish itself as the default before incumbents react. The 300+ developers and sub-200ms latency claim suggest the product works. But I've seen this movie before -- specialized infrastructure plays in hot categories where the incumbents have hundreds of millions in capital and strong developer relationships. The focus argument is real until it isn't, and one product sprint at Together or Fireworks could narrow the gap. What would change my mind is seeing evidence that the specialization creates compounding advantages -- proprietary data from video streams, developer lock-in through tooling, or customer relationships in domains where switching costs are high. I don't see those signals yet.

This is a competent team building a reasonable product in a crowded, well-funded market. I respect the technical execution. But nothing here activates the pattern recognition that has driven my best investments -- no one is telling me this is crazy, no one is rejecting these founders, no invisible system is being made visible for people who lack access. This is a professional bet, not a conviction bet, and I'm not the right investor for it.
