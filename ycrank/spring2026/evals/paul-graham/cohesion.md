# Cohesion -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Devon Krapcho spent five years as a fundamental equity analyst at Long Path Partners covering software companies, which means he spent five years doing exactly the job Cohesion is trying to automate. That's a real signal. When Drew Houston built Dropbox, he built it because he kept forgetting his USB drive -- a specific, personal frustration that happened to be universal. Krapcho's version of this story has the same structure: he sat at a desk monitoring earnings transcripts, podcasts, and X posts for signals relevant to his coverage universe, and he presumably thought "why am I doing this manually?" Matt Munns built AI for investors at T. Rowe Price, a firm managing over $1.5 trillion. Matthew McBrien built and maintained AWS Lambda, one of the largest compute platforms in the world. Cohesion's team composition maps almost perfectly to the problem -- domain expertise, ML engineering, infrastructure engineering. The organic problem discovery here is among the strongest I've seen at this stage.

But here's what troubles me. "AI for hedge funds" is the most consensus idea in fintech right now. YC put "AI-Native Hedge Funds" as a specific Request for Startups category for Spring 2026. Over 35% of new hedge fund launches in 2025 branded themselves as AI-driven. AlphaSense has $500M+ ARR and a $4B valuation. Hebbia raised $130M at $700M. Cohesion is building in a space where everyone immediately agrees the idea is good -- and I've written extensively about why that's the tell. The best startup ideas initially seem like bad ideas. Cohesion's idea seems like a great idea to everyone. When I see an idea that could appear unchanged in a TechCrunch trend piece about AI in finance, I know the returns have already been competed away. Nobody hears "autonomous AI agents for hedge fund analysts" and says "that will never work." They say "of course, why hasn't someone done this already?" -- and the answer is that dozens of teams are doing exactly this.

AlphaSense at $500M+ ARR with its Tegus acquisition ($930M) and Sentieo integration is the elephant. Cohesion's differentiation -- autonomous agents vs. search-and-retrieval -- is real but thin. AlphaSense doesn't need to build Cohesion from scratch; it needs to add an agent layer to its existing platform, and it has the data licensing agreements, enterprise relationships, and hundreds of millions in fresh capital to do it. The open-source ai-hedge-fund repo on GitHub demonstrates that the underlying technology -- LLM agents monitoring public data -- can be assembled from commodity APIs. Cohesion's moat has to come from domain-specific tuning and analyst workflow integration, but those are execution advantages, not structural barriers. Stripe's moat was regulatory complexity that took years to navigate; Cohesion's competitive insulation comes down to whether fund analysts prefer a specialized tool over a feature inside the platform they already use.

Cohesion reports being live with 10+ fundamental equity funds managing combined $10B+ AUM, which is notable traction for a pre-seed company. But I can't tell whether these funds are paying or on free trials ("limited early trial access during earnings season" is the language on the YC page). And I suspect this traction is network-driven -- Krapcho worked at Long Path Partners for five years and knows fund managers personally. Network-driven early adoption isn't bad, but it doesn't tell me what I really want to know: are analysts using Cohesion obsessively, or politely? Are there users who would be angry if Cohesion disappeared tomorrow? The dossier gives me no evidence either way. I see no revenue signal, no usage metrics, no testimonials, no indication of whether the product has crossed the threshold from "interesting demo" to "indispensable tool."

The strongest bull case for Cohesion is the Dropbox parallel: founder-as-user, building what's missing from their own daily workflow, with the technical team to execute. Krapcho doesn't just understand the market -- he was the market for five years. Munns doesn't just know ML -- he built ML systems for one of the world's largest asset managers. If they can translate that lived experience into a product that analysts reach for reflexively during earnings season, the 10+ fund relationships could compound quickly. The agent-first architecture (continuous monitoring vs. query-driven search) is a genuinely different paradigm from AlphaSense, and incumbents often struggle to cannibalize their own UX model. There's a version of this story where Cohesion becomes the Bloomberg Terminal of the AI era for fundamental equity research.

But I keep returning to the absence of the signals I trust most. Cohesion's founders have no prior startup exits, no public evidence of shipping products before this one, no stories of creative workarounds under constraint. The co-founders don't share overlapping employers or universities -- no public data on how they met or why they chose each other as partners. I can't see the "animal quality" from this dossier. Competent professionals building a logical product in a hot space is not what gets me excited. What gets me excited is founders who've already demonstrated they can bend reality -- sell cereal boxes to fund their company, build a prototype over a weekend, personally onboard every early user. Cohesion's founders may have done all of this, but I can't see it, and truly resourceful founders usually generate notable anecdotes. The condition that would flip me to Invest: evidence that Cohesion has paying customers (not trial users) who demonstrably changed their workflow -- analysts who canceled an AlphaSense seat or stopped a manual process because Cohesion replaced it. That would tell me the product has crossed from "interesting" to "necessary."
