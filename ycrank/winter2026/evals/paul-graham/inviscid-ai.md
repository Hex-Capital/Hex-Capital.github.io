# Inviscid AI -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the three case studies: HVAC vent optimization, coastal infrastructure, storm surge forecasting. Three different verticals, three different customer types, two founders. That's a technology looking for a problem. When a team leads with the breadth of what their tool can do rather than the depth of one customer's pain, it almost always means the idea originated in the lab, not in the field. The company name tells you everything -- "inviscid" is a term from fluid dynamics textbooks. This is an academic concept that became a startup, not a problem someone couldn't stop thinking about because they lived with it every day.

How did they find this problem? Ziming Qiu is a fifth-year PhD student at NYU working on machine learning. Kabir Jain "likes physics and AI." Neither founder has any documented history in building operations, data center management, facilities engineering, or HVAC. The genesis appears to be: "CFD simulations are slow; we can make them fast with neural networks." That's a technically sound observation, and it may even be a real business eventually. But it's the kind of idea you arrive at by scanning the landscape of computational bottlenecks, not by spending three years as a facilities manager cursing at your BMS while the server room overheats. The organic test fails here. When I compare this to how Stripe happened -- the Collisons were programmers who personally felt the pain of payment integration every time they built a web app -- I don't see an equivalent moment of lived frustration from these founders. The problem they're solving is real, but their relationship to it appears intellectual rather than experiential.

The schlep in building optimization is genuine, but it's not the kind of schlep these founders seem drawn to. The hard part of this business isn't making CFD faster -- it's integrating with legacy Honeywell and Siemens BMS systems, convincing risk-averse facilities directors to trust autonomous HVAC control, navigating procurement cycles that take six months, and getting IoT sensors to work reliably in buildings that weren't designed for them. That's a sales and integration schlep, not a research schlep. Nothing in either founder's background suggests affinity for spending months on-site in a data center debugging sensor calibration issues. PhD students who build elegant models are not typically the same people who pick up the phone and cold-call the VP of facilities at Equinix. Maybe they will be. But there's no evidence of it yet.

The bull case is worth taking seriously. If the 1,000x speedup at 95%+ accuracy claim holds at production scale, that's a step-function improvement that creates a genuinely new category -- real-time physics-based building optimization -- that incumbent CFD vendors (Cadence, ANSYS) are structurally unable to offer because their entire business model is built around selling expensive design-time tools to engineers, not operational SaaS to facilities managers. The data center cooling market is growing at 16% CAGR because AI workloads are creating thermal challenges that legacy approaches can't handle. And Trane's acquisition of BrainBox AI for $103M+ proves that large HVAC companies will pay significant premiums for AI capabilities they can't build in-house. A 2-person team with the right physics-ML architecture could plausibly be acquired for their technology before they need to solve enterprise distribution. If Ziming Qiu's research background gives them models that are genuinely better than what NVIDIA Modulus or Cadence's Reality Digital Twin platform can produce, the technology itself could be valuable regardless of go-to-market execution.

But I keep coming back to the competitive landscape. PassiveLogic has $125M and NVIDIA Ventures backing. BrainBox AI is inside Trane. EkkoSense is deployed in 300+ data centers. 75F has Carrier and Breakthrough Energy Ventures as investors. Cadence is an $80B public company with CFD expertise. This is not a problem hiding in plain sight because of schlep blindness -- it's a problem that dozens of well-funded teams are actively attacking. The fact that the problem is visible and attracting capital means the advantage of being small and scrappy is reduced. Stripe worked partly because nobody else was willing to deal with the banking schlep. Here, everyone is willing. The moat has to come from pure technical superiority, and physics-informed neural networks are an active area of academic research with published methodologies that any well-funded team can access. I don't see what prevents PassiveLogic or Cadence from building equivalent surrogate models with their existing physics expertise and vastly larger engineering teams.

The technical capability is the strongest signal. Qiu has real publications in IEEE and arXiv, and the benchmarks on the website -- while unvalidated by third parties -- suggest a working system that produces real speedups. This is not vaporware. But technical capability without organic problem-market connection produces what I've seen many times: a sophisticated solution that impresses other engineers but struggles to find and retain paying customers. The multi-vertical spread reinforces this -- a focused founder obsessed with data center cooling would not also be showcasing coastal infrastructure and storm surge forecasting. They'd be living inside one customer's problem until they owned it completely.
