# InLoop Robotics -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Nimble Robotics raised $221M and has FedEx as a strategic investor to deploy human-in-the-loop warehouse robots at scale. Dexterity raised $300M at a $1.65B valuation for manipulation tasks. InLoop Robotics is entering this exact arena with $500K from YC and a four-person team whose visible backgrounds are in machine learning and academic robotics community building -- not in warehouse operations, not in hardware manufacturing, not in the physical supply chain work that defines whether a robot company lives or dies. The architectural insight -- deploy imperfect policies immediately, use teleoperator interventions as training data -- is genuinely clever. But it's the same architecture Nimble already operates at 400x the capital. InLoop Robotics needs to explain why they'll execute this better, faster, or cheaper per unit of learning, and the dossier gives me nothing to anchor that belief on.

Zakariea Sharfeddine did ML at Bosch and BMW. Stepan Feduniak was doing robot learning research at 18. Pasha Rizali founded RoboTUM and the European Student Robotics Association. These are technically capable people from a strong ecosystem -- TUM's robotics community is real. But when I evaluate founders, I'm looking for the animal quality, and what I see here is closer to student quality. Pasha's LinkedIn headline reads "Aspiring Robotics Engineer | Computer Science Student with a Passion for Innovation" -- that's the language of someone still positioning themselves in a career, not someone who has crossed from professional into obsessive. I don't see stories of creative workarounds when blocked. I don't see evidence of shipping a product before thinking about a company. Martin Mohammed, who isn't even listed as a co-founder, has the strongest builder signals: self-taught programmer at 12, IBM reinforcement learning intern at 16, hackathon winner, co-founder of Eggi (YC-backed, $250K). If Martin were the lead founder with these co-founders supporting him, I'd read InLoop Robotics differently. The resourcefulness signal I'm looking for -- the Airbnb cereal boxes, Patrick Collison shipping Auctomatic as a teenager -- is absent from the people whose names are at the top.

The origin story of InLoop Robotics sets off the alarm I've described many times: it reads like a market opportunity identified through analysis, not a problem discovered through frustration. The dossier cites "2.1 million unfilled warehouse jobs projected by 2030" from Standard Bots -- that's the language of a pitch deck built on research reports, not the language of someone who spent six months in a warehouse watching workers struggle and couldn't stop thinking about it. None of the founders have visible warehouse, logistics, or fulfillment experience. They come from the TUM robotics ecosystem -- an excellent place to learn robot manipulation, a terrible place to develop the gut-level understanding of warehouse workflow that makes your early product decisions right. The way to get startup ideas is not to read Fortune Business Insights TAM projections. It's to live inside the problem. InLoop Robotics shows no evidence of that lived experience. Compare Dropbox: Drew Houston built it because he kept forgetting his USB drive. The frustration was his own. Here the frustration belongs to warehouse operators the founders have apparently never been.

The strongest bull case is this: the "deploy imperfect robots" architecture is the correct architecture, proven by Nimble's success. Covariant's acqui-hire by Amazon in August 2024 opened a gap in the independent warehouse AI market. Foundation models have reduced the cost of training manipulation policies enough that a four-person team can now build what required fifty people two years ago. The RaaS model with zero upfront cost eliminates the sales objection that killed earlier warehouse robotics companies. And $2.26B flowed into warehouse robotics in Q1 2026 alone -- InLoop Robotics is surfing real market demand. If the data flywheel works -- if every teleoperator intervention genuinely improves the next policy iteration -- then InLoop Robotics could accumulate a compounding advantage faster than its capital disadvantage suggests. Young technical founders from strong ecosystems have surprised me before; I backed Bindwell's teenage founders in pesticide AI. But Bindwell's founders were operating in a domain where almost no competitors existed. InLoop Robotics is competing against Nimble and Dexterity, companies with hundreds of millions in funding and enterprise deployments at FedEx, UPS, and GXO already generating the same training data at vastly greater scale.

The hardware gap concerns me specifically. InLoop Robotics claims to have built "Loop V1," a bimanual robotic system, yet no team member with visible mechanical engineering, manufacturing, or supply chain credentials appears in the dossier. The team's strengths -- ML policy training, computer vision, data engineering -- cover half the problem. The other half is building reliable physical hardware, managing component suppliers, handling field service when robots break in warehouses. InLoop Robotics claims "zero robot breakdowns reported among deployed units," which implies at least one deployment exists, but with no customer names, dates, or deployment count, I can't assess whether this means one robot ran for a week or fifty robots ran for six months. The claim is unfalsifiable from the dossier. For a company that needs to bridge the gap between software intelligence and physical manipulation, the absence of visible hardware expertise is not a minor omission.

I'm passing on InLoop Robotics. The decisive factors are two: the founders haven't demonstrated the resourcefulness I need to bet against competitors with 400x their capital, and the problem discovery appears to come from market analysis rather than lived experience with warehouse operations. The condition that would flip me to Invest is specific: show me that one of these founders spent meaningful time working in or running a fulfillment operation -- that the problem is personal, not researched -- and show me autonomy rate data from a real deployment demonstrating faster policy improvement per intervention than Nimble's published benchmarks. That would change this from a technically capable team chasing a hot market into the kind of organic, obsessive problem-solvers I back.
