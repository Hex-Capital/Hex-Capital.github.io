# Meridian -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Meridian's core technology stack -- RAG over document stores, LLM-based generation, template-driven output -- is the exact architecture I described when I talked about passing on AI wrappers. Anyone with an OpenAI API key and a weekend can build "search your documents and generate a deck." The founders know this, which is why they frame the bet differently: "behavioral signal on how consulting work actually gets done. You can't download it. You have to earn it one engagement at a time." That's a sophisticated thesis. But at four months in, the behavioral data layer is aspirational, not actual. Glean has $200M ARR and 610 million dollars in funding -- if consulting-specific behavioral data is the prize, they can afford to pursue it. Kenley, backed by YC S24, is building the same vertical play with the same buyer persona. Meridian is making a data-moat argument without yet having the data.

The specific knowledge question is where Meridian falls short of my threshold. Kashyap Nathan's Palantir FDE stint and OpenAI "ChatGPT for Gov" work give him deployment-level understanding of how enterprises consume AI tools. Chris Farrington's Deloitte internship offers a sliver of consulting-side perspective. These are adjacencies, not depth. Nobody on this team spent years inside a consulting firm watching institutional knowledge evaporate when a senior partner walked out the door. Compare this to how I evaluated Perplexity -- Aravind Srinivas didn't identify AI search as a market opportunity; he'd been building the underlying systems for years at Google Brain and OpenAI. Nathan identified consulting knowledge management through the lens of an enterprise AI deployer, which is useful but replaceable. If Nathan disappeared tomorrow, another competent enterprise AI engineer could build the same product. That's the test, and Meridian doesn't pass it.

The leverage audit reveals a structural tension. Meridian's software scales with zero marginal cost per additional user within a firm -- that's code leverage, which is good. The AI generation layer (proposals, decks, data extraction) works while consultants sleep -- multiplier leverage. But enterprise consulting software requires founder-led sales to managing partners, customer success for onboarding, and template customization per firm. This is labor leverage creeping in at the distribution layer. The claimed metric of "managing partners referring us unprompted" would mitigate this if independently verified, but it's self-reported on the a16z Speedrun portal alongside all of Meridian's other traction claims. No customer testimonials, no press coverage, no third-party validation. Self-reported metrics from a fundraising portal are signals, not evidence.

The principal-agent diagnostic offers Meridian's strongest signal. Nathan dropped out of Georgia Tech. All three co-founders left institutional roles -- Palantir, Sandia, Robinhood -- to build this. Dropping out to build when you could stay on the Palantir career track is a real principal commitment, not an agent playing founder. The YC batch departure is ambiguous but potentially reinforces this: they had a16z Speedrun's $1M and enough customer pull that they chose to ship product over sitting in a batch. Agents optimize for prestige signals like "YC-backed." Principals optimize for building. The departure, viewed charitably, is a principal move.

The strongest bull case for Meridian: $700K contracted revenue in four months with a team of two or three would be extraordinary output per person. If real, that's Cursor-level team-to-value leverage -- revenue velocity that validates the product before the data moat even materializes. Managing partners referring the product unprompted means the pull is organic, the exact signal I look for. And the consulting vertical may be narrow enough that Glean's horizontal sales motion (targeting CIOs and IT buyers) structurally cannot address it -- consulting managing partners buy differently than enterprise IT. The vertical specificity could be Meridian's actual moat, not the data. For this to work, the traction has to be real, the team has to stay lean, and they have to accumulate enough behavioral data before Glean or Kenley catches up. That's three things that all have to be true simultaneously, which is a fragile conjunction.

Meridian lands in neutral territory for me because it fails the two tests I weight highest. Specific knowledge: the founders have enterprise AI deployment experience, not deep consulting domain expertise -- score sits at my absent-evidence default because the dossier shows adjacency rather than depth. Permissionless leverage: the product is built on commodity components (RAG + LLM + templates), and the claimed proprietary data layer requires sustained adoption that hasn't compounded yet. What keeps Meridian from a pass is the team's principal commitment and the claimed revenue velocity, which together suggest something real is happening even if the moat story is premature. I'd want independent verification of the $700K figure and evidence that the behavioral data flywheel actually compounds before writing a check.
