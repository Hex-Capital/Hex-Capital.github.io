# ZeroDrift -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Every time I hear "AI-powered compliance for financial services," I run the same mental exercise: who already owns the compliance relationship at Citi, at Vanguard, at every one of these firms ZeroDrift lists in their $2M qualified pipeline? Smarsh has 6,500+ customers and covers 18 of the 20 largest financial institutions. Global Relay is doing $189M in revenue and counts 61% of US hedge funds. These aren't sleepy incumbents waiting to be disrupted — they're deeply embedded, contract-holding vendors with the exact buyer relationships ZeroDrift needs to win. And here's the thing about "pre-send enforcement" versus "post-send surveillance" — it's not a paradigm shift, it's a feature. Smarsh doesn't need to cannibalize their archiving business to add pre-send checking. They can layer it on top as a premium module and bundle it into existing contracts at zero marginal cost to the compliance officer who already writes them a seven-figure check every year. The dossier itself concedes the point: "LLM-based content checking is technically reproducible." ZeroDrift is asking me to believe that encoding SEC and FINRA rules into machine-readable rulepacks is a durable moat — but Smarsh has been encoding regulatory knowledge for a decade. They just haven't pointed the AI at outbound messages yet. When they do, and they will, the switching cost argument evaporates.

Kumesh Aroomoogan is clearly a capable operator — twelve years building AI for financial services at Accern, $40M raised, Forbes 30 Under 30, exit to Wand AI. That's a real résumé. But my framework isn't about founder quality in isolation; it's about the sanity-to-insanity ratio. Aroomoogan is sane. The idea is also sane. Pre-send compliance checking is the kind of thing where every compliance officer nods and says "yeah, that makes sense." Nobody hears this pitch and thinks "that's fucking crazy." A reasonable person pitching a reasonable idea in a well-understood market — that's not where I find asymmetric returns. The $150K ARR in roughly two months post-launch is genuinely impressive execution, and the pipeline names are real institutions, which tells me Aroomoogan knows how to sell into this vertical from his Accern days. I don't doubt the founder. I doubt the bet.

ZeroDrift fails my AI kill test cleanly. This is a software-only tool that wraps LLM capabilities into a compliance workflow, sits on top of other companies' platforms (Outlook, LinkedIn, ChatGPT, Copilot), and has no physical-world moat, no protocol-level lock-in, and no network effects that compound with usage. Microsoft could build compliance checking directly into Outlook and Copilot — they already own the communication layer ZeroDrift intercepts. The "communication firewall" metaphor is aspirational, but firewalls work because they control the network layer. ZeroDrift doesn't control anything — it's a browser extension and an Outlook plugin asking for permission to sit in the data path. One API policy change from Microsoft or LinkedIn and the enforcement layer is gone. Compare this to Metropolis, where the AI amplifies physical parking infrastructure that cannot be replicated by code — ZeroDrift has no equivalent anchor in the real world.

The consensus mispricing angle doesn't exist here. Compliance software for regulated financial services is a well-understood, well-funded category. a16z Speedrun backed ZeroDrift, which means this is consensus-approved by one of the most mainstream capital allocators in the game. Nobody is uncomfortable with this bet. Nobody has dismissed this category. There's no narrative mispricing where the market is wrong and I can exploit the gap. The "why now" argument — AI-generated content creates new compliance risk — is real, but it's also obvious to every incumbent in the space. Global Relay is already reporting 3,000% year-over-year growth in ChatGPT content capture. The incumbents aren't sleeping on this.

The bull case for ZeroDrift requires believing that Smarsh and Global Relay are structurally incapable of adding pre-send enforcement because it conflicts with their post-send archiving business model — a classic innovator's dilemma argument. And there's some truth to it: compliance teams at these incumbents are organized around surveillance, not prevention. The cultural shift from "catch violations" to "prevent violations" is real organizational friction. If Aroomoogan can build enough enterprise relationships before the incumbents react — $2M qualified pipeline with Franklin Templeton, Nasdaq, Two Sigma — maybe he can create enough switching cost through firm-specific rulepack encoding to survive the inevitable competitive response. The SOC 2 Type II and ISO 27001 certifications at pre-seed are a signal that he understands regulated buyers need trust infrastructure, not just features. But this bull case requires the incumbents to be slow for three to five years, and I've seen enough enterprise software cycles to know that a Gartner Magic Quadrant Leader with 6,500 customers can ship a pre-send module inside eighteen months once the first big contract goes to a startup. The window is narrow, and it depends on execution speed, not structural advantage.

ZeroDrift is a capable founder building a sensible product in a consensus market where incumbents have distribution, relationships, and the technical capability to replicate the core value proposition. The two dimensions that lock this as a Pass are Consensus Mispricing — there's no gap between how the market prices compliance tech and the actual opportunity, so the risk/reward is symmetric — and Structural Durability, where pure software wrapping LLM capabilities for a workflow an incumbent can bundle into existing contracts is exactly the pattern I've been publicly calling dead. Aroomoogan will probably build a real business here. I just don't see the asymmetric return that justifies my capital.
