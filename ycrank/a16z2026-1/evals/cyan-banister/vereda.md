# Vereda -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

R$167 billion. That's what flows through Brazil's agricultural input distribution layer every year — a system where roughly half of all farming inputs pass through middlemen who add opaque markups to farmers who have no visibility into what things actually cost. When I see a number like that sitting behind a wall of intermediaries extracting value from people who can't see the pricing, something in me wakes up. Vereda is attacking this exact structure: using a WhatsApp-native AI agent called "Vera" to aggregate small farmer demand and connect directly to manufacturers, claiming up to 30% cost reduction. The structural parallel to Affirm is immediate and specific — Max Levchin made credit card pricing transparent and monetized from merchants; João Souza and Pedro Galindo are making input pricing transparent and monetizing from suppliers. Free to farmers. Zero. R$0,00. The invisible tax is the distributor margin, and Vereda is naming it.

The invisible system here runs deeper than the margin itself. Vereda's most formidable competitor, Orbia, is owned by the extractors: Bayer at 68.8%, Bravium at 17.2%, Yara at 14%. These are input manufacturers and distributors who structurally cannot disintermediate their own distribution networks without cannibalizing existing revenue. That's not a competitive moat for Orbia — it's a structural trap. Agrofy raised $66M but built on web and app, requiring downloads that farmers in rural Goiás won't make. Vereda chose WhatsApp because 99% of Brazilian smartphones already have it. The distribution insight is elegant: don't ask farmers to come to your platform, go where they already live. Every procurement conversation through Vera generates structured data on farmer demand, credit behavior, and purchasing patterns — data that manufacturers desperately want but distributors have no incentive to share.

João Aguiar de Souza was raised in a farming family and then went inside the financial machinery at BTG Pactual, facilitating over $1B in agribusiness transactions. Pedro Galindo grew up in Brazil's largest agricultural hub, invested in agricultural assets at Patria Investments — the most active PE investor in LatAm agriculture — and then taught generative AI at USC. They both saw the system from inside the financial architecture that supports it. The biographical connection is real: these are people from agricultural communities who went through elite institutions and chose to come back to build for the farmers they grew up around rather than staying in São Paulo banking or Silicon Valley tech. I respect that choice. But I'll be honest — when I ask "tell me your story," I'm listening for something more than proximity. I'm listening for the moment the system broke someone, or nearly did, and they decided they couldn't live with it anymore. The dossier gives me farming family roots and prestigious career pivots. That's genuine founder-market fit. It's not the biographical forge that makes me lose sleep thinking about a founder — the kind where the problem found them through suffering, not observation.

The defensibility question keeps me honest. Vereda claims $155M GMV across 800K+ acres in 45 days, with "$8M in potential revenue" — but that word "potential" matters enormously. Expressed demand is not captured revenue. Facilitated intent is not closed transactions. If manufacturers haven't actually agreed to bypass their distributors and sell through Vereda, the GMV figure is a measure of farmer enthusiasm, not a functioning marketplace. The dossier found no evidence of signed supplier agreements. The demand side looks real — 300K+ hectares, 50+ cities, 20+ product categories. But a two-sided marketplace without confirmed supply-side adoption is a prayer, not a business. The 45-day traction window also maps suspiciously well to a single planting season in central Brazil. I need to see durability across crop cycles before treating those numbers as structural rather than seasonal.

The bull case is strong enough to articulate clearly. If supplier adoption materializes — and it should, because manufacturers selling directly at a 5% take rate is far cheaper than maintaining distributor relationships — Vereda builds a compounding data asset. Every farmer transaction generates credit signals, demand patterns, and behavioral data that enables embedded financial services. In a country where Plano Safra channels R$475B in subsidized agricultural lending, the company that owns the procurement data pipeline has a credible path to becoming the credit infrastructure for small Brazilian agriculture. The WhatsApp-native approach at the LLM capability inflection point is the right technology at the right time — pre-2023, a chatbot couldn't handle multi-SKU agricultural procurement negotiation in Portuguese. The demand aggregation network effect is real in theory: more farmers means better manufacturer pricing means more farmers. If this flywheel spins, Vereda becomes agricultural procurement infrastructure for an entire country. That's a ten-year vision I can see.

What keeps this from crossing my conviction threshold is the gap between the invisible system clarity and everything else. The structural thesis is among the strongest I've seen at pre-seed — a R$167B intermediary layer with trapped incumbents, a WhatsApp-native entry point, and clear information asymmetry being dismantled. But the founders, while well-positioned, don't carry the biographical inevitability that drives my highest-conviction bets. The space is underserved rather than stigmatized — building in Brazilian agriculture is overlooked by most investors, but it doesn't provoke the discomfort that Anduril or Contraline did. And the core technology — a conversational AI agent on WhatsApp — is operationally complex but technically replicable. The moat has to come from speed of farmer adoption and supplier lock-in, not from the AI itself. I'm watching Vereda closely. The invisible system dimension is exactly what activates my framework, and if I see confirmed supplier agreements and sustained traction across multiple crop cycles, my conviction changes. For now, the strongest single dimension isn't enough to override the moderate signals everywhere else.
