# Gikl, Inc -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the tagline: "Coding assistants turned millions of people into software developers — we're doing the same for science." That sentence tells me almost everything I need to know about how this idea was born. It's reasoning by analogy from a trend. Someone looked at GitHub Copilot and Cursor, observed they were working, and asked "what else can AI democratize?" and landed on science. That's not how the best startup ideas emerge. The best ideas grow from personal frustration so specific that the founder can describe the exact moment they encountered the problem. Drew Houston forgot his USB drive. The Collison brothers couldn't believe how painful Stripe's predecessor APIs were. Here, the problem statement reads like a TED talk — "0.1% of humans do science, it takes 12+ years to start contributing" — which is a true observation about the world but not evidence that anyone in this company stumbled into it through lived experience.

Now, Yamamoto has a real scientific background. Physics PhD from Rice, publications in PNAS and Physical Review Letters, co-authored with a Fields Medalist. That's genuine domain experience. But he left active science over a decade ago — post-doc ended around 2013, then UBS, Palantir, Grand Rounds, and nine years at Meta doing ML engineering. If the pain of scientific bottlenecks were burning in his chest, you'd expect him to have returned to science rather than spending a decade building LLMs at a social media company. Vajda's background is pure AI engineering — generative media at Meta, no scientific research career. Together they're an impressive AI team building a science platform, but the problem chose them less than they chose the problem.

The bull case deserves a genuine hearing, because these founders are among the most technically credentialed I've seen at this stage. Vajda directed Media Generation at Meta — Emu, Movie Gen, the whole pipeline. Yamamoto was in the Core Llama group at Meta's Superintelligence Labs. They have 77+ combined publications and nearly a decade of working together. If AI truly can compress scientific onboarding from twelve years to hours, these two might be among the fifty people on earth who could build it. And the community angle is structurally different from what Google and OpenAI are doing — those companies are building autonomous AI scientists that replace humans, while ScienceSwarm is building a platform where AI augments human contributors collaboratively. That's a genuine insight about how science works. Science isn't a single-player game; it's fundamentally collaborative. The Polymath Project proved that crowdsourced mathematics can produce real results. If ScienceSwarm could become the platform where AI-augmented citizen scientists actually solve open problems, the network effects would be extraordinary. For this to work, though, you'd need to see early evidence that non-experts using AI on the platform can produce contributions that actual domain experts find valuable — and there is zero evidence of that today.

What concerns me beyond the manufactured origin is the breadth. Six domains at launch — mathematics, biology, physics, computer science, engineering, chemistry. The best startups start deliberately narrow. Facebook was Harvard only. Stripe was developers only. When you launch across six scientific disciplines simultaneously, you're either spreading yourself impossibly thin or you haven't yet figured out which domain your approach actually works in. The "submit a problem" and "rally a team" features suggest they're trying to build a marketplace, and marketplace cold-start problems are among the hardest schleps in startups. But the schlep of building the marketplace itself doesn't count if the core hypothesis — that AI-augmented non-experts can make real scientific contributions — hasn't been validated in even one narrow domain first.

The traction picture is a blank page. No users, no revenue, no waitlist, no community, no press, no Product Hunt, no Discord. The website claims "real contributions happening right now" but nothing in the public record confirms it. At pre-seed I don't demand traction, but I do look for any organic pull signal — a handful of enthusiastic beta users, a researcher who stumbled onto the platform and couldn't stop using it, a Twitter thread that went viral. Something. The total absence of any signal, combined with an idea that reads as manufactured from analogy rather than discovered through experience, leaves me without the conviction I need to write a check. These are talented people building something that sounds important in the abstract. But "sounds important in the abstract" is exactly the class of ideas I've learned to be most skeptical of.
