# Congruent -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's what grabbed me immediately: radar is the forgotten sensor. It's in 90% of US vehicles, costs $50 versus $1,000+ for lidar, works in rain and fog when cameras go blind -- and it's completely locked out of the most important architectural shift in autonomous driving. Tesla's FSD v12 proved that end-to-end neural networks trained on raw sensor data obliterate traditional modular perception stacks. Cameras have raw data access and simulators. Lidar has raw data access and simulators. Radar has neither. It throws away 99% of its data before any AI model ever touches it. Congruent is building the missing infrastructure layer -- raw-data radar hardware plus a generative radar simulator -- to make radar a first-class citizen in end-to-end training. The structural gap is real, and that's what makes this interesting from my framework.

The founder-idea pairing calibrates well. Clement Barthes was Lead Perception Software Engineer at Zendar, one of the handful of radar startups specifically targeting autonomous vehicles. He sat inside that world and concluded the entire industry's approach was architecturally wrong -- everyone is competing to produce higher-resolution processed point clouds while the paradigm is shifting to raw data. That's the signal I look for: an insider who sees the structural flaw and walks away to build the correct thing. Evan Carnahan brings radar-plus-ML research depth from UT Austin, and their founding ML scientist has specific diffusion model expertise -- the exact generative modeling technique needed for the simulator. These aren't random founders chasing a hot market. They're radar people who understand why the existing products cannot serve the emerging paradigm. The "insanity" lives in the ambition: a two-person team building both novel radar hardware and a first-of-its-kind generative simulator, competing against companies sitting on $70M-$195M in funding. But the insanity is anchored in deep technical credibility, which is the calibration I want.

What I notice that a typical investor might not: the simulator isn't just a product -- it's potentially a new primitive. If Congruent's radar data format and simulation environment become what AV developers train their end-to-end models on, that's a protocol-layer position. Every company that integrates Congruent's raw data schema into their training pipeline creates switching costs that compound over time. This pattern is closer to what I saw in Aleo -- where zero-knowledge proofs looked like an academic curiosity until they became infrastructure -- than it looks on the surface. The simulator could become the standard that an ecosystem builds on, which is where the compounding network effects live. Meanwhile, the hardware component creates a physical-world moat that no amount of clever software can replicate. Automotive-grade radar design, manufacturing, qualification -- these are operational barriers that fit my thesis about businesses where software amplifies but cannot replace the core value. AI doesn't erode this moat; it deepens it, because better AI models are hungrier for raw sensor data.

The strongest argument against: Tesla dropped radar entirely and went camera-only for FSD. If the industry converges on cameras as the sole sensor for end-to-end driving, Congruent's entire thesis evaporates. And the automotive qualification timeline is brutal -- years of AEC-Q testing before any OEM puts this in production. A two-person pre-seed team faces a capital-intensity problem that could require hundreds of millions in follow-on funding before meaningful revenue. The bull case requires you to believe three things simultaneously: (1) radar will be included in end-to-end stacks, not just cameras; (2) the raw data approach wins over processed point clouds; and (3) a startup can beat incumbents like NXP and TI who could add raw data output modes to next-gen chipsets. If all three are true, this is a generational infrastructure company. The $50 price point versus lidar's $1,000+ and radar's superior all-weather performance make a strong case for inclusion. The incumbents' architectural commitment to processed data makes the pivot harder than it looks -- it would require cannibalizing their existing product lines. And the simulator, trained on proprietary real-world radar data, creates a data flywheel that chipmakers aren't positioned to replicate.

The narrative can expand meaningfully beyond automotive. Radar is fundamental to defense, industrial automation, robotics, drones, weather systems -- any domain where depth sensing matters and cameras fail. If Congruent establishes raw radar data plus simulation as the standard for autonomous vehicles, the same infrastructure extends to autonomous systems broadly. That's a credible infinity story. But today, there's zero revenue, zero announced customers, and zero traction signals beyond YC acceptance. No LOIs, no pilots, no prototype demonstrations in public. The floor here is essentially zero -- if the end-to-end radar thesis doesn't materialize, there's nothing to cushion the fall. This is a pure conviction bet on the structural thesis and the founders' ability to execute against heavily capitalized competition over a multi-year timeline. I've made those bets before -- Helium took seven years from seed to unicorn -- but I need to be honest about the risk profile.

This sits right at my threshold. The physical-world moat, the protocol potential of the simulator, the "AI makes it stronger not weaker" dynamic, and the sane-person-insane-ambition founder pairing all activate my framework. What holds me back from higher conviction is the absence of any traction signal and the dependence on an industry paradigm shift that hasn't fully materialized. I'd want to talk to these founders -- specifically about their simulator traction with AV developers, their hardware timeline, and their capitalization strategy for the automotive qualification gauntlet. The structural thesis is compelling enough that I'd take the meeting and seriously consider writing a check, but I'm not reaching for my checkbook on the dossier alone.
