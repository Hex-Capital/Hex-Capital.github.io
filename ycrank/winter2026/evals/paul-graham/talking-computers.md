# Talking Computers -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the category. When Gartner reports a 1,445% surge in multi-agent system inquiries in a single year, and Microsoft, Google, and OpenAI are all shipping competing solutions, you're not looking at an opportunity hiding in plain sight. You're looking at an idea that's hiding in full view because everyone already sees it. This is the opposite of schlep blindness. The problem with multi-agent orchestration isn't that smart people unconsciously avoid it -- it's that every large company in tech is racing to solve it right now. CrewAI has $24.5 million. Relevance AI has $24 million. Microsoft has effectively unlimited resources and is merging AutoGen into their Agent Framework with enterprise GA targeted for this quarter. When I said the most impressive YC companies I've seen recently are not working on AI, this is the kind of company that observation was about.

The origin question is what concerns me most. How did these founders arrive at "making computers talk"? Both have relevant backgrounds -- Parsa interned at TextQL, Zayaan did inference engineering at Modular and worked at Yutori, an AI agent startup. They've been proximate to the space. But proximity to a trend is different from discovering a problem organically. Nothing in the dossier suggests a specific moment where they hit a wall that nobody else had solved, a particular workflow that broke, a concrete pain point that wouldn't let them sleep. "Making computers talk" is a mission statement, not a problem description. Compare this to Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive or the Collisons getting frustrated integrating payments. Those are specific. This is abstract. And the apparent mid-batch rename from "Speedtrain" to "Talking Computers" suggests the idea itself may still be crystallizing, which at pre-seed isn't fatal but combined with a consensus category makes me wonder what the actual insight is.

The strongest case for this company is the founders' technical depth. This is real. Parsa has substantial open-source contributions to the Rust/Conda ecosystem -- a SAT-based package resolver, compiler work, competitive programming in graph theory. Zayaan brings GPU programming, TensorRT, parallel computing, and actual inference optimization experience at Modular, which is one of the more serious ML infrastructure companies out there. These are genuine systems hackers, not people assembling APIs. If they've built something technically novel during the batch -- a communication primitive or protocol layer that's genuinely faster or more reliable than existing frameworks -- there could be something here. The complementary pairing of systems programming and ML inference expertise is exactly what you'd want for building infrastructure at this layer. They went to Waterloo together, which means the co-founder relationship predates the startup, another positive structural signal.

But here's what would have to be true for this to work: they'd need to have found a specific technical insight that makes their communication layer structurally better than what CrewAI, LangGraph, or the platform incumbents can build. Not incrementally better -- fundamentally different in a way that matters to users. And they'd need that insight to translate into something enterprises pull toward them, not just something technically elegant. The "workplace" framing hints at application-layer differentiation -- maybe the insight is that developers don't want another framework, they want an interface for managing agent teams. That could be genuine. But I can't evaluate an insight that isn't visible yet. The website is a title page. There's no product, no demo, no users, no waitlist. At this point in a YC batch, I'd expect at least a working prototype with a few early users providing feedback.

The combination of consensus category, absent organic backstory, no visible product, and formidable incumbents makes this a pass for me. The technical founders are the one genuinely strong signal, and if they'd brought this same caliber of systems expertise to a problem that was ugly and obvious but that everyone else was unconsciously avoiding -- the kind of thing that makes you think "why hasn't anyone done this?" -- I'd be much more interested. Instead they've chosen the problem that everyone is consciously racing toward. Technical skill is necessary but not sufficient when you're competing with Microsoft's distribution and Google's protocols. I'd want to see them again after they've shipped something and can show me users who'd be upset if it disappeared.
