# Talking Computers -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Talking Computers is that it's building in one of the most popular categories in all of technology right now. Multi-agent AI orchestration isn't weird. It isn't unpopular. It isn't difficult to assess. Gartner tracked a 1,445% surge in multi-agent system inquiries in just over a year. CrewAI raised $24.5 million. Relevance AI raised $24 million from Bessemer. Lindy pulled in $35 million. Microsoft is merging AutoGen into a unified Agent Framework with enterprise GA planned for Q1 2026. Google launched A2A protocol. OpenAI has Swarm. When I look at my portfolio -- SpaceX when private space was lunacy, Crusoe when mining Bitcoin on gas flares made people laugh, Contraline when male contraception made VCs squirm -- the common thread is that nobody else wanted to touch these ideas. The signal I've trained myself to read is the absence of competition, the sound of doors closing in a founder's face. Here, every door in Silicon Valley is wide open. The weirdness has been completely arbitraged away.

The second thing I notice is what I can't find: the founders' story. Not the business -- the story. I want to know how Parsa and Zayaan arrived at "making computers talk" as the thing they have to build with their lives. What I see instead are two technically capable Waterloo CS graduates with internship-level experience at AI companies. Parsa worked on Rust package management tooling at prefix.dev and interned at TextQL. Zayaan did inference engineering at Modular and was a member of technical staff at Yutori. These are solid engineering credentials. But there's no biographical thread connecting these founders to this specific problem -- no personal pain, no years of obsession, no story of being told they were wrong and refusing to give up. And then there's the name change: Parsa's own GitHub still lists him as "CTO @ Speedtrain (YC W26)," not Talking Computers. A mid-batch rebrand suggests they're still searching for what this company actually is, which is the opposite of the deep conviction I need to see.

Let me be honest about the bull case, because I try to be fair. The technical complementarity between the founders is real -- Parsa's systems and compiler background in Rust paired with Zayaan's GPU programming and inference optimization skills covers exactly the stack you'd need to build performant AI agent infrastructure. The "workplace for AI" framing is potentially differentiated from developer-focused frameworks like CrewAI -- if they're building an application-layer product where non-technical operators manage teams of AI agents, that's a different market than selling to developers. And the timing argument is genuine: MCP and A2A protocols are creating the HTTP-equivalent standards that make a neutral interoperability layer possible. If Microsoft and Google are incentivized to lock agents into their respective ecosystems, a vendor-neutral coordination layer could carve out real value. For this to work, though, I'd need to believe these specific founders -- early in their careers, with no prior startup experience, in the most competitive AI subcategory -- can outrun every well-funded competitor and every incumbent platform. That's a bet on pure execution speed against teams with more resources, more distribution, and more enterprise relationships. It's possible, but it requires a level of founder conviction and resilience that I simply can't assess from what's available.

What's missing for me is the dimension I care about most after the founder's story: does this company create economic access or make an invisible system visible? Uber wasn't a logistics optimization platform to me -- it was a poverty alleviation tool that created income for drivers. Affirm wasn't fintech infrastructure -- it made predatory credit card fees transparent. Flock Safety made community safety data visible. Talking Computers is enterprise AI plumbing for companies that already have AI agents deployed. It serves the technically sophisticated. There's nothing wrong with that as a business, but it doesn't light up the part of my brain that connects investing to impact. I don't feel the jolt. I feel like I'm looking at a competent team chasing a hot market -- exactly the pattern I've trained myself to avoid.
