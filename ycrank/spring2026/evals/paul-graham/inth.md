# Inth -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Guillermo Rauch calling c15t "@shadcn for Consent Management" to 79,000 people is the kind of signal that's easy to misread. Most people see it as a celebrity endorsement. What I see is a distribution lock: the CEO of Vercel, the company that controls the Next.js ecosystem where Inth's primary users live, publicly identified c15t as the canonical solution to a problem every one of his customers faces. Vercel isn't just listed as a customer on Inth's site — Rauch hosted a Vercel Community Session for it. Expo, Zed, and Infisical are also listed customers. These aren't logos bought with discounts; they're developer tools companies whose engineering teams chose c15t because it fit their architecture. That pattern — real developer tools companies adopting your product because it's genuinely better, not because your sales team called — is exactly how Stripe's earliest traction worked. Patrick Collison didn't sell banks on Stripe; he made it so obviously superior for developers that they pulled it into their stack and then told their friends.

Christopher Burns built Everfund before Inth — a nonprofit donation platform that raised $2.15M, processed payments across 33+ countries, and required navigating multi-jurisdiction regulatory compliance. That's where the organic discovery happened. You don't wake up one morning and decide to build consent infrastructure from reading a Gartner report. You build it because you've spent years wrestling with OneTrust scripts that add 500ms to your page load, because you've written jurisdiction-detection code by hand for the third time, because you've felt the specific pain of GDPR and CCPA and UK GDPR all demanding slightly different things. Burns has 10 years of TypeScript and web platform experience, contributed authentication code to RedwoodJS, and hosts the FSJam podcast. He's embedded in the developer community the way the Stripe founders were embedded in the developer community — not as observers but as practitioners who got frustrated and built what was missing. The Everfund-to-Inth trajectory has the structural signature of organic discovery: prior company forces you into the schlep, you learn the domain, and then you build the tool you wished existed.

Consent management is a genuine schlep. Nobody wakes up excited about cookie banners. The problem repels builders because it's simultaneously boring and dangerously complex — you're implementing UI widgets that must satisfy lawyers in 30 jurisdictions, and if you get it wrong, the fine is 4% of global revenue. OneTrust built a $500M business by selling dashboards to compliance officers, but their 514ms script overhead and $10K+ minimum ACV left a vacuum beneath them. Inth's c15t fills that vacuum with 0.6KB total size and 30ms load time versus OneTrust's 514ms and Ketch's 709ms. The architectural bet — headless, embeddable SDK versus injected third-party script — is the same structural move that let Stripe displace payment processors who sold to finance teams rather than developers. Inth sells to engineers, OneTrust sells to lawyers. That channel conflict is the moat: for OneTrust to compete, they'd have to rebuild their core delivery model and retrain their sales force, which would cannibalize their existing $500M revenue stream.

The 411 releases across roughly 11 months — nearly daily shipping — and the expansion from consent widgets to DSAR SDK (alpha), CookieBench, and integrations for Google Tag Manager, Meta Pixel, PostHog, TikTok, and LinkedIn signal iteration speed that I weight heavily. Inth's npm ecosystem shows 1.8M total downloads with 388.3K in April 2026 and a 26.9% growth rate. The v1.0-to-v2.0 progression (May 2025 to April 2026) with stargazers from employees at Expo, Adobe, Vercel, and Google shows that technically sophisticated developers are not just trying c15t but watching its evolution. Burns created CookieBench as a companion benchmarking tool — a clever positioning move that frames every CMP evaluation around the metric where Inth wins. It's self-referential, which enterprise buyers might question, but for developer adoption it establishes the performance conversation Inth wants to have.

The bear case has teeth. Inth has no public pricing, no revenue signal, and no visible monetization strategy as of April 2026. The open-source model creates a genuine tension: the better c15t works self-hosted, the weaker the conversion funnel to a paid tier. Unlike Stripe, where self-hosting payment processing is functionally impossible for most companies, self-hosting consent management is straightforward — you just npm install and deploy. CookieYes serves 1M customers at €9/month; Inth hasn't demonstrated it can charge anyone anything. The team is 3 people, but only Burns is publicly named — I can't evaluate the other two, and my pattern is strongly against solo founders precisely because the startup grind requires co-founders to "talk you out of stupid decisions and cheer you up when things go wrong." The regulatory maintenance burden across GDPR, CCPA, IAB TCF 2.3, UK GDPR, Quebec Law 25, DPDP Act, PIPL, and CPPA scales linearly with jurisdiction count, which is a resource-intensive commitment for a team this small. If Burns is effectively a solo founder with two employees rather than co-founders, that changes the risk profile materially.

The condition that would flip me to Pass: if, upon meeting Burns, I discovered that the other two team members are junior hires rather than true co-founders, and that Burns hasn't tested any pricing with even a single customer willing to pay. The open-source-to-SaaS conversion is the existential question for Inth, and absence of any monetization experiment after a year of shipping suggests either a deliberate strategy I can't see or an avoidance pattern that concerns me. The condition that confirms my Invest lean: if Burns can show me even $2K MRR or three signed LOIs from companies willing to pay for the hosted tier, or if the unnamed team members turn out to be strong technical co-founders with complementary skills. Inth fits my "hackers building for hackers" pattern, operates in a schlep domain that repels competitors, and has demonstrated real developer pull through the npm and GitHub ecosystem. The Rauch endorsement and Vercel customer relationship, combined with Burns's multi-jurisdiction regulatory experience from Everfund, make this a bet I'd take — but at the lower end of my conviction range, gated primarily on the monetization uncertainty and the co-founder question.
