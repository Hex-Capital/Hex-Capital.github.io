# Probe -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Probe is a Harvey-clone in incident management — a Spring 2026 pre-seed entering a category where incident.io already raised $62M in April 2025 explicitly "to build AI agents that resolve incidents" (incident.io blog) and runs $9M ARR at a $400M post (GetLatka, TechCrunch). The catalyst is real; the leader is already shipping the same product. Probe's analytical signature in my portfolio is the inverse of **Harvey** (2023): same category inflection, but on the wrong side of the leader-declared line, with a dossier that lists zero revenue, zero customers, zero press, zero open jobs, and only one named founder for a 3-person team.

On market catalyst, **Anduril** (2022) is the rung that matters: Ukraine had created a specific 24-month window where Pentagon software inadequacy was newly visible, and the catalyst was *under-priced* because peers still read defense as a graveyard. Probe has a real catalyst too — LLM tool-use crossing the usability threshold for autonomous triage, evidenced by the incident.io $62M raise — but it is the opposite of under-priced. The dossier itself names incident.io, Rootly ($15.3M raised), FireHydrant ($32.5M raised), and PagerDuty as already in the agent-SRE motion. When the catalyst is being acted on by a $400M-valuation leader with shipping product, Probe is not skating where the puck is going; Probe is skating where the puck already is, behind a body that got there first.

**Harvey** (2023) is also the LOW rung on revenue velocity — except inverted. The Harvey signature was "$3B → $5B → $8B in months" because real growth is impossible to hide (TechCrunch, Nov 2025). The Probe dossier's revenue line reads "No public data found," and the customer line reads "No public data found," and the press line reads "No public data found." I know the pre-seed calibration says not to penalize absence of metrics, and I won't penalize a Spring 2026 company for not having $9M ARR. What I will penalize is the AI Market Clarity test: when the category has a declared leader shipping the identical product, the entrant's only legible signal is a wedge — design-partner logos, a precision/recall benchmark, a distribution hack — and Probe's dossier surfaces none of these. "Something's fundamentally broken" is too strong for pre-seed, but "nothing is yet working" is the honest read.

On founder execution rhythm, the **Perplexity** (2022) template is Aravind pinging me on LinkedIn and showing up a week later with the thing we discussed built. Mahit Namburu's profile reads competently — UC Berkeley EECS with SpecDec research, LoRA inference at Databricks, machine translation at Apple, declined a Together AI MLE offer — and that is real ML infrastructure muscle that maps to building retrieval-grounded agents. What the dossier does not surface is a single shipped independent artifact, a GitHub footprint, or evidence of the between-meetings shipping rhythm I filter on. Compounded over 18 months, the risk is straightforward: an inference-pedigree founder builds the cleanest agent loop in the category, and incident.io ships a B+ version of it to 800 existing enterprise customers first. The two co-founders are also unnamed on the YC page — for a 3-person team, that is a diligence blocker, not a stylistic detail.

The index-test is what kills this for me. **Stripe** (2010) is the canonical index on e-commerce because every entrant in the category had to route through it. Probe sits in a category whose workflow path already routes through PagerDuty (NYSE:PD, ~$450M revenue), incident.io, and Rootly — Probe has to *win share against* those, not ride alongside them. The "agent-native vs. co-pilot" framing is real architectural differentiation, the same Snowflake-vs-Teradata argument cloud-native players used a decade ago, and it can work. But it works only when the entrant shows a customer count that proves the architecture wins in the field. Probe's dossier shows zero.

On single-miracle architecture, Probe is cleaner than I expected: the miracle is high-precision retrieval-grounded agents acting autonomously across heterogeneous logs, code, and runbooks. That is one miracle, not three — which is the **Decagon** (2023) shape, not the science-project shape I avoid. The drag is that the miracle is not yet validated — arXiv 2508.14231 documents agent unreliability in production triage, and Probe has published no benchmark refuting it. A counterfactual flip: if Probe shipped a precision/recall result beating incident.io AI SRE on a public incident corpus, the single-miracle score would move from soft-pass to soft-invest in one stroke.

My 36-month forecast: Probe acqui-hires into incident.io, Rootly, or PagerDuty at a sub-$30M valuation by mid-2029, with under $500K ARR at acquisition — the same pattern as most #2 entrants in a category where the leader was already shipping the identical product at the moment the entrant launched. The mechanism is the AI Market Clarity logic: when the category leader is declared and shipping, the consensus-priced entrant cannot pencil unless they bring a distribution wedge, and Probe's dossier surfaces no wedge — no GTM disclosure, no logos, no community, no benchmark.

The bull case that stings: Mahit's Databricks LoRA-inference background is genuinely the right ML-systems pedigree for building an inference-native agent loop, and incident.io is structurally a workflow platform with AI bolted on — exactly the disruption pattern that lets architecturally-native entrants beat retrofitted incumbents (Snowflake on Teradata, Figma on Sketch). If Probe ships a 10x cheaper, 5x more accurate autonomous-remediation agent and lands five named enterprise design partners within 90 days, the "agent-native" framing becomes a real wedge and incident.io's $96M raise becomes a liability rather than a moat. I would flip to Invest on one verifiable condition: five named enterprise design-partner logos closed within 90 days, OR a published precision/recall benchmark beating incident.io AI SRE on a public incident corpus. Until either appears, Probe is a #2 in a category whose #1 already declared — and I passed on **Lyft** Series C for exactly that reason.
