# Ressl AI -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that jumps out at me here isn't the founders or the product -- it's the competitive landscape. Netic just raised $43 million at a $450 million valuation from Founders Fund, my former firm. Avoca has nearly $48 million. WorkHero raised a $5 million seed. ServiceTitan, a public company doing $772 million in revenue, launched its own AI division. When I see that kind of capital flooding into a category, I know this is no longer the kind of bet I make. The most promising companies are not popular. They are difficult to assess. Home services AI in early 2026 is popular and easy to assess -- every VC in town can see the thesis. The weirdness has been completely arbitraged away, and whatever edge existed for a first mover is now buried under hundreds of millions in competitor funding.

When I turn to the founders' story -- which is always where I start -- I'm looking for the biographical thread that makes this founder inseparable from this problem. Arushi Gandhi is clearly sharp: Microsoft intern, Google Scholar, hackathon winner, built CRMs for PE firms at Fundinc. Abhishek Eswaran brings IIT Bombay engineering depth. But neither founder has ever worked in a collision shop, run an HVAC business, or dealt with the pain of manually copying estimates between CCCOne and QuickBooks. Their own YC description tells the story plainly: "We started as an AI consulting firm talking to businesses across every industry. After a month, the pattern was obvious." That's market analysis, not lived experience. They pivoted from "Devin for Salesforce" to trades AI agents because the data pointed that way -- which is rational and smart, but it's not the kind of founder-problem bond I invest on. Compare this to how I found Uber: years of personal conversations with my husband about the taxi medallion racket, then meeting Roger, my driver, and feeling the problem viscerally. I need that personal thread, and I don't see it here.

The bull case deserves a real hearing, because there's genuine substance underneath the competitive noise. $440K in revenue with four people is efficient, even if some of that traces back to the Salesforce product. The agent-on-existing-software approach is genuinely smart -- small trades businesses cannot afford to rip out their tools for ServiceTitan, and the overlay model meets them where they are. The PE roll-up distribution channel is clever and could be explosive: one relationship with a PE firm that owns thirty collision shops gives you thirty customers in a single conversation. Arushi's Fundinc background building CRMs for PE firms means she actually knows that buyer. If the trades-specific workflow data they accumulate -- estimating patterns, vendor pricing, insurance coordination protocols -- creates real switching costs, there could be a compounding data moat here that the well-funded competitors haven't built yet because they're focused on different slices of the problem. I can see a version of this story where Ressl becomes the Carta of trades back-office -- making invisible operational workflows transparent and automated. That version would genuinely excite me.

But I keep returning to what this company actually does in the world. When I invested in Uber, I saw a company that would create income for hundreds of thousands of drivers. Postmates did the same for delivery workers. Affirm gave transparent credit to people locked out of the financial system. Ressl's core value proposition is eliminating "glue employees" -- the two to four administrative staff at a small business whose jobs become unnecessary. The primary economic beneficiary is the business owner or, more likely, the PE firm that acquired the business and is optimizing margins across a portfolio. That's a legitimate business, but it's not the kind of positive-sum economic access creation that makes me feel that jolt of intuitive energy. The tradespeople -- the plumbers, electricians, HVAC techs -- aren't being empowered here. Their back-office colleagues are being replaced.

The co-founder dynamic also gives me pause, though it's less a red flag than an absence of signal. Arushi and Abhishek met at Entrepreneurs First, which is literally designed to pair strangers into founding teams. Some great companies come out of EF, but I've learned the hard way -- HQ Trivia being the most painful lesson -- that co-founder relationships need to be stress-tested before I can trust them. A recent pivot, a hot competitive market, and well-funded competitors breathing down your neck is exactly the kind of pressure that reveals whether a founding team holds together or fractures. I have no evidence either way, and at this stage that's understandable, but it means I'm carrying that uncertainty into my assessment.

This is a competent team building a reasonable product in a market that has already attracted consensus capital. Everything about it makes sense on paper, which is precisely the problem. The opportunities I've made my career on -- SpaceX, Crusoe Energy, Contraline -- never made sense on paper. They made sense in the gut, in the founder's story, in the feeling that everyone else was wrong and I could see something they couldn't. I don't have that feeling here. I wish Arushi and Abhishek well, genuinely. But this isn't my bet.
