# Tepali -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that catches my eye here isn't the product or the market -- it's the Lotus-to-Tepali rename. Chrisvin's personal website still references founding "Lotus, an AI startup backed by Y Combinator (W26)" with a different email domain. A GitHub repo called "lotus-mcp" sits on his account. Something shifted during the batch, and that shift tells me a story about how these founders arrived at medspas. They didn't walk in the door burning to solve this problem. They found it -- through analysis, through pattern-matching from Rilla and Dripos, through the kind of smart market mapping that investment bankers and operators do well. That's a fundamentally different origin than the one I look for. When I backed Crusoe Energy, those founders had arrived at gas-flare computing through their own obsessive path that nobody else could see. When I think about Uber, it was years of conversations about taxi medallions before the investment ever materialized. The biographical thread connecting founder to problem is what tells me someone will keep going when everything breaks. I don't see that thread here.

If I could sit down with Chrisvin and Vishnu and ask "tell me your story -- not the business," I suspect I'd hear about smart career moves and sharp pattern recognition. Chrisvin went from PJT Partners to Rilla, saw AI transforming high-ticket field services, and recognized the structural analogy to medspas. That's intelligent. Vishnu built all-in-one operational software for multi-location coffee businesses at Dripos, which maps cleanly to the multi-function platform Tepali is building. These are genuinely relevant credentials. But credentials and conviction aren't the same thing. Neither founder has worked at a medspa, managed patient records under HIPAA pressure, or experienced the frustration of reconciling ad spend to patient lifetime value in a spreadsheet at 11 PM. The problem they're solving is real -- I believe the pain exists. But the question I always ask myself is whether this specific founder is genuinely the right person to tackle this specific problem, and I can't answer yes with the information I have.

The "AI-native operating system for vertical X" frame is also one of the most popular categories in the current YC landscape -- it's practically consensus. I invest best when I'm uncomfortable, when the idea sounds a little insane, when other investors are actively passing. Medspas as a software vertical isn't weird; it's a well-documented $100M+ U.S. software market growing at 14% CAGR with clear incumbents. The AI-native angle is the differentiation play, but Boulevard just raised $80M explicitly to invest in AI, and every incumbent in the space will have LLM-powered features within 18 months. The marketing-to-LTV attribution module is legitimately clever and addresses a gap no competitor fills natively today -- but that's a feature, not a company. Features get copied. The structural bet here is that being AI-native from the ground up creates an architectural moat that bolted-on AI can't match. Maybe. But that's a technology thesis, and at pre-seed with no disclosed customers, I have no way to validate whether the architectural advantage translates to a product experience that medspa owners actually prefer enough to switch from Boulevard or Zenoti.

Let me give the bull case its due, because I want to be honest about what could make this work. If Vishnu's Dripos experience means he can ship a genuinely superior integrated product fast -- faster than incumbents can rebuild their architectures -- and if they target the wave of new medspa locations opening every month (locations with no legacy system, no switching cost barrier), this could be a real land-grab. The marketing intelligence hook is smart go-to-market: show a medspa owner exactly which ad dollars are driving which patients, and you've earned the right to sell them everything else. And sometimes the best companies are built by operators who see a structural opportunity clearly, not by founders with tragic origin stories. I acknowledge that my framework has a blind spot here -- it over-indexes on biographical connection and might miss excellent execution-driven founders. But even granting all of that, I'm looking at a 2-person team entering against $2 billion in combined competitor funding, in a consensus category, with no public traction signal beyond 11 YC Launch upvotes. The risk-reward doesn't trigger that jolt of energy I need to write a check.

What's missing for me is the dimension of economic access and real-world impact. Medspas serve primarily affluent clients seeking aesthetic treatments -- Botox, fillers, laser procedures. Helping those businesses run more efficiently is fine, but it doesn't create the kind of economic opportunity for underserved populations that drew me to Uber (driver income), Postmates (delivery worker income), or Affirm (transparent credit access). If Tepali were building tools that helped aestheticians build independent practices or gave medspa employees equity visibility, I'd feel something different. As it stands, this is a B2B SaaS play optimizing operations for a luxury service category. That's a legitimate business, but it's not the kind of bet that keeps me up at night imagining how the world changes.
