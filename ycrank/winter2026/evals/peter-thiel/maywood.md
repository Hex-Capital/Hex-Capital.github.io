# Maywood -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The first question I always ask is: what is the secret? What does this company know about the world that most intelligent people would actively dispute? And when I look at Maywood, I find something that is the opposite of a secret -- I find the single most obvious application of large language models in finance. Ask any managing director at any investment bank whether AI could automate CIM generation and pitch deck creation, and they will not disagree with you. They will nod vigorously. McKinsey has already quantified the value at $200-340 billion annually for banking. Every consulting firm has published the whitepaper. Every banker who has opened ChatGPT has typed in "draft a teaser for this deal." When the entire industry already agrees with your thesis, you do not have a secret. You have a consensus observation dressed in startup clothing.

The mimetic dynamics here are deeply unfavorable. "AI for [professional vertical]" is the most crowded category in venture capital today -- AI for legal, AI for accounting, AI for consulting, AI for real estate. They all share the identical structural thesis: this industry still creates documents manually, and LLMs can automate them. Maywood is not escaping competition; it is walking directly into the most Girardian landscape in technology. The fact that the current named competitors -- Datasite, Finalis, Midaxo -- occupy adjacent but different layers is irrelevant. The competitive threat does not come from incumbents. It comes from the inevitable wave of AI startups that will target this exact layer, and from the incumbents themselves: Datasite has already acquired BlueFlame AI and Grata, signaling that bolting generative capabilities onto an existing installed base is precisely their strategy. When the acquirer has $2 billion in funding and owns the customer relationship, building the feature they want to buy is not a monopoly path.

The founding team is competent in a way that does not excite me. Drake Goodman left Blackstone private equity -- genuine domain experience, and walking away from that compensation signals real commitment. Kent Goodman brings MIT EECS credentials and BCG consulting experience. Esteban Vizcaino has MIT EECS and hedge fund AI work at Balyasny. These are impressive resumes. They are also precisely the profile I would expect from a company that identified the most obvious opportunity in its field. When I backed Zuckerberg, he was a nineteen-year-old who could barely make eye contact, building something most people thought was a toy. When I co-founded Palantir, the intelligence community actively rejected the idea that commercial software could replace their analysts. Maywood's founders come from the establishments they are selling to, and they are building what those establishments already want. The domain expertise is real, but domain expertise in a consensus market produces a good business, not a monopoly. I note the "men in suits" pattern that troubled me in cleantech -- finance and consulting pedigrees rather than the technically obsessive, socially unconventional founders who tend to build genuinely discontinuous companies.

The strongest bull case deserves serious consideration. If you believe that execution in enterprise AI is everything and that the first company to achieve deep integration with banking workflows will create durable switching costs -- firm-specific style learning, compliance infrastructure, institutional muscle memory -- then Maywood has a plausible path to a valuable company. The SOC 2 Type II certification, single-tenant deployment, and self-hosted options represent real investment that most AI startups have not made, and these matter enormously to regulated buyers. The customer testimonials -- "doubled capacity," "on pace to double deal count with the same headcount" -- suggest the product genuinely works. If Maywood could monopolize the middle-market investment banking segment before anyone else achieves equivalent compliance and integration depth, there is a version of this story where switching costs compound into something durable. The Stripe parallel is tempting: Stripe's thesis was not contrarian either (developers should be able to accept payments easily was obvious), but execution and integration depth created a $95 billion company. The critical difference is that Stripe sat in the payment flow of millions of businesses, creating two-sided network effects that compounded with scale. Maywood sits in the document workflow of individual bank teams, where switching costs are real but linear, not exponential. There are no network effects. Each bank's usage does not make the product more valuable for any other bank.

The technology itself is not discontinuous. Maywood applies existing LLM capabilities -- which are available to every competitor -- to a domain-specific vertical. The proprietary element is the fine-tuning on firm-specific data and the compliance wrapper, not a fundamental technical breakthrough. Neural stylistic transfer is clever but not an order of magnitude better than what a well-resourced competitor could build in twelve months. This is a 2-3x improvement in banker productivity -- valuable, certainly -- but not the kind of technological discontinuity that makes the prior approach structurally obsolete. When bankers can use their own LLM tools or when Datasite bolts on generative features, Maywood's advantage narrows to its integration depth and compliance posture, which are defensible but not monopolistic.

This is a company that will likely build a real business serving middle-market investment banks. But real businesses are not what I invest in. I invest in companies where the upside, if the thesis is correct, is so extreme that a single position can return the entire fund. Maywood's ceiling is constrained by its addressable market (M&A deal teams at banks), the absence of network effects, and the inevitability of competition from both well-funded incumbents and the next wave of AI startups. The power law demands that I back companies built around secrets so profound that success creates a monopoly. What I see here is a well-executed application of consensus technology to an obvious use case by a credentialed team -- and that is precisely the kind of investment I pass on.
