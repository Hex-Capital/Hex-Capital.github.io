# Proximitty -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's my structural problem with Proximitty in one sentence: the company's core technical differentiation -- computer vision-based browser automation that bypasses API integrations -- is being actively shipped as a feature by Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google simultaneously. When I see a startup whose moat depends on a capability that three of the most well-capitalized companies on Earth are racing to commoditize, I don't think "defensible" -- I think "feature waiting to get absorbed." This is the inverse of what I look for. My entire framework is built around finding businesses that get *stronger* as software gets cheaper. Proximitty gets weaker. Every improvement in foundation model browser automation makes it easier for a lending institution to build this in-house or for an incumbent like Fiserv to bolt it onto their existing platform. The no-API deployment model is clever GTM, not a moat.

On the sane-person-insane-idea filter, I see two credible operators chasing a deeply consensus opportunity. Ho's McKinsey credit risk advisory background plus scaling Taptap Send from $75M to $200M ARR is genuinely impressive operational credibility. Zhang's Bloomberg infrastructure engineering at 300K+ terminal scale combined with ACI.dev's 4.7K GitHub stars in AI agent tooling is specifically relevant technical experience. These are "sane" people. But the idea -- AI-powered loan servicing and collections -- isn't remotely insane. Prodigal Technologies has been doing this since 2018 with $14.2M raised. Skit.ai has $22.4M in 2024 revenue doing voice AI for collections. TrueAccord has raised $78M. This isn't a non-consensus bet that provokes skepticism from most investors. It's a category with a half-dozen funded players and obvious market demand. When I find a sane person with a sane idea, I know the returns are already priced in by the competitive dynamics.

The deepest issue is that this company fails my "cherry on top" test completely. Strip away the AI and what's left? Nothing. There's no physical-world operation, no installed hardware base, no protocol-layer infrastructure that exists independently. The AI *is* the business. That's precisely the configuration where incumbents with distribution -- your Black Knights, your Fiserv platforms, your existing LMS vendors -- will eat your lunch. They have the customer relationships, the compliance certifications, the integration depth. When browser automation becomes a commodity API call (and it will), they'll offer it as a feature toggle. The compliance monitoring layer is the closest thing to a genuine moat here, but regulatory compliance is table stakes for any vendor selling to banks -- it's a cost of entry, not a source of compounding advantage.

Let me steelman the bull case, because the founder-market fit is real. If Ho's McKinsey network delivers rapid enterprise adoption and the "no-code, no-API" wedge gets Proximitty embedded in workflows before incumbents react, the switching costs from converted SOPs could create genuine stickiness. The claim of serving "publicly traded fintechs and banks globally" -- if substantiated -- would be remarkable pre-seed traction in a notoriously slow-moving market. And the borrower-level data flywheel on collection strategies could theoretically compound into something proprietary. The bull case requires believing that execution speed in a specific vertical can outrun commoditization of the underlying technology -- that Proximitty becomes so embedded in institutional workflows that even when browser automation is freely available, ripping out Proximitty is harder than keeping it. I've seen that movie work before, but it requires a business that would still be valuable pre-AI, and this one wouldn't be.

This is the kind of company I'd use as an example on CNBC to illustrate why most AI startups are bad investments. Credible founders, real market, legitimate technology -- and structurally positioned to get squeezed from every direction. The foundation model providers commoditize the browser automation layer from below. The existing LMS vendors add AI features from above. And better-funded direct competitors like Prodigal and Skit.ai compete head-on with years of training data and customer relationships. I'd rather fund a collections agency that uses AI to optimize its human agents than an AI company trying to replace them -- at least the agency has operational complexity that software can't replicate.
