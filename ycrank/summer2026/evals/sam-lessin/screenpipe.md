# screenpipe -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Louis Beaumont is a real technical founder building in a category I actively de-rate — that's screenpipe's signature. He's on his second run at screen-capture-to-automation after Mediar (SAP data-entry, 2024), he's got 19k GitHub stars and 250k users on a source-available Rust codebase, and he's competing head-on with Microsoft Recall (bundled at $0 marginal cost on Copilot+ PCs) and Meta-owned Rewind. That is a missionary operator in a sleeve I've publicly called "just more internet" (Joe Lonsdale podcast #113, 2024) — not the profile that flips me to yes, but not the sanitized YC AI-agent deck I pass on before the meeting either.

Let me walk the founder-conviction ladder against Drop.io as the HIGH rung — because when I look at Beaumont, the mechanism that loads the analogy is *serial obsession with a single wedge before the market noticed*. I spent 2007-2010 dragging a file-sharing primitive through a wall of free incumbents until Facebook bought it; Beaumont did Mediar on SAP screen automation in 2024, wrote a five-figure video-game-bots business at fourteen per his own LinkedIn posts, and is now on his second commercial swing at "watch the screen, automate the workflow" as screenpipe. He publishes on GitHub (@louis030195, screenpipe/screenpipe at 19k stars), he ships in Rust which is not the fashionable AI-agent stack, and the pricing page is already live at $25/$50/$150 rather than a "book a demo" pre-revenue vaporshell. Where screenpipe diverges from the Drop.io rung: I had a co-founder in Darshan and eventually an acquirer's roadmap; Beaumont is solo on the YC page with a team of six spanning macOS/Windows/Linux plus 48+ integrations plus an on-device PII-scrubbing model, which is a single-point-of-failure exposure I've screened against ever since the Fin era taught me what non-linear founder time actually costs.

The canonical 2026 YC AI-agent seed at $30M post — that is the LOW rung on category sleeve, and the failure mechanism is that "AI is just more internet" (Joe Lonsdale podcast #113, 2024) means the round is priced like disruption when the underlying is commodity exposure. Does that pattern appear in screenpipe? Partially. Screenpipe *is* an AI productivity SaaS in an S26 batch and the pitch cleanly could have been written by any of the other agent-flavored teams in the same cohort — that's the anti-signal. What partially rescues it is that the tier-1 cloud incumbents have visibly *quit* the always-on-capture category: Rewind's desktop app was disabled Dec 19, 2025 after Meta acquired it (GeekWire, 2026), Recall took repeated security disclosures one year post-launch (GeekWire, 2026), and no one has re-underwritten a local-first alternative at seed. So screenpipe is *not* the pure consensus AI-agent pattern — it sits in an abandoned corner of the AI sleeve, which is closer to a "what if they're right?" inversion (Antoine Buteau essay, ~2023) than to a batch-median AI vertical shell.

Round dynamics — mechanism analysis over 18 months. Screenpipe has no disclosed round price in the dossier, and my absent-evidence default is that silence means consensus pricing. But the compounding question is whether the abandoned-category framing survives 18 months of Meta and Microsoft distribution. If Meta re-launches Rewind bundled into a Ray-Ban glasses memory layer and Microsoft ships Recall cross-OS by mid-2027, screenpipe's local-first-Rust-source-available wedge collapses into a niche compliance play — and the seed price won't matter because the regatta never opens. The tack-count read on screenpipe is bad: it needs 4-5 more financing tacks to reach default-alive against $0-marginal-cost incumbents, and each tack is underwritten against Recall shipping-or-not, which is not an edge I can price.

Capital structure — counterfactual. For screenpipe's capital structure to flip a tier, the enterprise SKU at $150/seat/month with SSO/SAML would need one publicly disclosed Fortune 500 contract by the next tack — Datadog, Atlassian, Salesforce are all cited as user-logos on the homepage but there's no named enterprise LOI. Give me one signed 500-seat deal at $150/seat/month and the "regatta model" (Sam Lessin tweet, December 5 2024) reads entirely differently — screenpipe becomes a compliance-driven enterprise wedge with a self-funding motion, not a consumer-productivity OSS project force-fit into a venture cap table. Absent that LOI, the pricing page is a hopeful list.

Founder-brand — single-anchor parallel to John Fish / Bookshelved (January 2024). Fish had a named-creator distribution channel that pre-existed the company by two-plus years and could pull free acquisition into a niche literary hub; Beaumont has a *project*-brand (screenpipe/screenpipe at 19k stars, 250k reported users) but a modest *personal* brand (479 GitHub followers, no substantive Twitter or newsletter footprint). That's a real distinction under the Slow Creator Fund frame — the founder-as-brand thesis rewards owned distribution attached to a person, not a repo. Screenpipe has repo-brand, which is closer to a developer-tools flywheel than a creator-fund asset, and repo-brand cannibalizes cleanly (atzamis/screen-pipe, EvolvingSoftware/screen-pipe forks on GitHub already exist, and the license update from open-source to source-available has probably capped the star curve).

Thirty-six-month forecast: screenpipe gets acqui-hired by an enterprise workflow or observability incumbent — Datadog, Atlassian, or Notion are the plausible acquirers — at $50-150M on the strength of the Rust local-first codebase and the enterprise SKU, having reached roughly $3-8M ARR but not $30M standalone; the alternative outcome is death-by-Recall-bundling once Microsoft ships cross-OS. The mechanism is straightforward: Beaumont is exactly the kind of technical operator who ships a real local-first substrate that a bigger enterprise buyer wants to slot into a compliance-privacy narrative, but the standalone paid-seat model gets structurally squeezed between Microsoft's zero-marginal-cost bundle and Meta's re-emergent Rewind.

The strongest bull case against my pass leans on the abandonment pattern — local-first privacy is genuinely a wedge Meta and Microsoft cannot structurally serve (Meta because of trust, Microsoft because Recall is welded to Copilot+ hardware and Windows), and a source-available Rust codebase with 250k users could compound into the enterprise privacy-first standard the way Sentry did in error tracking or GitLab did in DevOps. If that reads, screenpipe absorbs the Recall/Rewind threat instead of dying to it — the Robinhood override applies (Known Exception): early entry at the abandoned moment lets subsequent consensus mark up the position. The dossier evidence that dismantles this: the license already moved from open-source to source-available (Screenpipe license-update blog), which is the *reverse* of the Sentry/GitLab community-goodwill compounding curve — you get one such license change before the fork ecosystem eats you, and the forks already exist. The verifiable condition that flips me to yes: a signed 500+ seat enterprise contract at the $150/seat/month tier with a named Fortune 500 counterparty before the next tack, disclosed by name — that would tell me screenpipe is a Sublime-Security-shaped enterprise entry priced pre-consolidation rather than an OSS developer tool force-fit into a SaaS cap table.
