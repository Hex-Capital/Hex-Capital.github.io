# Userlens -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Userlens claims Linear, Deel, Calendly, Framer, and McDonald's as customers on its website -- and that list, for a four-person pre-seed company out of Helsinki, is either the most impressive early traction signal I've seen this batch or the most misleading. The dossier notes that only Quartr and Smartly have published case studies; the rest are logos without evidence of engagement depth. I've learned to be skeptical of logo walls because free trials and paid contracts produce the same pixel on a homepage. If Calendly is paying $500/month for Userlens, that's a real signal. If they signed up for a free trial three months ago and never logged back in, it's decoration. The Quartr numbers -- scaling from 40 to 200 accounts per CSM, 18% NRR increase in three months -- are genuinely striking if accurate. But I can't distinguish Userlens's verified traction from Wudpecker's legacy. The $660K revenue figure from Getlatka may entirely represent the prior product, and the Userlens blog still lives on wudpecker.io. When a company hasn't moved its blog to its own domain, the pivot isn't finished.

Ankur Dahama and Hai Ta have one quality I always weight heavily: they shipped something before. Wudpecker reached 25,000 users and over 1,000 customers on a €330K pre-seed raise. That's not a side project; that's a real product with real distribution. The fact that a third Wudpecker co-founder, Joona Jokivuori, isn't listed on the Userlens YC page is something I'd want to understand -- co-founder departures during pivots can be clean or they can be fractures. But the Ankur-Hai relationship has survived multiple years and a product pivot, which is a positive durability signal. What I don't see are stories. When I evaluate founders, I look for the specific anecdotes that reveal how they behave when they're blocked -- the Airbnb cereal boxes, the time they personally called every churning customer, the weekend they rebuilt the data pipeline because the integration broke. Userlens's dossier gives me credentials and metrics but no narrative texture. Ankur's GitHub shows Unity games and ML algorithm implementations -- a tinkerer, not someone whose entire identity is consumed by the work. I can't find the "animal" quality from this distance.

Userlens's origin story is the strongest thing about it from my framework. The founders didn't read a Grand View Research report about the $2.9 billion customer success market and decide to build a product. They ran Wudpecker, watched customers vanish without warning, and had no tool that told them which accounts were dying. That's the Dropbox pattern -- Drew Houston built Dropbox because he kept forgetting his USB drive, not because he analyzed the file synchronization TAM. Ankur and Hai lived in a future where SaaS founders have hundreds of accounts and no visibility into account health, then built what was missing. That organic discovery is real, and it's worth a lot in my calculus. The pivot itself also shows the right kind of stubbornness -- persistent about building a valuable company, flexible about what product achieves that. They didn't sink-cost themselves into the AI meeting assistant space when they found a bigger problem.

"AI agents that predict churn months before it happens" is exactly the kind of idea that sounds great to everyone in a pitch meeting. And that's my core problem with Userlens. Gainsight has ~$200M in revenue and just announced AI features. ChurnZero does $180.8M. Vitally raised $40.2M. Amplitude, PostHog, and Mixpanel -- Userlens's own data sources -- could add account-level health scoring as a native feature tomorrow. When I apply the Black Swan test, Userlens fails it cleanly: nobody hears "AI churn prediction for SaaS" and says "that will never work." They say "that's smart." In my experience, "that's smart" is one of the most dangerous reactions a startup can get, because it means the returns have already been competed away by everyone else who also thought it was smart. The positioning as an "AI CSM" that generates QBR decks rather than dashboards is a genuine product insight, but it's a feature insight, not a structural one. Gainsight can ship that in a quarter.

The bull case for Userlens is real and I want to state it honestly: these founders found a problem organically, pivoted quickly, and appear to have landed impressive early customers. The Quartr case study showing 152% increase in upsell pipeline and $30K average value added per FTE is the kind of concrete ROI that makes enterprise sales straightforward. The "why now" is sound -- LLMs crossed a capability threshold in 2023-2024 that makes natural language querying of product data feasible, and the SaaS market's shift from growth to retention creates demand pull. If the customer logos are real paid relationships and the $660K revenue belongs to Userlens specifically, this is a company with product-market fit at pre-seed. That would be exceptional. But I don't have verification, and the commoditization risk from incumbents is structural, not speculative -- Gainsight is already shipping AI features into its platform. The one condition that would flip me to Invest: confirmed, independently verifiable evidence that three or more of those named logos (Linear, Deel, Calendly) are paying customers on annual contracts, with Userlens-specific revenue exceeding $300K ARR. That would demonstrate that the product insight is durable enough to resist incumbent absorption.

Userlens scores well on organic problem discovery -- among the best signals I evaluate -- but falls short on the dimension I weight most: the idea sits in consensus territory rather than the contrarian sweet spot where returns compound. The founders are competent builders who shipped Wudpecker and pivoted intelligently, but I don't see evidence of the extraordinary resourcefulness or obsessive intensity that separates the founders I back from the founders I wish well. This is a real product solving a real problem, built by capable people. It's just not my kind of bet.
