# Saudara AI -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the thing that immediately jumps out: this company's entire thesis is a tariff spread. China at 54%, Indonesia at 32%, brands scrambling to redirect supply chains. That's not a company — that's a trade. And I've said this a hundred times: if your business model requires a favorable regulatory outcome to be profitable, you're holding a lottery ticket, not building an enterprise. The scooter companies needed cities to cooperate. Saudara AI needs the US government to maintain a specific tariff differential between two countries indefinitely. What happens when Indonesia's tariff goes to 50%? What happens when Vietnam's goes to 15%? The entire geographic bet reshuffles overnight, and you're left with an AI wrapper pointed at the wrong country. That's not a risk you can engineer away — it's structural fragility disguised as a macro tailwind.

Now let's talk about mispricing, because that's where I always start. Is the market wrong about AI-powered sourcing? No. The market is extremely right about it. Alibaba's Accio already has two million users doing exactly this across every geography. SourceReady covers 1.2 million suppliers in a hundred countries. Every trade publication on earth is running "China+1" stories. This is consensus dressed up as insight. When I backed Eaze, nobody wanted to touch cannabis — the discomfort *was* the opportunity. When I seeded Solana at four cents, every serious crypto person had written off non-Ethereum L1s. Where's the discomfort here? Where's the narrative the market has wrong? "Brands need help sourcing from Indonesia" is on the front page of CNBC. There's no asymmetry in this bet.

The primitive test is even more straightforward. This is a sourcing agency that uses AI. It says so right in the tagline: "AI-native Sourcing Agency." Sourcing agents have existed for decades. Alibaba has existed for two decades. Adding an LLM to supplier matching is a feature, not a primitive. Nobody is going to say "I Saudara'd my supply chain" the way people say "Venmo me." There's no new verb here, no new behavior, no new financial instrument. It's a marginally better execution of something that already exists, constrained to a single geography. When I invested in Patreon, "paying creators directly" was a behavior that didn't exist as a category. When I backed Venmo, peer-to-peer mobile payments were genuinely new. This isn't that. This is "better sourcing agent for one country."

The bull case, and I want to be honest about it: Jennifer Prasetyo's family manufacturing network spanning fifty-plus years is a real physical-world asset. That's the Metropolis pattern — software amplifies the business, but the on-the-ground relationships can't be replicated by an app. If you squint, there's a version of this that looks like Teamshares for Indonesian manufacturing — a slow, patient roll-up of supplier relationships that compounds into something no horizontal platform can touch. An Amazon PM and a Microsoft/Meta engineer with genuine Indonesian manufacturing roots is decent founder-market fit. The combination of US tech experience with multi-generational Indonesian factory access is legitimately hard to assemble. If the tariff environment stabilizes and this team methodically builds a supplier network that becomes the definitive Indonesia sourcing infrastructure, you could see something interesting emerge over a decade. But "could" is doing a lot of work in that sentence.

The AI kill test is where this really falls apart for me. Every single thing Saudara AI does on the technology side — supplier matching, communication automation, status tracking — Alibaba can add to Accio in a quarter. The defensible piece is the family relationships, but those are personally dependent on one co-founder and don't scale without a systematic onboarding process that doesn't exist yet. And I notice there's no visible evidence these founders have a deep pre-existing relationship — "no shared prior employer or university overlap" is in the dossier. My best returns come from founders I've watched across multiple cycles together. Cold co-founder pairings, especially ones assembled around a geographic thesis, don't give me the same confidence. The idea is sensible. The founders seem competent. But sane people with a sane idea is just... boring. I need the insanity. I need someone telling me something that makes me go "what the hell" and then slowly realizing they might be right. "Help brands source from Indonesia with AI" doesn't produce that reaction in anyone.

Pass. This is a services business with an AI veneer in a consensus-hot category, dependent on a tariff differential set by politicians, competing against Alibaba's existing infrastructure. The physical-world element is real but personally dependent and unscalable in its current form. There's no mispricing, no new primitive, and no insanity. It's a reasonable bet for someone who invests in reasonable things. That's not me.
