# Dayjob -- Elad Gil Evaluation

$450K ARR seven months after first sales in a vertical where customers historically schedule routes with spreadsheets -- that number stopped me. For a pre-seed company selling AI scheduling to skip hire operators in the UK, that's exceptional velocity. Dayjob is hitting the pattern I've seen work repeatedly: boring operational category, clear payer with budget authority, minimal competition from prestige-seeking founders. Gusto built a multi-billion dollar business in payroll -- a market nobody wanted to touch -- because universal demand and deep workflow integration created compounding defensibility. Dayjob has analogous structural ingredients: waste operators need scheduling every day, the ERP integrations (Weighsoft, PurGo, Wastelogics) embed Dayjob into daily workflows, and the VWS co-marketing partnership announced January 2026 gives them distribution through an installed base they didn't have to build. The "boring but critical" infrastructure pattern is one of my highest-conviction categories precisely because the absence of competition IS the competitive advantage.

George Postlethwaite's five years at Deliveroo launching the grocery vertical gives him direct operational experience in fleet scheduling and last-mile logistics at a company that scaled through exactly the kind of route optimization complexity Dayjob now automates for waste. Fred Fooks' supply chain analytics work at Deloitte and their shared 18 months building Gaea -- the waste/recycling ERP that generated $150K in revenue before they pivoted -- mean Dayjob's founders spent over a year inside the waste industry's operational reality before identifying scheduling as the highest-leverage intervention point. That Deliveroo-to-waste-industry trajectory mirrors the operator-founder pattern I trust: someone who experienced logistics scaling firsthand at a hypergrowth company, then went deep into a specific vertical. Clear CEO/CTO split with George and Fred, no ambiguous authority structure.

The single-miracle test is where Dayjob scores strongest. Route optimization is computationally tractable -- classical algorithms have done this for decades, and modern AI improves the real-time rescheduling experience incrementally rather than representing a step-function breakthrough. Dayjob doesn't need a technology miracle. The one miracle is product-market fit execution: can they build a domain-specific scheduling tool good enough that skip hire operators switch from spreadsheets? The $450K ARR and customer testimonials -- Marc Williamson at Coastal Recycling citing "5-10% increase in jobs per driver hour," Stuart Pearce calling it "best route-planning software I've used in 10 years" -- suggest this miracle is already happening. No regulatory barrier to clear, no distribution channel to invent (ERP partnerships provide it), no simultaneous breakthrough required. One miracle, already underway.

Where my framework surfaces real concern is market structure timing. When I backed Harvey, there was a specific GPT capability threshold being crossed that unlocked legal document analysis after a decade of failed attempts -- the market had been stuck, and a measurable exogenous shift cracked it open. With Dayjob, I can't identify an equivalent structural catalyst. Route optimization software has existed for years (Routific, OptimoRoute, AMCS). Waste management operators have always needed better scheduling. AI agents make the product somewhat better -- real-time rescheduling with natural language exception handling -- but this is an incremental improvement on existing capability, not a market that was impossible two years ago and suddenly became feasible. The "why now" in the dossier is inferred, not demonstrated: AI capability thresholds plus margin pressure from fuel costs and labor shortages. Neither is the kind of structural shift that creates a nonobvious window. Every vertical SaaS investor knows waste management needs better software. This market isn't nonobvious -- it's just small.

The ambition ceiling is the dimension that most concerns me for venture returns. Dayjob's initial addressable market is skip hire operators using UK waste ERPs -- a subsegment measured in tens of millions, not billions. The broader route optimization market ($8.51B per Grand View Research) and waste management software market ($11.13B per Verified Market Reports) are large, but Dayjob's actual slice is a fraction of these figures. Expanding from skip hire scheduling to general waste collection to broader industrial logistics requires new domain tuning, new ERP integrations, and new geographic sales infrastructure for each step. AMCS, with $225M raised, already offers route optimization within its full-stack waste ERP -- and could add AI-driven real-time scheduling as a feature upgrade to its installed base. Dayjob's differentiation (ERP-agnostic overlay) matters for operators NOT on AMCS, but narrows as the competitive response materializes. The infrastructure-layer positioning is more application-overlay than platform: Dayjob sits on top of ERPs rather than becoming a layer others build on, and there's no API-first architecture or network effects that would create Stripe-like compounding.

The strongest bull case: Dayjob is the Samsara of waste management -- start narrow with fleet scheduling, expand into a broader industrial operations platform, ride the "boring infrastructure" pattern to a multi-billion outcome. The $450K ARR trajectory, ERP partnership distribution channel, and genuine founder-market fit (Deliveroo logistics experience plus 18 months inside waste operations) support this narrative. The VWS partnership is exactly the kind of channel leverage that could accelerate growth without proportional sales spend. If Dayjob demonstrates expansion beyond skip hire into general waste collection within the next 12 months while maintaining the current ARR growth rate, I'd revisit -- that would signal the TAM ceiling is breakable and the product has platform potential beyond its initial niche. But today, the structural market timing signal is too weak and the infrastructure positioning too application-layer for me to write a check with high conviction.

Dayjob scores well on execution metrics that matter -- single-miracle viability is high, founder-market fit is genuine, and the traction is impressive for stage. But my framework weights market structure timing and infrastructure positioning as the two highest criteria (55 of 100 points combined), and Dayjob is average on both. The market isn't structurally shifting in a way that creates a nonobvious window, and the product is an application overlay, not an infrastructure platform. Strong operators in a boring category with real traction -- but not the kind of structural market opening that drives my highest-conviction investments.
