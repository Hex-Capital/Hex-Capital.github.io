# 9 Mothers -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that caught me here wasn't the product — it was a name on the team page. Roman Khomenko, CTO, holds a master's from Kharkiv National University of Radioelectronics and served in the Police of Ukraine. Kharkiv. If you've paid any attention to the last four years, you know what that city has endured — relentless bombardment, FPV drones turning neighborhoods into kill zones. When someone from Kharkiv pivots from building QA automation to building counter-drone weapon systems, I don't need them to tell me their story explicitly. The problem found him. That's the kind of biographical signal I weight most heavily — not "I saw the TAM slide" but "I watched this happen to people I know." And the fact that he and Russell Smith worked side by side at Rainforest QA for eight years means the co-founder relationship has been stress-tested, which matters enormously to me after what I watched happen at HQ Trivia. But — and this is the tension — Smith is the CEO, and his visible path runs through Ruby, DevOps, CI/CD pipelines, and a no-code testing platform. That's a long way from autonomous weapon systems. I don't see the forge for the person at the helm. A great CTO biographical signal paired with a CEO whose connection to the problem isn't legible creates an asymmetry that concerns me.

Here's where I struggle with the contrarian test. When I backed Anduril, defense tech was politically radioactive in Silicon Valley — founders were being uninvited from dinner parties for building products for the military. That was genuinely uncomfortable territory, the kind of space where VCs shift in their chairs. Counter-drone in 2026 is not that. The Army just handed Anduril a $20B framework contract for C-UAS. The DoD is pouring $49.9 billion into defense tech procurement. DroneShield is a public company printing revenue at 276% year-over-year growth. This is not a space that makes sophisticated investors uncomfortable anymore — it's a space they're racing to get into. The "autonomous weapon system" framing adds some edge, because the human-in-the-loop policy debate is real and advocacy groups are pushing hard against fully autonomous lethality. But the broader counter-drone thesis is about as consensus as defense tech gets right now. I need the weird, and this doesn't feel weird enough.

The bull case is real, and I want to be honest about it. If you squint at this the right way, you see a team that's been building together for nearly a decade, led by a second-time YC founder, with a CTO whose biographical connection to the drone threat may be the deepest of anyone in the startup C-UAS space. The acoustic ML angle — passive detection that doesn't broadcast your position the way radar does — is genuinely clever and technically differentiated. The niche they're targeting, cheap portable autonomous C-UAS at the tactical edge, is exactly where the defense primes have a business model conflict: Raytheon and Northrop won't cannibalize their big-ticket platform sales to ship a man-portable unit at a fraction of the price. And the timing inflection from edge AI inference making this technically feasible at low power and small form factor is real. If 9 Mothers can get a system into soldiers' hands and start collecting proprietary acoustic drone signatures at scale, that data flywheel could become a genuine moat. The path to something important is there.

But I keep coming back to the competitive reality. Anduril has Lattice, billions in contracts, and a full-stack defense platform. Epirus has directed-energy systems with $550M+ in funding. DroneShield has deployed in 70+ countries and is generating hundreds of millions in revenue. A pre-seed startup with no disclosed contracts, no pilot programs, and no public evidence of a working prototype is entering a space where the incumbents aren't sleepy defense primes anymore — they're aggressive, well-capitalized startups that have already solved the procurement puzzle. The defense procurement cycle itself is a 12-to-36-month structural challenge that can kill a startup before its product ever gets tested. And while acoustic ML is interesting, it's a technique, not a fortress — any well-funded competitor could hire DSP engineers and build a competing capability in a year. I don't see the infrastructure-grade moat that compounds with each deployment, not yet.

What I don't find here is my deepest pattern — the invisible system made visible. The defense procurement complex is opaque, sure, but 9 Mothers isn't attacking that opacity the way Affirm attacked credit card terms or Ownwell attacked property tax assessments. They're building a better product for an underserved use case within an existing procurement system. That's valuable, but it's a different kind of bet than the ones that have defined my portfolio. The cascading impact of protecting soldiers and eventually civilians from drone threats is genuine and meaningful — lives saved is about as real as impact gets. But the structural expansion of access that I look for, the creation of new economic participants, the power shift from gatekeepers to ordinary people — that's harder to trace here.

This is a competent team in a real market at a genuine inflection point, and Khomenko's biographical connection to the problem resonates with me on a human level. But the space has shifted from contrarian to consensus, the competitive landscape is brutal, and the CEO's path to this domain doesn't read as inevitable. I'd want to sit across from Russell Smith and hear his story — not the business, the story. If there's a biographical forge I'm not seeing in the public record, that changes things. As it stands, this feels like a solid bet for an investor who weights timing and market size over the signals I weight. For me, it's a pass — but one I'd revisit if the founder stories turn out to run deeper than what's visible here.
