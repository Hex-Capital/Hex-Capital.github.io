# Panacea -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Panacea is a fixed-price FDA regulatory consultancy with an AI layer -- Haris Javed-Akhtar and Yaman Ziadeh pairing "ex-FDA consultants" with a proprietary platform across 10 submission pathways. Read through the leverage taxonomy, Panacea's highest dimension is founder fit (MBBS plus ex-Anterior AI platform) and its lowest is the architecture itself: this is labor leverage with a software wrapper, sold one engagement at a time, the structural inverse of the **Stack Overflow** (2008) pattern I keep funding.

Run the leverage check first, which is what I always do. **Stack Overflow** (2008) was permissionless code-leverage at its purest -- Atwood and Spolsky shipped a product where every developer answer compounded into the asset and the marginal cost of the millionth user was zero. Panacea's primary distribution is "Book my free consultation" at cal.com/haris-panacea/call, and the revenue mechanism is consultant-time priced by milestone. The "12x faster" claim on withpanacea.com is an internal productivity multiplier on billable work, not a code artifact that distributes itself; the AI compounds the *consultants'* output, but Panacea still has to hire, vet, and retain ex-FDA reviewers to deliver each engagement. That is the exact "labor and capital require permission" architecture I wrote nav.al/product-media against -- the gatekeeper here is the bench of regulatory humans Panacea has not publicly named.

The Pinterest pass haunts me because the leverage architecture was right and I let aesthetics override the math. Pinterest's failure mode for me was *me* -- not the company. Panacea's lowest-rung analog on the network dimension is the inverse: a single-sided B2B service where the Nth biotech client makes the (N-1) prior clients no more valuable. **Uber** (2010) compounded riders and drivers into a flywheel that produced 4,000x; **Twitter** (2007) compounded posters and readers into a 400x media protocol. Panacea processes a 510(k) for client A, then processes an independent 510(k) for client B; the only compounding asset is the internal submissions corpus, and the dossier itself flags this is "unproven now because the company is two founders at YC pre-seed stage with no disclosed submission count." That is not the upside geometry that underwrites a 1x-downside / 10,000x-upside check.

Specific-knowledge filter -- mechanism analysis over 18 months. Haris is MBBS Imperial College and Yaman shipped AI platform work at Anterior, which is a regulated-healthcare AI company; the combination is real founder-market fit and clears my "ex-FAANG PM identified market opportunity" red flag. But the load-bearing claim on withpanacea.com is "ex-FDA, decades of experience, hundreds of filings" -- and *neither founder is that person.* The specific knowledge Panacea sells lives in unnamed contractors, which means in 18 months the moat is "did Panacea retain its ex-FDA bench against Weave Bio's $30M war chest and Parexel partnership." That is a hiring story, not a specific-knowledge story.

Market and pivot-room -- counterfactual. The dossier names a $256.2B medical device manufacturing market, but Panacea's actual SAM is FDA regulatory consulting, which is "no public TAM/SAM data found" and is structurally a single vertical with hourly-billed incumbents (RQM+, ProPharma, MDI). For Panacea's pivot-room score to flip a tier, the dossier would need to show an adjacent expansion path -- post-market surveillance, clinical trial submissions, EU MDR, an open data product for biotech founders -- and instead it shows 10 FDA pathway acronyms inside one regulatory regime. The "first idea never works" axiom means Panacea has to pivot inside FDA consulting, and FDA consulting does not have the shape that absorbs two pivots without refinancing.

Buffett trio -- single-anchor parallel. Both founders worked together at Anterior, which is exactly the **Notion** (2013) co-founding pattern I like -- prior shared context resolves the "Picking a co-founder is your most important decision" risk before Day 1. No partner litigation, no self-promotion about ethics, no contested exits. Clean read. The 10-year-partner question I cannot answer from the dossier because there is no public writing, no X presence for either founder, no GitHub repos -- and I optimize for founders who compound through media. Neutral here, neither a flag nor a pull.

36-month forecast: Panacea is a $2M-$5M ARR boutique regulatory-consulting shop by 2029, either acquired by Parexel/ICON-class CRO for sub-$50M or grinding profitably as a lifestyle business, having watched Weave Bio capture the SaaS-platform narrative the AI-native FDA category will be remembered for. The mechanism is exactly the high/low signature -- decent founder-market fit selling labor with a software wrapper, in a vertical that does not compound, against a competitor with a $20M Series A from USVP and a named Parexel partnership announced October 2025.

The strongest bull case: **Anchorage Digital** is my own Known Exception that proves "permission-as-moat" can flip the leverage rule when regulatory complexity itself becomes the structural barrier -- and FDA regulatory expertise is exactly that kind of permission-shaped moat. If Panacea quietly accumulates a submissions corpus that no SaaS competitor can replicate without doing the consulting work first, the AI tooling eventually eats the consultants and Panacea emerges as the data-advantaged platform Weave Bio is racing to become. The reason it does not win me: Anchorage had ex-Treasury talent on the cap table at founding and Diogo Mónica was a named specific-knowledge anchor; Panacea's central marketing claim ("ex-FDA, decades of experience") attaches to no public person, and at angel size I will not underwrite a permission-moat thesis when the permissioned humans are unnamed. **The verdict flips if Panacea names two ex-FDA reviewers on the team page with verifiable submission histories and discloses 5+ signed engagements with named biotech/medtech clients within six months** -- that is a Weave-Bio-with-better-domain-credentials story, and worth a second look.
