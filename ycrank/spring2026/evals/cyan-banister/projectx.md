# ProjectX -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that jumps out at me about ProjectX isn't the product or the market -- it's the fact that three out of four original co-founders are gone. The July 2024 press lists Rounak Adhikari alongside Subhrakamal Das, Ronit Banerjee, and Suraj Kuncham. By the time the YC Spring 2026 page goes up, it's Rounak Adhikary, Bishal Karmakar, and Sourya Majumder -- an entirely different team except for Rounak. I learned the hard way with HQ Trivia that co-founder conflict can kill even a perfectly timed product with the right market. That was one departure, one irreconcilable split. Here we're looking at three co-founders leaving before the company has even raised a proper seed round. Maybe there's a benign explanation -- early co-founders who were classmates and drifted away as the ambition scaled, or a healthy pruning before YC. But I can't evaluate what I can't see, and this pattern is the single loudest signal in the dossier for how I think about risk.

Where ProjectX does genuinely light up for me is the economic access story. Seventy-five percent of students in India can't access high-quality computing devices. When I think about Uber, I don't think about logistics optimization -- I think about Roger, my driver, and the fact that the platform created income and economic participation for millions of people who didn't have it before. ProjectX has that same DNA. A student in Kalyani, West Bengal -- which happens to be where Rounak grew up -- shouldn't be locked out of Blender or Unreal Engine or AI tools because they can't afford a $2,000 GPU workstation. Streaming those applications in a browser tab for usage-based pricing is the kind of access play that makes me lean in. The IIT Bombay pilot and $2.4 million in signed LOIs from Indian institutions tell me the demand is real, not theoretical. If I were evaluating purely on whether this company creates meaningful opportunity for underserved people, I'd be writing a check.

But I keep coming back to a question: is this weird? Is this the kind of bet that looks ridiculous to other investors? And honestly, no. Cloud GPU streaming is one of the most crowded categories in infrastructure right now -- 95 tracked companies, $20.8 billion in collective funding, hyperscalers circling. Google acquired Cameyo in May 2024 specifically for app virtualization. Shadow PC has raised over $100 million. CoreWeave has raised billions. The per-app-in-browser-tabs angle is clever engineering, but it's not the kind of paradigm-breaking insight that made Crusoe Energy feel insane in 2019 or SpaceX feel delusional in 2008. When I wrote my first angel check into SpaceX, nobody else wanted to touch private space. When I look at ProjectX, I see a space where everybody and their venture-backed cousin is building something adjacent. The best investments I've made looked bad to most investors at the time. This one looks reasonable to most investors -- and that's actually the problem from where I sit.

Rounak's biography has elements I respect -- founding a company at 19 to fund his vision, hustling from a small city in West Bengal into Draper University, Stanford ASES, and YC. Winning Tiger Launch at Princeton against global competition. There's real drive there. But when I ask myself "tell me your story, not the business," I'm listening for the hardship that forged an unbreakable bond between this person and this specific problem. I don't hear that clearly enough. The biographical thread connecting a young design student to building GPU streaming infrastructure isn't as tight as what I look for. Compare that to how the Contraline founder spent years in academic research on male contraception before anyone would fund him -- the problem and the person were inseparable. Here the founder is impressive but the connection feels more entrepreneurial ambition than biographical inevitability.

The bull case is real and I want to be honest about it: if ProjectX nails sub-3-second cold starts for GPU apps in browsers at a price point that works for Indian educational institutions, they could become the access layer for an entire generation of students and professionals in emerging markets who are currently locked out of professional creative and AI tools. The $2.4M in LOIs at pre-seed is genuinely strong traction. The TAM at the intersection of GPU-as-a-Service and Desktop-as-a-Service is enormous and growing at 35%+ CAGR. And if they execute in India first and expand globally, the playbook of starting where compute access is most constrained and moving upmarket is sound. A different investor with a different framework might see this as a compelling infrastructure bet with proven demand signals. For me, though, the co-founder churn, the consensus nature of the category, and the capital intensity problem -- $204K against competitors with hundreds of millions -- add up to a pass. I'd need to sit with Rounak, hear his story unfiltered, understand what happened with the original team, and feel that jolt of conviction before I'd reconsider.
