# Antihero Studios -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The most striking feature of Antihero Studios is the density of credential: three co-founders who collectively held senior design, creative, and technical leadership on Brawl Stars, Clash Royale, Candy Crush, and Hearthstone — eight billion-dollar-per-year franchises across three different studios. Frank Yan did not merely work on Brawl Stars; he drove it from a revenue nadir to 8.8x growth, then spoke at GDC about the mechanics of that recovery. This is not a team guessing at what makes mobile multiplayer games work. They have operated the machinery at peak output. But the very trajectory of their careers reveals the structural problem: these founders left billion-dollar franchises at Supercell, Blizzard, and King because those franchises did not create the kind of lock-in that prevents talent and attention from migrating. If the builders of Brawl Stars can walk away, so can its players. The credentials prove execution capacity; they simultaneously disprove monopoly.

What is Antihero Studios' secret? The claim appears to be that extraction shooters — validated by ARC Raiders' 14M copies and ~$500M in PC/console revenue — can be translated to mass-market mobile through top-down perspective and five-minute sessions, and that incumbents cannot follow because Tencent's Arena Breakout targets hardcore players and Supercell's Brawl Stars would cannibalize itself by adding extraction mechanics. This is a market-gap observation dressed in strategic language, not a contrarian truth that sophisticated observers would reject. Every gaming VC can read the same Sensor Tower data showing Arena Breakout's 95% China revenue concentration. The cannibalization thesis borrows from Clayton Christensen, not from any proprietary insight about how games create structural dominance. More damaging: the dossier itself concedes that "the top-down extraction shooter format on mobile is technically reproducible" and that "any well-funded studio with multiplayer netcode expertise could build a similar product." When the company's own defensibility analysis admits the product has no technical moat, the secret test has been failed explicitly.

The comparison that most illuminates Antihero Studios' structural position is Wish — my portfolio's most expensive lesson. Wish operated in a massive addressable market with no proprietary technology, no network effects, and no switching costs, competing on execution and marketing against players with vastly greater resources. Antihero Studios operates in a $121 billion mobile gaming market with no proprietary technology, no structural switching costs, and a product that competes on creative taste against Tencent — a company with $86 billion in annual gaming revenue that owns both Arena Breakout and Supercell. The asymmetry is not in Antihero's favor. The "brand like A24" aspiration compounds the concern: A24 succeeded through curatorial taste, which is neither plannable nor monopolistic. You cannot engineer your way to becoming A24; the aspiration is definitionally indefinite. Brice Laville Saint-Martin's background as Supercell's Marketing Creative Director gives him brand instincts, but instincts are not moats.

The bull case deserves honest articulation. If Frank Yan's specific game-design knowledge — demonstrated by rescuing Brawl Stars from decline — translates to MISFITZ, and if the 70,000 pre-alpha players with 50-minute average daily playtime represent genuine product-market resonance rather than self-selected enthusiast engagement, Antihero Studios could establish the defining casual extraction franchise on mobile before Tencent mobilizes. The creator program — 750 creators generating 250 content pieces during pre-alpha, anchored by KairosTime from Supercell's own community — represents distribution thinking more sophisticated than most gaming startups demonstrate. The IDFA-driven disruption of performance marketing is a real structural shift that advantages studios with organic distribution capability. If all of this compounds, MISFITZ could reach Brawl Stars scale. But "if the game is good enough and fast enough" is an execution bet, not a monopoly thesis. Supercell itself, with unlimited resources and the same talent pool, has launched games that failed. Hit-driven entertainment resists definite optimism more stubbornly than even biology — at least cancer cells follow physical laws.

Antihero Studios has zero atoms-based leverage. No hardware, no physical-world manipulation, no regulatory barrier, no government customer, no infrastructure integration that creates switching costs. The product is a mobile game — pure bits, distributed through Apple and Google's app stores where Antihero controls neither the platform nor the customer relationship. This is the furthest possible position from the General Matter or Anduril pattern where physical-world technology creates multi-year replication barriers. In my portfolio's revealed logic, pure software entertainment products without structural defensibility receive the lowest conviction tier regardless of team quality, because the question is not whether the founders can build the product but whether anyone can prevent the next team from building something equivalent.

The decisive dimensions are Monopoly Secret and Physical-World Technology Moat. Antihero Studios has exceptional founders pursuing a competitive market with reproducible technology — the exact configuration my framework identifies as a losing bet regardless of execution quality. Frank Yan's Brawl Stars credentials and the 70K-player pre-alpha engagement lift this above a dismissive pass, but the absence of any structural path to monopoly in a hit-driven entertainment category makes this someone else's investment, not mine.
