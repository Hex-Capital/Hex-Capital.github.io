# PayPath -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

$2M ARR in under 12 months with a three-person team managing $500M+ in debt assets — that's the number that stops me. Dean Glas and Matthew Lippl built PayPath to that pace, and the execution discipline is undeniable. But execution and conviction are different things, and when I ask my first question — tell me their story, not the business — what comes back is a serial entrepreneur who built GeoGif (animated location overlays, acquired by TikTok in 2019) and SellX (a sales talent marketplace), and a product designer who led UX at CoinMarketCap and Lavender. These are builders. Dean has been through the fire of founding, failing (Bivid, FinalStage), and exiting. That forge is real. But it's a forge for building platforms — not for this specific problem. Neither founder has documented experience inside the debt industry, and debt resolution is a world with its own language, its own regulatory body scars (CFPB, TCPA, FDCPA, state licensing), and its own deeply entrenched relationships. When I backed Affirm, Max Levchin wasn't just building fintech — his entire PayPal history was about making financial systems legible to ordinary people. The problem had found him decades before Affirm existed. With PayPath, the founders found the problem, and they found it well, but that's a different signal in my framework.

The debt system is one of the most opaque, extractive structures in the American economy — $348 trillion in global obligations, and the people caught inside it often have no idea what they owe, to whom, or what their rights are. That's exactly the kind of invisible system I recognize from my own life. When I was fifteen and navigating systems designed to exploit people without information, I learned what it feels like to be on the wrong side of opacity. PayPath lives adjacent to that problem, but here's what a generic analyst might miss: PayPath sells to debt resolution firms, not to the people trapped in debt. The self-enrollment portal and client dashboard give consumers some visibility into their resolution process, and that's real. But the core business is making collectors and settlement companies more efficient at their existing work. Affirm attacked credit card opacity on behalf of borrowers. Ownwell attacks property tax opacity on behalf of homeowners. PayPath optimizes the machinery of debt management for the firms running it. The invisible system isn't being exposed — it's being streamlined. That distinction matters deeply to me.

Dean and Matt's prior company together, SellX, was also a workflow and platform play, and the fact that they've co-founded before with clear role delineation — Dean as CEO, Matt as CXO/CPO — is a genuine positive. I've learned the hard way (HQ Trivia) what happens with ambiguous co-founder dynamics, and this pair has a documented working relationship with defined roles. The pattern continuity from SellX (platform infrastructure for a fragmented professional workflow) to PayPath (platform infrastructure for fragmented debt workflows) also makes structural sense. But the transition from SellX to PayPath is opaque — SellX raised $2-4M in seed funding, and Dean's LinkedIn still reads "Stealth AI Startup" rather than PayPath. What happened to SellX? The dossier doesn't say, and that gap nags at me.

The space itself isn't one that makes VCs shift in their chairs. Debt collection technology is unsexy, which is different from contrarian. Aktos raised $4.4M from 8VC, Prodigal pulled in $14M from YC, Accel, and Menlo Ventures — sophisticated firms are already comfortable writing checks here. "AI Operating System for Modern Debt" is a clean pitch, not a disorienting one. When I backed Flock Safety, the surveillance angle made civil liberties advocates nervous. When I wrote my check into Anduril, defense tech was politically radioactive in Silicon Valley. PayPath doesn't provoke that productive discomfort. It's a vertical SaaS play in an underdigitized industry, powered by AI that's increasingly commoditized. The competitive risk from Aktos, Prodigal, and Tratta — plus the modular availability of the same AI communication and workflow capabilities through Twilio and OpenAI APIs — is the structural concern. PayPath's unified-platform approach and the switching costs from replacing four or five tools simultaneously are genuine defensibility signals, and $500M+ in managed assets starts a data flywheel. But I've been burned by companies where the moat was "we bundle more features" rather than "we have something structurally irreplaceable."

The strongest bull case: Dean Glas is a proven builder who shipped a product to $2M ARR faster than Prodigal (which has $14M in funding and ~$1.6M ARR). The debt collection software market is growing at 10% CAGR toward $14B. Regulation F modernized communication rules, creating demand for compliant digital platforms. Legacy vendors like FICO Debt Manager face the classic innovator's dilemma — cannibalizing license revenue to rebuild as AI-native platforms. If PayPath's data flywheel kicks in and its compliance tooling becomes the de facto standard for mid-market debt firms, the switching costs compound and it becomes the Carta of debt. That's a real outcome. The execution velocity suggests these founders can build fast enough to stay ahead of better-funded competitors. If I'm wrong about biographical forge mattering here, and the market rewards pure execution speed plus platform breadth, PayPath wins.

But I keep coming back to the two dimensions that define my highest-conviction bets, and PayPath is weak on both. The founders' biographical connection to debt resolution is absent — not thin, absent. And the invisible system test comes back ambiguous at best, because the product empowers the industry rather than exposing it. The traction is impressive and the defensibility signals are emerging, but those are execution metrics, not thesis alignment. This is a competent team building a real business in a real market — I'm just not the right investor for it. The biographical forge and invisible system dimensions together account for 50 of my 100 points, and PayPath doesn't activate either one.
