# Burt -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

HappyRobot has $62 million in the bank, eight-figure revenue, and DHL on its customer list. It's doing exactly what Burt is doing — AI agents for logistics — and it's two years ahead. When I see a pre-seed company entering a space where a well-capitalized incumbent already proved the model, grew 10x in a year, and raised at a $500M valuation, that's not a signal of opportunity. That's a signal that the window for contrarian entry has closed. Add two more YC W26 companies — Lanesurf and Lighthouz — attacking the same customer base, and you have a crowded race where the consequence of winning is... what, exactly? Becoming the third or fourth AI automation vendor selling to freight brokers? My best investments are in companies where the experts said it couldn't be done. Here, the experts already did it.

Let me apply my first-order filter: if Burt succeeds completely — wins the mid-market, automates every back-office task for thousands of small brokerages — what changes? Freight brokers spend less on people chasing proof-of-delivery documents. Invoice validation happens faster. Load quoting gets automated. The brokerage industry keeps operating exactly as it does today, just with lower back-office costs. That's a workflow efficiency gain, not an industry transformation. The $19.5 billion freight brokerage market doesn't get disrupted — it gets served slightly more cheaply. The company's own positioning makes this explicit: their AI teammates "work within your existing systems." They are optimizing the incumbent structure, not replacing it. When I backed Square, the consequence wasn't that existing POS systems got cheaper — it was that the entire payment infrastructure changed. When I backed Juniper, the consequence wasn't faster routers — it was that Cisco's architecture became obsolete. Burt's consequence of success is a successful vertical SaaS company. That's fine. But it's not a bet I make.

The strongest bull case goes something like this: HappyRobot locked up enterprise (DHL, Werner, Schneider), leaving the long tail of thousands of small-to-midsize brokerages — fragmented, underserved, operating on email and spreadsheets — wide open. A configurable "AI teammate" platform that integrates with their existing TMS could capture that mid-market before HappyRobot moves downmarket. The founders have genuine LLM agent-building experience from Replo, where they built coding agents. The timing is right — LLMs just crossed the reliability threshold for parsing logistics-specific unstructured data. And the pivot from a general model fine-tuning platform to logistics-specific AI teammates could indicate rapid learning: they tested a general approach, found that vertical specificity is where value lives, and committed. If they can build deep TMS integrations and accumulate process-specific workflow data across hundreds of brokerages, switching costs compound over time. In the best case, they become the mid-market standard for logistics AI automation before enterprise players like HappyRobot notice or care. That's a real business — potentially a $200-500 million outcome. But a $500 million outcome is precisely what I mean when I say the consequences of success are inconsequential relative to the effort. These founders are too capable to spend years optimizing freight broker back-offices.

The founders are competent engineers — Bobby Zhong was a founding engineer at Pirros (YC W23) and built coding agents at Replo, Kurt Sharma studied EECS at Berkeley and also worked at Replo. They can clearly build. But I see no evidence of the outsider courage that characterizes my highest-conviction bets. They pivoted from a general fine-tuning platform to logistics because it looked like a good vertical, not because they have a thesis about why the freight industry must fundamentally change. Neither founder has logistics domain experience, which could be an outsider advantage — except they're not attempting anything the industry considers impossible. They're doing what HappyRobot already proved works, just for smaller customers. That's execution arbitrage, not invention. When I backed Peter Beck at Rocket Lab, he was attempting orbital launch from New Zealand with no aerospace pedigree — the industry laughed. Nobody is laughing at the idea of AI automating freight broker tasks in 2026. It's consensus.

On timing trajectory, the underlying LLM capability curve is real and still accelerating — that's favorable. But Burt isn't early to this curve. They're arriving after HappyRobot validated the market, after multiple competitors entered, after industry analysts projected 15% of spot-market truckloads would be automatically booked by 2026. The rate of change in the underlying technology is strong, but the rate of competitor entry is equally strong. When I invested in OpenAI in 2019, AI was "laughable" and I was tracking the slope of improvement before anyone else noticed. Here, everyone noticed. The puck arrived.
