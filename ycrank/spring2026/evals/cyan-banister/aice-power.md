# AICE Power -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Verdigris Technologies has been building clip-on CT sensors with AI disaggregation for commercial buildings since 2011, with $41.8M in funding behind them. AICE Power is entering that same technical territory — same sensor type, same analytical approach — and differentiating primarily by targeting smaller venues: supermarkets, retail chains, fast food restaurants. That's not a contrarian bet. That's a segment play on validated technology. When I evaluate whether something is "magically weird," I'm asking whether it would make a room of sophisticated VCs shift in their chairs. Energy monitoring for small commercial buildings? Every investor nods along. The EU's Energy Performance of Buildings Directive is mandating this. Climate tech is the most consensus-attractive category in venture right now. AICE Power is swimming with the current, not against it — and in my experience, the companies that change everything are the ones swimming upstream while everyone else watches from the shore.

Grégoire Chomette brings an MIT MS in Aeronautics & Astronautics, NASA Ames asteroid risk work, and EPFL biogas supply chain research. Rémi Bouteiller brings ENS Paris-Saclay embedded systems training, Amira Health founding engineer experience with PCB design and Shenzhen manufacturing, and a French Eurobot Championship. These are credentialed, technically capable people. But when I ask my first question — tell me your story, not the business — I don't hear a biographical forge connecting either founder to building energy waste. Chomette's EPFL biogas paper is a research thread, not a life-shaping encounter with the problem. Bouteiller's hardware skills are directly applicable but transferable to any hardware startup. AICE Power reads like two talented French engineers who identified a market gap, not two people whose biographies made them incapable of working on anything else. That absence matters to me. The founders I've backed who built the biggest things — they couldn't have done anything else. The problem found them before they found the market.

AICE Power's claim of deploying at "5% the cost of traditional Building Management Systems" from Honeywell and Siemens does touch something real — small commercial buildings have genuinely been locked out of energy visibility by high-cost, weeks-long BMS installations. There's an opacity here: a supermarket operator gets an electricity bill and has no idea which refrigerator case is bleeding kilowatts or whether their HVAC is cycling inefficiently. But I distinguish between an underserved market and an extractive system. When I backed Affirm, credit card companies were deliberately obscuring lending terms to profit from consumer confusion — there was a villain, an information asymmetry maintained on purpose. AICE Power is solving a real problem, but the "hidden system" is more about economics (it hasn't been worth serving these buildings) than about gatekeepers extracting value through opacity. That's a meaningful difference in my framework.

Defensibility is where AICE Power concerns me most. The dossier states it plainly: "clip-on CT sensors are commodity hardware" and "software disaggregation via NILM is an active research area with open-source implementations." AICE Power's data moat is described as "unrealized at current scale" with just 10+ sites live. Compare this to Flock Safety, where each camera installed in a neighborhood created network effects that made the next camera more valuable — a compounding infrastructure moat. AICE Power's switching costs are moderate (physical hardware removal, re-baselining), but a well-funded competitor like Verdigris deciding to move downmarket, or Facilio with its $45.9M and 25K+ buildings adding a hardware layer, could replicate AICE Power's position. The 10+ live sites tracking 5+ MW and logos like E.Leclerc and Accor are real traction for pre-seed, but they don't constitute a structural moat.

The strongest bull case I can construct: AICE Power is Crusoe Energy for building energy — both start with a "boring" surface (gas flaring, electricity monitoring) that hides infrastructure potential. As AICE Power deploys across hundreds of E.Leclerc supermarkets and Accor hotels, its disaggregation models train on diverse equipment signatures, building a data advantage that compounds. The EU EPBD creates a regulatory inflection that forces adoption across millions of small commercial buildings. Bouteiller's Shenzhen manufacturing experience solves the hardware scaling challenge that kills most hardware startups. The 6-month ROI and 15-20% savings create a self-selling value proposition. Give this team two years and they're the infrastructure layer for European commercial building energy — and then they cross the Atlantic. I've seen this pattern work. But when I pressure-test it against my framework, the bull case relies on execution in a known space rather than on biographical inevitability, contrarian conviction, or structural disruption of an extractive system. Crusoe's founders were building portable data centers in North Dakota oil fields — that was genuinely strange, genuinely uncomfortable for investors. AICE Power is doing something sensible that every climate-focused fund would happily back. The condition that would flip me: if Chomette or Bouteiller had a personal story of watching a family business bleed money to invisible energy waste, or had built and failed at this problem before, or if AICE Power had been rejected by fifty investors because the hardware bet scared them — that biographical and contrarian signal would change my calculus entirely.

AICE Power is a competent team building a useful product in a growing market with real early customers. That's not nothing — it's more than most pre-seed companies have. But my framework exists to separate companies I admire from companies I need to fund with my own money. The two dimensions that lock this verdict are Founder's Biographical Forge (no evidence of personal inevitability connecting either Chomette or Bouteiller to building energy waste) and Contrarian Conviction (energy monitoring for commercial buildings is consensus-attractive, regulatory-tailwinded, and technically well-precedented by Verdigris's thirteen years in the space). AICE Power will likely find investors who see the EU regulatory wave and the underserved small-commercial segment and write enthusiastic checks. I wish them well, but this isn't a Long Journey bet.
