# Zatanna -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Zatanna is the pivot trail. Search engine caches show at least two prior identities — "lawtech company focused on jury trials" and "Adaptive Healthcare Admin Agents" — before the current scraping infrastructure product. Multiple pivots during a YC batch are not inherently disqualifying; some great companies found their footing through rapid iteration. But three distinct product directions — legal, healthcare, and developer infrastructure — don't form a coherent search pattern. They suggest a team trying doors rather than pursuing a market conviction. The strongest counter-signal is that the CTO's actual experience is in anti-bot bypass at Pikkit, which maps directly to protocol-level scraping. This final pivot may be the team landing on what one of the founders actually knows how to build. But landing on founder-market fit through elimination is a weaker signal than a founder who attacks a market they've been obsessing over for years.

On the structural moment question — what has changed in the last 12-24 months to make this market possible now — the answer is mostly consensus. Yes, AI agents and LLM applications have dramatically increased demand for structured web data. Firecrawl's trajectory proves this: YC S24, $14.5M raised by August 2025, 81K+ GitHub stars. But that's exactly the problem. The structural moment in AI-driven data extraction has already been identified, capitalized on, and substantially funded. Firecrawl is 18 months ahead with significant traction. Bright Data generates $300M in annual revenue. Apify did $13.3M in 2024. This is not a "too boring" or "too niche" market that other investors dismiss — it is a well-understood, actively funded market with a clear competitive hierarchy. When I invested in PagerDuty, operations alerting seemed unglamorous and niche. Scraping infrastructure in 2026, post-AI wave, is the opposite: it's a hot market with incumbents who already have distribution.

The protocol-level technical approach — reverse-engineering HTTP requests instead of spinning up headless browsers — is a genuine performance improvement. Sub-500ms versus 3-10 seconds matters for real-time applications and cost-sensitive workloads. But the trade-off is critical: each target site requires bespoke reverse-engineering, and each time a site changes its authentication or TLS configuration, the integration breaks. This creates maintenance burden that scales linearly with coverage. A browser-based scraper is target-agnostic; Zatanna's approach is target-specific. As AI agents need data from thousands of sources, general-purpose approaches may win on breadth even if they lose on speed per request. The dossier itself identifies this: "creates ongoing maintenance burden that scales linearly with the number of supported targets, unlike browser-based approaches that are target-agnostic." This is closer to what I'd call "software-aware" than "software-driven" — the competitive advantage is in human reverse-engineering labor, not in software that compounds.

The strongest bull case would go like this: a small number of high-value data targets (Amazon, LinkedIn, major financial data sources) account for the vast majority of enterprise scraping demand. If Zatanna can build and maintain protocol-level integrations for the top 50-100 targets with dramatically better performance and reliability, they don't need to be general-purpose. The integration library becomes a compounding moat, and Alex Blackwell's anti-bot expertise from Pikkit gives them a genuine edge in the hardest part — evading increasingly sophisticated bot detection. The business becomes "boring but critical" infrastructure for AI data pipelines, similar to how Samsara became essential IoT infrastructure. For this to work, Zatanna would need to demonstrate that protocol-level integrations can be maintained cost-effectively at scale, that the performance advantage translates to willingness to pay a premium, and that they can build distribution against funded incumbents. That's at least two miracles — technical scalability and competitive distribution — which is one too many.

On the product-to-distribution dimension, I see no evidence of multi-product thinking. The demo-request-only GTM with no self-serve suggests a services-oriented sales motion, not a developer platform play. Stripe's genius was making the payment API so easy to integrate that it became the developer relationship through which Treasury, Billing, and Atlas flowed. Zatanna's custom-integration model works in the opposite direction — each customer engagement likely requires bespoke engineering, which limits the flywheel potential. Without a self-serve wedge or a platform architecture that enables third-party integrations, the ceiling is a profitable but structurally capped scraping services company.

The founding team has relevant domain skills — Blackwell's anti-bot work is genuinely differentiated experience — but the combination of recent graduates (2022-2026), no prior exits, no confirmed co-founder working history, and three pivots in rapid succession gives me pause on execution trajectory. At pre-seed, I'm willing to bet on young teams, but I need to see either extraordinary domain conviction or extraordinary shipping velocity. The pivot pattern suggests searching, and the absence of any public traction signal — no revenue, no users, no GitHub activity for the product — makes it hard to assess velocity on the current product.
