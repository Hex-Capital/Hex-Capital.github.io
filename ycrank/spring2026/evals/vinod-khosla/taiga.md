# Taiga -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Taiga is a percentage-of-collections billing service for independent doctor's offices dressed in AI clothing, and its analytical signature is a founder-market-fit story (Nanda Guntupalli and Adam Wax both grew up inside their physician parents' practices) paired with a consequence-of-success so small that even a perfect execution leaves the U.S. healthcare cost curve untouched. That pairing — the highest dimension being a soft "founders are not career experts" read, and the lowest being consequence magnitude — tells me exactly what Taiga is: a competent labor-arbitrage services business that happens to live inside a category I do not fund.

On consequence, the **OpenAI** (2019) check sits at the top of my ladder because the success-state was civilizational; the success-state of Taiga is that Cheskis Family Therapy and a few thousand peers pay a smaller percentage of collections to a smarter intermediary. The dossier names the U.S. RCM market at $60-63B in 2025 growing to $137B by 2033 — that is a real number, but the incumbent regime is "in-house billers and outsourced billing firms," and collapsing that regime moves no civilizational variable I care about. This is the **Pass Example** pattern almost exactly: a workflow inside one persona at one type of customer, indistinguishable in scale from the GPT-4 sales-summarizer pitch I dismiss in thirty seconds. Taiga is closer to that bottom rung than to **DoorDash** (2013), because DoorDash at least restructured the flow of consumer commerce across millions of merchants; medical-billing-as-a-service restructures the back office of a clinic.

On technical and capital risk that repels consensus VCs, the failure mechanism I watch for is the software wrapper on a commodity model — and Taiga is that wrapper with a services bow on top. The dossier itself lists Candid Health at $99.5M raised, Adonis at $95M+ with a $40M Series C in March 2026, and Akasa at $205M, all chasing the same generative-coding thesis; when three well-funded incumbents already exist, the underlying ML capability is, by definition, widely buildable. Taiga's claimed moat is ten named EHR integrations (DrChrono, athenahealth, eClinicalWorks, Tebra, Elation, et al.) and a self-reported 98% touchless claim rate with no denominator — neither is a miracle, both are integration grunt work that any of the funded competitors can replicate. There is no fusion-scale physics here, no **QuantumScape** chemistry, no **Genesis AI** (2025) foundation-model training cost. A Tier-1 generalist can write this check and probably already has.

Guntupalli and Wax (Penn CS, friends since third grade, both with physician parents) earn the only signal Taiga has on founder-defies-consensus, and it is a quiet one: they are not the eight-years-at-Salesforce profile my TED Talk warns against, and they have lived inside the actual customer's pain. Over 18 months, this dimension compounds only if they convert that proximity into a non-obvious product decision — say, a coding model trained on denial patterns specific to small-practice payer mixes that the Candid Health multi-site SaaS architecture cannot replicate. Nothing in the dossier suggests they have done that yet; the differentiation listed is "two-week onboarding" and "full-stack service for independent practices," which is positioning, not defiance. The McKinsey 1986 cellphone forecast (<1M by 2000, actual 109M) is the kind of expert blindness I reward founders for naming; Taiga's founders have named no equivalent orthodoxy they intend to break.

Founder learning rate would flip up a tier only if the dossier showed a documented pivot — the kind of moment that justified the $100M Series C into **Slash** (2024) after the Kanye West story. Taiga shows none: one named customer, one stated thesis, one product since inception, and founders young enough (Class of 2028) that there has not been time for a pivot. I do not penalize aggressively for absence at pre-seed, but I cannot reward what is not there either.

Capital profile is where Taiga most cleanly disqualifies itself from my check. **Commonwealth Fusion** (2019) absorbed my $115M Series A lead because the roadmap required that depth; Taiga is a services business with percentage-of-collections unit economics that reaches $1M ARR on a $500K pre-seed and Series A from any generalist. I am over-resourced for this deal by an order of magnitude, and the deployment story for a $50M Khosla check into AI billing-services would be embarrassing to write.

Thirty-six-month forecast: Taiga reaches $2-4M ARR on a few hundred independent practices, fails to raise a competitive Series A as Candid Health and Adonis move downmarket with their incumbent capital, and gets acquired by Tebra or athenahealth for a low-eight-figure outcome that returns a YC seed but not a venture fund — or it stalls at $1M ARR and the founders pivot into a vertical EHR for solo practices. The mechanism is straightforward: the moat is integrations and onboarding speed, both of which the $95M-$205M-funded incumbents can buy faster than Taiga can build them.

The strongest bull case borrows from my **Slash** (2024) exception: YC pedigree plus founder-market fit plus a services business that quietly becomes a vertical SaaS once the labor margin compounds. Slash violated my consequence-magnitude prior and I funded it at $100M Series C because the documented Kanye pivot proved learning rate. Taiga has the YC pedigree and the founder-market fit, but it does not have the pivot — Guntupalli and Wax have executed one linear plan, and the dossier offers no "we were wrong about X" moment to underwrite. The verifiable condition that would flip my verdict: Taiga publicly documents a non-obvious pivot — say, abandoning the percentage-of-collections services model for a payer-side denial-prediction product that the multi-site SaaS competitors structurally cannot ship — within 12 months. Absent that, this is the Pass Example with better founder-market fit, and I pass.
