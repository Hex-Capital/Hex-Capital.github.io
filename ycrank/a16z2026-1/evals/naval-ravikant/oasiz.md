# Oasiz -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Abel Dagne built a social app that reached #1 on the App Store with 500K+ downloads before Oasiz existed. That's the fact I anchor on. Consumer mobile distribution -- understanding what makes millions of people download, share, and return to an app -- is deeply specific knowledge. You cannot teach it in a Stanford lecture or an a16z sprint. It's learned through watching download curves collapse and rebuild, through the visceral pattern recognition that comes from shipping something that caught fire once. The question for Oasiz is whether Dagne's distribution-layer specific knowledge is sufficient when the creation layer -- AI game generation from text prompts -- is available to anyone with an API key. Rosebud AI has $15M from a16z and Khosla. CodeWisp and Playabl.ai are both YC-backed. Every one of them can generate games from prompts. None of them have demonstrated they know how to make millions of people care.

The structural parallel that clarifies Oasiz for me is Twitter at its earliest stage. Ev Williams' specific knowledge from Blogger was not about the mechanics of writing -- it was about how public communication distributes virally online. The creation layer (typing 140 characters) was trivially accessible; the distribution layer was the entire company. Oasiz has the same architecture: AI commoditizes game creation the way the text box commoditized tweet creation, and the social feed becomes the moat. Dagne's prior #1 App Store result is the equivalent of Williams' Blogger years -- demonstrated knowledge about the distribution graph, not the content format. The 6.5M organic TikTok views and 230K+ plays from a 4-person team with no marketing budget confirm the pattern is activating. Twenty-one App Store ratings after two months is thin, but Twitter had fewer users than that when I first saw it.

The leverage audit is clean. Oasiz runs on three forms of permissionless leverage simultaneously: AI generates games at zero marginal cost, users create the content library (5,000+ playable experiences in closed beta), and the social feed distributes without gatekeepers. Four people. No sales team. No content moderation army. This is the shrinking-company thesis made concrete -- Midjourney does $500M with 163 employees; Oasiz is attempting something structurally similar for casual gaming with 4. Jonathan Dinh's background from Berkeley's ROAR AI lab and Amazon search optimization provides the technical architecture for the discovery feed, while his top-0.01% mobile gaming ranking means he understands engagement loops from the inside, not from a product requirements document.

Roblox is the incumbent I'd normally fear, but Roblox faces the exact principal-agent conflict that makes startups viable against giants. Their $1B+ annual developer payout structure has created 3.5M active creators who are agents of the current Lua-scripting economy. Pivoting to AI-generated casual games cannibalizes the economic foundation those agents depend on. Roblox's leadership cannot move here without destroying their own marketplace -- the same structural bind that prevented Google from building Perplexity. Oasiz, as a principal with no legacy developer economics to protect, can occupy the space Roblox's agents cannot.

The access democratization signal is strong and maps to my deepest portfolio pattern. AngelList gave everyone access to startup investing. CoinList gave everyone access to token launches. Oasiz gives everyone access to game creation -- previously restricted to people who could write Lua or Unity scripts. Five thousand games built in closed beta, by users who couldn't have built them six months earlier, is the same structural expansion I've backed repeatedly. The Atari IP catalog offer, if real, suggests incumbents already recognize Oasiz as a distribution channel worth partnering with rather than competing against.

The risk I'd need to monitor: the co-founder relationship. No shared prior employer or university between Dagne and Dinh surfaces in the dossier. I evaluate co-founder pairs through the principal-agent lens -- co-founders without pre-existing trust are agents to each other before they're principals to the company. This doesn't trigger my red flag for "met at a co-founder event" -- there's simply no data on how they connected -- but it's the single dimension where I'd want a direct conversation before wiring money. The commoditization risk on the AI layer is real but structurally identical to the risk Twitter faced with text input: when creation is free, distribution is everything, and Dagne has demonstrated he knows distribution. If Oasiz reaches 1M DAU before Rosebud or Playabl.ai ship a social feed, the network effects compound and the game is over. The 6.5M TikTok views suggest the viral mechanics are already working; the question is conversion to retained users, and that's where Dagne's #1 App Store experience becomes load-bearing.

Oasiz earns an Invest from me on two dimensions: Dagne's demonstrated consumer distribution specific knowledge (not theoretical -- he shipped a #1 social app) and the leverage architecture that lets four people generate a content library of 5,000+ games with zero marginal cost. The principal authenticity signal and co-founder relationship are the gaps that keep this from the top of the range. I'd take the meeting and ask Dagne one question: "Were you building games for friends before you decided this was a company?" His answer determines whether I write the check at dinner or sleep on it.
