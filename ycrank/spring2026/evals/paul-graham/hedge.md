# Hedge -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Hedge is a schlep-domain bet wrapped in a buzzword tagline: Luke Button has gone and gotten CA surplus-lines license #6018729, signed 30+ carrier markets, and onboarded 140+ retail agencies in 36 states — that's real, unglamorous, regulator-shaped work — but the YC page calls Hedge "AI native" without saying what the AI actually does, and the founder's prior company Fernstone (YC F25) is still alive on the web with no public account of how it relates to Hedge. The signature pairing here is high schlep-blindness with thin earnestness, and that is exactly the company shape that makes me lean in *and* squint at the same time.

Idea That Repels is where Hedge places highest. The HIGH rung on this ladder is Stripe — Patrick and John Collison willingly waded into PCI compliance and bank integrations because that was the moat; the LOW rung is the McKinsey-flavored B2B "workflow automation" pitch from my Pass Example, where everyone in the room nods. Hedge sits closer to the HIGH rung than the middle: state-by-state surplus-lines licensure, NAIC model-bulletin scrutiny on bias and explainability, carrier-appointment politics, ACORD ingestion — most YC founders' unconscious won't even let them see this domain. But Hedge is competing for attention against Federato, Cytora, and Sixfold, and "AI for insurance" in 2026 is no longer a sentence that makes a room go quiet. The schlep is real; the contrarianism is half-real. Between Stripe and ClassDojo, leaning toward ClassDojo — schlep without the sweet contrarian edge.

Earnest Builder Identity is where Hedge places lowest, and this is the dimension I keep returning to. The HIGH rung is Watsi — I took my only board seat there because the founders' motivation was so unmistakable I trusted them with my time, not just my money. The LOW rung is the deck-driven founder who leads with valuation and TAM. Hedge sits between MID and LOW: Luke Button shipped Fernstone less than a year ago as "AI-powered insurance brokerage for businesses" and is now running Hedge as "AI native specialty insurance" — same domain, similar tagline, unresolved relationship. That is not, by itself, damning; founders pivot. But pivots earn trust when the founder *names* the pivot. Public sources don't. Combined with a tagline that asserts "AI native" without specifying a single concrete AI capability, what I see is a builder who is fluent in the vocabulary investors want to hear and has not yet shown me he is obsessed with the *problem* underneath.

Founder Resourcefulness Under Constraint places Hedge between Reddit-tier MID and Airbnb-tier HIGH. The Airbnbs sold $40 cereal boxes; Hedge has done the regulated-industry equivalent of cereal boxes — Button got a surplus-lines license, talked 30+ carriers into appointments, and convinced 140 retail agencies to send him submissions before he had institutional capital. That's genuine. There is no cereal-box anecdote in the dossier, but the artifact list is the anecdote. Organic Problem Discovery is similar: Drew Houston is the HIGH rung (forgot his USB drive), the McKinsey deck is the LOW rung; Hedge sits mid-high because Button operated Fernstone for a full cycle before concluding the wedge was specialty wholesale, not direct retail brokerage — that is "live in the future" learning, even if the public record of what he learned is thin. Founder Learning Rate places Hedge near Patrick Collison's Auctomatic→Stripe rung: Fernstone to Hedge in under twelve months, with Luke Rosa shipping AI-agent automation over $20MM in Traba revenue as the technical complement. The slope is steep; what I can't see is whether it points anywhere superlinear.

My 36-month forecast for Hedge: it reaches $5–15M in commission revenue as a respectable AI-enabled wholesaler in two or three E&S lines (likely contractor liability and cyber), gets acquired by Amwins, CRC, or RT Specialty for a modest multiple, and does not become a thousand-x outcome. The mechanism is the dimensional signature: schlep-blindness gives Hedge a real head start over carrier-side SaaS like Federato and Cytora, but the moat is workflow speed and licensure, not structural — a CRC skunkworks with the same LLM stack neutralizes it — and the missing earnestness signal predicts a founder who optimizes for the exit available rather than the company that has to be built.

The strongest case against me is the Stripe analogy taken seriously. Payments was also a regulated, schlep-heavy domain that "everyone was already funding" in 2010 — Square, Braintree, WePay — and the Collison brothers won anyway because they out-executed on the schlep. Button is a second-time founder in this exact vertical, which is the Auctomatic-before-Stripe pattern I have explicitly rewarded before. If Fernstone turns out to have been a deliberate first-pass that taught Button the wholesale wedge is where the real margin lives, this is a stronger setup than the dossier reads. What dismantles the bull case for me today is the silence: no public narrative of the Fernstone-to-Hedge transition, no founder writing of the kind I trust as a thinking proxy, and a tagline ("AI native") that is doing work the product page should be doing. The single condition that flips my verdict: a clear public accounting from Button of why Fernstone became Hedge, what specifically the "AI native" layer does at submission ingestion, and one named carrier willing to go on the record about delegated authority. Until then, Hedge is interesting but not yet a check from my own pocket.
