# Valgo -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The founders of Valgo authored the MIT Press textbook on algorithmic safety validation, teach the corresponding Stanford course, and one of them spent seven years on the team that built the FAA-certified collision avoidance system now used by every commercial aircraft worldwide. This is the deepest possible founder-market fit for this specific problem — they didn't enter the autonomous systems validation space because it was hot, they created the academic field it draws from. When I backed Color Health, the founding insight was that someone who understood both biology and software engineering could restructure genetic testing at population scale. Valgo has the same structural DNA: people who understand both probabilistic safety validation and the insurance industry's data requirements, building at their intersection. The addition of an insurance executive with $5 billion in M&A experience (if the credentials verify) rounds out the team in exactly the right way. This level of domain authority is rare and creates a genuine technical complexity moat — you cannot replicate "wrote the textbook and built the FAA standard" by hiring a few engineers.

The structural moment is real but imprecise, and that's where my conviction softens. Waymo is operating commercially in multiple cities. Aurora has launched autonomous trucking. Insurers genuinely cannot price these vehicles with traditional actuarial models because the claims history doesn't exist — that's a factual structural gap, not a speculative one. But autonomous vehicle deployment has been "two years away" for a decade, and the pace of commercial scaling depends on regulatory approvals, public safety incidents, and technical challenges that don't follow predictable curves the way LLM scaling does. When I invested in Harvey, I could track the GPT scaling curve and predict with reasonable confidence when model fidelity would cross the legal-work threshold. With Valgo, the underlying capability curve — AV commercial deployment — is driven by dozens of external variables I can't model with the same precision. The market archetype is right: "insurance risk quantification for robots" is the kind of boring, niche, regulatory-complex problem that makes most investors' eyes glaze over, and those are exactly the markets where I've found the best asymmetric returns. But I need the timing signal to be crisper than "it's starting to happen."

The dual-sided adoption challenge concerns me through my single-miracle lens. Valgo needs autonomy companies to share proprietary simulation data AND it needs insurers to accept simulation-derived loss estimates for rate filing. These are two distinct institutional hurdles. The bull case collapses them into one: if simulation-based risk quantification proves actuarially valid, autonomy companies will share data because they need insurance to operate commercially. That's clean. The bear case is that these are genuinely independent obstacles — autonomy companies may resist sharing competitive simulation data even when they need insurance, and state insurance commissioners may require years of traditional actuarial backing before accepting simulation-based models. This isn't quite a two-miracle plan, but it's closer to 1.5 miracles than I'd like.

The strongest bull case is that this is the Gusto pattern for physical AI. Every autonomous system that operates commercially will need insurance. No insurer can price it without tools that translate simulation data into loss estimates. The team that literally wrote the textbook on the underlying algorithms is building the infrastructure layer. The regulatory complexity — insurance regulation, autonomous vehicle certification, actuarial standards — creates structural barriers that deter competition from both software companies and traditional insurers. If AV deployment follows the Goldman Sachs projection of 50% insurance cost disruption, Valgo's API becomes the connective tissue between every autonomy company and every insurer, with data network effects compounding as each new customer improves the risk models. The product-to-distribution trajectory is also genuinely interesting: start with AV insurance risk quantification, expand to drones, warehouse robots, autonomous ships, and eventually become the risk quantification standard for all autonomous systems. An API that ingests simulation data and outputs actuarially usable loss estimates is a wedge, not a ceiling.

What keeps me at neutral rather than invest is the gap between the timing thesis and the timing evidence. Koop Technologies has been operating in this exact space since 2021 with $7M in funding and a Lloyd's partnership, and the autonomous insurance market still hasn't produced visible revenue figures for any player. That's a signal about market readiness, not just about Koop's execution. When a defined competitor has been in market for four years and the total market revenue is still too small to report, the deployment curve may not have crossed the threshold yet. I've seen this pattern before — correct thesis, premature execution. The founders' academic authority and regulatory expertise are genuinely exceptional, and if AV deployment accelerates in the next 18-24 months, this team is perfectly positioned. But I want to see evidence that autonomy companies are actively seeking insurance solutions and that at least one insurer is willing to pilot simulation-based underwriting before I write a check.
