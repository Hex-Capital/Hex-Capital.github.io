# Crebit -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The GENIUS Act signing in July 2025 was a genuine regulatory catalyst for stablecoin-powered payments -- the kind of exogenous shift I always look for. The founders showed good instinct by starting to build immediately after it passed. But here's where Crebit's timing argument breaks down: by the time they launched in February 2026, Stripe had already rolled out stablecoin payouts across 100+ countries, Mastercard unveiled end-to-end stablecoin transaction capabilities, and Brex announced stablecoin payments. When the three largest payment infrastructure players in the world have already moved on your thesis, it's no longer nonobvious -- it's consensus. Compare this to Coinbase in 2012, when I invested because the structural shift in digital asset infrastructure was invisible to mainstream investors. The crypto market was dismissed; there were no incumbents validating the thesis. Crebit faces the opposite situation: the thesis is validated, but so is the competition. The specific niche -- inbound tuition payments to U.S. universities -- adds some differentiation versus Latitude's outbound focus, but the core mechanism (stablecoin settlement replacing correspondent banking) is now table stakes for every major payment company.

Crebit's 45+ U.S. university partners in three months is the traction signal that deserves scrutiny. For a two-person pre-seed team, that's a striking distribution number. But $1M in FX volume across 45+ universities implies roughly $22K per university -- which could mean students at those schools can technically use Crebit, not that Crebit is deeply embedded in bursar payment portals. The Flywire comparison is instructive: Flywire does $623M in TTM revenue with deep incumbent university relationships built over years. Crebit is running a playbook that mirrors Flywire's B2B2C university-first GTM, but Flywire can add stablecoin settlement as a feature without rebuilding its university relationships from scratch. That's the application-layer vulnerability I worry about. Crebit isn't building the stablecoin infrastructure itself -- it's using rails built by others to serve a vertical. I'd rather invest in the picks-and-shovels layer (the Stripe stablecoin API, the compliance infrastructure enabling stablecoin payments) than in a vertical application built on top of contestable infrastructure.

The single-miracle test is where I have the most nuanced read. The technology miracle -- stablecoins settling cross-border payments faster and cheaper than correspondent banking -- is already solved exogenously. Crebit doesn't need to invent new settlement technology. The real miracle they need is distribution: convincing universities to switch from entrenched incumbents or add Crebit alongside them. That's one miracle, which passes my threshold. But operating across Brazil, Mexico, Nigeria, Philippines, UAE, the U.S., and Europe with a two-person team introduces a second miracle: multi-jurisdiction money transmission compliance. Each country requires its own licensing, banking partnerships, and regulatory relationships. For Jensen Coonradt and Simmi Sen -- both of whom graduated high school in 2024 -- navigating seven-country regulatory compliance while simultaneously building university distribution is not a single-miracle company. It's at least two, and possibly three if you count the immigration policy macro risk that Flywire itself flagged as a ~$30M revenue headwind.

The strongest bull case for Crebit centers on the wedge strategy: international student tuition is a boring, operationally essential payment flow where the payer (the student or their family) is highly price-sensitive and the incumbent markup (2-10% via traditional rails) creates real economic pain. If Crebit can lock in university portal integrations before Stripe or Flywire adopt stablecoin settlement natively, switching costs could compound. Jensen's blockchain credentials -- Lendora acquired by BankSocial, MLH Top 50 out of 600,000 hackers, MIT EECS -- are genuinely impressive for a 19-year-old. And the university tuition payment market is the kind of boring-but-critical category I've historically found attractive in companies like Gusto and Checkr. The problem is that those companies built deep workflow integration that created daily dependency; tuition payments happen one to four times per year, limiting the retention flywheel. For this bull case to work, Crebit would need to expand beyond tuition into the broader international student financial life -- rent, living expenses, remittances -- turning a low-frequency wedge into a high-frequency platform. I don't see evidence they're building toward that.

Jensen and Simmi are clearly talented young builders, but this is a regulated, multi-jurisdiction financial services business that demands operator maturity neither has demonstrated. Jensen's prior project Lendora was acquired by BankSocial, which is a meaningful signal, but building a hackathon project that gets acqui-hired is categorically different from navigating money transmission licensing across seven countries while selling to university CFOs. No advisors or board members with fintech regulatory experience were found in the dossier, which compounds the concern. The margin architecture is unclear -- no pricing page exists, and if Crebit is pricing below Flywire's 2-3% and below banks' 2-10% markups, the per-transaction margin on a stablecoin-mediated payment is likely thin, especially after compliance costs.

My verdict is Pass. The two dimensions that lock this are Nonobvious Market Timing and Infrastructure-Layer Positioning. The GENIUS Act is a real catalyst, but when Stripe, Mastercard, and Brex have all moved on the same thesis, the window is visible to everyone -- and at that point, the infrastructure players have the advantage, not the application-layer startup. Crebit is building on contested infrastructure rather than owning the infrastructure layer itself, which means its cost advantage can be replicated by any payment company that adds stablecoin settlement to their existing university relationships. If Crebit were building the compliance and licensing infrastructure that *enables* other companies to offer stablecoin payments -- the regulated API layer, not the end-user application -- I'd score this very differently.
