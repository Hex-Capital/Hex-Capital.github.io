# Carson -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I notice first about Carson: their tagline is "more capable than Cowork, more secure than OpenClaw." They are literally defining themselves against products backed by Anthropic and OpenAI — two organizations that have raised a combined $30 billion or more. That's not the kind of positioning I associate with outsider founders who've found something nobody else sees. That's a comparison chart. It tells me these founders are reading the same market maps everyone else is reading and slotting themselves into a gap that is visible to every enterprise SaaS investor in San Francisco right now. The most promising companies I've backed — SpaceX, Crusoe, Contraline — were not positioning against category leaders. They were creating categories that didn't exist yet, in spaces nobody else wanted to enter.

When I evaluate founders, I always start with their story — not the business, not the market, the human story of how they arrived at this specific problem. And what I see here are four talented people with elite credentials — Harvard, Stanford, Palantir, Snowflake, Robust Intelligence — who have assembled themselves into a logical founding team for a logical company in a hot market. What I don't see is the biographical thread that makes any of them the person who *has* to build this. Nobody on this team came from sales operations. Nobody struggled with the broken workflows they're trying to fix. Nobody arrived at this problem through a path that only they could have walked. This is a team that identified a market gap through analysis, not through lived pain. I respect the analysis — it's smart — but it's not the signal that activates my conviction.

The bull case here is real, and I want to be honest about it. Milan Bhandari is a repeat YC founder whose last company Bolto reached over a million in revenue and raised $5.6 million. That is genuine execution signal at pre-seed — most first-time founders can't claim that. Ketan Agrawal worked at Robust Intelligence, which was an AI security company acquired by Cisco, making him potentially the most domain-relevant person on the team for what Carson claims to differentiate on. The Palantir DNA across the founding team means they understand enterprise procurement, security requirements, and the patience required for long sales cycles. If the thesis is "the enterprise security gap in AI agents is a real moat, and this team can fill it before Anthropic and OpenAI bother to," then I can see a path. Palantir itself won government contracts not by having the best AI but by having the best compliance and security posture — and these founders would know that playbook intimately.

But I keep returning to what this company is actually doing in the context of its competitive landscape. Clay has raised $210 million and has $100 million in ARR. Artisan raised $46 million. 11x raised $230 million. Salesforce is building Agentforce. Every well-funded AI startup and every CRM incumbent is converging on exactly this space — enterprise AI agents for sales workflows. This is consensus investing territory. When I backed Uber, nobody wanted to fund ride-sharing because it was illegal. When I invested in Contraline, other VCs physically recoiled at male contraception. When Crusoe pitched mining Bitcoin on gas flares, people thought it was a joke. Carson is building an AI agent platform for sales teams with enterprise security features. That is the single most popular category in B2B SaaS right now, and the "enterprise security" wedge, while smart, is exactly what anyone would predict as the next iteration. There's no moment here where I feel that jolt of energy, that sense of having stumbled onto something the rest of the market is too scared or too conventional to see.

I also have an unresolved question about the CTO's transition from Bolto, which is still active with its other co-founders and had real traction. After what happened with HQ Trivia, I've learned to ask hard questions about founder dynamics — why someone leaves a company that's working, how the split happened, whether there are unresolved obligations or tensions. Four co-founders is already a lot of alignment to maintain, and having one whose recent history includes departing a funded, revenue-generating startup introduces uncertainty I'd want resolved before writing a check. The shared Harvard and Palantir backgrounds among three of the four founders are a positive signal for baseline trust, but I've learned that shared pedigree doesn't substitute for tested conflict resolution.

This is a competent team building a reasonable product in a crowded, well-funded, highly visible market. For an investor who evaluates on market sizing, competitive positioning, and team credentials, this might score well. But that's not how I invest. I'm looking for the founders that other people have overlooked, building things that other people think are strange, in spaces that other people find uncomfortable. Carson is none of those things. It's a logical bet, and logical bets aren't what I write first checks into.
