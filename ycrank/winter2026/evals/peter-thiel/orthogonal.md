# Orthogonal -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The most revealing fact about Orthogonal is not what the founders are building but who else is building it simultaneously. Stripe -- a company I backed at Series A precisely because the Collisons had identified a genuine secret about developer payments -- has already launched its x402 protocol and Agentic Commerce Protocol, processing roughly 50 million transactions. Nevermined raised $4 million. Nightmarket exists. Prava raised a seed. Mastercard announced Agent Pay. When I see five companies and an incumbent converging on identical territory within the same 18-month window, I am not looking at a market opportunity. I am looking at a Girardian cascade -- companies imitating each other into a space where no one holds a defensible position. The defining characteristic of a secret is that most intelligent people disagree with you. "AI agents will need to pay for APIs" is a thesis that Stripe, Coinbase, Mastercard, and a half-dozen startups all agree on. That is not a secret. That is a consensus.

The specific positioning -- a curated API marketplace with unified billing, abstracted away from blockchain settlement -- is an implementation preference, not a contrarian insight about the world. Orthogonal is essentially arguing that the right abstraction layer is "fiat-denominated, curated, off-chain" rather than "crypto-native, permissionless, on-chain." This may even be correct as a product design choice. But a correct product design choice in a crowded market is not a monopoly. It is a feature that Stripe can ship in a quarter. The core technology -- MCP server integration, API key aggregation, usage-based billing -- is openly standardized and technically straightforward. Nothing here is 10x better than what exists; it is a convenience wrapper. Convenience wrappers attract competition because they are easy to build and easy to replicate.

The founders bring relevant domain experience -- Pickett from Coinbase payments and Vercel billing, Sogut from Google's API infrastructure and competitive programming at the ICPC world level. These are competent engineers who understand both sides of the product surface. But competence in a competitive market is not sufficient. I look for founders who hold a vision the world actively resists, who can articulate exactly why the consensus is wrong and what the future looks like when they win. Nothing in the dossier suggests that level of conviction. The strategy reads as responsive -- "agents need API access, let's aggregate it" -- rather than definite. There is no visible theory of why Orthogonal, specifically, becomes the last mover in this space. No articulation of what structural advantage compounds over time to make this position unassailable.

The strongest bull case requires believing three things simultaneously: that Stripe's on-chain approach permanently alienates mainstream developers, that the curated YC-network marketplace achieves sufficient density before any competitor, and that the two-sided network effects of 16 API integrations compound into a defensible moat before Stripe simply adds an off-chain billing option. The first premise is fragile -- Stripe has historically excelled at abstracting complexity away from developers, and nothing prevents them from offering fiat-denominated agent billing alongside x402. The second premise -- YC network as a supply-side moat -- has some merit as a beachhead strategy, but YC companies are also the most likely to integrate with Stripe directly. The third premise asks me to believe that 16 integrations create meaningful switching costs, which strains credulity. If this bull case were true -- if a curated API marketplace for agents really did exhibit winner-take-all dynamics -- then the winner would almost certainly be Stripe extending its existing two-sided network of millions of merchants and developers, not a two-person pre-seed startup.

I have seen this pattern before. A real technological shift creates genuine demand -- and then dozens of startups rush to serve that demand in structurally similar ways, destroying each other's margins. The agentic payments space in 2026 looks like food delivery in 2014: obvious consumer need, multiple funded players, no proprietary technology, weak network effects, and an incumbent (Stripe, in this case) with overwhelming distribution advantages. The founders are not wrong that agents will need to pay for APIs. They are wrong -- or at least have not demonstrated -- that this insight gives them any monopoly power whatsoever.
