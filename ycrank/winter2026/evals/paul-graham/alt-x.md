# Alt-X -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Ryan Samadi sat in the chair. He was the analyst at Citadel copying numbers from PDFs into Excel models. He was the guy at BofA and IIFG rebuilding spreadsheets cell by cell. He ran a student hedge fund at Stanford. When he says analysts spend 30-50 hours per deal on manual spreadsheet work, he's describing his own life. That's the signal I always start with -- did the founder stumble into this problem or sit down and brainstorm it? Samadi stumbled into it, which means the problem is real and the motivation runs deeper than "AI is hot, let's apply it to finance." This passes my organic test.

But here's where it gets complicated. The schlep in alternative-asset underwriting is real -- understanding cap rates vs. MOIC vs. IRR, mapping unstructured CIM data into auditable models, satisfying IC approval processes that demand cell-level transparency. That's genuinely tedious work that a generic Excel AI tool won't nail without deep domain knowledge. The problem is that this particular schlep isn't the kind that creates lasting structural barriers. Stripe's schlep was dealing with banks, fraud systems, and payment regulations -- institutional relationships and regulatory approvals that take years to build and that physically prevent fast followers. Alt-X's schlep is mostly about training extraction models on financial documents and building the right templates. That's hard work, but it's the kind of hard work that well-funded competitors can replicate in months, not years. And Alt-X faces competitors with 50-150x more capital: Endex at $14M with OpenAI backing, Shortcut at $78M+ with Sequoia. When the schlep is primarily technical rather than institutional, capital advantages compound fast.

The co-founder situation concerns me. I don't see any public evidence that Samadi and Wachsman knew each other before starting this company. That's one of my most reliable red flags. I've seen it fail too many times -- in one YC batch, nine companies added co-founders they didn't know well, and all nine fell apart within a year. Maybe they have a deep friendship I can't see in the public record. But the CTO's prior roles aren't even independently confirmed as belonging to the same person -- the dossier flags "multiple individuals share this name." When I can't verify who the CTO is or how the co-founders connected, my confidence drops significantly.

The strongest bull case would go like this: Endex and Shortcut are horizontal Excel AI tools. They're trying to be good at everything -- DCF models, FP&A, general spreadsheet automation. Alt-X is vertical, specifically targeting alternative-asset underwriting where the KPI vocabulary and document structures are specialized. Horizontal tools always look more powerful, but vertical tools often win in domains where the details matter enough that customers can tell the difference. And in institutional finance, the details always matter -- an IC memo with incorrect cap rate extraction kills deals. If Alt-X goes narrow and deep on real estate and PE underwriting, builds the best extraction models for those specific document types, and grows bottom-up through the $30/month analyst seat, they could own the niche before Endex or Shortcut get around to specializing. That's a real path. The $30/month price point is smart for bottom-up adoption -- it's below procurement thresholds, so individual analysts can just start using it without approval committees. That's essentially the Stripe playbook: start with the individual developer (here, analyst), then expand to the organization.

But I'm not fully convinced. The absence of visible traction beyond vague "piloting with family offices" is notable. No named customers. No revenue figures. No user counts. No evidence of organic sharing or word-of-mouth. The website couldn't even be rendered at research time. At pre-seed I don't penalize companies for being early, but I do want at least one signal that someone outside the building cares -- a specific analyst who says "I can't live without this," a named fund that signed an LOI, anything concrete. And the pricing question nags at me: $30/month in a world where these analysts' time costs their firms $200/hour suggests either the founders haven't yet discovered how much value they're creating, or the value isn't as large as the pitch implies. Either way, pricing for institutional finance tools at consumer SaaS levels is unusual enough to require explanation.

The organic discovery is genuine and that counts for a lot in my framework. Samadi is the right person to build this. But genuine problem discovery alone isn't enough when the competitive landscape is this crowded, the technical moat is this thin, and the founding team's cohesion is this uncertain. I'd want to see two things before investing: evidence that the co-founders have a real relationship, and at least one paying customer who can articulate why Alt-X is meaningfully better than doing it manually or using Endex. Without those, this is a competent team in a real market that I can't differentiate from several other bets in the same space.
