# Andustry -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Andustry is who built it. Johann Sturken spent four years at McKinsey and Airbus before starting this company. I've seen this profile hundreds of times in YC interviews — a consultant identifies a "massive market opportunity," builds a deck, and applies. My skepticism here is structural, not personal. When someone tells me they spent years in strategy consulting and then identified a procurement inefficiency, what I hear is that they applied frameworks to find an idea rather than stumbling into a problem that wouldn't let them sleep. The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas. It's to look for problems you have yourself. And consultants are professionally trained to look for other people's problems through structured analysis — which is precisely the wrong muscle for founding a startup.

But there's a wrinkle that complicates the clean narrative: Johann grew up in a family that trades industrial goods. That's not McKinsey discovery — that's dinner table discovery. He watched his family navigate the exact mess Andustry is trying to automate. This is the kind of organic exposure that can't be faked. When I invested in Stripe, part of what I trusted was that Patrick had been programming since he was a kid and had viscerally experienced the pain of accepting payments online. Johann's family background is a weaker version of the same signal — he didn't personally suffer through industrial procurement as a buyer, but he grew up adjacent to it. The question is whether adjacency is close enough. I think it earns partial credit. He knows the problem is real because he watched it from the inside, even if his path to solving it ran through McKinsey rather than a garage.

What concerns me more than the origin story is the absence of a clear technical builder. Neither founder has a GitHub presence. For a company whose entire value proposition is an AI-agent platform — Smart Supplier Search, Automated Outreach, Smart RFQ Processing — someone needs to be writing the code, and I can't tell who. Het Dave's background at Aries Global doing SaaS-to-AI transformation is the closest thing to a technical signal, but it's thin. One of my hard requirements is that the founding team includes someone who can build the product themselves. Iteration speed in the first year is everything, and outsourced or hired engineering at this stage means you're translating between the person who understands the problem and the person who writes the software. That translation layer kills startups.

The idea itself is my biggest concern. "AI to automate industrial procurement" sounds good to everyone. I mean everyone — LightSource Labs raised $33M doing something adjacent, Matchory raised €6M, Cavela raised $8.6M, MESH Works has processed $884M in RFQs. When I can list five funded competitors off the dossier alone, the idea has already been competed to the point where returns depend on execution advantages, not insight advantages. My entire framework in "Black Swan Farming" is built on the premise that the best investments initially look wrong to most people. This one looks right to most people. That's the tell. The commoditization risk section even states the obvious: "LLM-based RFQ generation and supplier matching are reproducible." If your core technology is reproducible and your competitors have 10-50x your funding, you need something extraordinary in the founders to overcome that gap. I don't see evidence of that extraordinary quality here.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. If the 500K+ supplier database turns out to be genuinely proprietary — verified through direct relationships rather than scraped from public directories — that's a real asset that compounds over time. Industrial procurement is authentically one of the least digitized large markets, and the EUR 1M+ in beta transaction volume suggests real transactions are flowing through the platform, not just demos. The Munich location gives them access to German Mittelstand companies, which are exactly the mid-size industrial buyers who still source machinery through trade shows and phone calls. If Andustry can become the default procurement layer for German industrial SMEs before the well-funded competitors expand from their current niches, the two-sided marketplace dynamics could create a durable position. This is a scenario where Johann's family network and domain credibility would matter enormously — he can walk into those conversations in a way that a Silicon Valley AI startup cannot. For this to work, though, they'd need to demonstrate that their network density in specific industrial verticals is already creating switching costs, and I see no evidence of that yet.

I keep coming back to a pattern I've described before: the most dangerous startup profile is competent founders with a good-sounding idea in a crowded space. It's dangerous because it feels investable — the founders are smart, the market is real, the timing makes sense — but it lacks the asymmetric insight that produces outlier returns. Andustry has genuine domain proximity through Johann's family, which elevates it above the median "consultant finds market opportunity" pitch. But it doesn't have the contrarian edge, the technical obsessiveness, or the evidence of scrappy resourcefulness that I need to write a personal check. If these founders come back to me in a year with a story about how they personally called 500 suppliers in Bavaria, built their own classifier for industrial certifications because no API existed, and have 20 manufacturing companies that can't imagine sourcing without them — that's a different conversation.
