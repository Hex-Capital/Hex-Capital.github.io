# Gikl, Inc -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the structural problem with ScienceSwarm that jumped out at me before anything else: Google has already deployed its AI Co-Scientist across all 17 DOE National Labs and major research universities. OpenAI has publicly committed to building a fully automated researcher by September 2026. Sakana AI raised $379 million to automate end-to-end scientific paper generation at $15 per paper. When I look at a seed-stage company entering a space where the three most powerful AI organizations on earth are actively building and distributing free tools, I don't see a contrarian opportunity -- I see a company that needs to explain why it won't get vaporized. And the explanation here -- "we're the community layer, not the automation layer" -- is the exact kind of distinction that sounds defensible on a whiteboard but dissolves on contact with reality. Communities don't defend against free tools from incumbents with billions of users.

The founders are remarkably credentialed. Peter Vajda directed Media Generation at Meta for eleven years -- Emu, Movie Gen, the generative AI work that actually shipped to Instagram. Seiji Yamamoto was Senior Staff in Core Llama at Meta Superintelligence Labs, holds a physics Ph.D. from Rice, published in PNAS and Physical Review Letters, and co-authored with a Fields Medalist. Nine years of concurrent work at Meta. Stanford connections for both. On the "sane" axis, this is about as strong as it gets for AI founders. But that's exactly where my calibration breaks: these are sane people with an extremely sane idea. "AI for scientific discovery" is possibly the single most consensus AI thesis in 2026. Every major lab, every foundation, every AI-native startup in the space has converged on this narrative. The idea that AI can accelerate science is not insane -- it is the prevailing wisdom. And when the prevailing wisdom agrees, the returns are already priced in. The crowdsourced angle adds a wrinkle, but citizen science platforms have existed since Foldit in 2008, and none have achieved scale that would suggest a new market primitive is emerging.

My kill test here is straightforward: would this business be compelling if AI didn't exist? Without AI, ScienceSwarm is a forum where non-experts post approaches to unsolved scientific problems. That's essentially a subreddit. The AI IS the product -- it's what supposedly compresses twelve years of training into accessible contribution. But when AI is the product, you're building in the exact zone where incumbents with distribution will absorb your value proposition. Apple, Google, Meta -- they control the AI ecosystem. The tagline on the YC page draws an explicit analogy to coding assistants: "Coding assistants turned millions of people into software developers -- we're doing the same for science." From my perspective, that analogy actually argues against investing. Coding assistants are features being absorbed by Microsoft (GitHub Copilot), Google (Gemini), and every IDE. The same pattern will play out in science, faster, because the distribution channels are even more concentrated. Research institutions buy from Google and Microsoft, not from two-person startups.

The structural moat question is where this falls apart most completely. There's no physical infrastructure. No regulatory position. No proprietary data at launch. No protocol-layer lock-in. The stated defensibility is community network effects and accumulated contribution data -- but these are the softest moats in technology, and they're getting softer as AI makes it trivial to generate and synthesize contributions. The "built for AI agents" language on the site is revealing: if the platform is designed for AI agents to contribute alongside humans, what happens when Google's AI agents can do the same work without the platform? The community switching costs argument -- "reputation and history like Stack Overflow" -- ignores that Stack Overflow itself is being disrupted by AI chat. This is a software platform whose moat theory is already being disproven in the analogous domain they cite as precedent.

The strongest bull case for ScienceSwarm would require believing that crowdsourced human-AI collaboration on scientific problems creates a fundamentally different product category than either autonomous AI scientists or AI-augmented literature tools -- and that this category develops genuine protocol properties where the contribution format, verification framework, and problem taxonomy become standards that other institutions integrate with. If ScienceSwarm became the canonical layer through which citizen science contributions get validated and fed into the academic record, that would be a new primitive worth investing in. Yamamoto's legitimate scientific credibility -- not just AI engineering, but actual peer-reviewed physics at the highest level -- provides the kind of domain authority that could make this credible to the research community. That combination of genuine scientific credentials plus Meta-scale AI infrastructure experience is rare and real. But the dossier shows zero traction signals: no user counts, no community metrics, no press, no revenue, no even a waitlist number. At pre-seed I don't penalize absence of revenue, but I need some signal that the cold-start problem is tractable, and there's nothing here.

I keep coming back to the Birchbox lesson. Narrative magnitude without structural moat produces the worst possible venture outcome. "Democratize science for all of humanity" is a massive narrative -- genuinely infinite in scope. But the narrative is available to every competitor in this space, and the structural position to own it belongs to Google and OpenAI, not to a two-person pre-seed startup building a crowdsourced platform. The founders should be building something where their exceptional AI credentials are the "cherry on top" of a business with operational or physical-world complexity that software alone can't replicate. Instead, they're building in the exact center of the AI hype vortex, competing against the most well-funded organizations on earth. Pass.
