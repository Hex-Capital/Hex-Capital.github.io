# REV1 -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the competitive dynamics. DraftAid — a YC W24 company building the same core product — launched in August 2023, claims 210K+ drawings generated, and has $150K in paid pilot contracts. SolidWorks shipped a beta Auto-Generate Drawing command in its 2025/2026 release. Leo AI raised $9.7M for a broader mechanical engineering copilot. REV1 is entering a space where the structural moment has already been identified and acted upon by a direct competitor, the dominant incumbent, and a well-funded adjacent player. When I evaluate a market, I ask what has changed in the last 12-24 months that makes this possible now. For automated GD&T drawing generation, the answer — LLM and computer vision capabilities crossing the threshold for interpreting 3D geometry — is real. But it's not REV1's insight. DraftAid saw it two years earlier and has been executing against it. That's not a structural moment REV1 is uniquely positioned at; it's a validated market where they're a late entrant.

The "too boring" market archetype does apply here, and I take that seriously. Engineering drawing documentation is the kind of tedious, compliance-heavy workflow that most investors dismiss — similar in spirit to what Checkr did for background checks or Gusto for payroll. Mechanical engineers spending 25% of their time manually annotating drawings with 300+ pages of GD&T rules is a genuine pain point, and I've seen "boring but critical" infrastructure become large businesses. The regulatory dimension is interesting too: GD&T accuracy in aerospace and medical device manufacturing is safety-critical, which means a tool that achieves demonstrably higher annotation accuracy could build a quality moat that's hard to displace. But the market for 2D drawing automation is a sub-segment of a sub-segment of the $24B CAD market. There's no publicly available data on the SAM, and the dossier's TAM citation ($23.75B CAD market) is the wrong number entirely — REV1 doesn't replace CAD, it automates one downstream task within it. The addressable spend per engineering team is structurally capped unless REV1 expands well beyond drawings.

That expansion is the product-to-distribution question, and I don't see evidence the founders have thought about it. The dossier notes that expansion into BOM generation, tolerance stack-up analysis, and drawing change management "would be necessary but is unannounced." For a company to become a generational outcome in my framework, the initial product needs to create a customer relationship that becomes a distribution channel for additional products. Stripe's payment API distributed Atlas, Billing, and Treasury. Gusto's payroll distributed HR, benefits, and compliance. REV1's drawing automation tool, as described, is a point solution. The PLM synchronization feature hints at deeper integration, but there's no articulated vision for how this becomes a platform. A tool that saves engineers two hours per drawing is valuable; a tool that becomes the post-CAD automation layer for all manufacturing documentation is transformational. I need to hear the latter from the founders, and it's absent.

The bull case I'd want to believe: Alex Rivero's founder-market fit is genuinely strong. He's a mechanical engineer who made these drawings at Tesla (Cybertruck, Robotaxi) and Apple (iPhone 17), and he built a CNC router venture with seven prototypes. Louis Liu brings ML research and platform engineering from Tesla and AWS. They've lived inside this problem. The CAD-agnostic angle — serving companies that use SolidWorks, Siemens NX, and PTC Creo simultaneously — is structurally interesting because no single incumbent has incentive to support competitors' file formats. If REV1 accumulates proprietary training data from customer drawings and develops company-specific annotation standards that improve over time, that data flywheel could create switching costs that a SolidWorks bolt-on feature never will. And if they can prove that their GD&T accuracy exceeds what DraftAid and SolidWorks offer for safety-critical applications, accuracy-as-moat in regulated manufacturing is exactly the kind of compliance barrier I've invested through before.

But the plan as it stands requires multiple things to go right simultaneously: out-execute DraftAid despite a two-year deficit, stay ahead of SolidWorks' native AI features, build accuracy sufficient for aerospace and medical device tolerances, and expand beyond drawings into a broader platform — all while competing for the same YC-network customers, investors, and talent as DraftAid. That's approaching multiple miracles. The single miracle should be GD&T accuracy reaching production-grade reliability. But the competitive miracle — displacing an established direct competitor — is layered on top, and those compound. I'd want to see REV1 demonstrate something DraftAid fundamentally cannot do, or a distribution wedge that makes the competitive gap irrelevant, and neither is evident from what's public.
