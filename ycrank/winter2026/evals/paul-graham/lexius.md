# Lexius -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Lexius is that this problem is not hiding. Five well-funded competitors — Veesion at €53M, Everseen at ~$90M, Spot AI at $93M, Auror at NZ$82M — are already chasing AI-powered retail theft detection. Another startup, ArcadianAI, uses the identical tagline "AI Security Guards." This is the opposite of schlep blindness. When a problem has attracted over $300 million in venture funding across multiple companies, it is by definition visible. The question I always ask is: why hasn't someone done this already? Here, lots of people already have. That changes the nature of the bet entirely — from "can you solve a problem nobody else sees?" to "can you outrun well-capitalized competitors in a race everyone has already entered?"

The organic origin of this idea is unclear to me. David Elskamp studied computer science and data science, ran a media agency as a teenager, and is described as a "2x founder." Liam Webster researched AI and privacy at Berkeley's BLUES Lab. Neither appears to have worked in retail, loss prevention, or physical security. The first customer was a convenience store in Enschede — the same city as Elskamp's university — which suggests a local opportunistic pilot rather than years of lived frustration with the problem. When I look for the moment where the founder personally encountered the pain and couldn't stop thinking about it — Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive, the Collisons cursing at payment APIs — I don't find it. This looks like two technically skilled people surveyed the landscape and identified where computer vision could create value. That's not a disqualifying origin story, but it's a weaker foundation than organic discovery because the motivation to endure years of retail sales grunt work has to come from somewhere other than personal obsession.

That said, the customer traction is genuinely impressive for a pre-seed company, and I don't want to dismiss it. 7-Eleven, Erewhon, and Prada are real brands. FineFare reporting a 63% theft reduction in 30 days, Erewhon reporting $3,000 in monthly savings per store — these are specific, quantified results that suggest the product actually works. A waitlist of "retailers managing over 4,000 stores" accumulated without marketing spend. This is evidence of wanting. The ROI is concrete enough that a store manager can calculate the payback period on a napkin, which is exactly the kind of demand signal that sustains itself. If these are genuine paying relationships and not free pilots dressed up as case studies, this is top-decile traction for pre-seed. The sub-10-minute remote install is the distribution insight — it's a version of the Collison installation where you remove every barrier between interest and deployment. That matters in enterprise sales, where a 6-month integration cycle kills momentum.

The technical team is credible. Webster's EECS degree from Berkeley and his AI/privacy research directly map to the core technical challenge — building CV models that detect behavior without facial recognition. His PoliPY project on privacy policy analysis and his work at the BLUES Lab on privacy-preserving AI aren't accidents; they're the intellectual foundation for Lexius's no-biometrics approach, which becomes more valuable as the EU AI Act tightens surveillance regulation. Elskamp founding a six-figure media agency at 14 with clients like Adidas demonstrates a precocious ability to sell to large organizations — a skill that plausibly explains how a pre-seed team landed 7-Eleven and Prada. These aren't kids who need to learn how to talk to enterprises.

The bull case here is straightforward: retail shrinkage is a $112 billion and growing problem, the software-only deployment model creates a wedge that heavier competitors can't easily replicate, and the cross-store offender database becomes a genuine network effect moat at scale. If Lexius can grow faster than Veesion and Everseen because frictionless install compresses sales cycles from months to minutes, they could own the US mid-market before better-capitalized competitors consolidate it. The founders' technical skill combined with Elskamp's sales instincts could produce exactly the kind of product-led growth that wins land grabs. But this requires believing that distribution speed beats funding advantage in a category where multiple companies are already at thousands of deployments. I'm not convinced that's true. When the competitive moat is primarily "we're easier to install," incumbents can narrow that gap with a single engineering sprint.

This is a competent team building a product that clearly works, in a market with genuine and growing pain. But it lacks the thing I weight most heavily: the organic founder-problem connection that produces the irrational persistence needed to win a crowded market over a decade. In a space with no competitors, technical skill and fast deployment would be enough. In a space with $300M+ of funded competition, you need founders who are fighting for something deeper than a market opportunity. I'd want to see evidence that this team has a specific insight or advantage that the five better-funded competitors structurally cannot replicate. The privacy-first approach and frictionless install are real differentiators, but they're replicable differentiators. I'd watch this company closely, but my own check would wait for evidence that they're winning against Veesion and Everseen in direct competitive deals.
