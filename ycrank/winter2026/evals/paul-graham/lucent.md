# Lucent -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the pivot. Lucent raised $1.3M in October 2025 for "behavioural datasets for browser agents" -- training data for AI to navigate the web. Somewhere between that raise and the W26 batch, the company became "AI that automatically watches your session replays to detect bugs." These are different products solving different problems for different buyers. When I see a pivot this early, the question isn't whether pivots are good (they can be -- Justin.tv became Twitch), it's whether the new idea was discovered organically or manufactured under pressure. The dossier gives me no evidence that Alisa Wu was ever drowning in unwatched session replays. She was an engineer at an ad creative platform and built an edtech tutoring app. The new idea reads like it was generated from proximity to the browser agent space rather than from personal frustration with the session replay problem itself. The rrweb fork on GitHub tells me she's been exploring the technical surface area, but exploring a technology is different from living with a problem.

The deeper issue is the schlep test, and Lucent fails it in a way that worries me. "Apply AI to session replay data" is not a problem hiding in plain sight because people unconsciously avoid the pain. It's a problem that every product manager at FullStory, Sentry, LogRocket, and PostHog has already thought about. The reason nobody has shipped a great AI session watcher yet isn't schlep blindness -- it's that the AI capabilities to do it well only recently became available, and the incumbents are working on it too. When Stripe launched, no one wanted to deal with the banks and the fraud and the regulation. That institutional ugliness was the moat. What's the equivalent here? There's no regulatory complexity, no logistical nightmare, no institutional schlep. The challenge is purely technical -- building models that reliably identify bugs from DOM and visual data -- and that's the kind of problem every well-funded competitor with existing session data can throw engineers at. Sentry has $100M in revenue. PostHog is open source with a passionate community. This is a feature these companies will ship, not a product they'll unconsciously avoid building.

I want to be fair about what's genuinely impressive here. Alisa Wu is 22 and has already built and exited a company (Stella AI, acquired within nine months), worked as a founding engineer at a startup that Canva acquired, and raised $1.3M in 36 hours. She relocated from Sydney to San Francisco. These are not the actions of someone who lacks determination. Two exits before 23, even small ones, put her ahead of most founders I see. The 36-hour raise suggests she's persuasive and moves fast. If I were evaluating the founder independent of this specific idea, I'd be interested. The problem is that founder energy needs to be pointed at the right problem, and I don't see the founder-problem fit here that I'd need to get excited.

The bull case would require believing three things simultaneously: that Wu discovered genuine insight about session replay analysis during her browser agent work that incumbents haven't grasped, that a two-person team can build AI models sophisticated enough to outperform what FullStory and Sentry will ship with 100x the engineering resources, and that the product can acquire enough customers and build enough of a data moat before the incumbents absorb this feature. If all three are true, there's a real company here -- the problem of unwatched sessions is real, and the first team to make automated analysis actually reliable will create value. But each of these requires its own miracle, and I've written about why startups that need multiple miracles to succeed are bad bets. The comment that she used ChatGPT as her "Chief Technology Officer" during fundraising also gives me pause for a product that demands deep ML expertise to work at all.

I'd watch Alisa Wu closely. She's the kind of founder who figures things out and ships fast, which is rare at any age. But this particular idea -- AI analysis of session replays in a market where the incumbents already own the data and the customer relationships -- isn't the kind of problem where being small and scrappy is an advantage. The best version of this company might not be the current one, and if she pivots again into something with more genuine schlep and organic founder-problem fit, I'd want to look again.
