# Asendia AI -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The pivot is the first thing I notice, and it tells me more than any product feature list. Asendia launched as a B2C interview coaching tool -- "Sarah, your AI Job Coach" -- ran two Product Hunt campaigns in late 2024 and mid-2025, and has now repositioned as a B2B agentic recruitment platform for staffing agencies. Every public traction metric in the dossier (286 upvotes, 15K interview prep minutes, 53.5K website visits) belongs to the prior product. The current B2B product has zero verified customer evidence. I'm not inherently anti-pivot -- many great companies found their market on the second or third try -- but I need to evaluate the current business on its current evidence, and right now there's a blank page where B2B traction should be.

The market structure timing is where this falls apart for me. AI-powered recruiting is one of the most obvious application categories in the current AI wave. hireEZ has $42.6M in revenue. Paradox raised over $250M before Workday acquired them. Humanly bought three companies in 2025. Fetcher raised $40M. Every AI application pitch deck I see has a "recruiting" variant. Compare this to when I wrote checks for Coinbase -- crypto infrastructure was considered unserious by most of Sand Hill Road -- or when I backed Perplexity before anyone believed AI could challenge Google's search moat. Those were structural shifts that consensus actively dismissed. AI recruiting is a structural shift that consensus has been funding aggressively for three years. The Paradox/Workday acquisition is real and does create a gap, but it simultaneously signals that the dominant HCM platforms are absorbing this capability natively. When Workday integrates Paradox's conversational AI into its core product, that compresses the standalone opportunity rather than expanding it.

The infrastructure-layer test also fails here. Asendia integrates with 50+ ATS systems -- Bullhorn, Greenhouse, Lever, Ashby -- which means it sits on top of existing infrastructure rather than becoming infrastructure itself. Bullhorn is the dominant ATS for staffing agencies. If Bullhorn decides AI screening and sourcing agents are core to their platform (and Workday's Paradox acquisition gives them every reason to), Asendia gets disintermediated by the infrastructure layer it depends on. This is the exact pattern I warn about: building an application on a contested platform where the platform owner has both the incentive and distribution to absorb your functionality. Stripe became the payments API that thousands of companies built on. Asendia is building on APIs that someone else owns.

The strongest bull case requires three things to be true simultaneously: that staffing agencies are a sufficiently distinct vertical that horizontal AI recruiting tools can't serve them, that Bullhorn and other ATS incumbents will leave the AI layer unbundled long enough for Asendia to build defensible switching costs, and that the team can execute a B2B enterprise sales motion with no prior B2B GTM track record. That's more than one miracle. The technology miracle -- LLMs crossing a threshold for real-time voice and video screening -- is happening exogenously, which is favorable. But the distribution pivot from B2C to staffing agency sales, combined with the competitive defense against incumbents who own the workflow, represents compounding odds that I find uncomfortable. If I'm being generous, there's a world where the 50+ ATS integrations and compliance certifications create enough friction that agencies stick with Asendia once embedded. But the compliance claims (SOC 2, GDPR, HIPAA) from a 3-person team founded in 2024 warrant verification -- those certifications typically require significant investment and third-party audits that seem implausible at this stage and team size.

On the founder side, I see competent technical professionals rather than operator-scalers. Rihab's year at Google Cloud and time at Microsoft are legitimate enterprise credentials, and Badis's six years at Infineon provide engineering depth. They've been working together since at least 2019. But neither has operated inside a company during a hypergrowth phase -- the kind of 50-to-500 scaling experience that predicts whether a founder can navigate the transition from building product to building organization. Neither has direct staffing agency operational experience. The prior B2C product shows they can ship, but shipping a B2C Product Hunt launch and selling six-figure contracts to staffing agencies are fundamentally different muscle groups. Finally, the TAM gives me pause: a $656M AI recruitment market growing at 7.2% CAGR -- a modest growth rate for an AI category -- with the staffing agency sub-segment being a fraction of that total. Even in a strong execution scenario, the market ceiling may not support the kind of 10x-plus upside I require to justify deploying capital.
