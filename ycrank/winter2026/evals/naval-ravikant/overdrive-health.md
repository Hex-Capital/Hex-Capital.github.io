# Overdrive Health -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The most interesting thing about Overdrive Health isn't the AI and isn't the billing. It's the acquisition. These founders bought a traditional ambulance billing agency -- ~20 providers, $50M+ in claims -- and are deploying AI agents to compress the labor out of it. This is not a software company selling picks and shovels. It's an operating company using AI as internal leverage. That distinction matters because it determines the entire leverage architecture: instead of multiplying their output through code that serves millions, they're improving unit economics within a services business. Revenue per FTE doubled. That's meaningful operationally, but it's not the kind of nonlinear leverage where one engineer's work serves a million users. It's margin expansion on a labor base. When I backed Notion, one codebase served millions of knowledge workers. Here, each new ambulance provider likely requires onboarding, integration, and ongoing operational attention. The leverage ratio is better than traditional billing -- but it's not qualitatively different.

The founders' specific knowledge is real and relevant, but not the kind that took a decade of obsessive curiosity to accumulate. Both Schroeder and Inge built AI agents at EliseAI, scaling it from $35M to north of $100M ARR, with direct exposure to healthcare automation as EliseAI expanded into that vertical. Schroeder also worked at Up&Up, a vertically integrated property manager -- so the acquire-and-operate playbook is familiar to him. Inge has a Harvard CS dual degree and built trading systems at Jane Street. These are strong engineering backgrounds with directly applicable AI agent experience. What I don't see is the kind of deep, non-transferable domain knowledge in medical billing specifically -- the years of immersion that produce insights no one else has. They're applying a general AI-agent deployment skill (learned at EliseAI) to a new vertical (ambulance billing). That's a valid strategy, but it means their competitive advantage is execution speed, not proprietary understanding. Any competent AI engineering team that spent six months studying EMS billing workflows could reach similar conclusions about where to deploy automation.

The consensus problem is significant. "AI will automate medical billing" is not a contrarian bet -- it's a thesis with $400M+ already deployed against it across AKASA, Candid Health, and CodaMetrix. Within the specific EMS vertical, two other YC companies (Amby Health W25, CareSwift S25) are pursuing the same customer segment. The full-stack services approach is somewhat differentiated from the SaaS copilot model, but "acquire a billing agency and deploy AI" is replicable by anyone with capital and engineering talent. The fact that incumbents face business model tension -- deploying AI cannibalizes their labor-based revenue -- creates a real window, but that window is visible to every AI investor in healthcare. Nobody smart is dismissing this idea. They're racing toward it.

The strongest bull case: this could be a capital-efficient rollup of a fragmented $4B+ market. Traditional ambulance billing agencies are probably cheap to acquire, and if AI genuinely doubles revenue per FTE, the post-acquisition margin expansion funds the next acquisition. Roll up fifty agencies, automate all of them, and you're processing billions in claims with a fraction of the traditional workforce. The EliseAI playbook -- operational AI agents in regulated, phone-heavy industries -- is battle-tested and directly transferable. If the founders execute well, the operational moat (client relationships, claims data, payer-specific patterns) compounds with each acquisition. The EMS market's fragmentation and the incumbents' AI-deployment paralysis create a genuine opening. This is a business that could work. The question is whether it compounds in a way that produces asymmetric returns, or whether it builds linearly toward a solid but bounded outcome.

For my framework, the answer is clear enough. The technology doesn't compound distinctively -- the AI capabilities (voice agents, claims processing, denial management) are built on generally available LLMs, and the data advantage from $50M in claims is modest at this stage. The leverage architecture is services-augmented-by-AI, not code-as-infinite-leverage. The timing is consensus, not contrarian. The founders are capable engineers applying proven skills to a new vertical, not domain obsessives who've discovered something the market doesn't yet believe. I'd expect this to produce a workable business -- potentially a good one -- but not the kind of asymmetric, leverage-compounding outcome that justifies angel capital. Competent team, real market, wrong leverage structure for my portfolio.
