# RefortifAI -- Paul Graham Primary Source Research: Structured Quote Record Evaluation

The first thing I notice about RefortifAI is that they've walked straight into a problem most founders' unconscious wouldn't let them see. "Your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps," and building a drop-in replacement for vLLM and SGLang that also obfuscates model weights while preserving inference performance is about as schlepy as it gets. This is the same pattern that made Stripe sit unsolved for a decade — "a lot more intimidating to start a startup like this than a recipe site." The "anti-cheat for AI models" framing is clever, because it signals the founders understand this is a cat-and-mouse game requiring continuous engineering investment, not a one-time product you ship and forget. That's honest framing. Most founders in security oversell the fortress; these founders are telling you it's an arms race. That kind of intellectual honesty is a data point.

The team composition is genuinely interesting for this specific problem. You have a CTO who did RTL digital design at Texas Instruments and interned at Tenstorrent doing CPU architecture — the kind of person who thinks at the register-transfer level about how hardware actually executes instructions. His GitHub confirms this: an operating system written in i386 Assembly, a 6502 emulator in C. These aren't weekend tutorial projects; they're the work of someone who understands machines at the metal. Pair that with a COO who did security research under Somesh Jha at UW-Madison — Jha is one of the serious people in adversarial ML and systems security — and a CEO with AI engineering at KLA (semiconductor inspection, where inference speed on edge hardware actually matters), and you have a team whose collective domain expertise maps unusually well onto "build a hardened runtime that resists weight extraction across GPU architectures." "The component of entrepreneurship that really matters is domain expertise," and this team has the right kind.

But here's what gives me pause: I have zero signal on determination. Technical credentials tell me they can build the thing. They don't tell me what happens when a big customer says no, when the first obfuscation scheme gets cracked by a researcher posting on arXiv, when they're three months from running out of money. "The most important predictor of success is determination," and the dossier gives me nothing to evaluate it. No prior startup. No evidence of doing things that don't scale. No sign of the cockroach energy that made the Airbnbs sell $40 cereal boxes. The CTO's low-level projects show obsessive technical depth — he might pass the animal test on the engineering side — but I can't tell if any of these three would sell cereal to make rent. "Good people can fix bad ideas, but good ideas can't save bad people," and I simply don't have enough data on the people beyond their resumes.

The structural concern is the one that would keep me up at night: software-only DRM has been broken in every adjacent domain. DVD CSS lasted about three years. Game anti-tamper systems like Denuvo typically get cracked within weeks of major releases. Media DRM was a multi-decade, multi-billion-dollar effort that mostly failed. When your adversary has physical access to the hardware running inference, memory inspection is always theoretically possible. The founders seem to understand this — the "anti-cheat" framing implicitly acknowledges it's an ongoing arms race, not an unbreakable vault. But can a three-person startup sustain an arms race against motivated adversaries who might include nation-state actors interested in extracting frontier model weights? That's a legitimate question with no good answer at this stage.

The bull case is real, though, and I want to lay it out honestly. First, DRM doesn't need to be perfect — it needs to make stealing more expensive than licensing, and for many enterprise deployments, even modest protection changes the calculus. Second, the "drop-in replacement" wedge is genuinely smart. If you're an AI company already running vLLM and you want to start licensing your model for on-premise deployment, the friction of adding RefortifAI's runtime is low. That's the kind of distribution insight I like — "you can't wait for users to come to you," but you can make it trivially easy for them to adopt you. Third, the adjacent M&A validates the budget: Palo Alto paid $500M+ for Protect AI, F5 bought CalypsoAI — enterprises are spending on AI security now, and weight protection is the obvious gap in the current tooling landscape. If RefortifAI can get even a handful of model providers dependent on their runtime, they'd be an attractive acquisition target for exactly those incumbents. The question is whether "attractive acquisition target" is the same as "a company designed to grow fast," and I'm not sure it is.

What I keep coming back to is: this is an idea I'd want to see executed by founders I've watched operate under pressure. The technical ingredients are right. The market timing is plausible. The schlep factor means they won't have much competition from people who find the problem too intimidating. But "if you can't convince a friend to join, that's concerning" — three friends founding together is fine, but I have no signal on the quality of that relationship under stress. No signal on traction means no signal on whether they're making something people want, and that's the test that matters above all others: "If you make something users want, you'll probably be fine, whatever else you do or don't do. If you don't make something users want, then you're dead." I'd take the meeting. I'd watch them closely through the YC batch. But today, from the dossier alone, I don't have enough conviction to write a check with my own money.
