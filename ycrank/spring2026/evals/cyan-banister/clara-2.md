# Clara -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

$100M+ in annualized revenue at Circle Medical. That number stops me. George Favvas, Zeeshan Ahmed, and Caitlin Swift didn't just work in digital primary care — they built a company together, scaled it for a decade, and are now reuniting to build the AI-native version. I know the power of teams that come back together after the first war. When I followed the thread from Postmates to Serve Robotics, I was betting on operators who had already proven they could survive the domain and were now building the next evolution. Clara carries that same portfolio-thread energy — the Circle Medical playbook for clinical hiring, compliance, state licensing, EHR integration is not something you can download from a pitch deck template. Favvas has done the unglamorous work of scaling a healthcare practice across regulatory boundaries, and Swift operationalized the clinical workflows that make human-in-the-loop medicine actually function at scale. Ahmed designed the EHR system used by 200+ providers and cut patient-detail entry time by 25%. These credentials are real. But here's where I pause: when I ask "tell me their story, not the business," what I hear from Clara's founders is "we built a successful healthcare company and saw the AI opportunity." That's domain mastery, not biographical forge. I don't know what happened to Favvas that made primary care find *him* — I know he found a market. The difference matters to me because when the company nearly dies, and in healthcare it always nearly dies, the founder who is solving their own wound fights differently than the founder who identified a gap.

Counsel Health has raised $36M from a16z and Google Ventures and already reports 100,000+ members with a near-identical AI-plus-physician model. Galileo has $108M+ and operates across 50 states. Forward Health raised $650M and shut down in November 2024. Clara enters this landscape at pre-seed against competitors with 70-200x more capital, in a space where every major venture firm is actively hunting for the winner. When I look at this through my contrarian lens — would this idea make a room of sophisticated VCs shift uncomfortably? — the answer is the opposite. AI healthcare is the standing ovation at every partner meeting in 2026. Nobody needs courage to articulate this thesis at a dinner party. My biggest wins came from spaces that made people *nervous* — Anduril in defense, Flock Safety in surveillance, Contraline in male birth control. Clara operates in a space where the competition for investor attention is fiercer than the competition for patients.

Clara does touch something real about invisible systems. Primary care in America is genuinely opaque — patients can't access their own records, don't understand their bills, and navigate a fragmented system designed to serve providers and payers, not people. Clara importing records from 150,000+ facilities and making clinical reasoning conversationally accessible through AI — that's a version of making the invisible visible. When I was fifteen and had no power, the systems that hurt me most were the ones I couldn't see or understand. Healthcare is full of those. But Clara isn't restructuring the extractive system the way Affirm restructured credit card opacity or Ownwell restructured property tax appeals. Clara is putting a better interface on top of the existing fee-for-service structure. The hidden tax — inflated visit costs, insurance complexity, fragmented records — remains structurally intact. Clara makes it easier to navigate the labyrinth; it doesn't tear down the walls.

The bull case for Clara is compelling, and I want to articulate it honestly before I give my verdict. These founders have operational playbooks that Counsel Health's team, regardless of a16z's capital, cannot buy. A decade of clinical hiring, regulatory navigation, and patient acquisition in digital primary care is a genuine advantage — it's not just about the AI, it's about who can operate a medical practice at scale while integrating new technology. The timing is real: LLM capabilities crossed clinical utility in 2023-2025, the 21st Century Cures Act made record import feasible at 150,000+ facilities, and Forward Health's hardware-heavy failure cleared the conceptual space for Clara's asset-light model. If Clara can leverage Favvas's network and YC's distribution to acquire patients faster than its capital disadvantage suggests, the operational depth could compound into a data moat that's genuinely hard to replicate. I've seen teams outrun better-funded competitors when they have ten years of domain knowledge — that's how Flexport built logistics infrastructure that capital alone couldn't replicate.

But when I run the ten-year question — where does Clara end up in a decade? — I see a strong digital primary care practice, not a piece of infrastructure that transforms what's possible. The AI layer is built on foundation models that Clara doesn't own. The clinical licensing and compliance infrastructure is essential but replicable with capital and time, as the dossier itself acknowledges. Clara's defensibility is operational, not structural — there's no network effect where each new patient makes the product exponentially harder to displace, no data moat that compounds beyond the individual patient relationship. Compare this to Crusoe Energy, where portable data centers at flare sites created infrastructure that no one else had built because no one else was willing to go to North Dakota oil fields. Clara's infrastructure is excellent but conventional — every health system with an EHR vendor can integrate AI chat into their existing patient portal, and Epic or Athenahealth have the patient relationships already.

The condition that would flip me to Invest: if I sat across from Favvas and heard a biographical story that explained why he's solving this problem from a place of personal wound rather than market observation — something that tells me he'd be building Clara even if no one would fund it — and if Clara showed me early patient retention data demonstrating that the AI-first interface creates switching costs that competitors' human-first models cannot match. That combination would change my calculus. Right now, Clara has extraordinary operators in a consensus space without the contrarian conviction or structural disruption that drives my highest-conviction bets. The founders' Circle Medical experience earns my respect, and the problem is real, but the space is crowded, the technology is commoditizing, and I don't feel the forge beneath the surface.
