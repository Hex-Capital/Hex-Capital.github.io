# Hex Security -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The competitive landscape here tells you everything about the timing. XBOW has $117M. Novee has $51.5M. Aikido is at $84.5M and a billion-dollar valuation. Multiple YC batches — Casco in S25, MindFort in X25, now Hex Security in W26 — are building autonomous pentesting agents. Open-source alternatives are emerging simultaneously. "AI will automate penetration testing" is not a contrarian insight in 2026; it is a consensus PowerPoint slide. When I backed Uber, ride-sharing was illegal. When I backed Bitcoin, serious people called it a Ponzi scheme. There is no disagreement to profit from here — every smart person in security already agrees this category is real. That means the asymmetric upside has already been competed away at the pricing level, and what remains is an execution race where the team with $500K is running against teams with 100-200x more capital.

On specific knowledge: Ahmad Khan has roughly a decade of application security work at Cruise, Salesforce, and IBM — real professional experience doing black-box pentesting and static analysis. That matters. But this is resume knowledge, not the non-transferable kind that comes from years of obsessive personal exploration. Thousands of senior appsec engineers at large tech companies have similar credentials. Compare this to Novee's founders coming from IDF elite cyber units, where the training environment and operational experience create knowledge that literally cannot be acquired in the private sector. Or XBOW's team, which already has top HackerOne rankings as empirical proof of their offensive capabilities. Khan is qualified, but he's not uniquely qualified in a way that makes Hex Security the team that must win. The other two founders — one with general software engineering and ATS reverse-engineering experience, the other with AI coding agents — bring adjacent skills, but only one of three founders has direct offensive security depth. That's a thin bench for the hardest technical problem in the category.

The leverage architecture is real but incremental. Replacing human pentesters with AI agents is labor substitution — you're taking something that exists (manual pentesting at $200+/hour) and making it cheaper and continuous. That's valuable. But it's not creating a new form of leverage that didn't exist before. When I invested in Replit, the insight was that millions of people who would never become traditional software engineers could suddenly build software — a qualitatively new capability for a new population. Hex Security doesn't enable a new type of security testing that was previously impossible; it delivers existing capability faster and cheaper. The continuous testing angle is the strongest leverage argument — a company that tests once per year has a fundamentally different security posture than one testing continuously — but that's a feature, not a platform.

The bull case deserves genuine consideration. If the reinforcement learning approach creates a compounding flywheel — each deployment surfaces vulnerability patterns that improve the agents, which surface more patterns — Hex Security could build a knowledge infrastructure analogous to what Stack Overflow built for developer Q&A. The YC traction is a real signal: finding SQL injection exposing billions of records and demonstrating a proof-of-concept worm across actual production systems shows the product works, not hypothetically but concretely. If Khan's decade of hands-on pentesting translates into training data curation and agent architecture decisions that generalist ML teams cannot replicate, the team's specific knowledge may be more defensible than it appears on paper. And the structural argument is compelling — traditional pentest firms cannot automate without cannibalizing their own revenue, and defensive security vendors lack the DNA for offensive tooling. If one team consolidates this category, the returns would be enormous. The question is whether that team will be the one with $500K competing against the one with $117M.

I keep returning to a principle I've stated clearly: anyone chasing hot markets gets killed. The autonomous pentesting category in 2026 is exactly the kind of market where capital has already flooded in, multiple competent teams are racing, and the winner will be determined by execution speed and distribution — not by the kind of specific knowledge advantage that compounds over decades. The technology could compound through RL, but so could every competitor's technology. There is no secret here, no non-consensus bet, no founder insight that makes me think the crowd has mispriced this. Smart, hardworking team in a real market, but this is not an asymmetric bet — it is a competitive footrace where Hex Security starts several laps behind.
