# Minicor -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the pivot. Minicor was Laminar — a low-code integration platform — before it became a desktop automation company. The sign-in portal still resolves to app.laminar.run. This matters because my primary filter is specific knowledge: what does this founding team know about legacy desktop automation that couldn't be replicated by any competent engineering team that read Anthropic's computer use announcement? The answer, as far as I can tell, is nothing distinctive. Faizaan built things at Microsoft and early-stage YC companies. Saheed did Android Auto at Ford and worked at Morgan Stanley and Kinaxis. These are solid enterprise engineering backgrounds, and the Android Auto work gives Saheed some relevant experience with complex UI integration. But the trajectory reads as engineers who spotted an opening when vision models crossed a reliability threshold — not people who spent years accumulating non-transferable insight about why RPA breaks, how healthcare workflows actually fail, or what Citrix environments specifically demand. When I backed Stack Overflow, the founders had lived inside the developer knowledge problem for a decade. Here, the insight — "use vision models to make RPA self-healing" — is available to anyone paying attention.

The leverage architecture is the strongest dimension. A single API call spins up a desktop workflow on a Windows VM, scales horizontally, and includes built-in observability with video recordings and screenshot debugging. This is genuine infrastructure leverage — one engineer's integration work serves every customer who needs that workflow. The compliance layer (SOC 2 Type II, HIPAA) adds platform value. If I'm evaluating this purely as a picks-and-shovels play for the AI desktop automation era, the architecture is sound. But here's the structural problem I keep returning to: the shovel is built on someone else's metal. The self-healing capability — the entire core value proposition — depends on foundation models from Anthropic, OpenAI, or Google. When I invested in Alchemy, the infrastructure layer they built was deeply differentiated because they understood the blockchain stack at a level the protocol designers didn't optimize for. Minicor's differentiation is deployment infrastructure: Windows VMs, Citrix support, compliance certifications. Important, but a different kind of moat — it's operational complexity rather than technological depth.

The bull case deserves genuine consideration. Desktop and Citrix environments are a real, underserved deployment surface. Browser-only competitors — Browserbase at $300M valuation, Browser Use with $17M, Skyvern — explicitly don't address this. UiPath's script-based approach means they'd have to cannibalize their own product to compete with AI-native self-healing. Healthcare alone is an enormous vertical: EHRs aren't getting APIs anytime soon, and the claim of 25,000 patients processed daily suggests real production usage, not a demo. The regulatory compliance creates genuine switching costs — once a healthcare org validates a HIPAA-compliant automation pipeline, they're not ripping it out for a marginal improvement. If Minicor executes well in healthcare and expands to financial services and government — all legacy-desktop-heavy verticals — this could be a meaningful infrastructure business. The timing window is real: foundation models just made this possible, incumbents are structurally inhibited from responding quickly, and the migration from desktop to cloud-native SaaS will take decades in regulated industries.

But I keep running the contrarian timing test and getting an ambiguous signal. "AI agents that automate computer tasks" is the most consensus category in tech right now. Every investor, every accelerator, every foundation model provider is thinking about this space. The desktop-specific angle is a real differentiation — most competitors went browser-first — but it's a tactical positioning choice, not a contrarian bet that smart people actively dismiss. Nobody is saying "self-healing desktop automation is a bad idea." They're saying "that's obviously going to happen." When the consensus is that your category is inevitable, the question becomes: why you and not the twenty other teams building toward the same destination, including the model providers themselves? Anthropic's computer use capability is literally the raw material. The foundation model providers have strong incentives to move up the stack into enterprise-ready deployment, and they have partnerships with the incumbents to do it.

The founding team's prior relationship — co-founding Software For Love together at the University of Ottawa — is a genuine positive. Teams that have worked together before survive the stress of early-stage building better than strangers who met at a co-founder matching event. And the 25,000 patients/day metric, if accurate, represents real production deployment at a stage where most YC companies are still iterating on prototypes. But I can't separate the Laminar traction from the Minicor traction, and the pivot recency makes me cautious about reading too much into operational metrics. The question I'd need answered before writing a check: what specific insight did these founders develop — through the Laminar experience or through their enterprise backgrounds — that gives them a durable advantage in understanding how desktop applications actually fail and how AI agents should recover? If they can articulate something that surprises me about the failure modes of legacy desktop software, something that took them years to learn and couldn't be taught in a weekend, the investment calculus changes. Right now, I see a competent team building useful infrastructure in a consensus category, powered by rented technology. That's a pass for me, though I'd want the conversation.
