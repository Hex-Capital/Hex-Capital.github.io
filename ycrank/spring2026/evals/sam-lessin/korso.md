# Korso -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Korso is that five other funded companies are already building essentially the same product. Paperless Parts has $30M and an established customer base. Uptool raised $6M from Khosla, Eclipse, Bessemer, and KP -- in 2024. Gendra, Mercura AI, Endeavor AI are all in the mix. When I see a competitive field that crowded around an AI application for an existing workflow, I know exactly what I'm looking at: a consensus opportunity masquerading as innovation. My entire framework is built around finding non-consensus -- and "use LLMs to automate manufacturing quoting" is about as consensus as it gets in the current AI cycle. Every VC with a manufacturing thesis has already funded this exact idea. The "insane idea" premium has been fully arbitraged away, and you're buying narrative at market price.

The founder-idea pairing doesn't rescue this. Three current or recent UPenn undergrads -- Daichi Hiraoka with electrical engineering and ML research, Martin Pan with a GM automations engineering internship, Alex Liu who dropped out with no prior companies found. These are smart, motivated people. I'll grant that two of them building together through Penn Electric Racing and sharing roots in Irvine suggests genuine relational depth, which I value over accelerator-matched co-founders. But "sane person" in my framework means operator credibility that specifically validates the ability to execute THIS idea. Running a quoting automation tool into manufacturing operations requires understanding how a shop floor actually processes an RFQ, what makes a quote competitive, how pricing history informs margin decisions. An internship at GM and some robotics research doesn't give you the ten thousand hours of manufacturing operations knowledge you need to build software that manufacturers will rip out their existing workflows to adopt. Against Uptool's team with Khosla backing and Paperless Parts' established distribution, the credibility gap is significant.

Now let me run the test that really matters to me: does this business get stronger or weaker as software gets cheaper? Korso is tagged as SaaS on its YC page. The likely business model is subscription revenue for AI-powered quoting software. The underlying technology is RAG over business documents -- a capability that is broadly available and getting cheaper by the month. The dossier itself concedes that "differentiation will depend on depth of manufacturing-domain training, integration quality, and speed of customer acquisition rather than proprietary model architecture." That sentence is a polite way of saying there is no technical moat. When I evaluate a company, I ask whether it would be a good business if AI didn't exist. Without the LLM layer, Korso is just another quoting tool competing against Paperless Parts with a fraction of the funding and none of the distribution. The AI IS the product, and when AI is the product, you're building a feature that incumbents will absorb or that the next model release will commoditize. SAP and Oracle have business model friction preventing them from building point solutions, sure -- but Paperless Parts doesn't have that friction, and they're already adding AI capabilities to an established platform.

The strongest bull case I can construct: manufacturing is genuinely physical-world, adoption of AI in procurement is only 35%, and the first team to nail the full quote-to-order lifecycle with a conversational AI interface could build enough accumulated pricing data and workflow integration to create real switching costs. "The Intelligence Layer for Manufacturing" has narrative elasticity -- you could expand from quoting into procurement, inventory, production scheduling, and plausibly tell an infinity story about becoming the operating system for mid-market manufacturers. If the founders have the operational obsession to go deep with a specific manufacturing vertical -- machine shops, sheet metal fabricators, whatever -- and build so tightly into their customers' workflows that ripping out Korso means losing years of institutional knowledge encoded in the system, there's a version of this that works. But that version requires the kind of deep domain intimacy and customer empathy that typically comes from founders who've actually run manufacturing operations, not from fresh engineering graduates applying LLMs to a problem they've studied from the outside.

I keep coming back to the structural test. This is a software company where software is the entire business, not a tool that amplifies physical-world operations. There's no paint crew, no parking infrastructure, no hardware installation creating barriers to entry. The "data moat potential" the dossier identifies is aspirational -- it doesn't exist at launch, and by the time it could theoretically develop, better-funded competitors with head starts will have accumulated their own data. I'd rather fund a manufacturer who's building technology to run their own operations better than a technology company trying to sell into manufacturers. The former has operational complexity as its moat; the latter has a pitch deck.
