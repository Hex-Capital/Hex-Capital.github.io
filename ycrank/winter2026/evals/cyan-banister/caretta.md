# Caretta -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I notice first: this is a company building AI for sales reps in one of the most crowded, most consensus-validated categories in venture right now. AI for sales. Every seed fund in San Francisco has a sales AI company in their portfolio. Gong has $332 million in revenue and just raised another $250 million to keep building. Salesken, Balto, and Cresta are all well-funded and already doing some version of real-time call assistance. When I look at a landscape like this, my instinct is to walk the other direction. The most promising companies are not popular. They are difficult to assess. Caretta is popular-shaped -- it sits in a category that every investor understands immediately, that every sales VP has on their roadmap, and that multiple well-capitalized incumbents are racing toward. The "real-time versus post-call" distinction is a clever product insight, but it's a timing play within a consensus market, not a fundamentally different way of seeing the world.

When I sit down with founders, I don't start with the product. I want to hear how three CS graduates from TU Delft in the Netherlands ended up deciding that B2B sales call assistance is the problem they were born to solve. And what I find in this dossier is... silence on that question. Kayra organized humanitarian relief through the Turkish Student Association, which tells me something about character. Omar scaled a Discord bot to 30,000 users, which tells me he can ship. They built VoysAI before Caretta, which shows iteration. But where's the thread? Where's the personal stake that keeps you building when Gong ships a competitive feature and your pipeline dries up? The founders who persist through real adversity are the ones solving problems rooted in their own experience. I don't see that connection here. These are talented technical builders who identified a market gap -- that's valuable, but it's not the signal I invest on. Compare this to the Contraline founder who'd spent years in academic research on male contraception before anyone would fund it, or to my own experience building Zivity after 50-plus rejections. The biographical commitment creates a kind of stubbornness that market analysis cannot substitute for.

I should note that Long Journey participated in the $1.3 million pre-seed, and I respect what my partners saw in the team. Three technical co-founders from the same university who've already shipped a product together and navigated a rebrand -- that's not nothing. The shared TU Delft foundation and the VoysAI-to-Caretta evolution suggest they can work through disagreements and pivot when needed, which is a real positive after what I witnessed with HQ Trivia. But three technical co-founders building a product for salespeople is a structural tension. Who on this team has sat across from a prospect and felt the specific pain of not knowing the answer to a deeply technical question during a live call? Who has run a sales team? The product targets revenue leaders and sales reps, but the founding team reads as engineers solving an engineering problem they observed from the outside.

The strongest bull case is about timing and defensibility. Real-time in-call assistance requires fundamentally different architecture than post-call analytics, and incumbents like Gong have built their entire product, pricing, and customer relationships around what happens after the call ends. The data flywheel is genuine -- every conversation deepens the organizational knowledge base, creating real switching costs. And LLM latency just crossed the threshold where this is technically viable with sub-second response times. If Caretta executes well and accumulates enough customer-specific knowledge before Gong catches up, they could own the real-time layer that Gong can't build without cannibalizing their existing business. That's a real strategic wedge. But this argument applies equally to Salesken, Balto, and Cresta, all of whom have more capital, more customers, and more data. The moat is theoretical until it's built, and building it requires winning enterprise sales cycles against incumbents with a four-person team.

What I don't see here -- and what would need to be true for me to get excited -- is any dimension of economic access or making invisible systems visible. Caretta helps employed salespeople at B2B companies perform better on calls. That's valuable, but it doesn't create new economic opportunity for people who lack it. It doesn't bring transparency to an opaque system. It optimizes an already well-served function for an already well-resourced buyer. When I backed Uber, I saw a world where anyone with a car could earn income. When I backed Affirm, I saw transparent credit replacing hidden fees. I don't have that vision here. This is a productivity tool for knowledge workers, and while there's nothing wrong with that, it doesn't generate the kind of conviction that makes me want to be the first check into something difficult.
