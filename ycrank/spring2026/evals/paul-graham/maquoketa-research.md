# Maquoketa Research -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that strikes me hardest about Maquoketa is the competitive landscape they're walking into. This isn't a market where incumbents are asleep — it's a market where the *startups* have already raised hundreds of millions. Helsing at €12B valuation. Anduril at $14B. Stark at $500M with $100M raised. Neros with $121M and a US Army PBAS selection. And here's a five-person team with no disclosed contracts, no revenue, no public product, and a website returning a 403 error. There's a version of this where "tiny team vs. giants" is the Airbnb cereal-box story — the audacity itself is the signal. But with Airbnb, the audacity was paired with dozens of specific stories about creative survival. Here I have Berkeley transcripts and silence.

I always evaluate founders first. What I'm looking for is the animal quality — people who do what they do so well they've crossed from professional into obsessive. The CTO, Hernandez, has genuine credentials that matter: Berkeley Wireless Research Center, phased array systems, RF design. That's not something you pick up from a tutorial. Phased array design is the kind of deep hardware expertise that takes years to develop and can't be faked. If I were sitting across from him in an interview, I'd want to hear him talk about specific systems he's built — the debugging stories, the failures, the moments where he figured out something subtle about beam steering or signal integrity that nobody taught him. That's where the real signal would be. But from this dossier, I have the credential without the stories. Yeager's background at SpiderRock and Berkeley EECS is solid but generic. Williams is a complete blank — no education, no prior work, no online presence at all. A three-person founding team where one member is invisible to research is unusual.

The problem discovery here worries me. The dossier frames the opportunity around Ukraine's demand for expendable drones, Germany's €9B procurement shift, and the Army's PBAS program. These are all real. But they're also on the front page of every defense publication. When someone tells me they "identified an opportunity" because a war demonstrated market demand, what I hear is that they read the same articles as every other aspiring defense-tech founder. The best startup ideas come from lived experience — Drew Houston forgot his USB drive, the Octopart founders couldn't find electronic parts for their physics lab. Nobody on this team appears to have military experience or a personal encounter with the problem of inadequate precision munitions. They're building for a customer segment they don't belong to, which means they're doing customer development from the outside. It's not impossible to succeed this way, but it's the harder path, and I don't see evidence that they've compensated with extraordinary depth of engagement.

Here's the bull case I have to honestly consider: the convergence of factors right now is genuinely unusual. Large defense primes are structurally unable to pivot to high-volume, low-cost expendable platforms because their entire profit model depends on expensive, low-volume systems. AeroVironment's new factory won't be operational until late 2026 or 2027. Governments are so desperate for capacity that Germany awarded Stark a €268M contract despite the company failing all four test strikes. In that environment, a small team with genuine RF/EW-resistance capability — not just COTS drone assembly — could find a procurement pathway through OTAs or SBIRs that bypasses the normal 12-36 month cycle. And Hernandez's phased array expertise is a real technical differentiator. Most pre-seed drone startups are snapping together off-the-shelf components. If Maquoketa can build guidance and electronic warfare resistance that commodity FPV assemblers can't replicate, that's a defensible wedge. The Stripe analogy would be: everyone could see that online payments were a big market, but only the Collisons were willing to do the painful integration work. Maybe Hernandez is willing to do the painful RF engineering work that drone assemblers skip.

But the Stripe analogy breaks in an important way. When Stripe started, there were no well-funded startup competitors in payments infrastructure — just legacy incumbents with bad APIs. Maquoketa is entering a field where multiple venture-backed startups are already years ahead, already fielding products in active combat, and already winning billion-dollar procurement contracts. The schlep of defense procurement creates a moat, but only if you're early enough that the moat protects you. Here, the moat is already protecting Anduril, Helsing, and Neros — and it's keeping Maquoketa out. The regulatory barriers (ITAR compliance, security clearances, facility requirements) that theoretically protect entrants from casual competitors also impose enormous costs on a five-person team that shows no evidence of having navigated them yet. And this is the furthest thing from my sweet spot — I've built a portfolio around hackers building tools for hackers. I have no edge in evaluating defense procurement dynamics, no network to help them get into the right rooms at the Pentagon, no instinct for which autonomous targeting claims are technically credible and which are vapor. This is a bet I'd need to know much more to make, and what I'd need to know isn't available in any dossier — it's in the founders' stories, which I don't have.
