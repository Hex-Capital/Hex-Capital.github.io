# Mercury -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Mercury is building in the single most consensus-hot category in AI right now. No-code multi-agent orchestration — everyone hears that and nods approvingly. Nobody says "that will never work." CrewAI has raised $24.5M. Lindy has raised roughly $50M. Relevance AI closed a $24M Series B last year. Dust is at $7.3M ARR. The space is flooded with capital and attention. In my framework, that's not a positive signal — it's the tell. When an idea sounds great to everyone immediately, the returns have already been competed away. The sweet spot for startups is the intersection of "seems like a bad idea" and "is a good idea." Mercury sits squarely outside that intersection, in the zone where the idea seems obviously good to anyone who reads a single TechCrunch article about AI agents.

The Palantir FDE background of Stefano Delmanto and Naveen Sharma is the strongest thing Mercury has going for it, and it deserves honest examination. Forward Deployed Engineers at Palantir aren't consultants in the McKinsey sense — they embed with clients and wrangle complex data systems in production environments, bridging technical infrastructure and non-technical operators. That's real experience with the coordination problem Mercury describes, and it's more authentic than "we did 50 customer interviews and identified a massive market opportunity." But there's a difference between professional proximity to a problem and the kind of personal frustration that produced Dropbox. Drew Houston didn't build Dropbox because he worked at a file storage company — he built it because he kept forgetting his USB drive. Mercury's origin reads more like "we were good at deploying complex systems at Palantir, so we're building a tool for deploying complex systems" than "we hit this wall ourselves and couldn't stand it anymore." That's a meaningful distinction. The manifesto frames it as "gap management" across 20+ tabs and 9+ tools, which is a real observation but reads like a product brief, not a personal pain point.

The structural position worries me more than the founders do. Mercury's orchestration layer sits atop Claude Code, Devin, and gstack — third-party agents that Mercury doesn't control. This is the thin-wafer problem. Anthropic, OpenAI (which already ships an Agents SDK with multi-agent orchestration), and Cognition all have direct incentive to build coordination features into their own platforms. Compare this to Stripe, where I invested $15K for 2%. Patrick Collison was building payments infrastructure — a genuine schlep involving PCI compliance, bank integrations, regulatory complexity across dozens of countries. That work repelled other developers because it was boring and painful. Mercury's coordination layer doesn't have that quality of repulsion. Worse, it depends on the continued cooperation of companies that could disintermediate it overnight. Stripe processed the payments themselves; Mercury routes to someone else's agents.

I notice no public GitHub repos for either Stefano or Naveen. For founders building AI infrastructure — even no-code AI infrastructure — the absence of any visible hacking history is unusual. My portfolio is dramatically overweight in hackers building for hackers: Stripe, Replit, Retool. Mercury is explicitly building for non-technical users, which breaks that pattern. And the third co-founder, listed only as "Jayson" on the manifesto with no last name, no role, no background — that's a real diligence gap on a five-person team. I need to understand who's on the founding team to evaluate whether they have the right combination of determination and flexibility.

The bull case for Mercury requires believing three things simultaneously: that the orchestration layer can accumulate enough workflow data and switching costs to survive platform integration by the agent providers themselves; that non-technical users will adopt multi-agent coordination before technical users have established the patterns (historically, developer tools precede no-code versions, not the other way around); and that Mercury can out-execute Lindy's $50M war chest and Relevance AI's 40,000 registered agents from a standing start with alpha-stage software. The Palantir FDE experience and the a16z Speedrun selection (70 from 19,000+ applicants) provide some founder-quality signal, and the dogfooding of a 5-agent sales organization shows Mercury is eating its own cooking. These aren't nothing. But they don't add up to the kind of evidence that would make me write a personal check into a consensus-hot space with $130M+ in competitor funding and fundamental platform dependency risk.

Mercury's decisive weakness is the absence of contrarian positioning — the idea attracts rather than repels, and the competitive landscape confirms it. The founders have relevant professional experience but no visible stories of the creative, slightly obsessive workarounds that separate relentlessly resourceful builders from competent operators. The name collision with Mercury Financial ($3.5B+ fintech at mercury.com) compounds the problem of competing for attention in an already crowded space. I'd want to see evidence that Mercury has discovered something non-obvious about multi-agent coordination — a specific insight that the CrewAIs and Lindys of the world are missing — before this becomes my kind of bet.
