# Miraka -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The thing about Miraka that most investors will get backwards is the services question. Every VC in the room at a16z Speedrun is going to look at a company that hires clinicians and think "that's a services business, it scales linearly, pass." And the software-only RPM players -- Cadence at $1B valuation with $144M raised, Biofourmis with $465M before getting acquired -- they look "venture-shaped" because they license platforms to health systems and never touch a patient. But here's the Metropolis question: what if the thing that makes VCs uncomfortable about Miraka -- that they actually employ the care team -- is exactly what makes it defensible? Metropolis manages parking. Not parking software. Parking. And that's a $5B company from seed because no LLM is going to operate a garage. Miraka manages cardiac patients. Not cardiac software. Patients. The 94% capture rate versus the 10% industry standard, if real, exists precisely because Miraka doesn't hand hospitals a tablet and wish them luck. They are the clinical workflow.

But I'm going to be honest: that's the bull case, and I don't think the mispricing is actually there. "Healthcare AI" in 2026 is about as consensus-hot as a category gets. The a16z Speedrun backing confirms it -- this is squarely in the institutional venture pipeline. Yes, the specific model of clinical delivery is less popular with VCs than pure SaaS, but that's a mild preference, not a categorical rejection. Nobody is looking at Miraka and saying "that's fucking crazy" the way people said Solana was crazy when Ethereum had won, or the way they said investing $1.7M in a YouTuber's future earnings was crazy. Nolan Abeyta, Kazuo Nakamura, and Jesse Abeyta are pitching a sensible idea -- better post-discharge cardiac care through continuous monitoring and clinical intervention -- in a validated market with clear comparisons. That's exactly the configuration where my framework says the upside is already in the price. The idea isn't insane. It's obviously correct. Everyone agrees cardiac readmissions are bad and that 10% capture rates are pathetic. The question is execution, and execution bets in consensus markets have capped returns.

The primitive test fails cleanly. Remote patient monitoring exists. Cardiac rehabilitation exists. Clinical staffing companies exist. What Miraka does is integrate them with AI-driven risk prediction -- and integration is not a primitive. Nobody will describe a future company as "the Miraka of X" because the underlying behaviors all predate Miraka. Compare this to Venmo, which created peer-to-peer mobile payments as a verb. Or Patreon, which created direct creator monetization as a behavior. Miraka is "better cardiac rehab with continuous data," which is a legitimately good product -- but it's competing in an existing category against Cadence, HRS, and Biofourmis, not birthing a new one. The >90% readmission reduction and 71% month-over-month growth in billable appointments are strong execution signals. $300K ARR at pre-seed from insurance reimbursement is real money, not vanity metrics. But strong execution in an existing market is a different game than what I'm looking for.

What gives me pause before a clean pass: the founder continuity is real. Nolan and Kazuo co-founded Overwatch Health together at Princeton in 2021 and have been iterating on cardiac care for four years. Jesse Abeyta -- likely a sibling given the surname -- was CTO of Overwatch and brings Amazon AI and military biometrics depth. This isn't a co-founder team assembled at a networking event; it's a multi-year relationship forged through a shared prior venture, which is the configuration my best returns come from. They sold into NewYork-Presbyterian as what they claim is the youngest team to sell into a top-5 heart hospital. The domain knowledge density is genuine. But the sane/insane ratio is inverted here -- these are sensible people building a sensible thing, and I need the idea to make smart people uncomfortable.

The structural durability analysis cuts both ways. On one side, Miraka passes the AI kill test better than its software-only competitors: an LLM cannot replace a credentialed clinician monitoring a patient's vitals and intervening before a cardiac event. Cadence's SaaS platform is frankly more vulnerable to AI commoditization than Miraka's clinical delivery model. That's real. On the other side, Miraka's entire revenue model depends on CMS billing codes for remote patient monitoring. The 2026 Physician Fee Schedule was favorable -- lowered RPM billing thresholds -- but CMS rules change annually, and if reimbursement rates compress or eligibility tightens, the unit economics break. That hits my explicit red flag: "companies dependent on regulatory permission to be profitable." The business survives AI disruption but may not survive a bureaucratic rule change in Washington. That's trading one existential risk for another.

Capital discipline is Miraka's strongest thesis-fit signal. Insurance-reimbursed revenue from day one means they're generating cash from operations, not incinerating venture capital on user acquisition. $300K ARR on what appears to be minimal external funding shows the founders treat capital as a tool, not a lifeline. If CMS rules stay favorable, this business could reach profitability without a massive Series A -- which is exactly the posture I want. But that single strength doesn't overcome the fundamental problem: this is a well-executed company in a consensus category, building an integration product rather than a primitive, with regulatory dependency baked into every dollar of revenue. The market isn't wrong about Miraka. The market just hasn't gotten around to pricing it yet.
