# Payna -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I noticed about Payna isn't the product or the team -- it's that they pivoted *away* from the idea that would have gotten my attention. A few months ago, these founders built Paena, an on-chain earned wage access platform connecting DeFi liquidity to workers living paycheck to paycheck. That's a company I would have wanted to hear the story behind -- who lives that paycheck-to-paycheck reality, why crypto rails, what personal experience drove them to build it. Instead, they pivoted during or after YC started to B2B compliance software for crypto companies getting licensed. They moved from a product that creates direct economic access for working people to a product that helps fintech companies fill out regulatory paperwork. I understand why the pivot happened -- compliance SaaS has clearer revenue and a known buyer -- but it tells me something about the founders' relationship to the problem. This doesn't feel like a mission born from lived experience. It feels like a market opportunity identified during batch.

When I try to apply my usual process -- tell me your story, not the business -- I hit a wall. Aaron Chen dropped out of Berkeley after his sophomore year as a chemistry major, which has a faint echo of the outsider path I respect, but dropping out of Berkeley to build crypto products is practically a YC archetype at this point, not the kind of forge-in-fire adversity that makes me sit up. His experience at Axal (a16z CSX-backed) as a sole designer is legitimate crypto-native credibility, and the ETHGlobal hackathon wins show he can ship fast. But his expertise is in product design and DeFi -- not in regulatory compliance, not in state licensing law, not in the dense machinery of NMLS and FinCEN that this product needs to encode accurately. Then there's He Song, the other co-founder, who has essentially no public footprint at all -- no education, no work history, no domain signal I can find. My entire evaluation method depends on understanding who the founder is and why they're doing this specific thing. When I can't see that, my primary assessment channel is blocked. I'm not saying He Song isn't talented -- the information simply doesn't exist for me to evaluate.

Here's the competitive reality that concerns me most: Brico has $21.6 million in funding, marquee customers like Marqeta and BitGo, Forbes Top 50 Fintech recognition, and claims 600% year-over-year revenue growth. They're building the exact same thing -- AI-powered money transmitter license automation. When I backed SpaceX, nobody else was doing private rocketry. When I wrote the check for Crusoe Energy, mining Bitcoin on gas flares sounded insane to every other investor in the room. Those were anti-consensus bets. Payna is entering a market where a well-capitalized competitor has already proven demand and is scaling rapidly. This is the definition of consensus territory -- someone else found the insight first, validated it, and is executing. The most promising companies are not popular and are difficult to assess. Licensing automation for crypto is now neither.

The strongest bull case I can construct is this: the regulatory surface area for crypto is genuinely expanding -- the GENIUS Act, new state-level VASP requirements, rising compliance costs. There's potentially room for multiple vendors the way Workday and SAP coexist. Aaron's crypto-native background means he understands the customer's world from the inside, which matters when building product. And the compliance space itself involves real jurisdictional complexity across 50+ states -- exactly the kind of hard, regulated territory I normally find attractive. If the founders have undisclosed domain expertise or advisors, if they've found a specific wedge Brico isn't serving, if their "compliance operating system" framing represents a genuinely broader product vision than Brico's licensing workflow focus -- then maybe there's something here. But I don't see evidence for any of those "ifs" in what's available. The product appears to be two to three months old at most, with no disclosed customers, no revenue, and placeholder metrics on the website.

I keep coming back to the same question I always ask: would I drop everything to work for this person? I can't answer that when I can't see the biographical thread connecting these founders to this particular problem. Compliance automation is important infrastructure, and the regulatory tailwinds are real. But this feels like a smart team that identified an opportunity, not founders who are solving something that keeps them up at night because they've lived it. My best investments have always been in people whose personal story and company mission are inseparable. I don't see that here.
