# Datost -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the structural trap. Salesforce paid $27.7 billion for Slack. Salesforce owns Tableau. Datost is building an AI data analyst inside Slack that replaces the need for Tableau dashboards. They are building the exact product that Salesforce's own business logic demands it build -- a unified AI analytics layer across both of its acquired properties. The principal-agent friction inside Salesforce is real -- the Tableau team won't voluntarily cannibalize seat-based licensing -- but Benioff has been shoving AI into every product line since 2023. This isn't Google versus Perplexity, where the ad revenue model creates a structural inability to move. This is a large company that is slow but pointed in exactly the right direction. Building your company's core value proposition inside a platform owned by the entity most motivated to replicate you is not contrarian positioning. It's a timing bet with a narrow window.

Now the specific knowledge question. If these two founders disappeared tomorrow, could you hire any two competent engineers and rebuild Datost in a month? Honestly, yes. Both are 21-year-old software engineers whose prior experience is at Traba, a staffing technology company. Nothing in the dossier suggests they spent years inside the data analytics problem -- no time as frustrated data analysts queuing up SQL requests, no prior projects in BI or data infrastructure, no evidence of the kind of obsessive domain engagement that becomes the company's engine. One co-founder's LinkedIn headline still reads "Software Engineer Intern." They identified a real problem, but identifying a problem through observation is fundamentally different from possessing specific knowledge that makes you the only team capable of solving it. When I backed Perplexity, Aravind had been thinking about retrieval systems since his PhD at Berkeley. The product was his specific knowledge made manifest. Here, the product is a competent engineering team calling an LLM API and wrapping it in Slack integration.

The leverage audit confirms the concern. On the surface, this is code leverage -- AI agent, zero marginal cost per query, scales with a small team. But it's borrowed leverage. The intelligence comes from the foundation model provider. The distribution comes from Slack's platform. The data connections are standard database drivers. When I say "permissionless leverage," I mean leverage you own. Notion owns its product. Stack Overflow owns its knowledge graph. Datost is renting OpenAI's reasoning capability and Slack's distribution surface. The moment the foundation model improves or Salesforce ships Einstein-in-Slack, the borrowed leverage evaporates. "Innovation without a moat ends up as consumer surplus" -- the value flows to the end user and eventually back to the platform owner.

Here is the bull case I genuinely considered: the data context flywheel. As Datost ingests an organization's docs, schema, Slack history, and codebase, it accumulates organizational knowledge that a fresh competitor would need time to replicate. This is real switching cost. And the Slack-native form factor is the right wedge -- meeting users where they already work eliminates the adoption friction that kills most BI tools. If the founders can ship fast enough to build deep integrations and earn enterprise trust before Salesforce unifies Slack and Tableau, the accumulated organizational context becomes the moat. The democratization angle is also legitimate and maps to my portfolio pattern -- most people in organizations cannot write SQL, and this genuinely removes a gatekeeper. But here is why the bull case doesn't convert me: the data context moat is thin. A competitor with superior reasoning capability can re-ingest a schema in hours. Switching costs in Slack bots are measured in days, not years. And the democratization is a property of the entire AI analytics category -- Julius AI, Defog, every Copilot feature in Power BI -- not a property unique to Datost.

Two things land on the positive side. The team structure is right -- two technical builders, no non-technical co-founders who need to hire engineers, AI-native product. That is the correct denominator. And they got into YC, which is a genuine filter. But team efficiency without specific knowledge is an engine without fuel. You can be lean and fast and still lose because you built something anyone could build. The "first AI data analyst with its own computer" framing suggests engineering depth in the execution sandbox, which is a real differentiator versus prompt-to-SQL tools that spit out queries without running them. But a sandboxed compute layer is infrastructure, not insight. It can be replicated by any team with devops competence.

I pass. The founders are young, capable, and building in the right form factor -- but this is a category play, not a specific-knowledge play. The product is the application of generally available AI capabilities to an obvious problem, distributed through a platform owned by the company most likely to build this feature natively. There is no non-replicable founder knowledge at the center. You get paid for being right first, but you also need to be building something that stays right after the rest of the market catches up.
