# ReasonBlocks -- Paul Graham Evaluation

ReasonBlocks is two technically formidable childhood friends — Sajeev Magesh out of Stanford CS and Rohan Vij out of CMU, building together since second grade per the dossier — executing competently in the single most consensus startup category of Spring 2026. The signature pairing is high founder substance against the lowest-possible reading on schlep blindness: AI agent infrastructure in 2026 is the opposite of a repellent idea. ReasonBlocks shipped 42% accuracy and 52% token reduction on SWE-bench Pro before raising, which is real work, but the category itself is exactly where everyone else's unconscious is also pointing.

On founder resourcefulness, the high rung is Airbnb selling cereal boxes; the low rung is the McKinsey duo with the polished B2B workflow deck and 50 customer interviews. ReasonBlocks sits between Replit-style competent shipping and the cereal-box anchor, and the pin is the SWE-bench Pro number plus the unverified "live with top Series B companies, 90% repeat usage" claim — a two-person team with what looks like working software in production is not nothing, but the dossier contains zero stories of constraint, no anecdote about shipping a prototype in a weekend, no bootstrapped revenue before YC. Eleven years of friendship is a real asset; eleven years of friendship is also not, by itself, a story about resourcefulness under pressure. I'd want to hear what Sajeev and Rohan did when the first benchmark run came back negative, and that artifact isn't in the dossier.

On the idea-repels axis — my dimension, the one I trust most — the high rung is Stripe wading into PCI compliance and bank integrations because nobody else would, and the low rung is the consultant duo whose pitch could appear unchanged in a TechCrunch trend piece. ReasonBlocks lives one notch above the McKinsey case, and the pin is the named competitive set: LangSmith, Braintrust, Langfuse (just acquired by ClickHouse in January), Helicone, Latitude. That is not a market that repelled the founders into it; that is a market that the founders walked into because everyone is walking into it. The "runtime intervention vs. post-hoc tracing" wedge is real but narrow, and Braintrust shipping a runtime SKU on top of trace data they already own is the obvious next move. ReasonBlocks needs the reasoning library to compound faster than incumbents can ship parity, and incumbents have the distribution.

On organic problem discovery, the high rung is Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive; the mid rung is Replit's Amjad scratching his own coding-environment itch. ReasonBlocks has no origin story in the dossier — Sajeev's published work is on poverty and air pollution ML, Rohan's prior is molecular dynamics and ENGIE distributed energy. Neither founder's public footprint shows them running production agents and burning out on token bills before they decided to fix it. Applying the absent-evidence default and tilting it down because the founder backgrounds are research-adjacent rather than agent-operator-adjacent. The earnest-builder dimension lands higher: Sajeev's Points of Light Changemaker award, the high-school cancer project at Amador Valley, the *Nature Sustainable Agriculture* publication — these are not status-chasers, these are humans who build because they like building. That moves ReasonBlocks toward the Replit rung on character, even if the company itself reads more like a category bet than a calling. Learning rate sits at the default with mild positive lean — Sam Altman at Loopt is the high rung, the year-flat dossier is the low rung, and ReasonBlocks's shipped benchmarks suggest iteration is happening even though the dossier shows no week-over-week curve.

My 36-month forecast for ReasonBlocks: acqui-hire by Braintrust, LangChain, or a model lab in the $20–50M range with $1–3M ARR, founders absorbed as the primary asset. The mechanism is exactly the high/low signature — strong founders in a category where the data gravity sits with the trace-owning incumbents. Sajeev and Rohan are exactly the kind of two-person team a Series B observability company writes a $30M check for once their reasoning-library framing has been validated on a few coding-agent customers. That is a fine outcome for them and a roughly 1x outcome for a YC-stage check.

The bull case that should sting: the "private reasoning library that grows with every run" is genuinely the right shape of moat for the agent era, and the dossier's 90% repeat-usage claim — if real — is the kind of crappy-version-one obsessive-use signal I tell people to look for in *How to Get Startup Ideas*. Childhood-friend founders with this much technical depth pivot well; if Sajeev and Rohan get one good customer-driven insight in the next six months, they could escape the observability-incumbent gravity well and become the runtime layer rather than a feature inside Braintrust. Reddit also looked like a mediocre idea in a crowded space — what mattered was the running-back quality of the founders, and I haven't met Sajeev and Rohan. The verdict flips on a single verifiable condition: an origin story showing one of them personally ran a production agent at a prior job, watched it burn $50K of inference, and built the first version of ReasonBlocks for themselves before they met a single customer. That converts this from a category bet into Dropbox.
