# Meridian -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Meridian claims to have won competitive bake-offs against Palantir. I co-founded Palantir. I know what a Palantir deployment looks like — forward-deployed engineers spending months embedding proprietary ontologies into an organization's operational infrastructure until the switching costs become functionally infinite. Meridian is a RAG layer over SharePoint documents that generates slide decks. These are not the same category of product, and the claim that they competed head-to-head tells me either the bake-off was a narrow feature comparison against a Palantir AIP demo (plausible) or the founders are inflating a minor evaluation into a competitive victory (also plausible). Either way, the claim reveals more about Meridian's self-positioning than about its competitive reality.

The secret test is where Meridian fails most decisively. What does Meridian know that the market disputes? Their stated thesis — "behavioral signal on how consulting work actually gets done, you can't download it, you have to earn it one engagement at a time" — is the standard defensibility pitch of every vertical SaaS company built in the last decade. Veeva said this about pharma CRM data. Toast said it about restaurant operations data. The insight that consulting firms hemorrhage institutional knowledge when people leave is not a secret — it is a problem statement that has appeared in McKinsey reports, Deloitte white papers, and the marketing copy of Guru, Bloomfire, and now Interloom ($16.5M seed). When five funded competitors and every knowledge management incumbent share your thesis, you do not have a secret. You have a crowded market. The existence of Kenley — a direct YC S24 competitor targeting the identical buyer with the identical product thesis — is the clearest signal that there is no contrarian insight here. Kenley's launch page describes AI agents for advisory workflows and due diligence. Meridian describes AI agents that capture knowledge and conduct intelligent interviews. Mimetic competition, Girard would say — two companies imitating each other's desire for the same object.

The Palantir analogy is instructive precisely because it reveals what Meridian lacks. Palantir's secret in 2003 was that intelligence agencies needed a proprietary software platform embedded at the operational level, and that once embedded, the switching costs would approach the structurally infinite — no agency would rip out a system that its analysts depended on for daily threat assessment. The monopoly mechanism was government embedding, reinforced by security clearances and classification requirements that no competitor could shortcut. Meridian targets consulting firms — organizations that are constitutionally allergic to vendor lock-in, that pride themselves on tool-agnosticism, and that cycle through technology platforms on 2-3 year evaluation cycles. Kashyap Nathan's Palantir FDE experience was apparently an internship-length stint, not the multi-year embedding that teaches you how institutional dependency actually compounds. A consulting firm's Meridian instance may become "irreplaceable after two months" as the a16z portal claims, but consulting firms replace irreplaceable things every budget cycle. The structural dynamics are opposite to Palantir's.

The $700K contracted revenue in four months deserves honest engagement as the strongest bull signal. If verified, this would represent remarkable early pull for a pre-seed company founded by college-age entrepreneurs. Managing partners referring Meridian unprompted — if true — suggests genuine product-market resonance in a buyer segment that is notoriously difficult to access. The bull case requires believing three things simultaneously: that the traction is real (it is entirely self-reported with no independent verification), that consulting-specific depth will compound into a defensible moat before Glean ($200M ARR, $610M raised) adds vertical features, and that the per-client data accumulation will create switching costs durable enough to sustain pricing power. Veeva achieved this in pharma — monopolizing a vertical so thoroughly that Salesforce, its own platform provider, could not displace it. But Veeva's moat was regulatory compliance data in a domain where FDA requirements created structural barriers to switching. Consulting knowledge management has no analogous regulatory lock-in. The realistic ceiling here, even in the success case, is a solid vertical SaaS business generating $50-100M ARR — a fine outcome for the founders, structurally irrelevant to fund-level returns.

The founding team — Kashyap Nathan (Georgia Tech dropout, Palantir intern, OpenAI), Christopher Farrington (Georgia Tech CS, Sandia ML, Deloitte intern), Shanay Champaneri (Cornell, Palantir, Robinhood) — have impressive credentials for their age but no evidence of the definite, irreversible commitment that signals conviction over iteration. The website lists pricing at $0. The model is described as hybrid SaaS with per-seat and API usage tiers — a pricing structure designed for experimentation, not for a founder who has decided exactly what the product is worth and refuses to negotiate. The YC batch departure is ambiguous but unexplained, and the discrepancy between two and three co-founders across different sources suggests organizational instability at the earliest stage. The entire technology stack — RAG over document stores, LLM-based generation, template-based output — is reproducible by any competent engineering team with access to the same foundation models. There is no atoms-based moat, no physical-world technology, no regulatory barrier, no proprietary hardware. This is pure software built on commodity infrastructure.

I pass on Meridian because competition defines its market and consensus defines its thesis. The canonical red flags converge: multiple funded competitors pursuing identical approaches (Kenley, Glean's inevitable vertical expansion, Microsoft Copilot's consulting firm penetration), a secret that is actually a McKinsey talking point, a TAM pitch ($23.2B knowledge management market) without a specific monopoly wedge, and a technology stack that any well-capitalized team could replicate in months. The decisive dimensions are the absence of a genuine monopoly secret — no non-obvious truth that, if correct, produces winner-take-all dynamics — and power-law magnitude that is structurally capped by the fragmented, vendor-agnostic nature of consulting firm technology procurement. Meridian may build a real business. But "nice business" is the most expensive phrase in venture capital, because it consumes the same attention and opportunity cost as a monopoly without producing monopoly returns.
