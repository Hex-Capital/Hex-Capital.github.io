# Verdant -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Verdant is the inverse of the shape I write checks into: the category is right -- municipal permitting is gloriously unsexy and regulated -- but the founder is a Georgetown undergrad named Aidan Ng whose biography intersects "Government major" and a policy lab, not a planning counter at 4pm on a Friday. That high-on-category, low-on-lived-experience pairing is the analytical signature of Verdant: a correct lane spotted by a student of governance rather than a refugee from it, entering eighteen months after GovWell raised a $25M Series A from Insight Partners for the same staff-review workflow.

Anduril 2017 is the rung I want every founder-fit pitch to brush against -- Palmer Luckey had shipped Oculus to scale before he ever sketched a Lattice slide, and the defense thesis loaded because the biography preceded the company. Verdant's Aidan Ng is documented as a Georgetown Government student affiliated with the Global Lab, with no shipped permitting product, no stint inside a planning department, no anecdote about the moment he watched a plan reviewer drown in PDFs. The verdantapp.com line "backgrounds in public service" is exactly the kind of unverified self-description I read as a tell -- when the dossier is silent on the lived moment, my default is that the founder discovered the problem in a syllabus, not at a counter. That is the Pass Example template, not the Anduril template.

HQ Trivia is the rung that taught me what unresolved co-founder authority costs -- Yusupov and Kroll's feud helped take the company down even after the tactile virality I could feel in my own thumb. Verdant has two founders, Aidan Ng listed as CEO and Jason Yi as co-founder with a LinkedIn headline I cannot even retrieve from public sources. The CEO title exists on Aidan, which is more than HQ had, but the dossier offers no documented operating agreement, no co-founder backstory, no shared employer or school. Verdant is not the HQ disaster shape yet -- it is the pre-HQ shape, where the conversation simply hasn't happened on paper, and at pre-seed I score that at the default, not below it.

Govtech permitting compounds beautifully over eighteen months when you actually win a jurisdiction -- the ordinance corpus, the reviewer feedback loops, the procurement-officer relationships all accrete into switching cost, which is the Vigil Labs / Mindbloom mechanism that pulls me toward "too unsexy" categories. Verdant sits squarely in that lane: Accela and Tyler intake-and-track contracts are visibly churning per the YC description, the Dataintelo $1.85B North America SAM is real, and AI plan review is a genuine product wedge over the incumbent. The problem is that the compounding clock started at GovWell, not at Verdant -- 130+ municipal customers across 34 states is the moat being built in real time, and Verdant arrives at the same RFPs with zero disclosed deployments. The category is right; the entry timing is two rounds late.

Crusoe was magically weird because no one wanted to fund Bitcoin-on-flares in 2018 -- the aesthetic pass from peers was the alpha. Verdant would have to be true that govtech AI permit review is still uninvestable, but Insight Partners just wrote a $25M check into GovWell in May 2026 and PermitFlow has raised $91M total across the adjacent applicant side. Verdant is entering a category the smart money has already priced, which is the structural opposite of "find them before it becomes consensus." Unsexy is not the same as non-consensus when six tier-one funds have already mapped the space.

Flock Safety I sourced from a Wi-Fi name at a Four Seasons -- I could see the camera on the wall before I ever met the founder. Verdant gives me a Calendly link and a feature page; the only people who can touch the artifact are municipal planning staff under NDA after a demo call. That is the developer-tools-abstraction shape from my Pass Example, dressed in govtech clothing -- I cannot install Verdant, photograph it, or hand it to a friend, and at pre-seed that absence is the absence of my entry point.

Verdant's most probable 36-month path is sub-$1M ARR with two or three pilot municipalities, followed by an acqui-hire into Accela, Tyler, or OpenGov under $15M, or quiet wind-down -- the mechanism is GovWell's $25M Series A buying eighteen months of land-grab against a two-person team with no disclosed wedge customer and no founder who has ever stamped a permit.

The bull case stings, and I want to be honest about it: govtech permitting is *exactly* my unsexy-regulated lane, incumbent contracts are churning per the YC description, the Dataintelo CAGR is real, and a Georgetown Government major affiliated with a global policy lab is precisely the kind of outsider trajectory I claim to favor over the Stanford CS / Goldman pitch. The HandUp exception exists -- I wrote into a modest-ceiling company because the founder's biography mirrored a mission. The dismantling fact is that HandUp's founder lived the problem; Aidan Ng studied it. The category fit cannot rescue a founder whose contact with permit review is academic when GovWell's reviewers are shipping AutoCheck into 130+ jurisdictions today. The verifiable condition that flips me to Invest: documented evidence that Aidan Ng or Jason Yi worked inside a municipal planning, building, or zoning department -- or shipped software adopted by one -- before founding Verdant. Absent that, this is the Pass Example wearing a govtech hat.
