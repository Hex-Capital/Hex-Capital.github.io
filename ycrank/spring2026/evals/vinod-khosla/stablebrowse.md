# StableBrowse -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

$96 million in aggregate competitor funding — Browserbase at $300M valuation with 50M sessions processed, Browser Use with 50K GitHub stars and $17M from Felicis, Steel.dev at $17M, Kernel at $22M — and StableBrowse walks in with standard YC capital. That's the first thing I see. Normally, a crowded field of well-funded incumbents doesn't scare me — I backed Juniper against Cisco, and that $3M became $7 billion. But Juniper had a structural technology wedge against a single complacent monopolist. StableBrowse faces five-plus hungry startups all racing to solve the same problem, most of them already shipping product with paying customers. This isn't a monopoly to disrupt. It's a land grab where the land is already being claimed.

StableBrowse's technical approach — stripping the rendering engine entirely and outputting accessibility trees, semantic markdown, and knowledge graphs instead of pixels — is the most architecturally interesting move in this space. 130.6 MB per instance versus Chromium's bloat, 4ms cold start, 85% token reduction. If those numbers hold at scale, that's not a wrapper around existing infrastructure — it's a fundamentally different engine. Browserbase and Steel.dev inherit Chromium's full rendering overhead; rebuilding around a rendering-free architecture would require gutting their core product. This is the kind of non-linear technical approach I look for. The problem is that these claims are self-reported on StableBrowse's website with zero production validation, no GitHub repos, no public benchmarks, and no customers to confirm them. When I backed Rocket Lab, Peter Beck had actually built and tested engines. Here, I'm being asked to take performance claims on faith from a team with no browser engine pedigree.

Somansh Shah from Purdue and Deepit Shah from UIUC bring relevant technical grounding — data science, systems engineering, Amazon internship — but these are recent graduates, not founders who've demonstrated the rapid learning velocity I prioritize. I don't need experience; I backed a 19-year-old at Mach Industries. But that founder showed learning trajectory visible in months, building weapons systems that worked. StableBrowse's founders have hackathon wins and college projects. More concerning: two of four founders — Sarthak Awasthi and Jay Mehta — can't even be confirmed as the same individuals referenced in public profiles. And Deepit Shah's LinkedIn apparently indicated he was returning to Google full-time. When I evaluate gene pool quality, I need to know the gene pool actually exists and is committed. StableBrowse has a verification gap that makes learning-rate assessment impossible.

The consequence magnitude question is where StableBrowse falls short of my framework. If StableBrowse fully succeeds — every AI agent in the world uses their machine-native browser — what changes? Agent operations get cheaper. Developers save on compute and tokens. That's useful infrastructure, but nobody's life fundamentally changes. The AI agents market may reach $183 billion by 2033 (Grand View Research), but StableBrowse is plumbing, not the water. Compare this to Commonwealth Fusion Systems, where success means limitless clean energy, or Impossible Foods, where success means feeding billions without animal agriculture. StableBrowse's success means a moderately profitable developer tools company in a market where OpenAI, Google, and Anthropic are building their own browsing infrastructure and could commoditize the standalone layer entirely. That platform risk alone — LLM providers bundling browser infrastructure — caps the consequence even in the upside scenario.

The bull case an advocate would make: StableBrowse's rendering-free architecture creates a structural moat that Chromium-based competitors cannot replicate without full re-architecture. CDP compatibility provides a frictionless migration path. The 85% token reduction could enable agent use cases that are currently cost-prohibitive, creating new demand rather than redistributing existing spend. The AI browser market at $4.5B growing to $76.8B by 2034 (Market.us) gives them a massive expanding market. And purpose-built infrastructure always beats repurposed infrastructure — just as Juniper's purpose-built routers beat Cisco's general-purpose boxes. It's a fair argument. But it breaks on three facts: (1) the technical claims are entirely unverified at scale and web compatibility of a rendering-free engine across the modern web is unproven, (2) the founders show no evidence of the learning velocity or first-principles thinking that would give me confidence they can execute this re-architecture against teams with $17-40M head starts, and (3) the consequence of success is infrastructure efficiency, not category creation. StableBrowse hits my red flag for "targeting redistribution of existing spend rather than creation of new demand" — companies already using Browserbase would switch for cost savings, which is share competition, not world-changing.

StableBrowse would flip me to Invest on one verifiable condition: demonstrated web compatibility across the top 1,000 sites with independent benchmarks confirming the 85% token reduction and 4ms cold start claims, combined with evidence that the rendering-free approach enables agent capabilities that are genuinely impossible — not just cheaper — with conventional browsers. That would move this from an efficiency improvement to a discontinuous capability shift, which is a different bet entirely. Without that evidence, StableBrowse is a competent infrastructure startup in a brutally competitive market where the consequence of full success is a better developer tool, not a changed world. Consequence magnitude and competitive positioning are the decisive dimensions — the first because infrastructure plumbing doesn't generate the asymmetric returns my framework requires, the second because $96M in competitor funding against YC-level capital in a converging market is a market risk problem, and I prefer technology risk to market risk.
