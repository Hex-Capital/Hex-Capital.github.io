# Stage -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

$334.5 million. That's the combined funding raised by CodeRabbit, Graphite, Greptile, and Qodo — the four companies already doing what Stage wants to do. CodeRabbit alone has $15M ARR and 8,000 paying customers. Graphite was acquired by Cursor for $290M in December 2025. Stage is entering this arena as a two-person team with a presentation-layer idea — organizing diffs into "structured chapters" — that any of these incumbents could ship as a feature update in a quarter. When I look at a competitive landscape like this, I need to see something beneath the surface that nobody else can see. With Crusoe, the "magically weird" Bitcoin-mining-with-flare-gas exterior was hiding an infrastructure company that could put data centers anywhere there was stranded energy. With Stage, the chapters concept is the whole product. There's no hidden infrastructure being built underneath.

Charles Pan and Dean Stratakos are capable engineers — Stanford CS, Five Rings, Apple internships, Citadel. Stratakos built an internal coding agent and led AI initiatives at a quantitative trading firm where code velocity matters. That's genuine domain experience. But when I ask my first question — tell me your story, not the business — what I hear is a career arc: Stanford to quant trading to startup. Pan's LinkedIn still shows "Revel Street," not even updated to Stage yet. Stratakos's experience building coding agents at Five Rings is the seed of the idea, and I respect that he lived inside AI-generated code before most engineers did. But living inside a professional workflow and being forged by a problem are different things. The founders who built Affirm weren't just experienced in finance — Max Levchin had watched credit card companies extract billions through deliberate opacity and it enraged him. I don't see that kind of biographical inevitability here. Stage reads as two smart engineers who spotted a real gap at work and decided to fill it. That's how most startups begin, but it's not what I write checks for.

Stage's core thesis — that AI-generated code needs better human review — is correct. The timing is real. Cursor, Copilot, Claude Code have flooded engineering teams with machine-written code that reviewers can't fully reason about. But being right about the problem doesn't make you the right company to solve it. Developer tools is the most consensus-attractive category in venture capital right now. Nobody shifts uncomfortably when you pitch "better code review for the AI era" at a dinner party — they nod and ask about your growth metrics. Compare that to Flock Safety, where I discovered the founder's WiFi network at a YC event and then had to explain to other investors why surveillance cameras in neighborhoods weren't dystopian. Or Contraline, where half the VCs in the room couldn't even discuss male birth control without squirming. Stage operates in a space where investors are tripping over each other to write checks. That's the opposite of the signal I look for.

The invisible system test is where Stage falls furthest from my thesis. Code review is already one of the most transparent processes in software engineering — GitHub literally shows you every line that changed, who changed it, and when. The problem Stage addresses is cognitive load, not opacity. Nobody is being exploited by information asymmetry when they review a pull request. When I backed Affirm, the thesis was that credit card companies were hiding the true cost of borrowing inside deliberately confusing terms — a hidden tax on consumers who didn't understand what they were signing. When I backed Ownwell, it was that property tax assessments operated in a black box that homeowners couldn't penetrate. Stage makes an already-transparent process more organized. That's useful. It's not the kind of structural revelation that drives my highest-conviction investments.

The bull case deserves honest construction. Stage is riding a genuine inflection point — the Cursor acquisition of Graphite at $290M proves code review is an acquisition target. Stratakos's hands-on experience building coding agents at Five Rings is precisely the domain expertise that matters. The "chapters" paradigm could be the right UX abstraction that wins developer hearts the way Notion won against Google Docs — through superior information architecture, not superior features. YC provides distribution to batch-mates and alumni companies. And at pre-seed, you're betting on wave and founders, not moats. Maybe Stage becomes the acqui-hire that the next Cursor-scale company buys for their review layer. But even in this bull case, I can't find the structural elements that define a Long Journey bet. The HQ Trivia exception — where product-cultural timing overrode my governance concerns — doesn't apply here because Stage isn't capturing a cultural lightning-bolt moment. The RTFKT exception — where a narrow timing window justified a consumer bet — doesn't apply because the AI code review window is wide open and well-populated. The condition that would flip me: if Stage had built proprietary infrastructure that learns from review patterns across thousands of repositories — a data flywheel that makes every subsequent review smarter in ways competitors can't replicate without the same corpus — that would change the defensibility picture entirely. But the dossier shows no evidence of this, and the product description points to a presentation-layer innovation, not a data infrastructure play.

Stage's cascading impact is limited to making already well-compensated software engineers slightly more efficient at their existing jobs. Better code review quality has downstream benefits for end users of software, but there's no mechanism here to expand economic access or shift power from gatekeepers to users. Serve Robotics expanded who could receive deliveries. Uber expanded who could get transportation. Stage makes engineers who already have great tools marginally better served. The two dimensions that lock my verdict are the absence of biographical forge (the founders spotted a gap at Five Rings, they weren't broken and rebuilt by this problem) and the absence of any invisible system being exposed (code review is transparent by design). Without either of those, Stage is a competent team in a crowded, consensus market — and that's not where I invest my own money.
