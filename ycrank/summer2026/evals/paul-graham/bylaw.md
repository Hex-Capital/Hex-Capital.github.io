# Bylaw -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Bylaw is a three-Waterloo-CS-first-years team shipping into the exact zone Galileo just open-sourced Agent Control in March 2026 -- a well-formed trio in a crowded observability adjacency, which is the inverse of the pattern I keep finding money in. The highest dimension here is cofounder configuration (Mazin Al-Ani, Farhan Ur Rehman, Gurshabd Singh Varaich met in first-year CS at Waterloo, so the relationship predates the company by years); the lowest is pre-funding animal resourcefulness, where the dossier searched for a GitHub trail on @gurshabdv and found "no notable starred repo identified," and searched for a Mazin Twitter and found nothing. That high/low pairing tells me what Bylaw is: a chemistry-correct team in a hot category with no visible tracks before the YC check arrived.

When I think about Bylaw against **Coinbase** (2012) on the animal axis, the contrast is what hurts. Brian Armstrong wrote a working Android Bitcoin wallet after I'd already mailed him a rejection -- that artifact was the entire reason **Coinbase** got into the batch. With Bylaw, the dossier says no Product Hunt launch, no Chrome extension, no public benchmark for the per-action latency of reconstructing an "evidence manifest across five places at once," no named pilot customer, no design partner logo. The site has no pricing page. Three Waterloo CS undergrads with Optiver, Meta and BitGo internships is a credible academic substrate -- but internships aren't artifacts. Animals leave tracks; the dossier looked for tracks and found absence, which I treat as evidence against rather than neutral.

The pass-example pattern I keep losing money to is the polished AI-wrapper pitched by clever teams who can articulate a real problem but have not personally suffered it. With Bylaw, the dossier says "[Inferred]: Co-founders cite internships at Meta, Optiver and BitGo... as the origin of the evidence-gate insight" -- and the "[Inferred]" prefix is doing all the work in that sentence. Drew Houston at **Dropbox** (2007) lost his USB stick on the Chinatown bus; the Collisons at **Stripe** (2010) had been hacking commerce since they were teenagers. Bylaw's origin story is that three first-year roommates noticed agent write-tools were unsafe -- which is plausible, but plausible is not the same as lived. There's no anecdote where one of them personally watched an agent destroy a refund at Optiver. The organic-idea filter wants founders who built it for themselves before anyone asked; Bylaw reads like founders who picked a thesis that's currently on every YC partner's whiteboard.

On schlep blindness -- this is where Bylaw most clearly fails my **Coinbase** (2012) test through inversion. **Coinbase** worked because Brian Armstrong ran toward bitcoin compliance grind in a batch where every other applicant was routing around it. Bylaw is running into a zone where Galileo ($68M raised, 834% revenue growth, six Fortune 50 customers including Comcast and Twilio) shipped open-source Agent Control in March 2026, Lakera just got bought by Check Point for ~$300M in November 2025, and NeMo Guardrails is sitting inside NVIDIA. That's the opposite of schlep blindness -- that's schlep visibility, with three well-capitalized incumbents already grinding the same problem. If Bylaw were doing the unglamorous work of manually integrating with twenty enterprise CRMs to verify customer-record agreement at write-time, I'd score this differently; but the dossier describes an SDK integration with LangSmith, Langfuse, OpenAI and Braintrust -- the same surface those vendors control.

On hacker genealogy -- compare Bylaw to **Reddit** (2005), where Aaron Swartz had co-authored the RSS specification before the seed check. Waterloo CS is a real hacker school, and the Optiver/BitGo internship vector is technically credible; financial infrastructure breeds the right paranoia about unverified writes. But the dossier looked for GitHub, looked for Twitter, looked for Hacker News presence, and came back with @gurshabdv "no notable starred repo identified" and no public account for the other two. Waterloo gets partial credit on substrate; the individuals get almost none on revealed work. This is the inverse of the Lisp-prize / RSS-spec pattern I over-index on.

On cofounder configuration -- this is the one dimension where Bylaw cleanly fits the pattern. Counterfactual: what would need to be true to flip this to a top-quartile score? Two of the three would need a publicly documented prior project they shipped together at Waterloo. The dossier says they met first year and are co-founding now, which is a multi-year relationship and a trio, both of which match **Airbnb** (2009)'s Chesky/Gebbia/Blecharczyk shape. What it lacks is differentiated coverage -- all three founders are SWE-track, and there's no operator or designer in the configuration. A trio of identical-shaped engineers is a working friendship but not necessarily complementary; the Airbnb trio worked because Blecharczyk shipped the code Chesky couldn't.

My 36-month forecast for Bylaw: acqui-hired by Galileo, Braintrust, or LangChain for under $25M as a team-and-tech tuck-in by mid-2029, with Mazin Al-Ani and Gurshabd Singh Varaich landing senior IC roles in the acquirer's agent-control product team. The mechanism is straightforward -- Galileo's Agent Control already covers the runtime-policy enforcement surface, the integration partners listed on usebylaw.com are the same vendors most likely to absorb the layer, and three first-time Waterloo undergrad founders without a published latency benchmark are unlikely to out-distribute a vendor with six Fortune 50 logos before runway pressure forces a conversation.

The strongest bull case against my pass: Bylaw's deterministic-policy compilation insight -- LLM helps compile policy, not enforce it at runtime -- is technically distinct from Galileo's approach, and the EU AI Act high-risk obligations applying August 2, 2026 create a forced-buyer wave that an SDK-shaped product can ride faster than an open-source control plane. Waterloo CS pairs and trios have shipped serious infrastructure before; the dossier's absence of a GitHub trail could simply reflect that students don't open-source production work. Counter: even granting the technical distinction, the dossier shows zero design partners, zero pilot logos, zero latency numbers on the critical path of agent execution -- and runtime evidence gates that add unmeasured latency to every write are an empirical question, not a thesis question. I would flip to invest if Bylaw published a named enterprise design partner (a Comcast-tier logo, not a YC-batchmate logo) with a measured false-block rate before Demo Day. Until then -- pass.
