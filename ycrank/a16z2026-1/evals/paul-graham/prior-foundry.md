# Prior Foundry -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Prior Foundry is tackling one of the gnarliest schleps in software: selling simulation tools to governments. Most AI founders I meet want to sell to developers or to enterprises with credit cards and 30-day sales cycles. Shirin Abrishami Kashani is selling to the OECD and Allegheny County, Pennsylvania — procurement offices, compliance reviews, budget cycles measured in fiscal years. That's a domain that actively repels the median YC applicant, and repulsion creates moats. When I invested in Stripe, part of what made the opportunity so large was that PCI compliance and bank integrations made every other developer-founder recoil. Prior Foundry's government schlep functions similarly — the $60K ARR they generated in their first two months of operation is remarkable precisely because government buyers almost never move that fast, which suggests genuine pull rather than founder hustle alone.

But here's where my framework starts to strain. Stripe was hackers building for hackers — Patrick Collison had been shipping products since he was a teenager, and the payment infrastructure problem grew directly from his frustration as a developer trying to accept money online. Prior Foundry is researchers commercializing their research. Jonne Kamphorst led the Stanford work on LLM-based behavioral simulation that became the product's core methodology; Shirin observed policy analysis inefficiency during her time as a junior analyst at the OECD's International Migration Division; Keshav Sivakumar brings Palantir's government deployment playbook plus a prior startup at Lumitics. The credentials map beautifully to the problem — maybe too beautifully. When a founding team's backgrounds read like a consulting firm's "why we're qualified" slide, I start wondering whether they discovered this problem or designed a company to match their resumes. Shirin's OECD experience is closer to organic discovery than market research — she was inside the institution watching policy analysis move at glacial speed — but it's institutional observation, not the visceral frustration that produced Dropbox or Octopart.

The idea itself is my biggest concern. "Simulate how entire populations respond to decisions before they're made" — who hears that and thinks it's a bad idea? Nobody. Every government official, every policy researcher, every journalist would nod approvingly. Compare this to Airbnb: "strangers will sleep in your apartment" made people visibly uncomfortable. Prior Foundry's pitch generates consensus enthusiasm, and consensus enthusiasm is precisely the signal I've learned to distrust. The best returns come from the intersection of "seems wrong" and "is right," and Prior Foundry sits squarely in "seems right" — which means either the returns have been competed away in advance, or the real difficulty is hidden somewhere the pitch doesn't surface. The simulation validity gap — 85% accuracy on survey replication versus actual policy outcome prediction — might be that hidden difficulty, but it reads more like a genuine technical risk than a contrarian insight the founders have cracked.

Keshav is the founder who most interests me. He co-founded Lumitics in 2016, raised seed funding, ran it for three years, then went to Palantir as a forward-deployed engineer — the role where you ship code inside government agencies with all the classification and compliance friction that entails. That's a founder who has built and shipped before, who understands government deployment at the ground level. Shirin brings the policy network that produced the OECD partnership across 38+ member governments. Jonne brings the science. As a team composition, this is unusually well-matched. But Jonne starting a full-time assistant professorship at Sciences Po Paris in January 2026 while serving as Chief Scientific Officer of a San Francisco-based startup — that's not burning the boats. That's keeping a very nice boat docked in Paris. At pre-seed, I want founders whose commitment is unambiguous. The foundational generative agent code is open-sourced on GitHub, which means Prior Foundry's scientific edge depends on Jonne's continued deep involvement in calibration and methodology — exactly the work that competes with his academic appointment for attention.

The bull case is genuinely strong. The OECD partnership gives Prior Foundry distribution leverage that most pre-seed companies couldn't achieve in five years of direct sales — 38 member governments through a single institutional relationship that Shirin built from her prior work there. The $1M+ pipeline through a distributor suggests this isn't just a pilot; there's real commercial demand. If simulation accuracy improves with more data and customer integrations — and it likely will, given the feedback loop between simulation outputs and real-world policy results — Prior Foundry could build a proprietary calibration advantage that the open-source foundation can't replicate. Government consulting is a market measured in hundreds of billions, and McKinsey can't build this product without cannibalizing their own billable hours. The "why now" is sharp: the Stanford paper demonstrating 85% behavioral accuracy landed in November 2024, creating a technical threshold that didn't exist eighteen months ago.

Prior Foundry scores well on team composition and early execution but falls short on the signals I weight most heavily. The idea doesn't repel — it generates immediate approval, which in my framework means the return premium has already been competed away or the real difficulty isn't where the founders think it is. The problem discovery is authentic but institutional rather than personal. I lack the resourcefulness stories — the creative workarounds, the weekend prototypes, the moments of desperate improvisation — that tell me founders have the running-back quality. Keshav's Lumitics experience and Palantir deployment work provide some of this signal, but the team as a whole presents as accomplished researchers entering the market, not obsessive builders who couldn't stop themselves from building. The $60K ARR in two months is the strongest counterargument to my skepticism, and it's a real one — but it's a single data point, and government pilots that start fast can stall for years before converting to scaled contracts.
