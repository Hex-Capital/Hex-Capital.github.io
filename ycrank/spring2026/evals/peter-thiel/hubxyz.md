# Hub.xyz -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The dossier lists Hub.xyz's direct competitors: Grass, valued at roughly a billion dollars with $17.5 million raised; Nodepay, with 1.5 million active users and $7 million in funding; and, by the company's own count, over 600 DePIN projects operating in the same broader category. This is not a competitive landscape slide — it is a confession. When multiple well-capitalized startups pursue identical architectures — distributed bandwidth collection, token incentives for node operators, AI training data as output — you are not looking at a company with a secret. You are looking at a commodity market forming in real time, with the participants racing to differentiate a fundamentally undifferentiated resource. The first question I ask any company is: what important truth have you discovered that the market denies? Hub.xyz's answer — that idle bandwidth can be converted into structured AI training data — is not denied by the market. It is affirmed by every other DePIN project in the sector. That is the opposite of a secret. It is consensus with a token attached.

The deeper structural problem is that the underlying data has no proprietary character. Hub.xyz's nodes collect information from the public internet — the same internet accessible to Grass, Nodepay, any centralized scraper, and any future entrant with sufficient capital to launch a token and recruit node operators. The "API for real-world training data" positioning attempts to differentiate on the structuring layer — the claim that Hub.xyz's data output is more organized, more multimodal, more enterprise-ready than competitors'. But structuring publicly available data is a feature, not a moat. Scale AI built a $29 billion business not on access to data but on a managed human workforce and deep enterprise integration — a genuine switching cost. Hub.xyz has neither the labor lock-in of Scale nor the proprietary dataset of a Clearview. It sits in a middle ground where the supply side (bandwidth contributors) faces zero switching costs — a user can and does run nodes on multiple DePIN networks simultaneously — and the demand side (enterprise AI teams) has no documented customers at all.

I will state the bull case plainly, because intellectual honesty requires it. If regulatory enforcement against centralized web scraping accelerates — and the trajectory of litigation in 2024-2025 suggests it might — then consent-based distributed data collection could become the only legally defensible pathway to fresh training data at web scale. In that scenario, DePIN bandwidth networks become structurally necessary rather than merely novel. Hub.xyz's YC affiliation gives it enterprise credibility that pure crypto competitors lack, and 500,000 node contributors represent real supply-side scale. The SwissBorg partnership with one million KYC-verified users is a genuine distribution asset for supply acquisition. If the regulatory thesis plays out and Hub.xyz executes the enterprise pivot better than Grass or Nodepay, this could become meaningful. But this argument applies identically to every company in the category — it is a thesis about the DePIN sector, not a thesis about Hub.xyz specifically. When the bull case for your company is indistinguishable from the bull case for your competitors, you do not have a monopoly path. You have a lottery ticket in a crowded drawing.

The team signals compound the concern. One co-founder, Léonie Nguyen, was appointed CEO of Meey Group — a Vietnamese proptech company — in October 2025 and is no longer listed on the YC page. When a co-founder departs to lead an unrelated company before the seed round closes, this is not a minor organizational adjustment. It signals that the founding team's commitment was contingent rather than absolute — the antithesis of the irreversible conviction I require. The remaining founders bring industrial engineering and business education credentials but no visible deep expertise in distributed systems, AI infrastructure, or the specific technical challenges of real-time multimodal data collection. The absence of any public GitHub repositories or technical artifacts reinforces the impression that this is a business-development-driven project wrapped in engineering language rather than a technically-anchored company with a business layer.

The token economy itself is a red flag I have seen before. When user growth is incentivized through IQ Points redeemable for $HUB tokens at a future Token Generation Event, you must ask whether the 500,000 users represent genuine demand for the product or speculative demand for the token. The cleantech pattern from a decade ago is instructive: companies that relied on government subsidies rather than technological superiority collapsed when the subsidies ended. Token incentives are the crypto equivalent — they attract participants whose loyalty extends precisely as far as the airdrop. When the token incentive disappears or competitors offer better yields, the network evaporates. This is not a foundation for monopoly. It is a foundation for mercenary capital that flows to the highest bidder.

This is a pure software middleware layer with no physical-world technology, no proprietary data asset, no government customer, and no visible secret beyond the category consensus. Competition has already formed. The company I would want to back in AI training data would possess something structurally unreplicable — perhaps a unique sensor network, a regulatory license, an exclusive institutional data partnership, or a technical approach to data synthesis that renders collection obsolete. Hub.xyz possesses none of these. I pass.
