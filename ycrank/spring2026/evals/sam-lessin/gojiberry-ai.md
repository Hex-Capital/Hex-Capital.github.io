# Gojiberry AI -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the thing that jumped off the page before I even got to the product: these founders listed the company for sale on TrustMRR for $11.15 million on March 9th — one week after their Product Hunt launch, and while they're sitting in a YC batch. That's not a red flag, that's a flare gun. It tells me exactly how the founders price their own company: as a cash-flowing SaaS asset worth a single-digit revenue multiple. And honestly? They're probably right. A $175K MRR tool with 75% margins and <5% churn is a beautiful small business. But I don't invest in beautiful small businesses. I invest in mispriced bets where the market is wrong. The founders themselves are telling you the market isn't wrong about this one — it's worth about $11 million. When the founder's own pricing matches the market's pricing, there's no asymmetry left for me.

Now look at the category. AI-powered sales outreach tools. Name a hotter consensus space in B2B SaaS right now — I'll wait. Apollo raised $251M and just acquired Pocus to do exactly what Gojiberry does. Clay hit $100M ARR at a $3.1B valuation. Instantly bootstrapped to $20M ARR. Lemlist is at $40M. HeyReach hit $10M. Every smart investor on the planet is looking at "AI + sales signals + automated outreach" and nodding vigorously. When every investor is nodding, the upside is already in the price. I look for the deals where investors are shaking their heads — where the discomfort creates the mispricing. There is zero discomfort in this category. This is the opposite of Solana at four cents when everyone said Ethereum had won.

The primitive test is even cleaner: this creates nothing new. Intent-based selling exists — 6sense and Bombora built the category a decade ago. LinkedIn automation exists — HeyReach and Expandi proved it. AI-generated outbound messaging exists — literally everyone does it now. What Gojiberry did is bundle these existing capabilities at a $99/mo price point for small teams. That's product packaging, not primitive creation. Nobody's going to say "Gojiberry me" the way people say "Venmo me." This is "better CRM for a specific segment" energy. Execution play, not category creation.

The AI kill test is where this gets brutal. LinkedIn signal detection plus LLM-generated personalized messages is exactly the kind of feature an incumbent bolts on in a quarter. Apollo acquiring Pocus in March 2026 isn't a rumor — it's proof the kill shot is already in motion. And here's the thing nobody wants to say out loud: LinkedIn itself could ship this as a native Sales Navigator feature tomorrow. Your entire signal layer and outreach channel depend on a single platform that could restrict API access, tighten automation enforcement, or just copy you. When I backed Metropolis, the moat was physical parking infrastructure that no software company could replicate. When I backed Teamshares, the moat was ownership of actual small businesses. Gojiberry's moat is... prompting an LLM better than Apollo does? That's a feature, not a company.

The strongest bull case: these founders are genuinely exceptional operators. Three prior exits between them. Pierre-Eliott and Roman bootstrapped CoCo AI to €500K ARR and sold it. Dylan built Edusign to $3.3M revenue and 600K users. They went from zero to $175K MRR spending $380 a month on distribution. That's insane capital efficiency. If the thesis is "these founders will always find a way to build profitable software," I buy it completely. But that thesis is an argument for investing in them personally — maybe through an ISA-style deal like I did with Marina Mogilko — not for investing in this specific software company that sits directly in the crosshairs of every well-funded sales tech incumbent on earth. The founders' own sale listing tells the real story: they know how to build and flip. They've done it three times. This is number four.

The capital discipline here is genuinely best-in-class — 75% profit margins, $127 CAC, 1.3-month payback period. If every YC company ran this tight, the venture industry would be in a different place. But capital discipline in a commodity category just means you die profitable instead of dying broke. The outcome is the same: Apollo or LinkedIn absorbs the market and you're left with a nice seven-figure exit. Which, again, is exactly what the TrustMRR listing suggests the founders are angling for. Good for them. Not my bet.
