# Crebit -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Stripe launched stablecoin payouts across 100+ countries in 2025. Mastercard unveiled end-to-end stablecoin transaction capabilities the same year. Brex announced stablecoin payments. Latitude raised $8M from NEA to build stablecoin-based cross-border payouts. And now Crebit proposes to use stablecoins to settle international tuition payments into the U.S. I ask the canonical question -- what important truth do very few people agree with you on? -- and the answer comes back: "stablecoins reduce cross-border payment costs." This is not a secret. It is the consensus of every fintech investor, every payments infrastructure company, and every major card network on earth. When Stripe and Mastercard are building the same rails you claim as your innovation, you are not building zero to one. You are building one to n, in a race against opponents with infinitely more capital and distribution than a two-person team in San Francisco.

The Wish comparison is instructive. Wish competed on price in e-commerce against Amazon and Alibaba -- no proprietary technology, no network effects, no structural moat. It IPO'd at $14 billion and collapsed over 90%. Crebit competes on price in cross-border payments against Flywire ($623M TTM revenue, deep incumbent university relationships), Convera ($500M+ annual revenue, 30K+ global customers), and now the stablecoin divisions of the world's largest payments infrastructure companies. The "4-10% cheaper than traditional banks" claim is a cost advantage built on someone else's technology -- Circle's and Tether's stablecoin rails -- which means the advantage is available to every competitor who adopts the same settlement layer. Cost advantages without proprietary technology are not moats; they are temporary arbitrage that disappears the moment an incumbent decides to capture the same margin.

Crebit's founders, Jensen Coonradt and Simmi Sen, explicitly report they started building after the GENIUS Act passed in July 2025. A company born in response to regulatory change, rather than in defiance of consensus, is structurally reactive. My framework demands founders who see a future others cannot and make irreversible commitments toward it -- not founders who wait for Congress to clear the path. Jensen's technical credentials are real: MIT EECS, MLH Top 50 out of 600,000 participants, a prior project acquired by BankSocial. Simmi built a streetwear brand carried in boutiques across five cities and worn by recording artists. These are talented young people. But talent deployed in a competitive market is talent wasted. The co-founders met at a Bank of America scholarship program -- not in the trenches of a prior high-stakes venture where they proved they could survive together under existential pressure. They graduated high school in 2024. I created the Thiel Fellowship precisely because I believe exceptional young founders can build extraordinary companies outside the university system -- but the company they build must be a monopoly, and Crebit's market structure forecloses that possibility.

The strongest argument for Crebit is its distribution velocity: 45 U.S. university partnerships and $1M+ in FX volume within three months of launch. That is genuine pull, and the B2B2C playbook -- embed in the bursar's portal, capture the student flow -- mirrors the approach that built Flywire into a public company. University portal integration does create real switching costs. If Crebit could lock in hundreds of universities before Stripe or Flywire adopt stablecoin settlement, those institutional relationships might constitute a defensible position. The business model cannibalization argument also has merit: Flywire profits from the very FX spread inefficiency that stablecoins eliminate, creating a classic innovator's dilemma. But Flywire's $1.43 billion market cap defines the ceiling, not the floor, of what Crebit could become. Even perfect execution in this niche does not produce a power-law outcome. The company might succeed and still be irrelevant to fund returns -- which is precisely the structural problem with competitive markets.

Crebit's regulatory exposure compounds the structural issues. The dossier notes that Flywire flagged $30M in potential revenue pressure from immigration policy changes affecting international student flows. Crebit's entire customer base -- international students paying U.S. tuition -- is a population whose volume is determined by visa policy, not by product quality. A company whose revenue depends on both favorable stablecoin regulation (the GENIUS Act) and favorable immigration policy is doubly exposed to political risk -- the exact fragility my framework identifies when "the company's plan depends on favorable regulatory change." Meanwhile, operating across Brazil, Mexico, Nigeria, Philippines, UAE, the U.S., and Europe requires money transmission licensing in each jurisdiction -- a compliance burden that consumes a two-person team's resources without creating any technology that competitors cannot replicate.

The physical-world moat is nonexistent. Crebit is a pure software layer built on top of stablecoin infrastructure it does not own (Circle, Tether), local payment rails it did not build (PIX, UPI, M-Pesa), and university relationships that Flywire has spent years cultivating. There is no hardware component, no proprietary data asset, no physical barrier to replication. This is fintech plumbing -- useful, perhaps even profitable at scale, but the opposite of the atoms-based technological discontinuity that commands my highest conviction. The decisive dimensions are Monopoly Secret and Power Law Return Magnitude. The absence of any non-consensus insight -- when the entire payments industry is simultaneously pursuing stablecoin settlement -- means Crebit is competing, not monopolizing. And Flywire's $1.43B market cap as the established category leader caps the realistic upside well below fund-returner territory. Pass.
