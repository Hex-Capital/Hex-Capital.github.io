# Maquoketa Research -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Anduril has raised $6.9B and is building a $1B factory to produce tens of thousands of autonomous systems annually starting mid-2026. AeroVironment holds a $990M Army contract and is scaling Switchblade production to 1,200 units per month. Shield AI projects $540M+ in 2026 revenue. Helsing has a €268M German government contract. Maquoketa Research is a five-person pre-seed team in Elk Grove Village, Illinois, entering this exact market with approximately $500K in YC funding and no public traction. I backed Anduril when defense tech was considered uninvestable by Silicon Valley -- bipartisan procurement reform and the failure of legacy primes to innovate created a generational structural opening that most investors had dismissed. The structural shift Maquoketa Research is riding is the same one, except in 2026 it's no longer nonobvious. It's the consensus hottest category in venture capital. When I look at the loitering munition market ($5.36B in 2025, projected $13.26B by 2030 per MarketsandMarkets), I see a real and growing market -- but one where the timing window for new entrants has narrowed dramatically because the well-funded incumbents have already recognized it and are scaling aggressively.

Maquoketa Research builds end-product hardware -- autonomous one-way attack drones -- not infrastructure that other businesses depend on. Their GitHub organization contains nine public repositories, all forks of existing open-source projects (PX4-Autopilot, QGroundControl, MAVLink, HackRF, AM32 motor firmware). There's no original autonomy platform, no API layer, no developer ecosystem. Contrast this with how Stripe became the payments API that an entire generation of internet businesses built on, or how Harvey is embedding into legal workflows with compounding switching costs. Maquoketa Research is building a discrete hardware product for sale to government buyers -- no network effects, no platform dynamics, no compounding switching costs beyond whatever contract lock-in defense procurement provides. The vertical integration claim on their website is an operational strategy, not an infrastructure moat.

Evan Yeager (CEO, UC Berkeley EECS, previously at SpiderRock and Stylis.AI) and Alejandro Hernandez (CTO, UC Berkeley EECS, Berkeley Wireless Research Center) bring complementary technical backgrounds -- software systems and RF/phased array design respectively. Hernandez's phased array expertise is directly relevant to contested electromagnetic environments, which is a genuine technical differentiator if Maquoketa Research can develop proprietary RF capabilities that work in GPS-denied, EW-saturated battlefields. But I count the miracles: the autonomy stack must achieve performance that exceeds what Anduril's and Shield AI's far larger engineering teams are building (technology miracle), the team must navigate 18-36 month DoD procurement cycles with no existing defense relationships or security clearances (distribution miracle), and they must scale hardware manufacturing of custom electronics with pre-seed capital (manufacturing miracle). Three independent miracles, compounding odds. My framework is explicit: "If you have more than one, you have compounding small odds and that means you're very, very likely to fail." Neither founder has defense industry experience, prior exits, or evidence of operating at a scaling company -- the operator-scaler profile I look for after writing High Growth Handbook is absent here.

The strongest bull case: the DoD explicitly wants multiple domestic suppliers for supply chain resilience, the Replicator initiative is designed to onboard new entrants, and the market is growing fast enough ($13.26B by 2030) that even a sub-1% share produces meaningful revenue. Maquoketa Research's "built entirely in the USA" positioning and vertical integration address real NDAA compliance requirements. The structural barrier against legacy primes is genuine -- Lockheed and Raytheon face unit economics incompatibility because their revenue models depend on $100K+ munitions, creating economic disincentive to compete in the sub-$10K expendable segment. And the CTO's Berkeley Wireless Research Center background in phased array systems could produce a defensible RF capability that matters in near-peer conflict scenarios where electromagnetic warfare degrades GPS and communications. I checked my Anduril exception: "When Nonobvious Market Timing scores near maximum, accept multi-miracle risk." But that exception requires the market timing to be nonobvious, and defense tech in 2026 is the opposite -- it's consensus. Palmer Luckey brought a proven track record (Oculus, $2B exit) and the market was being ignored. Maquoketa Research brings first-time founders and the market is attracting billions in capital.

Maquoketa Research's margin architecture is hardware-dependent with no evidence of software licensing or recurring revenue. Per-unit hardware sales to government customers face margin pressure from both manufacturing costs (custom phased array electronics, airframes, propulsion systems) and procurement competition against AeroVironment's Switchblade 300 at ~$6,000 per unit with massive production scale advantages. Defense hardware businesses rarely achieve the 70%+ gross margins I look for, and there's no pricing power signal -- Maquoketa Research would be a price-taker in a market where incumbents can leverage production economies of scale. The business model looks more like my Wish cautionary tale than my Stripe infrastructure thesis: hardware product in a competitive market without structural margin protection.

The one condition that would flip me: if Maquoketa Research demonstrated a proprietary autonomy or RF capability -- specifically, autonomous terminal guidance performance in GPS-denied, EW-contested environments -- that measurably outperforms Anduril's Altius and AeroVironment's Switchblade in contested conditions, validated by a DoD test or OTA contract. That would convert the multi-miracle problem into a single-miracle problem (distribution follows from demonstrated technical superiority in defense) and would indicate a technology threshold advantage that capital alone can't replicate. Without that, Maquoketa Research is a pre-seed hardware startup entering an obvious market against competitors with a combined $11B+ in funding, requiring three simultaneous breakthroughs to reach product-market fit.
