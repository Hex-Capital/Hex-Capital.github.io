# Maquoketa Research -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Anduril has raised $6.9 billion and is building a $1 billion factory to produce tens of thousands of autonomous systems starting July 2026. AeroVironment holds a $990 million Army Switchblade contract and is scaling to 1,200 units per month. Shield AI projects $540 million in 2026 revenue. Into this arena walks Maquoketa Research — five people in Elk Grove Village, Illinois, with forked GitHub repos and a website that returns a 403 error. When I backed Anduril, Palmer Luckey was persona non grata in Silicon Valley, defense tech was politically toxic, and most VCs wouldn't return his calls. That was contrarian. In 2026, defense tech has attracted tens of billions in venture capital, and autonomous attack drones are the subject of urgent government procurement programs. Maquoketa Research is not entering a space that makes sophisticated investors shift in their chairs — they're entering a space where sophisticated investors are falling over each other to write checks. The audacity of the competitive mismatch is notable, but audacity against incumbents is different from audacity against consensus. I backed SpaceX when rockets were exploding; I backed Flock Safety when surveillance cameras provoked ethical outrage. Maquoketa Research is building something most VCs would enthusiastically fund — the contrarian element is only their size relative to competition, not the thesis itself.

Evan Yeager studied EECS at Berkeley and worked at SpiderRock and Stylis.AI. Alejandro Hernandez studied EECS at Berkeley and researched phased array systems and RF design at the Berkeley Wireless Research Center. David Muchow and Aidan Williams have "limited public data" in the dossier. When I sit down with founders, my opening question is always the same: tell me your story, not the business. Maquoketa Research gives me credentials without crucible. I see no military service, no defense industry background, no personal encounter with the problem of soldiers dying because they lack affordable precision strike. Hernandez's RF and phased array specialization is genuinely relevant — that Berkeley Wireless Research Center background maps directly onto contested electromagnetic environments where Maquoketa Research's drones would need to operate. But technical relevance is not biographical inevitability. These are smart Berkeley engineers who appear to have identified a hot market, and I cannot see evidence that this problem found them rather than the reverse. Two of the four co-founders are essentially invisible to me, which at pre-seed is not unusual, but combined with the absence of any biographical narrative for the visible founders, it leaves the dimension that matters most to me — the forge — almost entirely empty.

Maquoketa Research does sit at a genuine structural inflection. The dossier cites Ukraine demonstrating that cheap autonomous drones neutralize armored vehicles costing orders of magnitude more, the U.S. Army's fiscal 2026 budget proposing loitering munitions at brigade scale, and Germany committing roughly $632 million to loitering munition contracts in 2025. The market is projected to reach $13.26 billion by 2030 at 19.9% CAGR. And there is a real invisible system buried here that I find compelling in structure if not in personal resonance: defense primes like Lockheed and Raytheon depend on revenue models built around $100,000-plus precision munitions, creating an economic disincentive to cannibalize their own business with sub-$10,000 expendable drones. That is a genuinely extractive structure — taxpayers fund weapons systems whose pricing is protected by industrial incumbents who benefit from opacity in procurement. Maquoketa Research's vertical integration and "built entirely in the USA" positioning attack this from the manufacturing side. But the opacity here is institutional, not personal. Nobody's grandmother is being exploited by information asymmetry the way credit card holders are exploited by the system Affirm exposed. The disruption is real but it doesn't land in the part of my framework where I feel it in my bones.

The bull case an advocate would make is strong and specific: Maquoketa Research occupies the precise market position where the innovator's dilemma creates structural protection. AeroVironment and Anduril are scaling up, but neither can serve every infantry brigade — the DoD explicitly wants multiple domestic suppliers for supply chain resilience. The team's full-stack approach (GitHub forks of PX4-Autopilot, QGroundControl, MAVLink, HackRF, AM32 motor firmware) shows they are building across the entire drone stack from flight controller firmware through RF communications to ground control. Hernandez's phased array expertise could deliver real differentiation in GPS-denied and electronically contested environments where commodity FPV drones fail. The ITAR and NDAA compliance barriers protect against foreign competitors. And the timing — with the Army's fiscal 2026 budget explicitly funding brigade-level loitering munitions — could not be better. This is the kind of infrastructure-at-inflection bet I have made before and made well. I take this case seriously. But when I run the ten-year question — where does Maquoketa Research end up in a decade? — I see either a successful acquisition target for one of the giants, or a company ground down by procurement cycles and capital requirements that favor Anduril's $1 billion Arsenal-1 factory over a 5-person shop in Illinois. The path to independent, category-defining infrastructure company is obscured by the capital intensity of hardware manufacturing and the multi-year defense sales cycles that will stress a pre-seed balance sheet long before contract revenue arrives.

The condition that would flip me to Invest is specific and verifiable: evidence that one of Maquoketa Research's founders has a biographical connection to this problem that I cannot see in the dossier — military service, a family member's combat experience, firsthand witnessing of the problem they're solving — combined with a demonstration that their autonomy stack delivers measurably superior performance in contested RF environments versus Anduril's Altius or AeroVironment's Switchblade. The first would address the forge gap; the second would give me confidence in their path through the competitive gauntlet. Without both, Maquoketa Research is a technically competent team entering a consensus-hot market against competitors with three to four orders of magnitude more capital, and the dimensions that drive my highest-conviction bets — biographical inevitability, genuine contrarianism, personal resonance with an invisible system — remain unfilled.
