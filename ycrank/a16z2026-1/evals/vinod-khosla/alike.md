# Alike -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Let me start where I always start: what happens to the world if Alike fully succeeds? Enterprise teams coordinate better. Meetings shrink. Status updates automate. AI agents talk to each other instead of humans relaying messages between tools. And my honest reaction is: so what? The consequence of success here is a more efficient enterprise — useful, profitable maybe, but nobody's life fundamentally changes. No basic human need becomes radically cheaper. No cost structure collapses from $X to $X/100. When I backed Commonwealth Fusion Systems, the consequence was limitless clean energy. When I backed Impossible Foods, the consequence was feeding billions without animal agriculture. The consequence of Alike is that knowledge workers spend less time in Slack. I've seen this pitch dozens of times at YC — "large TAM, underserved by technology, we automate coordination overhead." My framework requires the world to look visibly different if the company succeeds. Alike's success produces a moderately successful SaaS company, which is fine for most investors but not for me.

Max Van Kleek's 9,012 Google Scholar citations in human-AI interaction and privacy-preserving systems represent genuine academic depth — this isn't a wrapper team slapping GPT-4 on calendar invites. But here's the problem: Max is still a full-time Associate Professor at Oxford while serving as CTO. Dust has raised $21.5M from Sequoia and is at $7.3M ARR. CrewAI raised $18M from Insight Partners. Slack is rolling out 30 new AI agent features and auto-provisioning for every new Salesforce customer. Alike is competing against these forces with a part-time CTO and no revenue. The technology risk I prefer over market risk requires a technology that creates an unfair advantage — and I see no evidence of a proprietary architecture or scientific breakthrough here. "Learns team coordination patterns and adapts" is a product description, not a technology moat. Google's open-source A2A protocol, backed by 50+ partners including Salesforce, SAP, and Workday under Linux Foundation governance, is commoditizing the exact interoperability layer Alike claims to build.

Addi Haran Diman completing a PhD at 21 after starting university at 13 — that signals raw intellectual horsepower. I respect that. But the founding team reveals a pattern I find concerning: Addi and Danial Hussain were consecutive Oxford Students' Union Presidents, Max is their professor, and they describe themselves as "best friends." When I talk about engineering the gene pool, I mean assembling founder-quality people with diverse, complementary capabilities. Two student politicians and their professor from the same university is a team optimized for comfort, not for the diverse cognitive collision that builds great companies. I see no evidence of what this team learned during their building phase, what surprised them, how they pivoted. The traction — 10 live deployments, 20 design partners, 100 waitlist companies — is entirely self-reported through the a16z Speedrun portal with no independent verification and no revenue signal.

The strongest bull case for Alike: Gartner reports a 1,445% surge in multi-agent system inquiries, and predicts 40% of enterprise apps will embed AI agents by 2026. If agents proliferate at that rate, someone becomes the coordination intelligence layer — the Cisco of agent communication. Alike's privacy architecture (per-person, per-channel granular controls) could be a genuine differentiator in regulated enterprises where Slack's native agent features can't meet compliance requirements. If I squint, there's a version where Alike's learned organizational knowledge creates switching costs that compound over time, making it defensible despite the commoditization of the underlying protocol layer. For this to work, though, Max would need to leave Oxford and commit full-time, the team would need to demonstrate that their coordination intelligence is technically discontinuous rather than incremental, and they'd need to move faster than Salesforce's product organization — none of which is evidenced in the dossier.

I check my known exceptions and none apply. DoorDash overrode my technical novelty requirement because the consequence magnitude of transforming food access was planetary-scale — enterprise coordination efficiency is not. Oscar Health overrode my regulatory risk aversion because the affordability thesis was transformative for healthcare — Alike has no radical affordability thesis. This is a competent team building an incremental improvement in a market being aggressively contested by incumbents (Slack/Salesforce) and well-funded startups (Dust at $7.3M ARR, CrewAI at $18M raised), with the interoperability layer itself being commoditized by an open standard from Google. The two dimensions that lock this as a pass: consequence magnitude — better enterprise coordination is not a changed world — and non-linear technical approach — no evidence of a 10x improvement or proprietary technology that creates an unfair advantage over competitors with 10-100x more capital.
