# Crosslayer Labs -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The most striking fact in this dossier is that Crosslayer Labs' founding team literally invented a global Internet security standard -- MPIC, now mandated by the CA/Browser Forum and running at Let's Encrypt, Google Trust Services, and Cloudflare, securing roughly 8 million certificates every single day. Prateek Mittal holds an ACM Grace Murray Hopper Award, shaped the Tor network, and is a tenured Princeton professor. Henry Birge-Lee coded the first MPIC implementation as an undergrad and has 15+ publications at the most competitive security venues. This is an absurdly credentialed team. If you're looking for "sane person," you will not find a more credible founding trio for Internet security infrastructure anywhere in this YC batch or probably any batch. The problem is: where's the "insane idea"? Cross-layer impersonation detection is the most logical, most sensible commercial extension of their research I can imagine. Every security VC in the world would nod approvingly at this pitch. Nobody is confused or scared by it. That means the non-consensus premium -- the thing I'm actually trying to buy at seed -- isn't in the price.

Here's the structural issue I can't get past. The team already built the protocol. MPIC IS the new primitive -- it's open, it's mandated, it's deployed globally. That's a home run that already happened in academia. Crosslayer Labs, the company, is building an application-layer monitoring product on top of that protocol-layer expertise. Most investors will look at this and say "they invented the standard, so the company is a protocol play." I see the opposite. The standard is open. The company is a SaaS monitoring dashboard -- it's literally tagged "SaaS, B2B, Cybersecurity" on the YC page. The inferred business model is subscription pricing for monitoring domains and assets. This is the exact category I've been publicly arguing is getting killed. Software companies that had moats are learning they aren't moats anymore, and a monitoring tool -- however sophisticated the signal correlation is today -- faces the reality that AI is going to make it cheaper and faster for competitors to build similar cross-layer detection capabilities. The knowledge moat is real in 2026. I'm less confident it survives to 2030.

The bull case is genuinely strong, and I want to be honest about what would have to be true for this to be a great investment. If the cross-layer correlation engine becomes a data primitive -- if Crosslayer accumulates enough attack pattern signatures across enough customers that their detection capability compounds in a way that new entrants can't replicate -- then this stops being a SaaS monitoring tool and starts being security infrastructure. Think of it like Cloudflare's trajectory: started as a CDN tool, became Internet infrastructure because of accumulated network intelligence. If Crosslayer's dataset becomes the authoritative map of how Internet impersonation attacks propagate, and other security tools need to integrate with their data, that's an infinity story. The MPIC mandate also creates a genuine market education tailwind -- CISOs who never thought about BGP-layer attacks are now aware of them because of these founders' own work. And the vertical focus on crypto, healthcare, and fintech puts them in segments where impersonation attacks cause catastrophic, measurable financial damage, which means high willingness to pay. If the team executes the academic-to-commercial transition well and builds genuine enterprise sales muscle, the ceiling is real.

But I keep coming back to the structural analysis. CrowdStrike has 15.8% mindshare in attack surface management. Palo Alto and Microsoft are in this space. When a large security platform decides to add cross-layer impersonation detection as a feature -- and they will, because these founders helpfully educated the entire market by publishing 15+ papers explaining exactly how these attacks work -- the standalone value proposition compresses. The founders' academic openness, which is what makes them credible, is also what makes their knowledge moat permeable. They published the playbook. The incumbents have the distribution. This is the exact dynamic I worry about in every security SaaS deal: specialized detection capability gets absorbed into platform bundles within 3-5 years.

The team's relationship structure is solid -- multi-year research collaboration at Princeton, not a speed-dating co-founder match -- and the advisor-turned-co-founder pattern with Mittal adds gravitas without the dysfunction risk of three strangers. There's real tizz potential here; people who push an Internet standard through the CA/Browser Forum know how to grind. But I see zero revenue signal, zero traction signal beyond YC acceptance, and a Calendly link as the go-to-market. At pre-seed I don't penalize heavily for that, but the complete absence of even a pilot customer or LOI means I'm investing purely on team credibility and narrative, with no downside cushion whatsoever.

This is a sane person with a sane idea, and in my framework, that's a competent bet at fair odds -- not the asymmetric opportunity I'm hunting for. The founders deserve enormous respect for what they've built for the Internet. But the company they're building off that foundation looks like a B2B security SaaS play in a market where incumbents have distribution and AI is compressing the value of software-layer differentiation. I'd want to see evidence that this is becoming infrastructure, not just a tool, before writing a check.
