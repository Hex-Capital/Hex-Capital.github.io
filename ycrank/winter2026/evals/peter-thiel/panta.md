# Panta -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The most important fact about Panta is not in its dossier -- it is in its competitive landscape. Harper, Fernstone, Acolite, COVU, and Panta are all pursuing the same thesis at the same time: that AI agents can automate the insurance brokerage. When five companies in overlapping YC batches converge on the same insight, I know one thing with certainty: there is no secret here. A secret is something important that few people agree with you on. "AI can replace human insurance brokers" is something that at least five funded founding teams, their investors, and Gustaf Alstromer all agree on. This is mimetic convergence -- companies imitating each other into a competitive market that will destroy value for everyone. The Girardian dynamics are precise: each company looks at the others and feels validated rather than alarmed, when they should feel the opposite. Harper has already raised $47 million and placed coverage for 5,000 businesses. Panta is entering a market where a well-capitalized rival is executing the identical playbook with a twelve-month head start and roughly a hundred times more capital. This is not zero to one. This is one to five.

Panta's claim to differentiation is its focus on the hardest-to-place risks -- construction, hazmat trucking, oil and gas -- within the Excess & Surplus market. This is a reasonable market segmentation choice, but market segmentation is not a secret. It is a positioning decision that any competitor can replicate once the niche proves profitable. Harper focuses on small-to-mid-sized businesses broadly today; nothing prevents them from moving upmarket into complex E&S placements tomorrow, armed with fifty million dollars and thousands of existing carrier relationships generating real placement data. The E&S niche gives Panta perhaps eighteen months of relative differentiation before Harper, or a well-funded vertical player, simply copies the focus. A genuine secret creates a structural barrier -- network effects, proprietary technology, economies of scale -- that makes replication impossible, not merely inconvenient.

The underlying technology compounds the problem. Panta's agents run on Mac Mini infrastructure navigating carrier portals, email, and phone systems using what is fundamentally large language model orchestration. This is competent systems engineering, not proprietary technology. The LLM stack is commodity infrastructure available to every AI startup on earth. The carrier portal integrations are bespoke but not defensible -- any team with sufficient funding can build the same integrations. When I invested in Palantir, the core technology was genuinely novel: algorithms adapted from PayPal's fraud detection that no one else had built. When Founders Fund backed Stripe, the API abstraction layer was an order of magnitude simpler than anything available. Panta's technology does the same thing faster -- it is a speed improvement on an existing process, not a capability that did not previously exist. The company claims 10x faster turnaround, which may be true, but the 38% cost reduction on a single placement is not a 10x improvement. This is a better brokerage, not a new category.

The bull case deserves a full airing because it is not trivial. Newfront was acquired for $1.3 billion with a human-heavy model generating $300 million in revenue. If Panta achieves even $50 million in brokerage commissions with a four-person team and near-zero marginal cost per placement, the margin structure would be extraordinary -- a service business genuinely operating with software economics. The E&S market is growing at 12.5% annually and the broker workforce cannot scale to meet demand, which means the businesses Panta targets are genuinely underserved. Vincent Chen's experience building agentic systems at Google -- NotebookLM, Vertex AI, the Ask Photos feature deployed to billions -- is directly relevant to the technical challenge. Both founders obtaining their own broker licenses shows they have immersed themselves in the domain rather than building from the outside. If Panta's accumulated placement data on carrier appetite creates a proprietary intelligence layer -- knowing which carriers accept which complex risks at what price -- that dataset could become genuinely defensible over time. The question is whether they can accumulate that data faster than Harper, and with $47 million less capital, the answer is almost certainly no.

The founders are technically capable but I see no evidence of the kind of conviction that defines my best investments. There is no indication that Chen or Wang have refused an easier path, articulated a civilizational vision, or demonstrated the intellectual independence that makes a founder ungovernable by market consensus. They built a good product in a real market. That is the profile of a competent startup, not a monopoly. The company's framing -- "brokerage run by AI agents, scales like software" -- is a business model description, not a thesis about how the world works. I look for founders who can tell me something true that I did not already know. "Insurance brokerage is inefficient and can be automated" is something everyone already knows; the proof is that five companies are doing it simultaneously.

This is a pass. The market is real, the team is capable, and the product works. But every great company is built around a secret, and Panta's insight is shared by a crowded field of competitors pursuing the same thesis with the same technology at the same time. I would rather invest in a company that is wrong about something important than one that is right about something everyone already believes.
