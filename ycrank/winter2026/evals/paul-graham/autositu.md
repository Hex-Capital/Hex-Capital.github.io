# AutoSitu -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Municipal zoning review is one of the ugliest schleps in American infrastructure. Every one of the roughly 19,000 municipalities in the U.S. has its own zoning code, its own building ordinances, its own departmental review workflow. There's no standardization. A site plan that passes in San Jose might fail in San Mateo for reasons that exist only in that city's specific ordinance language. Thousands of engineers have looked at this space and unconsciously flinched — not because the AI is hard, but because the regulatory fragmentation is nauseating. You'd have to encode the idiosyncratic rules of each jurisdiction, then embed yourself into municipal procurement processes that move at glacial speed, then deal with liability questions around automated code compliance for physical buildings. That's three layers of schlep stacked on top of each other. Most smart people's unconscious filters this out before it even reaches conscious consideration.

What caught my attention is how Xuanshu Lin found this problem. He didn't read a TechCrunch article about GovTech and decide to build an AI tool. He studied urban planning at Michigan, then went to Harvard's Graduate School of Design, and worked on actual policy and design projects in places like Detroit and Champaign-Urbana. He was inside the municipal review apparatus. He saw planners manually cross-referencing zoning codes against submitted drawings, department by department, for weeks on end. This is the kind of organic discovery I look for — the founder didn't choose the problem, the problem chose the founder. And his co-founder George Zhai brings the engineering complement: Georgia Tech, UIUC, production AI systems at GM and McLaren, seven patents, experience with LLM fine-tuning. One founder who lived inside the broken system, one who can build the AI to fix it. That's a well-constructed team for this specific problem.

The traction numbers are what push this from interesting to compelling. 1,500+ plans checked and engagements with eleven named cities — San Jose, Seattle, Long Beach, Boston, Los Angeles, Dallas, San Francisco, and others — for a two-person pre-seed team. I don't know how many of those are paid versus pilots, and that matters. But getting eleven cities to even try your software means navigating procurement bureaucracies that are specifically designed to prevent new vendors from getting in. That requires the kind of door-to-door, do-things-that-don't-scale intensity that separates founders who execute from founders who plan. The fact that Seattle's mayor signed an executive order in June 2025 directing AI review of all development applications tells me the market timing is converging from the demand side, not just the supply side.

The concern I keep circling is competitive density. PermitFlow has raised $91 million. Blitz AI claims to be the most widely adopted platform in its category. CivCheck got acquired by Clariti. Archistar has $22.7 million and real revenue. When I see five funded competitors in a space, my instinct is that the category is becoming consensus — and consensus ideas have already been priced. But when I look closer, AutoSitu's positioning is genuinely different. PermitFlow serves developers navigating the permit process from the outside. AutoSitu embeds inside the city's own review workflow, coordinating across departments. That's not the same product serving the same buyer. It's like the difference between building a tool for job seekers versus building a tool for HR departments — same industry, different wedge, different sales motion. The municipal-side positioning is actually harder to execute (longer sales cycles, more stakeholders), which means it's also harder for competitors to replicate. The schlep protects them.

The technical founder question gives me some pause. Zhai has impressive credentials — production AI at GM and McLaren, patents, ML experience — but I don't see public GitHub repos or evidence that he personally built the current product architecture. At pre-seed with a working product that has processed 1,500+ plan sets, someone built something real. But I'd want to understand how much is custom engineering versus prompt-chaining on top of existing LLMs. The commoditization risk is real: the core capability of "run a document through an LLM and check it against regulatory text" is reproducible. What's not reproducible is the jurisdiction-specific knowledge accumulated across those 1,500 reviews and the workflow integration with municipal systems. That's where the moat lives — in the operational depth, not the technology.

The bull case is strong enough that I'd invest. A domain expert who lived inside the broken system, paired with a production AI engineer, attacking a problem that's both obvious and avoided, with real municipal deployments already in hand, at a moment when cities are actively mandating AI review adoption. Government sales cycles are long, but once you're in, switching costs are enormous — cities aren't going to rip out their review infrastructure every year. If AutoSitu can convert even a fraction of those eleven city engagements into recurring contracts, they'll have a foothold that's very hard to displace. The housing crisis provides sustained political tailwind. This is the kind of boring, important infrastructure play that doesn't get attention at Demo Day but compounds quietly.
