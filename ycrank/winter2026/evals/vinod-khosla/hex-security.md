# Hex Security -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Hex Security is that nobody thinks autonomous penetration testing is a bad idea. XBOW has $117 million. Novee has $51.5 million. Aikido just hit a billion-dollar valuation with $13 million in ARR. Two other YC companies in adjacent batches are building essentially the same product. When I backed Juniper to build TCP/IP routers, Cisco's CTO told me they would never work. When I invested in Impossible Foods, people thought plant-based hamburgers were a joke. When I wrote my check to OpenAI in 2019, AI was laughable. The signal I look for — experts dismissing the approach, consensus saying it's impossible — is entirely absent here. Everyone agrees this is coming. That's not a signal to invest. That's a signal that the contrarian alpha has already been competed away.

The consequence magnitude question gives me pause. Hex frames the problem at $10 trillion in annual vulnerability losses, but the company's actual addressable market — penetration testing — is $2-4 billion. Even if you believe AI-automated pentesting captures the entire market and expands it somewhat, success means building a strong security testing business, not replacing a trillion-dollar system. Compare this to what drew me to Commonwealth Fusion Systems: if fusion works, the entire global energy infrastructure changes. If Hex Security succeeds, companies get better security reports faster and cheaper. That's valuable — but the consequence is operational improvement, not structural transformation of how the world works. The difference between automating a consulting service and eliminating an entire category of human activity is the difference between a good business and a world-changing one.

The bull case deserves honest examination. If autonomous offensive agents become sophisticated enough, the company that builds the best attack capability could become the foundation of a new security paradigm — not just pentesting but continuous, intelligent defense derived from understanding how attacks actually chain. The $10T framing isn't entirely hollow; the real prize would be becoming the AI that understands vulnerability at a fundamental level, across every codebase and infrastructure stack, compounding its knowledge through deployment. The RL approach, if it genuinely creates a flywheel where more deployments produce better models, could build a moat that even XBOW's $117 million can't easily replicate. And the structural conflict is real — traditional pentest firms can't automate without cannibalizing their billable hours, and defensive security vendors like CrowdStrike lack the offensive DNA to build attack agents. That gap is genuine. But the gap is already crowded with well-capitalized entrants racing to fill it, which means execution speed and capital efficiency become the differentiators — and those favor the better-funded competitors.

The founding team presents a pattern I've seen many times: an industry insider building what the industry expects. Ahmad Khan has a decade of application security experience at Cruise, Salesforce, and IBM. That's exactly the kind of expertise I treat with skepticism — he knows what the security industry considers possible, which means he's likely building within those boundaries. The most transformative security companies won't come from people who spent ten years doing manual pentests and decided to automate them. They'll come from someone who looks at the security problem from a completely different angle — maybe a game theory researcher, or someone from adversarial ML, or a biologist who understands immune systems. Prama's background building AI agents at Codegen is more interesting to me — that's adjacent expertise being applied to a new domain — but the overall team composition signals incremental automation, not fundamental rethinking. There's no evidence of the kind of contrarian courage I fund: attempting something the consensus deems impossible.

The timing is real but consensus. LLMs crossed the capability threshold for autonomous multi-step exploit reasoning in 2024-2025, and the cybersecurity talent shortage creates genuine market pull. But being right about timing when everyone else is also right about timing doesn't create advantage — it creates a race. When I invested in OpenAI in 2019, I was four years ahead of the crowd. Hex Security is arriving at the same moment as a half-dozen well-funded competitors. The rate-of-change in AI capability is clearly accelerating, which benefits the entire category, but doesn't differentially advantage Hex over XBOW or Novee. I look for companies skating to where the puck will be. In this market, a dozen players are all skating to the same spot simultaneously.

I'm passing. The problem is real, the timing is right, and the technology trajectory supports the category. But Hex Security is building what the industry expects, in a market that's already crowded with better-capitalized competitors, with a founding team of industry insiders rather than outsiders, targeting a consequence of success that — while commercially meaningful — doesn't rise to the scale of problems I consider too important not to work on. The company has de-risked itself into a competitive footrace where capital and speed matter more than vision. I'd tell these founders: you're clearly talented, your security instincts are sharp, but you're fighting over a $4 billion pie with companies that have 100x your capital. Find the problem in security that everyone says is impossible — the one that makes security testing itself obsolete, not just cheaper — and I'll write that check.
