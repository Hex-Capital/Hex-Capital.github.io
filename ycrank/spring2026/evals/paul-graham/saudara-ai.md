# Saudara AI -- Paul Graham Evaluation

"People repeatedly asked us: do you know any good Indonesian suppliers?" That origin story, pulled from Saudara AI's LinkedIn presence, is the most interesting thing in this dossier -- and it cuts in two directions simultaneously. On one hand, it's exactly the kind of organic problem discovery I look for. Nobody sits down to brainstorm and arrives at "I should become a middleman for Indonesian textile factories." This came from Edward Haryono and Jennifer Prasetyo's actual lives, their Indonesian heritage, their existing networks. Drew Houston built Dropbox because he kept forgetting his USB drive. The Saudara AI founders built a sourcing platform because people kept asking them for supplier introductions. The mechanism is the same: a recurring personal frustration that turns out to be widespread. On the other hand, the natural response to "people keep asking me for supplier introductions" is to become a sourcing consultant, not to build a startup. And I'm not sure Saudara AI has fully crossed that line.

Jennifer Prasetyo holds the CTO title at Saudara AI, but the dossier reveals no prior engineering experience, no GitHub repositories, no technical credentials of any kind. Edward Haryono's path -- business analyst at Amazon during the pandemic, Duke analytics degree, a blockchain book -- is the trajectory of a capable operator, not a hacker. Neither founder has publicly visible evidence of building software. This triggers one of my hardest red flags: "the founding team has no one who can build the product themselves." I've said you need three things -- tech expertise, an idea, and cofounders. Saudara AI appears to have the idea and the cofounders but I can't verify the tech expertise from anything public. At pre-seed, I accept that not every technical signal is visible, but the complete absence -- no repos, no prior engineering roles, no technical writing -- is unusual even for YC-stage companies. The "AI agent" layer Saudara AI describes could be a genuinely sophisticated system, or it could be a thin wrapper around API calls with most of the real work done by the humans in the loop. I can't tell, and that uncertainty matters when I'm writing a personal check.

Saudara AI's competitive position against Alibaba's Accio -- 10M+ monthly active users, 50M+ supplier profiles, backed by one of the largest companies on earth -- illustrates a structural problem. When I invested in Stripe, the Collisons were building infrastructure so technically deep that incumbents couldn't replicate it without rebuilding from scratch. Saudara AI's defensibility rests on supplier relationships and bicultural trust, not on technology. Those are real assets, but they're the assets of a traditional sourcing agency, not a software company. The "human in the loop at every stage" model, the 48-hour quote turnaround -- these are service-level commitments, not software metrics. I keep coming back to my definition: a startup is a company designed to grow fast. Services businesses grow linearly because revenue scales with headcount. Saudara AI needs to show me how the AI agent breaks that linearity, and right now the dossier gives me nothing to evaluate on that front.

The bull case for Saudara AI is real and I want to take it seriously. The "China Plus One" macro trend is not hypothetical -- Indonesian exports to the US hit $3.1B in a single month in July 2025, and the 23% year-over-year growth Saudara AI cites suggests accelerating demand. The schlep here is genuine: cross-border sourcing from Indonesia involves unreliable communications, opaque supply chains, language barriers, and multi-month vetting cycles. Most founders' unconscious filters would screen this opportunity out immediately. ClassDojo is my precedent for backing a company outside my usual "hackers building for hackers" pattern -- it tackled the unglamorous schlep of K-12 classroom management and reached unicorn status. Could Saudara AI be a similar override? The difference is that ClassDojo's founders demonstrated exceptional execution quality with visible product traction. Saudara AI has no public revenue data, no customer names, no usage metrics, and a CTO whose technical capabilities I cannot verify. The ClassDojo exception requires evidence of extraordinary execution to override my sector preferences, and that evidence is absent here.

Edward Haryono's LinkedIn headline -- "Boosting Indonesian Exports" -- signals the kind of earnest, mission-connected motivation I value. Saudara AI wasn't born from a TAM calculation or a trend report. The founders' Indonesian heritage gives them an authentic relationship to the problem that an English-only team couldn't manufacture. But earnestness alone doesn't overcome the structural concerns. The 19% US tariff on Indonesian goods, up from 3.3%, means Saudara AI's entire value proposition is one policy decision away from serious damage. Single-country concentration with tariff volatility is not a schlep that creates a moat -- it's a fragility that creates existential risk. And Sourcify, a YC W18 company already operating in Asian sourcing, could add Indonesia coverage without rebuilding from scratch.

The one verifiable condition that would flip me to Invest: show me that the AI agent can handle a sourcing workflow end-to-end with minimal human intervention, demonstrated by unit economics that improve as order volume grows rather than staying flat. That would prove Saudara AI is building a software company, not running a consultancy with a chatbot. Right now, I see an authentic problem discovered organically by founders with genuine cultural connection to the supply side -- and a business model that looks linear, built by a team whose technical capability I cannot assess. The organic discovery is strong enough to keep Saudara AI out of the Pass tier, but the founder signal isn't strong enough to push it higher.
