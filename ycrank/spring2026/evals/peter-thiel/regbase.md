# Regbase -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Natalie Aresta-Katz ran ESG regulatory research at Paul, Weiss and is now building the tool she wished she had — Regbase is the canonical domain-expert-tools-their-own-workflow company, which means its highest dimension is founder-market fit and its lowest is the structural moat that would make Regbase a power-law shape rather than a $30M trade sale. The signature is unambiguous: a founder who knows the exact pain, paired with an ex-Vercel staff engineer, attacking a market where Legora just printed a $5.6B valuation in April 2026. That signature names the outcome before I finish the dossier.

Stripe (2010) is the load-bearing analogy on the contrarian-secret dimension because Patrick Collison named a specific architectural failure mode of PayPal's developer experience that the incumbent could not fix without rebuilding its core. Regbase names competitors — Harvey, Legora, ChatGPT, local counsel — but the stated secret is that these "consistently miss" state-level, draft-bill, and non-English sources. That is a coverage gap, not an architectural blind spot. FiscalNote's $84.8M ARR pipeline is anchored on English-language federal data, true, but nothing in the Regbase dossier explains why FiscalNote or Legora *structurally cannot* extend to multilingual sub-national ingestion — just that they haven't yet. A secret that resolves to "we crawl more sources" is a head start, not a hidden truth. Aresta-Katz's Paul, Weiss workflow is the right place to find the secret; the dossier does not yet contain it.

Palantir (2003) is what monopoly trajectory looks like in this neighborhood: an integration moat so deep that the customer cannot rip you out without rebuilding their analytic stack. Regbase's hypothesized moat — accumulated alerts, saved queries, citation history — compounds in the right direction over 18 months only if customers configure thousands of jurisdictional watch-lists that would be painful to migrate. The problem is that Legora at $5.6B (TechCrunch, April 2026) and FiscalNote with PolicyNote already shipping have the capital to fund multilingual sub-national coverage before Regbase's switching costs lock in. Eighteen months of compounding for Regbase is also eighteen months of Legora's war chest pointed at the same wedge. The mechanism does not work unless Regbase reaches integration depth at a Fortune 100 before the incumbents close the coverage gap — and the dossier shows no named logo, no disclosed ACV, no signed LOI.

The dossier on Regbase's plan reads: "AI agents scan multi-jurisdictional sources across any country, state, or language." For the score to flip a tier on definite-vs-indefinite optimism, I would need a named 24-month roadmap — specific jurisdictions ingested by quarter, a stated accuracy benchmark Regbase commits to publishing, an architectural commitment to citation-grounded retrieval that Aresta-Katz refuses to pivot from under customer pressure. None of that surfaces. The Fondo START pod gave Regbase a stage to articulate a five-year thesis; the dossier extracts no such commitment. This is the indefinite-iteration default of a YC Spring 2026 cohort, not a SpaceX-style multi-year commitment.

Aresta-Katz is not Max Levchin, but the founder-market fit on Regbase is real in a way that the Affirm parallel partly illuminates: Levchin had shipped PayPal to scale and then went back into payments with a specific thesis about installment credit. Aresta-Katz oversaw the exact manual workflow Regbase automates — tracking laws across dozens of countries and all U.S. states for Paul, Weiss's ESG practice — and Ethan Shea built agentic infrastructure at Vercel as a Staff Engineer. That pairing is genuinely above the YC median. It is not, however, a shipped-to-scale operator re-up. There is no prior exit, no Olympiad-tier credentialed-outsider signal, no PayPal Mafia connection. Regbase's lineage is "competent domain expert plus competent senior IC," which clears the bar for a check from a generalist fund and falls below the bar for the Founders Fund concentration trade.

Regbase is software-only with a regulated end-customer, which is the Asana (2010) exception pattern — pure SaaS, no atoms — but without the Moskovitz-built-Facebook override that made Asana investable. Compliance and legal buyers do generate switching costs once Regbase becomes the system of record for jurisdictional alerts, but that is workflow lock-in, not regulatory certification depth. There is no Palantir-style classified-systems integration moat, no Anduril-style hardware capex barrier. The customer is regulated; Regbase is not.

Regbase reaches $3?8M ARR within 36 months serving boutique law firms and mid-market ESG consultancies through the Paul, Weiss alumni network, then gets acquired by Thomson Reuters, FiscalNote, or Bloomberg Government for $40?90M as Legora's multilingual feature ships and compresses the wedge — a competent outcome for the founders, a non-event for a power-law portfolio.

The bull case on Regbase stings on one specific point: multilingual sub-national regulatory ingestion is genuinely a schlep, and incumbents the size of FiscalNote (ARR down 22% YoY in their FY2025 10-Q) are visibly not executing. If Aresta-Katz's Paul, Weiss network converts into three named Fortune 100 contracts in the next twelve months, Regbase could compound into the system-of-record position before Legora reorients. I dismantle this with the Founders Fund manifesto read: Regbase's wedge is *coverage*, and coverage is what well-capitalized incumbents close in the eighteen months it takes a 3-person team to win three enterprise procurements. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict on Regbase: a signed Fortune 100 contract at disclosed ACV above $250K, or a third-party benchmark showing Regbase recalls 3x more regulatory events than Harvey or Legora on a controlled multilingual corpus. Until then, this is a competent acquisition target, not a fund-returner.
