# primitive -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I see here is a competitive dynamics problem that most pre-seed investors would wave away but that I think is structural. AgentMail -- also a YC company, one batch earlier -- has $6M from General Catalyst, Paul Graham as an angel, 500+ B2B customers, and user counts that tripled in a single week. When I wrote about "market-ending moves," the pattern I described was a category leader building compounding distribution advantages that make late entrants progressively harder to fund. AgentMail isn't at that stage yet, but it's building toward it, and Primitive is entering this market six to nine months behind with a team of one. The webhook-first versus mailbox-centric distinction is a real architectural difference, but it's not the kind of fundamental paradigm shift that lets a latecomer leapfrog an established player. It's a configuration choice. Stripe didn't win because it used a different HTTP method than PayPal -- it won because it reimagined the entire developer relationship with payments. I need to see that magnitude of architectural rethinking to get excited about entering behind a funded competitor.

The structural moment for agent communication infrastructure is genuine -- I'll give that. Agents proliferating from less than 5% to 40% of enterprise applications in roughly a year creates real infrastructure demand. AgentMail's growth spike after OpenClaw's launch is exactly the kind of adoption curve data I track. But here's what concerns me about Primitive's positioning: this market is no longer non-obvious. When General Catalyst writes a $6M check, when Google launches the A2A protocol through the Linux Foundation with 100+ partners, when Gartner is publishing adoption forecasts -- the structural moment has been identified, priced, and is being acted upon by well-capitalized players. My best returns come from markets that look wrong to everyone else. PagerDuty was "just alerting." Gusto was "just payroll." This market, right now, looks right to everyone. That's exactly when I start worrying about whether the asymmetric return opportunity has already been captured by earlier movers.

The bull case would require a few things to be true. First, that agent communication is genuinely a platform category, not a feature -- that email is just the wedge and the real opportunity is becoming the universal communication infrastructure layer across email, SMS, voice, and chat for every autonomous agent. If that's the case, the TAM is vastly larger than agent email alone, and there's room for multiple winners serving different architectural patterns. Second, that Byrd's experience building Actual AI (32 active pilots, $3.2M raised) gave him specific insight into agent communication pain points that the AgentMail team doesn't have -- that he's building from operator scars, not from market opportunity analysis. His engineering experience at Google, Meta, AWS, and Microsoft means he's seen large-scale communication infrastructure from the inside. If Primitive's webhook-first approach genuinely serves a different developer use case than AgentMail's inbox model -- the way AWS Lambda serves different workloads than EC2 despite both being "compute" -- then parallel success is possible. That's a real scenario, and Byrd's credentials suggest he could execute it.

But several things push me toward a pass. The relationship between Actual AI and Primitive is undocumented -- Byrd's LinkedIn still lists Actual AI. I need to understand whether this is a pivot, a parallel venture, or what happened to those 32 pilots and $3.2M in funding. That ambiguity alone would slow any diligence process. More fundamentally, I apply my "software-driven versus software-aware" test here: is routing email to webhooks a business where software is the core competitive advantage, or is it a thin orchestration layer on top of unchanged email transport infrastructure? SMTP doesn't change. DNS doesn't change. What changes is a routing rule. That's valuable -- but it's the kind of value that AWS, Google, or any agent framework company can replicate as a feature. The Google A2A protocol, governed by the Linux Foundation, is a structural headwind that reduces the need for proprietary communication layers. When I invested in Coinbase, regulatory barriers created a moat no open-source project could replicate. Here, an open standard backed by Google and 100+ partners could commoditize the proprietary layer Primitive is building.

I keep coming back to the single-miracle test. What's Primitive's one miracle? If it's "build better email routing for agents" -- that's a feature, not a miracle. If it's "become the default communication protocol for all agent-to-agent and agent-to-human interaction" -- that requires winning developer adoption against a funded competitor, avoiding commoditization by cloud providers, navigating around an open protocol backed by Google, and expanding beyond email into a multi-channel platform. That's at least three miracles compounding. The companies I back at this stage have identified their one impossible thing and organized everything around it. I don't see that clarity here.
