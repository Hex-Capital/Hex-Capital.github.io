# Dispatch -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most striking feature of Dispatch isn't the reentry physics or the heat shield test -- it's the upstream dependency structure. Dispatch is building logistics infrastructure for a market whose customers are themselves pre-revenue startups trying to prove that manufacturing in microgravity works. Their revenue depends on Varda-class companies scaling orbital production to the point where third-party downmass becomes a real purchasing decision. That's a miracle built on top of someone else's miracle, which is exactly the compounding low-probability pattern I avoid. When I backed Harvey, the single miracle -- LLM fidelity reaching legal-grade accuracy -- was being delivered by foundation model labs on a trajectory I could track. Dispatch's external dependency doesn't have the same predictable curve: the number of companies with active orbital manufacturing programs is in the single digits, and most of them (Varda, Space Forge) are vertically integrated, meaning they build their own return capability rather than outsourcing downmass.

The competitive landscape makes this harder. Varda has $329M in funding, $48M in 2024 revenue, and multiple completed orbital return missions. Inversion Space has $125M between equity and its Space Force contract. Space Forge has $50M with NATO backing. The Factories in Space database tracks 69 reentry vehicle entries. This is not a "too boring" or "too niche" market that other investors are ignoring -- it's a market where serious capital is already deployed and at least one company has a multi-year operational head start. When I look for non-obvious markets, I'm looking for categories that trigger dismissive reactions from other investors. Orbital manufacturing reentry is getting the opposite reaction -- it's consensus enough that 15+ companies are pursuing it, and government agencies are signing $50M+ contracts. The structural moment is real, but it's already been recognized and capitalized.

The 100x heat shield cost claim is worth pausing on, because if validated at orbital scale, that kind of cost advantage is exactly the sort of structural wedge I find compelling. The parallel to SpaceX's early reusability thesis is obvious: if you can dramatically reduce the cost of a critical component, you reshape the economics of the entire value chain. But ground-testing a heat shield behind a rocket engine in the Mojave is not the same as surviving sustained hypersonic heating during orbital reentry, managing plasma dynamics, nailing guidance and navigation, and recovering the vehicle intact. The gap between ground demonstration and orbital validation is enormous in both engineering complexity and capital requirements. This isn't a software company where you can iterate weekly -- each orbital test costs millions, takes months of regulatory preparation, and requires launch manifest coordination. The capital needed to close that gap dwarfs what a 2-person pre-seed team can deploy.

The bull case is that Dispatch is positioned at a Webvan-to-Instacart inflection for space logistics. If launch costs continue declining and even two or three of the orbital manufacturing companies reach production scale, the downmass market could expand 10-50x within a decade. Dispatch's platform vision -- "Autonomous Space Stations" providing lights-out manufacturing hosting with integrated return vehicles -- is a genuine distribution play. A reentry vehicle becomes the initial wedge, and then payload hosting, radiation testing, deorbiting, and cargo return flow through the same orbital infrastructure as additional product lines. That's the kind of product-to-distribution trajectory I look for. And the founders have relevant pedigree: both came from Astranis, Mello led avionics there and has Zoox and Apple on his resume. The heat shield test shows they can move from concept to hardware quickly. If I squint, I can see the structural moment -- but the honest assessment is that the market needs another 3-5 years of upstream development before Dispatch's specific niche has enough demand to build a real business.

I keep coming back to the capital intensity problem. This isn't the Anduril exception. When I led Anduril's Series D, I was investing in a company that had already achieved meaningful scale in a $750B+ incumbent defense market, with a structural vacancy created by Google walking away from Project Maven, and regulatory barriers (ITAR, security clearances) that created genuine moats for early movers. Dispatch is entering a small, emerging market with 15+ competitors, several of which have 500x more capital. The FAA licensing and ITAR requirements that all reentry vehicle companies face are barriers, but they don't create a differential moat for Dispatch specifically -- every competitor has to clear the same hurdles. The founders are technically capable, but the structural setup requires them to raise hundreds of millions to reach orbital demonstration while competing against companies that already have that capital and are further along the development curve.
