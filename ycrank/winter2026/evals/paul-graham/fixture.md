# Fixture -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Fixture is that the founders are genuinely impressive -- and the second thing I notice is that they're building in exactly the kind of crowded, consensus category I worry about most. Matt Riley and Quin Hoxie co-founded Swiftype in YC W12, got it acquired by Elastic, worked together there for years at senior technical leadership levels, and are now back for a second company together. A 10+ year co-founder relationship with a prior exit is one of the strongest structural signals you can find at pre-seed. When I funded Emmett Shear and Justin Kan the second time around, it was because I'd already seen what they were made of. Riley and Hoxie have an even stronger version of that signal: they didn't just survive failure, they executed a successful outcome and chose to come back. People who've already gotten rich and start another company are usually doing it because they genuinely want to build something, not because they need a paycheck.

But the idea makes me uneasy. "AI-native CRM for startups" is not a problem hiding in plain sight that everyone unconsciously avoids. It's the opposite. Attio has raised $116M and literally uses the phrase "AI-native CRM" as its positioning. Clay is at a $3.1 billion valuation with $100M in projected revenue. Folk, Affinity, and half a dozen others are all in this space. When I said the most impressive YC companies I'd seen recently were not working on AI, I was pointing at exactly this pattern: smart founders picking a hot category because it's hot, not because they stumbled into a problem nobody else wanted to touch. The CRM-for-startups problem has been visible for fifteen years. This is not Stripe, where everyone knew payments were broken but nobody wanted to deal with the banks. This is a market where dozens of well-funded teams are already fighting over the same territory.

The organic discovery question is where it gets interesting. Matt spent two batches as a YC Visiting Partner, sitting across the table from startups watching them struggle with HubSpot and spreadsheets. That's not the deepest form of organic -- he wasn't a startup founder who personally lost a deal because his CRM failed him -- but it's real. He was observing the problem from close range, repeatedly, with dozens of companies. And the search-infrastructure background from Swiftype and Elastic is directly relevant to building a context-aggregation layer. These aren't random founders who picked CRM from a trend report. They have technical DNA that maps to the core problem of unifying messy, multi-channel communication data. The question is whether "observed the problem as an advisor" generates the same depth of insight as "suffered from the problem as a user."

The strongest bull case for Fixture is the agent-native architecture bet. Most CRM companies are building better UIs with AI features bolted on. Fixture is betting that the primary interface for CRM will be AI agents -- that you'll access your deal context through Claude Code, not through a dashboard. If that bet is right, building for it from day one is a structural advantage that Attio and HubSpot can't easily replicate, because their entire revenue model depends on humans sitting in front of screens paying per seat. It's the kind of architectural insight that only technical founders would have, and it creates a distribution channel (developer tools, AI coding assistants) that traditional CRM companies won't pursue. This is genuinely interesting. But it requires two things to be true simultaneously: that AI agents become the primary GTM workflow interface, and that Fixture executes faster than competitors who see the same shift. That's not quite "multiple miracles," but it's close.

The technical founder signal is the strongest dimension here. Quin Hoxie has 63 public repos on GitHub, a Hugging Face presence, and was a Distinguished Engineer at Elastic. Riley has an EE background and was an early engineer at Scribd before co-building Swiftype. These are hackers who build things, not managers who hire engineers. That matters. But it's not enough to overcome the fundamental market-positioning concern. When your core positioning is indistinguishable from a competitor with $116M in funding, you need something other than "we'll build it better" -- and I don't yet see what that something is. The "zero data lock-in" philosophy is admirable but strategically puzzling; you're explicitly telling customers it's easy to leave, which means your retention depends entirely on being better every single day. That works if you're dramatically better. The evidence for that isn't here yet.

I'd be more excited about this company if the AI-agent-native bet were more validated -- if there were even a handful of technical founders using Fixture through Claude Code and saying "I can't go back to a dashboard." That would be the kind of organic pull signal that changes everything. Right now, I see a strong team that has chosen a crowded battlefield. The founders are the right founders to build this. I'm just not sure this is the right thing for them to build.
