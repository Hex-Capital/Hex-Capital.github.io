# matforge -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Here's what caught my attention immediately: the CEO discovered materials during his Stanford PhD that were adopted into Intel and TSMC interconnect roadmaps. At pre-seed. Before the company existed. I've seen thousands of founding stories, and it's extremely rare for a founder to walk in with their work already validated by the exact companies they plan to sell to. It's like if Drew Houston had built Dropbox and half of Silicon Valley was already using it before he applied to YC. That credential alone puts Akash Ramdas in a different category from the typical "we identified a market opportunity" founders. But I want to be careful — "adopted into roadmaps" could mean a lot of things. An academic citation in a research roadmap is very different from a purchase order. The dossier flags this ambiguity, and I can't resolve it from what's here.

The organic problem discovery is the strongest signal in this company. Akash didn't sit down and brainstorm "what's a big market I could apply AI to." He spent years of his life screening 15,000+ materials by hand using first-principles calculations, doing the painful computational and experimental work of semiconductor materials discovery at Stanford. He lived inside the future that Matforge is building for. When he says this process takes 10+ years, he's not quoting a market report — he's describing his own experience. That's exactly the origin story I look for. The best startups come from people who are building the thing they themselves desperately need, and Akash is building automation for the exact workflow he spent his PhD doing manually.

The schlep factor here is massive, and that's what makes me lean forward. Semiconductor materials discovery is one of those problems that makes most founders' unconscious recoil. You need to understand quantum mechanics, fab-compatible process integration, regulatory constraints around novel materials in production environments, and you're selling to maybe ten companies on Earth with 12-24 month sales cycles. The mega-funded competitors — Periodic Labs at $300M, Lila Sciences at $550M, CuspAI at $154M — are all going broad precisely because going narrow into semiconductor-specific materials requires the kind of deep domain expertise that money can't easily buy. A generalist AI-for-science platform can hire materials scientists, but they can't replicate someone whose specific compounds are already on Intel's roadmap. The schlep is the moat.

The founder pairing is well-constructed. Akash brings the domain depth — he is the semiconductor materials expert. Advaith brings the AI systems capability — he built long-horizon autonomous agents at Persona AI before it was acquired by Luma Labs, then worked on foundation model post-training at Luma. That's exactly the technical profile you need to build "a swarm of AI agents" that can autonomously navigate materials search spaces. They're IIT Madras batchmates from 2019, which means seven years of knowing each other — not strangers who met at a co-founder matching event. Advaith's work on AI4Bharat, publishing India's first sign language video dataset and winning Microsoft's AI for Accessibility Grant, tells me something about his character that his resume doesn't: he builds things because they matter to him, not because they're on a career optimization path.

What worries me is the prediction-to-synthesis gap. MIT Technology Review noted in December 2025 that there's been no "ChatGPT moment" in materials discovery, and the bottleneck is bridging AI prediction to physical synthesis and validation. Matforge's AI agents can screen millions of candidate materials computationally, but a semiconductor fab doesn't adopt a material because a model says it has good properties — they adopt it after months of physical testing. This is where the two-person team feels thin. Akash has bridged this gap in an academic context, but doing it commercially at startup speed requires either lab partnerships or wet-lab infrastructure they don't appear to have yet. And the concentrated customer base — fewer than ten major foundries — means each relationship is critical and each rejection is devastating. There's no "do things that don't scale" playbook for selling to TSMC.

The bull case is genuinely compelling: if Akash's Intel/TSMC relationships are real commercial relationships (not just academic citations), and if the AI agent system can reliably identify candidates that survive physical validation, then Matforge has a wedge into the most capital-intensive industry on Earth with essentially no competition at their level of specificity. The value creation from discovering even one novel interconnect material that gets adopted at scale would be enormous — we're talking about materials that go into every advanced chip manufactured globally. The IP licensing model could produce the kind of superlinear returns where how well you've done determines how well you do: each validated material builds credibility and data that makes the next discovery faster. That's the compounding dynamic I look for. The Bindwell comparison is apt — teenage founders tackling pesticide chemistry, an unsexy domain with real intellectual property at the end. Matforge has the same structure but with more credentialed founders and a clearer path to the customer.

What I can't see from this dossier is the resourcefulness. I have credentials and publications, but I don't have stories. I don't know what Akash did when his first synthesis failed, or how Advaith got Persona AI's agent system to work when the telecom clients kept changing requirements. The animal quality — that obsessive intensity that passes through professional into something else — I can infer it from Akash spending years screening 15,000 materials, but I haven't seen it in action. At pre-seed, I'm making a bet on founder-market fit and schlep blindness more than on demonstrated scrappiness, and that's a less confident bet than I'd like.
