# Arzana -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The competitive landscape tells you everything you need to know about whether a secret exists. Endeavor AI raised $7 million from Craft Ventures pursuing the same thesis — AI automation for manufacturing workflows. Canals AI converts customer requests into quotes across formats. Distro targets wholesale distributors with chatbot interfaces. When I can find three funded companies building variations of the same product in the same vertical within minutes of reading a dossier, there is no secret. There is a trend. "AI will transform manufacturing front offices" is not a contrarian insight that makes knowledgeable investors uncomfortable — it is the consensus of every enterprise software investor who has spent thirty seconds thinking about where LLMs intersect with document-heavy workflows. The existence of a competitive landscape slide comparing five similar startups is itself the diagnosis: this market is competitive by definition, and competition destroys value for everyone.

The pivot from Brilla — gourmet meal delivery on Stanford's campus — to manufacturing AI tells a familiar and unfavorable story. Founders searching for a market rather than founders who possessed a burning conviction about what the world needed and then built toward it. The founding date discrepancy (the website says 2023, the YC page says 2025) reinforces this reading: the entity likely predates the thesis. A CEO who has taken "only one coding class" building an AI product is not inherently disqualifying — I have backed plenty of non-technical founders — but it means the entire technical differentiation rests on two engineers with limited public track records, and the company cannot out-engineer Endeavor AI's larger, better-capitalized team. When your moat is execution speed in a market with no structural barriers, you are in a race, and races are won by whoever has the most resources and stamina. That is competition, not monopoly.

I will grant what Arzana has earned: the distribution is real and genuinely unusual for a pre-seed company. Twenty-plus plants served, nine named logos including Schneider National and Zeiss, a customer testimonial claiming quote turnaround dropped from five days to one. The dual presence in San Francisco and Okoboji, Iowa — the CEO's hometown — reveals a founder who understands that selling to manufacturers means showing up where manufacturers are, not sending cold emails from a WeWork. The "Founding Field Engineer" role is the right instinct: manufacturing procurement officers do not adopt software from a demo video, they adopt it when someone sits in their office and configures it against their specific ERP instance. This is distribution sophistication that most twenty-one-year-old YC founders lack entirely. If I were Paul Graham, this traction signal and founder grit would be the entire evaluation. But distribution excellence in a competitive market is a treadmill — you run faster and faster to stay in the same place, because your competitors are running the same playbook with more capital.

The bull case requires believing that the ERP integration web — eleven-plus connectors today — compounds into an insurmountable switching cost moat. Each integration deployed into a customer's operational stack creates friction against displacement. Over time, workflow-specific training data from real manufacturing RFQs, purchase orders, and customer communications could create a domain-specific AI advantage that horizontal competitors cannot replicate without equivalent deployment scale. If the incumbent ERP vendors genuinely face a business model conflict — building lightweight AI layers that cannibalize their professional services revenue — then the window for a specialized entrant could remain open longer than the competitive landscape would otherwise allow. In this scenario, Arzana's Iowa-rooted distribution network becomes the beachhead for a manufacturing-specific platform that eventually processes a significant share of American industrial commerce. I take this scenario seriously enough to acknowledge it. But the same integration-as-moat argument has been made about every middleware company in history, and it almost never produces winner-take-all dynamics. ERP integrations are a retention mechanism, not a monopoly structure.

The core technology — LLM-based document parsing and workflow automation — is fundamentally a software commodity. There is no hardware component, no physical-world manipulation, no regulatory barrier, no proprietary dataset that could not be assembled by a well-funded competitor within a year. Arzana serves manufacturers, which gives it atoms-adjacency, but it is not itself an atoms company. It is a software wrapper around other companies' physical operations, using foundation models that every competitor has equal access to. When I backed Palantir, the intelligence community needed something that did not exist and could not be built by casual entrants due to security clearance requirements, classified data handling, and the sheer complexity of intelligence analysis workflows. Arzana's customers need something that already exists in multiple versions from multiple vendors. The question is not whether the product works — it clearly does — but whether any structural force prevents the market from fragmenting into a half-dozen adequate competitors splitting the value pool.

I pass. These are energetic founders with genuine distribution instincts serving a real market need. But the absence of a contrarian secret, the presence of funded competitors pursuing identical theses, and the commodity nature of the underlying technology mean this is a company that could become a nice $50 million business while making its founders and investors modestly wealthy. That outcome is structurally irrelevant to how the power law distributes returns. I need the company that makes the last great development in its market and holds that position for decades. Arzana is making the same development as Endeavor, Canals, and Distro — simultaneously, in the same market, with the same tools.
