# Orthogonal -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I see here is Stripe. Not as a comparable -- as a direct competitor building the same product surface with 50 million x402 transactions already processed and distribution to millions of developers. I backed Stripe early because their developer API created a distribution channel that made it nearly impossible for competitors to displace them. That same distribution channel is now pointing directly at agentic API payments. When I write about "market-ending moves," this is what I mean: the incumbent with the strongest developer distribution in payments infrastructure is actively building in this exact category. Orthogonal's differentiation -- abstracting away blockchain settlement, curating API partners -- is a product feature, not a structural moat. Stripe could add fiat-denominated API billing to their existing merchant dashboard in a quarter.

The structural moment is real but it's consensus, not non-obvious. Every major payments player has announced an agentic commerce initiative in the past twelve months: Stripe's x402/ACP, Mastercard's Agent Pay, Coinbase's partnership with Stripe on Base settlement. When Mastercard and Stripe are both building in your category, the market has been identified -- and the question shifts from "is this market real?" to "can a two-person pre-seed startup capture it against incumbents with overwhelming distribution?" Agentic payments infrastructure is not PagerDuty circa 2012, where the market seemed too boring for anyone to notice. This is the opposite: it's the market everyone noticed simultaneously.

The bull case would require two things to be true. First, that agents will interact with hundreds of long-tail APIs that Stripe doesn't natively support, creating a genuine aggregation opportunity -- similar to how app stores aggregated mobile distribution even though individual developers could have built their own. Second, that Orthogonal's curated marketplace creates enough switching costs through API discovery and unified billing that developers stay even when Stripe offers a "good enough" alternative. If the agentic API ecosystem fragments into thousands of specialized services -- weather, legal data, background checks, compliance lookups -- and no single protocol captures them, then a developer-friendly aggregation layer could build real marketplace dynamics. The YC network seeding the supply side with companies like Fiber AI and People Data Labs is a smart cold-start strategy. But this path requires the long-tail API ecosystem to materialize at scale before Stripe's protocol approach standardizes the market from the top down. That's a race I wouldn't bet on a two-person team to win.

The founders have relevant domain experience -- Pickett built billing at Vercel and payments at Coinbase, Sogut built APIs at Google including Maps and reCAPTCHA. That's a strong combination for this specific problem. Sogut's ICPC credentials (24th place globally) signal serious engineering capability. They've shipped a working MCP server with 16+ integrations, which shows execution. But the single-miracle test breaks down here: the company needs marketplace liquidity AND to outrun Stripe's distribution. That's two miracles, and the second one is particularly low-probability given that Stripe's developer moat is exactly the kind of distribution channel advantage I look for. The core product -- API aggregation, unified billing, key management -- is technically replicable, as the dossier correctly identifies. Without a proprietary data advantage, regulatory moat, or technology that's genuinely hard to build, the defensibility rests entirely on marketplace network effects. And as I've written, network effects unwind just as fast as they build, especially when the marketplace has only 16 listings.

The technology cycle positioning is the one dimension where I'd give real credit. MCP was released in late 2024, AI coding assistants hit mainstream developer adoption through 2025, and agent-to-API interaction volume is clearly inflecting upward. Building the aggregation layer now, before the market crystallizes around a single protocol, is the right timing instinct. But being at the right point in the adoption curve matters less when the most formidable payments infrastructure company in the world arrived at the same point simultaneously. Harvey succeeded because no incumbent was building legal AI workflow tools -- the structural vacancy was real. Here, the vacancy is crowded.
