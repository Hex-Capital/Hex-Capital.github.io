# Oximy -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The core technology is a fork of mitmproxy. That single fact tells me nearly everything I need to know. When a company's foundational component is an open-source network proxy with a classification layer on top, you are not looking at a technological discontinuity -- you are looking at a systems integration project. CrowdStrike's Falcon agent already monitors every network connection from enterprise endpoints. Zscaler's proxy already inspects traffic at the network boundary. Adding a rule that flags traffic to `api.openai.com` or `claude.ai` is not a new company -- it is a feature request. The "3,500+ AI tools" taxonomy sounds impressive until you realize it is a URL classification list, the kind of thing a competent security engineering team builds in a quarter. The sensor is open-source. The detection logic is pattern-matching on known domains. Nothing here creates the kind of proprietary technological advantage that would make an existing approach structurally obsolete.

What is the secret? "Enterprises have dozens of AI tools with zero visibility." But this is not a secret -- it is a consensus observation. Every CISO I know is worried about shadow AI. Gartner is publishing reports about it. Nudge Security raised $22.5M in November 2025 explicitly to address AI governance. Grip Security has $66M. Productiv has $73M. When every analyst, every buyer, and every competitor agrees that the problem exists, you are not exploiting a contrarian truth -- you are entering a market that has already attracted mimetic competition. The Girardian dynamics here are unmistakable: multiple companies imitating each other's feature sets, fighting for the same CISOs, attending the same cybersecurity conferences, deploying the same "AI governance" messaging. This is a market where the thesis is obvious, which means no one will earn monopoly returns from it. The projected $68 billion TAM by 2035 is precisely the kind of enormous, generic market that signals competitive destruction rather than monopoly creation.

The bull case would require believing that Oximy's desktop agent approach -- system-level network monitoring distributed via MDM -- represents a structurally superior architecture that cannot be replicated by incumbents. If the agentless approaches of Nudge Security and traditional SaaS management platforms genuinely cannot detect browser-based and unsanctioned AI tool usage, and if endpoint security vendors like CrowdStrike decide not to add AI classification to their existing agents, then Oximy could own a narrow but real gap in enterprise visibility. The financial services and healthcare verticals are reasonable starting points where compliance pressure makes the pain acute. And the founder has genuine execution markers -- a prior startup with real revenue, Google coding competition finishes, experience at an AI company backed by Sam Altman. "Processing millions of requests daily" for enterprise customers, if accurate, suggests the product works and has found buyers. If you squint, there is a version of this story where the AI-specific focus creates a category that the broad SaaS management platforms cannot credibly enter, and where the desktop agent creates enough switching cost through MDM deployment that customers do not churn when CrowdStrike adds a competing feature.

But I do not believe that version of the story. The fundamental problem is that Oximy occupies a position where it is structurally vulnerable from above and from below. From above, endpoint security platforms that already have agents deployed on every enterprise laptop can add AI tool detection as a feature -- and they will, because their customers are already asking for it. From below, agentless approaches like Nudge Security's email-plus-browser method are lighter to deploy and face less procurement friction than installing yet another agent on corporate endpoints. Oximy's agent-based approach is caught between these two forces without the technology moat to withstand either. The mitmproxy fork operating as a network proxy also creates a specific adoption problem: enterprise legal and privacy teams will scrutinize a tool that intercepts all employee network traffic, which lengthens sales cycles in exactly the compliance-sensitive verticals the company is targeting. This is not a zero-to-one company -- it is a competent one-to-n execution in a crowded market, which is the structure that produces exactly the kind of margin compression that makes investing pointless.

Naman Ambavi shows execution velocity -- founding Easocare at 19, building a COVID tracker to 10M users, working at Induced AI on LLMs -- but I do not see the kind of definite, singular vision that would make me believe this founder will hold a contrarian position against the market. The company's positioning is standard enterprise SaaS language: governance, visibility, compliance. There is no intellectual framework here about why AI governance requires a fundamentally different approach, no thesis that the experts would reject. A solo founder running enterprise sales into financial services and healthcare with a team of five, competing against companies with 10x to 15x more capital, needs either a genuine technological edge or a secret that makes the competition irrelevant. I see neither.
