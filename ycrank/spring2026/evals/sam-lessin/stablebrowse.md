# StableBrowse -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's what jumps off the page: this company is entering one of the most consensus-hot infrastructure categories in AI right now. Browserbase has $67.5 million and a thousand customers. Browser Use has $17 million from Felicis and 38,000 GitHub stars. Kernel has Accel behind it. Airtop, TinyFish — combined, over $180 million has already been deployed into "browser infrastructure for AI agents." Every sophisticated seed investor in the Valley has already placed a bet in this exact space. So when I look at StableBrowse, the first question isn't "is this good?" — it's "where is the mispricing?" And I'm struggling to find it. The upside thesis for this category is already fully priced across five well-funded competitors. This isn't Solana at four cents where the market said Ethereum won and nobody else was buying. This is a category where the market is screaming "we believe" and writing hundred-million-dollar checks to prove it.

The architectural argument is the one thing that gives me pause. StableBrowse claims to strip the rendering engine entirely — no pixels, just semantic output. Every competitor is wrapping standard Chromium, which means they're paying the full rendering tax: half a gig of RAM per session, slow cold starts, the whole visual pipeline burning cycles to produce screenshots that an LLM then has to parse back into text. If StableBrowse's approach actually works — 85MB RAM, sub-80ms cold starts, 10x cost reduction — that's a fundamentally different cost curve. It's not "better Chromium management." It's a different architectural bet. I've made money on exactly that kind of divergence: everyone optimizing the existing approach while someone rebuilds from a different layer of the stack. That's the bull case. If you squint, it looks like the Solana argument: different consensus mechanism when everyone assumed Ethereum's architecture was the only game.

But here's why the analogy breaks down. First, building a browser engine that correctly handles the full chaos of the modern web — SPAs, Shadow DOM, iframes, WebSockets, every JavaScript framework's unique insanity — without a rendering pipeline is one of the hardest engineering problems in software. Chrome has thousands of engineers. Firefox has hundreds. These four founders have... an IBM Full Stack certification and a Purdue startup expo win. I have literally no evidence anyone on this team has ever built browser internals. No public GitHub repos. No open-source track record. No confirmed prior work at browser-engine scale. The ambition is insane, which is half of what I want — but I need the other half. I need the sane person who makes me believe the insane thing is actually achievable. I can't find that signal anywhere in this dossier.

Second — and this is the kill shot — this is AI infrastructure. I've been saying this for two years: AI is sustaining technology. It's perfect for incumbents. You know who has the best browser engine engineers on Earth? Google. The Chrome team could build a rendering-free headless mode as a feature flag. Microsoft could do the same with Edge. Any hyperscaler could offer "semantic browser as a service" tomorrow. The structural durability here is near zero. There are no physical assets, no protocol-level network effects, no regulatory moats, no creator audiences. The moat is "we built it first and developers adopted our API." That's an execution moat in a category where five better-funded competitors are already executing. And if Google decides this matters — which they will, because Gemini agents need to browse the web — it's over. This is the Adobe problem I keep talking about, except replace Adobe with Google Chrome.

The bull case requires believing three things simultaneously: (1) the rendering-free architecture is technically viable at production scale across the full web, (2) these specific four founders can execute it better than Browserbase's well-funded team or Google's browser engineers, and (3) developer ecosystem lock-in happens fast enough that incumbents can't catch up. If all three are true, you have a company that makes every Chromium-wrapping competitor obsolete overnight — a genuine infrastructure paradigm shift worth billions. That's a real outcome. But it requires three independent miracles from a team with no observable track record, in a category where the market has already priced in enormous upside. The risk is emphatically not in the price — it's hidden behind the assumption that "different architecture" equals "better outcome."

I keep coming back to capital discipline. To compete against Browserbase ($67.5 million), Kernel ($22 million), and the rest, StableBrowse will need to raise aggressively and fast. This is a land-grab market where developer mindshare compounds. That means factory-farm fundraising — seed to A to B in rapid succession, burning cash to acquire developers before the window closes. That's exactly the conveyor belt model I think is dying. The founders who survive the next cycle are the ones who can build a business that works if they never raise again. I see no evidence this team has thought about that, and the competitive dynamics of this market won't let them.

Pass. The architecture is interesting but the pricing is wrong — not wrong in the "market is too pessimistic" way that makes me money, but wrong in the "market is euphoric about this category and this is the least-capitalized player with the least-proven team" way. I'd rather be contrarian than consensus, and browser-infra-for-agents is about as consensus as it gets in 2026.
